Entropy is the basic numerical invariant of a discrete distribution. It measures the logarithmic effective size of the support, with probabilities counted according to their mass rather than only by their cardinality.

Entropy

Definition. For a finite random variable \(X\sim P\) on alphabet \(\mathcal X\), the entropy is \[ H(X)=H(P)=-\sum_{x\in\mathcal X}P(x)\log P(x), \] with the convention \(0\log0=0\). For a pair \((X,Y)\), define \(H(X,Y)\) from its joint law, and define conditional entropy by \(H(X\mid Y)=H(X,Y)-H(Y)\).

Chain rule and conditioning

Proposition. For finite random variables, \[ H(X_1,\ldots,X_n)=\sum_{i=1}^n H(X_i\mid X_1,\ldots,X_{i-1}). \] Moreover \(0\le H(X\mid Y)\le H(X)\), and equality on the right holds exactly when \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent.

Proof by factorization

Proof. The joint mass factors as \(p(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=\prod_i p(x_i\mid x_1, \ldots,x_{i-1})\). Taking \(-\log\), integrating with respect to the joint law, and using linearity gives the chain rule. Nonnegativity follows because each conditional entropy is an average of ordinary entropies. The inequality \(H(X\mid Y)\le H(X)\) is equivalent to nonnegativity of mutual information, proved from the log-sum inequality on the next page.

Interpretation as exponential volume

Remark. Entropy should be read as an asymptotic volume exponent: for independent samples from \(P\), most probability lies on about \(\exp(nH(P))\) typical strings. That operational interpretation is made precise by the asymptotic equipartition property.
