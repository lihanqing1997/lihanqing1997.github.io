10. Sanov theorem and information projections

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/information-theory/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/information-theory/method-of-types/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/information-theory/information-spectrum/" %}

Sanov theorem turns relative entropy into the rate function for empirical measures. The theorem belongs to large deviations, but its geometry is central to information theory.

Empirical-measure deviations

Theorem. Let \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\sim P\) iid on a finite alphabet and let \(\widehat P_n\) be the empirical law. For a set \(\Gamma\) of probability measures, \[ P(\widehat P_n\in\Gamma)\approx \exp\{-n\inf_{Q\in\Gamma}D(Q\|P)\}, \] with the usual interior and closure qualifications for exact lower and upper bounds.

Information projection

Definition. An information projection of \(P\) onto a constraint set \(\Gamma\) is a minimizer \[ Q^\star\in\operatorname*{argmin}_{Q\in\Gamma}D(Q\|P). \] It is the most likely empirical law among those satisfying the constraint.

Proof from types

Proof. Group sequences by type. The number of possible types is polynomial in \(n\), while the probability of a type class is exponential with rate \(-D(Q\|P)\). Polynomial factors vanish on the \(n^{-1}\log\) scale, so the largest admissible type controls the probability.

Conditional limit principle

Remark. Conditioned on the rare event \(\widehat P_n\in\Gamma\), empirical laws concentrate near the information projection when it is unique. This is the finite-alphabet form of maximum entropy under constraints.
