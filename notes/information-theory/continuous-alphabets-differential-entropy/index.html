Differential entropy is useful but delicate: unlike discrete entropy, it is not invariant under smooth changes of coordinates. Mutual information and relative entropy remain invariant quantities.

Differential entropy

Definition. If \(X\) has density \(f\) on \(\mathbb R^d\), its differential entropy is \[ h(X)=-\int f(x)\log f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x, \] when the integral is well-defined.

Change of variables

Proposition. If \(Y=g(X)\) for a smooth bijection \(g\), then \[ h(Y)=h(X)+\mathbb E\log |\det Dg(X)|. \] Thus differential entropy depends on the coordinate scale.

Invariant information

Theorem. Relative entropy and mutual information are invariant under one-to-one measurable transformations. In particular, \(I(X;Y)\) is the robust continuous-alphabet analogue of dependence information.

Proof by Jacobian cancellation

Proof. Under a smooth bijection, densities acquire reciprocal Jacobian factors. In ratios such as \(f/g\), or \(f_{XY}/(f_Xf_Y)\), these factors cancel. Differential entropy alone has no ratio and keeps the Jacobian term.
