8. Hahn-Banach theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/closed-graph-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/separation-of-convex-sets/" %}

Sublinear domination

Definition. A function \(p:X\to\mathbb R\) on a real vector space is sublinear if \(p(x+y)\le p(x)+p(y)\) and \(p(\lambda x)=\lambda p(x)\) for every \(\lambda\ge0\). A linear functional \(f\) on \(M\subset X\) is dominated by \(p\) if \(f(m)\le p(m)\) for all \(m\in M\).

Lemma. Let \(M\subset X\) be a subspace, \(x_0\notin M\), and \(f:M\to\mathbb R\) linear with \(f\le p\). There exists \(\alpha\in\mathbb R\) such that \(F(m+tx_0)=f(m)+t\alpha\) defines a linear extension to \(M+\mathbb Rx_0\) still satisfying \(F\le p\).

Proof. For \(m_1,m_2\in M\), sublinearity gives \(f(m_1)+f(m_2)=f(m_1+m_2)\le p(m_1+m_2)\le p(m_1+x_0)+p(m_2-x_0)\). Hence \(f(m_1)-p(m_1+x_0)\le p(m_2-x_0)-f(m_2)\).

Therefore the interval \([\sup_m(f(m)-p(m+x_0)),\inf_m(p(m-x_0)-f(m))]\) is nonempty. Choose \(\alpha\) in it. The inequalities \(\alpha\ge f(m)-p(m+x_0)\) and \(\alpha\le p(m-x_0)-f(m)\) are exactly the cases \(t>0\) and \(t<0\), after rescaling, needed to prove \(f(m)+t\alpha\le p(m+tx_0)\).

Hahn-Banach extension theorem

Theorem. Let \(p:X\to\mathbb R\) be sublinear on a real vector space and let \(f:M\to\mathbb R\) be linear on a subspace \(M\) with \(f\le p\). Then there is a linear \(F:X\to\mathbb R\) extending \(f\) such that \(F\le p\) on \(X\).

Proof. Partially order all dominated linear extensions \((N,g)\), where \(M\subset N\subset X\), by extension of domains. A chain has an upper bound obtained by taking the union of the domains and defining the functional consistently on that union. By Zorn's lemma there is a maximal dominated extension \((N_0,F_0)\).

If \(N_0\ne X\), choose \(x_0\notin N_0\). The one-dimensional extension lemma extends \(F_0\) to \(N_0+\mathbb Rx_0\) without losing domination, contradicting maximality. Hence \(N_0=X\).

Norm-preserving extension

Corollary. If \(M\subset X\) is a subspace of a real normed space and \(f\in M^*\), then there exists \(F\in X^*\) extending \(f\) with \(\|F\|=\|f\|\).

Proof. Apply Hahn-Banach to \(p(x)=\|f\|\,\|x\|\). Since \(f(m)\le |f(m)|\le\|f\|\|m\|\), the extension \(F\) satisfies \(F(x)\le\|f\|\|x\|\). Applying this to \(-x\) gives \(|F(x)|\le\|f\|\|x\|\), so \(\|F\|\le\|f\|\). The reverse inequality holds because \(F\) extends \(f\).

Point separation and norm recovery

Corollary. For every \(x_0\ne0\) in a normed space \(X\), there exists \(f\in X^*\) such that \(\|f\|=1\) and \(f(x_0)=\|x_0\|\). Consequently \(\|x\|=\sup_{\|f\|\le1}|f(x)|\).

Proof. On \(M=\mathbb F x_0\), define \(f(\alpha x_0)=\alpha\|x_0\|\) in the real case, and \(f(\alpha x_0)=\alpha\|x_0\|\) in the complex case. This functional has norm \(1\). Extend it norm-preservingly. The inequality \(\sup_{\|f\|\le1}|f(x)|\le\|x\|\) is immediate; the separating functional gives equality for each fixed \(x\).

Complex Hahn-Banach

Theorem. The norm-preserving extension theorem also holds for complex normed spaces.

Proof. Given a complex linear \(f\), let \(u=\operatorname{Re}f\). Then \(u\) is real linear and \(|u(m)|\le\|f\|\|m\|\). Extend \(u\) as a real linear functional \(U\) with the same norm by the real theorem. Define \(F(x)=U(x)-iU(ix)\). Then \(F\) is complex linear, extends \(f\), and \(\operatorname{Re}F=U\).

To control the norm, for \(F(x)\ne0\) choose \(\theta\) such that \(e^{i\theta}F(x)=|F(x)|\). Then \(|F(x)|=\operatorname{Re}F(e^{i\theta}x)=U(e^{i\theta}x)\le\|f\|\|x\|\). Thus \(\|F\|\le\|f\|\), and equality follows from extension.

Dual abundance

Remark. Hahn-Banach is the theorem that prevents the continuous dual from being too small. Weak topologies, convex separation, norm recovery, adjoints, and many minimax arguments all depend on this abundance of continuous linear functionals.
