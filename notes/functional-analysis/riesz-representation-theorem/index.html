16. Riesz representation theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/orthonormal-bases-and-fourier-expansion/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/adjoint-operators-and-spectrum/" %}

Hilbert-space representation

Theorem. For every continuous linear functional \(\varphi\in H^*\) on a Hilbert space, there is a unique \(y\in H\) such that \(\varphi(x)=\langle x,y\rangle\) for all \(x\in H\), with \(\|\varphi\|=\|y\|\).

Proof by projection

Proof. If \(\varphi=0\), take \(y=0\). Otherwise \(\ker\varphi\) is a closed hyperplane. Choose a unit vector \(u\in(\ker\varphi)^\perp\). Then every \(x\) decomposes into a kernel part plus a scalar multiple of \(u\), giving the representing vector.

Conjugation convention

Remark. The exact formula depends on whether the inner product is linear in the first or second variable. These notes use the linear-in-the-first convention, so functionals have the form \(x\mapsto\langle x,y\rangle\).

Self-duality of Hilbert space

Corollary. The Riesz map identifies \(H\) anti-linearly or linearly with \(H^*\), depending on convention. This self-duality is special to Hilbert spaces and generally fails in Banach spaces.

Adjoints

Corollary. For \(T\in\mathcal L(H,K)\), there is a unique \(T^*\in\mathcal L(K,H)\) such that \(\langle Tx,y\rangle_K=\langle x,T^*y\rangle_H\). The Riesz theorem constructs \(T^*y\) from the functional \(x\mapsto\langle Tx,y\rangle\).

Integration against an \(L^2\) function

Example. On \(L^2\), every bounded linear functional is integration against a unique \(L^2\) function: \(\varphi(f)=\int f\overline g\,\mathrm{d}\mu\) under the usual complex convention.
