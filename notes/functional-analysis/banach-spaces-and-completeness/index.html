2. Banach spaces and completeness

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/normed-spaces-and-bounded-linear-maps/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/examples-of-function-spaces/" %}

Complete normed spaces

Definition. A Banach space is a normed space in which every Cauchy sequence converges in norm. Completeness is not a linear-algebraic property of the vector space alone; it belongs to the chosen norm.

Proposition. A closed linear subspace \(M\) of a Banach space \(X\) is Banach. Conversely, a Banach subspace \(M\subset X\), with the inherited norm, is closed in \(X\).

Proof. If \((m_n)\) is Cauchy in \(M\), it is Cauchy in \(X\), hence converges to some \(x\in X\). Closedness gives \(x\in M\). Conversely, if \(m_n\in M\) and \(m_n\to x\) in \(X\), then \((m_n)\) is Cauchy in \(M\). Completeness of \(M\) gives a limit \(m\in M\), and uniqueness of limits in \(X\) yields \(x=m\).

Absolute convergence criterion

Theorem. A normed space \(X\) is Banach if and only if every series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty x_n\) with \(\sum_n\|x_n\|<\infty\) converges in \(X\).

Conversely, assume absolute convergence of series. Let \((y_n)\) be Cauchy. Choose a subsequence \(y_{n_k}\) such that \(\|y_{n_{k+1}}-y_{n_k}\|\le2^{-k}\). Then \(y_{n_1}+\sum_{k\ge1}(y_{n_{k+1}}-y_{n_k})\) converges by hypothesis, so \(y_{n_k}\) converges to some \(y\). Since the original sequence is Cauchy and has a convergent subsequence, the full sequence converges to \(y\).

Completion

Theorem. Every normed space \(X\) admits a completion: there are a Banach space \(\widehat X\) and an isometric linear map \(i:X\to\widehat X\) such that \(i(X)\) is dense in \(\widehat X\). The pair is unique up to unique surjective linear isometry fixing \(X\).

Proof. Let \(\mathcal C\) be the vector space of Cauchy sequences in \(X\), and let \(\mathcal N\subset\mathcal C\) be the subspace of sequences converging to \(0\). Define \(\widehat X=\mathcal C/\mathcal N\) and \(\|[(x_n)]\|=\lim_n\|x_n\|\), which exists because \((\|x_n\|)\) is Cauchy in \(\mathbb R\). This is a norm, and the map \(i(x)=[(x,x,\ldots)]\) is an isometry.

To prove completeness, take a Cauchy sequence of equivalence classes. Choose representatives successively so that a diagonal sequence approximates the classes within \(2^{-k}\). The diagonal representative is Cauchy in \(X\) and defines the limit in \(\widehat X\). Density follows because every class \([(x_n)]\) is the limit of the constant classes \(i(x_n)\). Uniqueness follows by extending the identity on \(X\) to the closure using uniform continuity of isometries.

Spaces of operators

Theorem. If \(Y\) is Banach and \(X\) is normed, then \(\mathcal L(X,Y)\) is Banach under the operator norm.

Proof. Let \((T_n)\) be Cauchy in operator norm. For each \(x\in X\), \(\|T_nx-T_mx\|\le\|T_n-T_m\|\|x\|\), so \((T_nx)\) is Cauchy in \(Y\); define \(Tx=\lim_nT_nx\). Linearity follows by passing limits through linear combinations.

Given \(\varepsilon>0\), choose \(N\) such that \(\|T_n-T_m\|<\varepsilon\) for \(m,n\ge N\). Letting \(m\to\infty\) pointwise gives \(\|(T_n-T)x\|\le\varepsilon\|x\|\), hence \(T_n\to T\) in operator norm and \(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\).

Neumann series

Theorem. Let \(X\) be Banach and \(A\in\mathcal L(X)\). If \(\|A\|<1\), then \(I-A\) is invertible in \(\mathcal L(X)\) and \((I-A)^{-1}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}A^n\). Moreover \(\|(I-A)^{-1}\|\le(1-\|A\|)^{-1}\).

Proof. The series is absolutely convergent in the Banach space \(\mathcal L(X)\) because \(\sum_n\|A^n\|\le\sum_n\|A\|^n<\infty\). Let \(S=\sum_{n\ge0}A^n\). Multiplying partial sums gives \((I-A)\sum_{n=0}^NA^n=I-A^{N+1}\) and the analogous right identity. Since \(\|A^{N+1}\|\le\|A\|^{N+1}\to0\), passing to the limit gives \((I-A)S=S(I-A)=I\). The norm estimate follows from the geometric series.

Banach quotients

Proposition. If \(X\) is Banach and \(M\subset X\) is closed, then \(X/M\) is Banach.

Proof. Let \(\sum_n(x_n+M)\) be absolutely convergent in \(X/M\). Choose representatives \(y_n\in x_n+M\) such that \(\|y_n\|\le\|x_n+M\|+2^{-n}\varepsilon\). Then \(\sum_n\|y_n\|<\infty\), so \(\sum_n y_n\) converges in \(X\). Applying the quotient map gives convergence of \(\sum_n(x_n+M)\). The absolute convergence criterion proves completeness.
