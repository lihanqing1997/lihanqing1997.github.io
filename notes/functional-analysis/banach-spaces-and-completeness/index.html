2. Banach spaces and completeness

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/normed-spaces-and-bounded-linear-maps/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/examples-of-function-spaces/" %}

Banach spaces and completeness records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the functional analysis note.

Definition

Definition. A Banach space is a normed vector space complete under the metric induced by its norm. Completeness ensures that norm-Cauchy approximation procedures converge inside the space.

Proposition

Proposition. If \(Y\) is Banach, then the space \(\mathcal L(X,Y)\) of bounded linear maps from a normed space \(X\) to \(Y\) is Banach under the operator norm.

Proof

Proof. A Cauchy sequence of operators converges pointwise on each vector. Uniform operator-norm control makes the pointwise limit linear and bounded, and convergence occurs in operator norm.

Remark

Remark. Completeness is what turns estimates into existence. Many theorems in functional analysis are fixed-point or limiting arguments in Banach spaces.
