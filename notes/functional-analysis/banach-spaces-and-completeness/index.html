2. Banach spaces and completeness

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/normed-spaces-and-bounded-linear-maps/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/examples-of-function-spaces/" %}

Banach spaces

Definition. A Banach space is a complete normed vector space: every Cauchy sequence converges in the norm. Completeness is what allows limiting constructions such as infinite series, fixed points, and operator inverses.

Absolute convergence implies convergence

Proposition. A normed space is Banach if and only if every series \(\sum x_n\) with \(\sum\|x_n\|<\infty\) converges. This criterion is often easier to use than Cauchy sequences directly.

Completion

Theorem. Every normed space \(X\) has a completion \(\widehat X\), unique up to isometric isomorphism, in which \(X\) embeds densely. Construct it from equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences.

Space of bounded operators

Proposition. If \(Y\) is Banach, then \(\mathcal L(X,Y)\) is Banach under the operator norm. A Cauchy sequence of operators converges pointwise, and uniform operator-norm bounds make the pointwise limit bounded.

Example. The polynomials are not complete in \(C[0,1]\) with the sup norm: their uniform limits include many non-polynomial continuous functions. Their completion is \(C[0,1]\) by the Weierstrass approximation theorem.
