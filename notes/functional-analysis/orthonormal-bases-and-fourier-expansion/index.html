15. Orthonormal bases and Fourier expansion

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/hilbert-spaces-and-orthogonal-projection/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/riesz-representation-theorem/" %}

Orthonormal systems

Definition. A family \((e_i)_{i\in I}\) in a Hilbert space is orthonormal if \(\langle e_i,e_j\rangle=0\) for \(i\ne j\) and \(\|e_i\|=1\). It is complete if the only vector orthogonal to every \(e_i\) is \(0\).

Bessel inequality

Theorem. For every orthonormal system and every \(x\in H\), \(\sum_i |\langle x,e_i\rangle|^2\le\|x\|^2\), with the sum interpreted over finite subsets and then taking the supremum.

Parseval identity

Theorem. An orthonormal system is complete if and only if \(\|x\|^2=\sum_i |\langle x,e_i\rangle|^2\) for every \(x\in H\). In that case \(x=\sum_i \langle x,e_i\rangle e_i\) in norm.

Existence of bases

Proposition. Every Hilbert space has an orthonormal basis, obtained by Zorn's lemma. A separable Hilbert space has a countable orthonormal basis and is isometrically isomorphic to \(\ell^2\).

Fourier series

Example. The trigonometric system is an orthonormal basis for \(L^2(\mathbb T)\). Fourier coefficients are Hilbert-space coordinates, and \(L^2\) convergence is convergence of orthogonal projections.

Basis versus Hamel basis

Remark. A Hilbert orthonormal basis usually represents vectors by infinite norm-convergent series. It is not the same as a Hamel basis, which uses finite linear combinations.
