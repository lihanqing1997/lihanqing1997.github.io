17. Adjoint operators and spectrum

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/riesz-representation-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/compact-operators/" %}

Banach-space adjoint

Definition. For \(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\), the adjoint \(T^*:Y^*\to X^*\) is \(T^*\varphi=\varphi\circ T\). It is bounded and \(\|T^*\|=\|T\|\).

Hilbert-space adjoint

Definition. On Hilbert spaces, the adjoint is the unique operator satisfying \(\langle Tx,y\rangle=\langle x,T^*y\rangle\). Self-adjoint means \(T=T^*\), unitary means \(T^*T=TT^*=I\), and normal means \(T^*T=TT^*\).

Spectrum and resolvent

Definition. For \(T\in\mathcal L(X)\), the resolvent set consists of \(\lambda\in\mathbb C\) such that \(\lambda I-T\) is bijective with bounded inverse. The spectrum \(\sigma(T)\) is its complement.

Basic spectral theorem

Theorem. The spectrum of a bounded operator on a complex Banach space is nonempty compact and lies in \(\{\lambda:|\lambda|\le\|T\|\}\). The resolvent is analytic, and Liouville's theorem proves nonemptiness.

Spectral radius formula

Theorem. The spectral radius satisfies \(r(T)=\sup\{|\lambda|:\lambda\in\sigma(T)\}=\lim_{n\to\infty}\|T^n\|^{1/n}\). This connects algebraic spectrum with asymptotic operator growth.

Finite-dimensional analogy

Remark. In finite dimension the spectrum is the set of eigenvalues. In infinite dimension, spectrum also includes approximate and continuous spectral behavior not represented by eigenvectors.
