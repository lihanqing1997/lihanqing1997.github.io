19. Spectral theory for compact self-adjoint operators

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/compact-operators/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/bounded-spectral-theorem-and-functional-calculus/" %}

Compact self-adjoint operators

Definition. Let \(T\) be compact and self-adjoint on a Hilbert space \(H\). Self-adjointness gives real quadratic forms; compactness gives enough finite-dimensional behavior to find eigenvectors.

Existence of eigenvalues

Theorem. If \(T\ne0\) is compact self-adjoint, then either \(\|T\|\) or \(-\|T\|\) is an eigenvalue. Maximize or minimize \(\langle Tx,x\rangle\) on the unit ball using compactness of the image and Hilbert geometry.

Spectral decomposition

Theorem. There is an orthonormal set of eigenvectors for nonzero eigenvalues, and \(H\) decomposes as the orthogonal direct sum of eigenspaces and \(\ker T\). Nonzero eigenvalues have finite multiplicity and can accumulate only at \(0\).

Expansion formula

Corollary. For compact self-adjoint \(T\), one can write \(Tx=\sum_n \lambda_n\langle x,e_n\rangle e_n\), with \(\lambda_n\to0\) if infinitely many nonzero eigenvalues occur.

Positive compact operators

Remark. If \(\langle Tx,x\rangle\ge0\), all eigenvalues are nonnegative. Positive compact operators behave like diagonal matrices with nonnegative diagonal entries tending to zero.

Integral kernels

Example. A symmetric continuous kernel \(K(s,t)=\overline{K(t,s)}\) defines a compact self-adjoint operator on \(L^2\) under standard hypotheses. The spectral theorem becomes an eigenfunction expansion for the kernel.
