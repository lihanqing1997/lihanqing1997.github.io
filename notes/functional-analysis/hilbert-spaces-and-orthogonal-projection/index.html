14. Hilbert spaces and orthogonal projection

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/reflexive-spaces/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/orthonormal-bases-and-fourier-expansion/" %}

Hilbert spaces

Definition. A Hilbert space is a complete inner-product space. The norm is \(\|x\|=\sqrt{\langle x,x\rangle}\), and the inner product satisfies the parallelogram identity.

Projection theorem

Theorem. If \(C\) is a nonempty closed convex subset of a Hilbert space \(H\), then every \(x\in H\) has a unique nearest point \(P_Cx\in C\). If \(M\) is a closed subspace, then \(x-P_Mx\in M^\perp\).

Proof idea

Proof. Choose a minimizing sequence in \(C\). The parallelogram identity shows it is Cauchy, hence converges to a point of \(C\). Convexity gives uniqueness by comparing two hypothetical minimizers with their midpoint.

Orthogonal decomposition

Corollary. For every closed subspace \(M\subset H\), \(H=M\oplus M^\perp\). The projection \(P_M\) is linear, bounded, self-adjoint, and satisfies \(P_M^2=P_M\).

Best approximation

Example. Least-squares problems are projection problems: approximate \(x\) by an element of a closed subspace \(M\), and the error is characterized by orthogonality to \(M\).

Hilbert geometry

Remark. Hilbert spaces keep enough Euclidean geometry for projections and angles, while allowing infinite-dimensional analytic limits.
