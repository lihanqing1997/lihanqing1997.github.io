14. Hilbert spaces and orthogonal projection

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/reflexive-spaces/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/orthonormal-bases-and-fourier-expansion/" %}

Inner products

Definition. A Hilbert space is a complete inner-product space \(H\). We take \(\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle\) linear in the first variable and conjugate-linear in the second. The norm is \(\|x\|=\langle x,x\rangle^{1/2}\).

Theorem. Cauchy-Schwarz inequality: \(|\langle x,y\rangle|\le\|x\|\|y\|\) for all \(x,y\in H\).

Proof. If \(y=0\) there is nothing to prove. Otherwise, for any \(\lambda\in\mathbb F\), \(0\le\|x-\lambda y\|^2=\|x\|^2-2\operatorname{Re}(\lambda\langle y,x\rangle)+|\lambda|^2\|y\|^2\). Choose \(\lambda=\langle x,y\rangle/\|y\|^2\) with the convention matching the inner-product linearity. The resulting inequality is \(\|x\|^2-|\langle x,y\rangle|^2/\|y\|^2\ge0\).

Parallelogram and polarization

Proposition. The norm of a Hilbert space satisfies \(\|x+y\|^2+\|x-y\|^2=2\|x\|^2+2\|y\|^2\). Conversely, a norm satisfying the parallelogram identity comes from an inner product by the polarization identity.

Proof. Expanding \(\langle x\pm y,x\pm y\rangle\) gives the parallelogram identity. Conversely, define the inner product by the real or complex polarization formula. The parallelogram identity is exactly the algebra needed to prove additivity; homogeneity and positivity follow from norm properties.

Projection theorem

Theorem. Let \(C\subset H\) be nonempty, closed, and convex. For every \(x\in H\), there is a unique \(p=P_Cx\in C\) minimizing \(\|x-c\|\) over \(c\in C\). It is characterized by \(\operatorname{Re}\langle x-p,c-p\rangle\le0\) for all \(c\in C\).

Proof. Let \(d=\inf_{c\in C}\|x-c\|\) and choose \(c_n\in C\) with \(\|x-c_n\|\to d\). Since \(C\) is convex, \((c_n+c_m)/2\in C\). The parallelogram identity applied to \(x-c_n\) and \(x-c_m\) gives \(\|c_n-c_m\|^2\le2\|x-c_n\|^2+2\|x-c_m\|^2-4d^2\), which tends to \(0\). Thus \(c_n\) is Cauchy, converges to some \(p\in C\), and \(\|x-p\|=d\).

If \(p,q\) were two minimizers, the same parallelogram argument with their midpoint gives \(\|p-q\|=0\). For the variational inequality, minimize \(\|x-(p+t(c-p))\|^2\) over \(0\le t\le1\) and take the right derivative at \(0\). Conversely, that inequality implies \(\|x-c\|^2=\|x-p\|^2+\|c-p\|^2-2\operatorname{Re}\langle x-p,c-p\rangle\ge\|x-p\|^2\).

Closed subspaces

Corollary. If \(M\subset H\) is a closed subspace, then \(H=M\oplus M^\perp\). The map \(P_Mx\) is linear, bounded, self-adjoint, idempotent, and \(\|P_M\|\le1\).

Proof. For \(p=P_Mx\), the variational inequality applied to \(p\pm m\) and \(p\pm im\) gives \(\langle x-p,m\rangle=0\) for all \(m\in M\). Thus \(x=p+(x-p)\in M+M^\perp\). The intersection is \(\{0\}\). Linearity follows from uniqueness of the decomposition. Boundedness follows from \(\|P_Mx\|\le\|x\|\), and self-adjointness follows from \(\langle P_Mx,y\rangle=\langle P_Mx,P_My\rangle=\langle x,P_My\rangle\).

Normal equations

Example. Least squares is projection. If \(A:\mathbb F^p\to H\) has closed range \(M\), the best approximation to \(y\in H\) in \(M\) is \(A\hat\beta=P_My\), and the residual satisfies \(A^*(y-A\hat\beta)=0\). In matrix language this is \(A^*A\hat\beta=A^*y\), the normal equation.
