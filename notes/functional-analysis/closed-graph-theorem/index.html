7. Closed graph theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/open-mapping-and-inverse-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/hahn-banach-theorem/" %}

Closed graphs

Definition. For normed spaces \(X,Y\), the graph of a linear map \(T:D(T)\subset X\to Y\) is \(G(T)=\{(x,Tx):x\in D(T)\}\subset X\times Y\). The operator is closed if \(G(T)\) is closed in \(X\times Y\).

Proposition. If \(D(T)\) is equipped with the graph norm \(\|x\|_T=\|x\|_X+\|Tx\|_Y\), then \(T\) is closed iff \(D(T)\) is complete whenever \(X,Y\) are Banach and \(D(T)\) is viewed with this graph norm.

Proof. The map \(x\mapsto(x,Tx)\) is an isometry from \((D(T),\|\cdot\|_T)\) onto \(G(T)\) up to the equivalent product norm \(\|(x,y)\|=\|x\|+\|y\|\). A subspace of the Banach product \(X\times Y\) is complete iff it is closed.

Closed graph theorem

Theorem. Let \(X,Y\) be Banach spaces. If \(T:X\to Y\) is linear and its graph is closed in \(X\times Y\), then \(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\).

Proof. Since \(G(T)\) is a closed subspace of the Banach space \(X\times Y\), it is Banach. The projection \(\pi_1:G(T)\to X\), \(\pi_1(x,Tx)=x\), is bounded and bijective. By the bounded inverse theorem, \(\pi_1^{-1}:X\to G(T)\) is bounded. The second projection \(\pi_2:G(T)\to Y\) is bounded, and \(T=\pi_2\pi_1^{-1}\). Hence \(T\) is bounded.

Sequential closedness

Proposition. For operators between normed spaces, \(T\) is closed iff whenever \(x_n\in D(T)\), \(x_n\to x\) in \(X\), and \(Tx_n\to y\) in \(Y\), one has \(x\in D(T)\) and \(Tx=y\).

Proof. This is exactly the sequential characterization of closed subsets in metric spaces applied to \(G(T)\subset X\times Y\). Convergence \((x_n,Tx_n)\to(x,y)\) in the product norm is equivalent to \(x_n\to x\) and \(Tx_n\to y\).

Closable operators

Definition. A densely defined operator \(T:D(T)\subset X\to Y\) is closable if \(\overline{G(T)}\) is the graph of an operator. Its closure is denoted \(\overline T\).

Proposition. A densely defined operator \(T\) is closable iff \(x_n\to0\) and \(Tx_n\to y\) imply \(y=0\).

Proof. If \(\overline{G(T)}\) is a graph, it cannot contain both \((0,0)\) and \((0,y)\) with \(y\ne0\). Conversely, the stated condition gives uniqueness of the second coordinate over each first coordinate in \(\overline{G(T)}\): if \((x,y_1)\) and \((x,y_2)\) lie in the closure, subtract approximating graph sequences to get a sequence tending to \(0\) whose image tends to \(y_1-y_2\), hence \(y_1=y_2\).

Differentiation as a closed operator

Example. On \(C[0,1]\) with the sup norm, define \(D:C^1[0,1]\to C[0,1]\) by \(Df=f'\). If \(f_n\to f\) uniformly and \(f_n'\to g\) uniformly, then \(f(t)-f(0)=\lim_n\int_0^t f_n'(s)\,\mathrm{d}s=\int_0^t g(s)\,\mathrm{d}s\). Thus \(f\in C^1\) and \(f'=g\), so \(D\) is closed. It is not bounded, because \(\|\sin(nt)\|_\infty\le1\) while \(\|n\cos(nt)\|_\infty=n\).
