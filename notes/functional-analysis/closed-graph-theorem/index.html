7. Closed graph theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/open-mapping-and-inverse-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/hahn-banach-theorem/" %}

Closed graph

Definition. The graph of a linear map \(T:X\to Y\) is \(G(T)=\{(x,Tx):x\in X\}\subset X\times Y\). It is closed if \(x_n\to x\) and \(Tx_n\to y\) imply \(y=Tx\).

Closed graph theorem

Theorem. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are Banach spaces and \(T:X\to Y\) is linear with closed graph, then \(T\) is bounded. Thus sequential compatibility with limits is enough for continuity.

Proof by inverse theorem

Proof. The graph is a Banach space under the product norm. The projection \(G(T)\to X\) is a bijective bounded linear map. Its inverse \(x\mapsto(x,Tx)\) is bounded by the bounded inverse theorem, giving boundedness of \(T\).

Closed versus bounded

Remark. For operators defined on all of a Banach space, closedness implies boundedness. Unbounded closed operators can exist only when their domains are proper subspaces.

Practical criterion

Example. To prove a linear operator is bounded, it can be easier to show that whenever \(x_n\to x\) and \(Tx_n\to y\), the candidate limit must satisfy \(y=Tx\).

Relation to analysis

Remark. Many differential operators are closed but unbounded on their natural domains. The closed graph theorem explains why domain choice is essential.
