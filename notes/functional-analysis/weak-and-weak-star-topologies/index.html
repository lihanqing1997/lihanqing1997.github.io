11. Weak and weak-star topologies

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/dual-spaces-and-annihilators/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/banach-alaoglu-theorem/" %}

Weak topology

Definition. The weak topology on \(X\) is the coarsest topology making every \(f\in X^*\) continuous. A net \(x_\alpha\) converges weakly to \(x\) exactly when \(f(x_\alpha)\to f(x)\) for every \(f\in X^*\).

Weak-star topology

Definition. The weak-star topology on \(X^*\) is \(\sigma(X^*,X)\), the coarsest topology making each evaluation map \(f\mapsto f(x)\) continuous. It is usually weaker than the weak topology on \(X^*\).

Weak convergence and boundedness

Proposition. Every weakly convergent sequence in a Banach space is norm bounded. For each \(f\in X^*\), the scalar sequence \(f(x_n)\) is bounded; uniform boundedness applied to \(x_n\) as functionals on \(X^*\) gives \(\sup_n\|x_n\|<\infty\).

Convex sets

Theorem. A convex subset of a normed space is norm closed if and only if it is weakly closed. Hahn-Banach separation proves that a point outside a closed convex set can be separated by a continuous linear functional.

Weak versus norm convergence

Example. In \(\ell^2\), the standard basis vectors \(e_n\) converge weakly to \(0\) but not in norm. Weak convergence records all continuous linear observations, not norm distance.

Nets versus sequences

Remark. Weak and weak-star topologies are often not first countable, so nets are the natural language. In separable or reflexive settings, bounded sets may allow sequential formulations.
