4. Baire category theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/examples-of-function-spaces/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/uniform-boundedness-principle/" %}

Nowhere dense and meagre sets

Definition. A set is nowhere dense if its closure has empty interior. A set is meagre if it is a countable union of nowhere dense sets. Baire category separates topological smallness from measure-theoretic smallness.

Baire theorem

Theorem. A complete metric space is not meagre in itself. Equivalently, a countable intersection of dense open sets is dense. Banach spaces therefore cannot be exhausted by countably many closed sets with empty interior.

Nested balls proof

Proof. Given dense open sets \(G_n\), choose closed balls \(\overline B_n\subset G_n\cap B_{n-1}\) with radii tending to zero. Completeness gives a point in all balls, hence in every \(G_n\).

Principle of condensation

Corollary. If a Banach space is a countable union of closed sets, then at least one of those closed sets has nonempty interior. This simple consequence powers the uniform boundedness, open mapping, and closed graph theorems.

Typical behavior

Example. Many genericity results in analysis say that a property holds on a dense \(G_\delta\) set. Baire's theorem ensures such a set is topologically large in a complete metric space.

Why completeness appears

Remark. Without completeness, nested shrinking balls may converge outside the space. Baire category is a topological expression of completeness.
