21. Unbounded operators

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/bounded-spectral-theorem-and-functional-calculus/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/locally-convex-spaces-and-seminorms/" %}

Operators with domains

Definition. An unbounded operator \(T\) from \(X\) to \(Y\) is a linear map defined on a linear domain \(D(T)\subset X\). The domain is part of the operator; changing it changes closedness, adjoints, and spectrum.

Closed operators

Definition. The operator \(T\) is closed if its graph is closed in \(X\times Y\). It is closable if the closure of its graph is still the graph of an operator.

Closed graph contrast

Remark. A closed operator defined on all of a Banach space is bounded. Therefore genuinely unbounded closed operators must have proper domains.

Adjoint of a densely defined operator

Definition. If \(T:D(T)\subset H\to H\) is densely defined, \(y\in D(T^*)\) when the functional \(x\mapsto\langle Tx,y\rangle\) is bounded on \(D(T)\) with respect to the Hilbert norm. Then \(T^*y\) is the representing vector.

Self-adjoint versus symmetric

Proposition. A densely defined operator is symmetric if \(T\subset T^*\), and self-adjoint if \(T=T^*\). Equality of domains is crucial. Many differential operators are symmetric on small domains but become self-adjoint only after boundary conditions are chosen.

Derivative example

Example. On \(L^2[0,1]\), differentiation is unbounded on natural Sobolev-type domains. Boundary conditions determine whether the operator is skew-adjoint, self-adjoint after multiplying by \(-i\), or merely symmetric.
