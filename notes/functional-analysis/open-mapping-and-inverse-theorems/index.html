6. Open mapping and inverse theorems

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/uniform-boundedness-principle/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/closed-graph-theorem/" %}

Open mapping theorem

Theorem. A surjective bounded linear map \(T:X\to Y\) between Banach spaces is open: it sends open sets to open sets. In particular, \(T(B_X(0,1))\) contains a ball around \(0\) in \(Y\).

Baire proof mechanism

Proof. Since \(Y=\bigcup_{n\ge1} n\overline{T(B_X)}\), Baire gives an image closure with interior. Linearity moves this to a neighborhood of \(0\), and a summation argument removes the closure.

Bounded inverse theorem

Corollary. If \(T:X\to Y\) is a bijective bounded linear map between Banach spaces, then \(T^{-1}\) is bounded. Algebraic invertibility plus completeness gives topological invertibility.

Equivalent norms

Corollary. If a vector space is Banach under two norms and one norm dominates the other while the identity map is bijective, the bounded inverse theorem often proves the norms equivalent.

Necessity of completeness

Remark. The theorem can fail for incomplete normed spaces. Completeness is what turns surjectivity into quantitative control over preimages.

Solving linear equations

Example. For a well-posed linear equation \(Tx=y\), the bounded inverse theorem says solutions depend continuously on data: \(\|x\|\le C\|y\|\). This is the functional-analytic form of stability.
