13. Reflexive spaces

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/banach-alaoglu-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/hilbert-spaces-and-orthogonal-projection/" %}

Canonical embedding

Definition. A Banach space \(X\) is reflexive if the canonical isometry \(J_X:X\to X^{**}\), \(J_Xx(f)=f(x)\), is onto. Equivalently, every continuous linear functional on \(X^*\) is evaluation at a unique point of \(X\).

Proposition. Finite-dimensional normed spaces are reflexive.

Proof. If \(\dim X=n\), then \(\dim X^*=n\) and \(\dim X^{**}=n\). The canonical map \(J_X\) is injective by Hahn-Banach and is a linear map between vector spaces of the same finite dimension, hence onto.

Goldstine theorem

Theorem. The image \(J_X(B_X)\) is weak-star dense in \(B_{X^{**}}\).

Proof. It suffices to meet a basic weak-star neighborhood of \(F\in B_{X^{**}}\), determined by \(f_1,\ldots,f_n\in X^*\) and \(\varepsilon>0\). Consider \(S:X\to\mathbb F^n\), \(Sx=(f_1(x),\ldots,f_n(x))\). If the vector \((F(f_1),\ldots,F(f_n))\) were not in the closure of \(S((1+\varepsilon)B_X)\), finite-dimensional separation would give scalars \(a_i\) such that the functional \(g=\sum_i a_if_i\) satisfies \(|F(g)|>(1+\varepsilon)\|g\|\), contradicting \(\|F\|\le1\). Hence there is \(x\) with \(\|x\|\le1+\varepsilon\) and \(|f_i(x)-F(f_i)|<\varepsilon\). Scaling \(x\) by \(1+\varepsilon\) and absorbing the resulting small error gives a point of \(B_X\) in the prescribed neighborhood.

Weak compactness criterion

Theorem. A Banach space \(X\) is reflexive iff its closed unit ball is compact in the weak topology.

Proof. If \(X\) is reflexive, then \(J_X(B_X)=B_{X^{**}}\), and the weak topology on \(B_X\) corresponds to the weak-star topology on \(B_{X^{**}}\). Banach-Alaoglu makes \(B_{X^{**}}\) weak-star compact, so \(B_X\) is weakly compact.

Conversely, suppose \(B_X\) is weakly compact. The map \(J_X:(B_X,\sigma(X,X^*))\to(B_{X^{**}},\sigma(X^{**},X^*))\) is continuous, so \(J_X(B_X)\) is weak-star compact and therefore weak-star closed. By Goldstine theorem it is weak-star dense in \(B_{X^{**}}\), hence \(J_X(B_X)=B_{X^{**}}\). Scaling gives \(J_X(X)=X^{**}\), so \(X\) is reflexive.

Closed subspaces

Proposition. Every closed subspace \(M\) of a reflexive Banach space \(X\) is reflexive.

Proof. The closed unit ball \(B_M\) is weakly closed in \(X\), because closed convex sets are weakly closed. It is contained in \(B_X\), which is weakly compact. Therefore \(B_M\) is weakly compact. The weak topology inherited from \(X\) agrees with \(\sigma(M,M^*)\), because every \(m^*\in M^*\) extends to some \(x^*\in X^*\) by Hahn-Banach. The weak compactness criterion gives reflexivity of \(M\).

Quotients

Proposition. If \(X\) is reflexive and \(M\subset X\) is closed, then \(X/M\) is reflexive.

Proof. The quotient map \(q:X\to X/M\) is weak-to-weak continuous because every functional on \(X/M\) pulls back to a functional on \(X\). Hence \(q(rB_X)\) is weakly compact for every \(r>0\). The closed unit ball of \(X/M\) equals \(\bigcap_{n\ge1}q((1+1/n)B_X)\): inclusion \(\subset\) follows from the definition of quotient norm by choosing representatives of norm at most \(1+1/n\), and the reverse inclusion is immediate. This intersection is weakly closed inside the weakly compact set \(q(2B_X)\), hence weakly compact. The weak compactness criterion gives reflexivity.

Uniform convexity

Theorem. Every uniformly convex Banach space is reflexive.

Proof. This is the Milman-Pettis theorem. Let \((C_i)\) be a decreasing family of nonempty closed bounded convex sets. Uniform convexity implies normal structure: a closed bounded convex set with more than one point contains a point whose maximal distance to the set is strictly smaller than the diameter. This prevents the finite-intersection construction from losing all points and implies weak compactness of closed balls. The weak compactness criterion then yields reflexivity. Nonreflexive spaces

Example. The spaces \(\ell^1\), \(c_0\), and \(L^1[0,1]\) are not reflexive. For instance, \(c_0^*=\ell^1\) and \((c_0)^{**}\cong\ell^\infty\), so the canonical embedding misses bounded sequences that do not converge to \(0\). In \(\ell^1\), weak compactness of the unit ball would force a weakly convergent subnet of the unit vectors, but mass can escape to infinity.
