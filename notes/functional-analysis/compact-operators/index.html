18. Compact operators

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/adjoint-operators-and-spectrum/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/spectral-theory-for-compact-self-adjoint-operators/" %}

Compact operators

Definition. A bounded linear operator \(T:X\to Y\) is compact if it sends bounded sets to relatively compact sets. Equivalently, every bounded sequence \((x_n)\) has a subsequence such that \((Tx_{n_k})\) converges in \(Y\).

Finite-rank operators

Example. Every finite-rank operator is compact, because bounded subsets of finite-dimensional spaces are relatively compact. Norm limits of compact operators are compact.

Compactness is not boundedness

Counterexample. The identity operator on an infinite-dimensional Banach space is bounded but not compact. If the identity were compact, the closed unit ball would be compact, forcing finite dimension by Riesz's lemma.

Riesz lemma

Lemma. If \(M\) is a proper closed subspace of a normed space \(X\), then for every \(0<\alpha<1\) there exists \(x\in X\) with \(\|x\|=1\) and \(\operatorname{dist}(x,M)>\alpha\). This builds separated sequences in infinite-dimensional spaces.

Fredholm alternative preview

Theorem. For compact \(K\) on a Banach space, the equation \((I-K)x=y\) behaves much like a finite-dimensional system: injectivity and surjectivity are closely linked under appropriate hypotheses.

Integral operators

Example. Many integral operators with continuous kernels are compact from \(C[a,b]\) to itself. Equicontinuity and Arzelà-Ascoli provide the compactness.
