18. Compact operators

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/functional-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/functional-analysis/adjoint-operators-and-spectrum/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/functional-analysis/spectral-theory-for-compact-self-adjoint-operators/" %}

Compact linear maps

Definition. A bounded linear map \(T:X\to Y\) is compact if \(T(B_X)\) has compact closure in \(Y\), equivalently if every bounded sequence \((x_n)\) in \(X\) has a subsequence \((Tx_{n_k})\) converging in norm in \(Y\).

Proposition. Every compact linear map is bounded.

Proof. If \(T\) were unbounded, choose \(x_n\) with \(\|x_n\|\le1\) and \(\|Tx_n\|\to\infty\). Then \((Tx_n)\) cannot have a convergent subsequence, contradicting compactness.

Finite-rank operators

Proposition. Every finite-rank bounded operator is compact.

Proof. If \(\operatorname{Ran}T\) is finite-dimensional, then \(T(B_X)\) is bounded in a finite-dimensional normed space. Closed bounded sets in finite dimension are compact, so \(\overline{T(B_X)}\) is compact.

Norm limits

Theorem. The norm limit of compact operators is compact.

Proof. Let \(T_n\to T\) in operator norm and let \((x_k)\) be bounded. Choose a subsequence on which \(T_1x_k\) converges. From that subsequence choose a further subsequence on which \(T_2x_k\) converges, and continue. The diagonal subsequence makes \(T_jx_{k_m}\) converge for each fixed \(j\).

Let \(\|x_k\|\le M\). Given \(\varepsilon>0\), choose \(j\) with \(\|T-T_j\|M<\varepsilon/3\). Since \(T_jx_{k_m}\) is Cauchy, \(Tx_{k_m}\) is Cauchy up to the two approximation errors. Completeness of \(Y\) gives convergence.

Composition ideal

Proposition. If \(T:X\to Y\) is compact and \(A:W\to X\), \(B:Y\to Z\) are bounded, then \(TA\) and \(BT\) are compact.

Proof. The image of a bounded set under \(A\) is bounded, so compactness of \(T\) makes \(TA\) compact. If \(K=\overline{T(B_X)}\) is compact in \(Y\), then \(B(K)\) is compact in \(Z\) by continuity, and \(BT(B_X)\subset B(K)\).

Compactness and weak convergence

Proposition. If \(X\) is reflexive, \(T:X\to Y\) is compact, and \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\) weakly in \(X\), then \(Tx_n\to Tx\) in norm provided every subsequence has the same possible norm limit. In particular compact operators turn weakly convergent sequences into norm-convergent sequences.

Proof. The sequence \((x_n)\) is bounded. Compactness gives every subsequence a further subsequence such that \(Tx_{n_k}\to y\) in norm. Since bounded linear maps are weak-to-weak continuous, \(Tx_{n_k}\rightharpoonup Tx\) weakly. Norm convergence implies weak convergence to the same limit, so \(y=Tx\). If the full sequence did not converge in norm to \(Tx\), some subsequence would stay \(\varepsilon\)-away, contradicting the existence of a further norm-convergent subsequence to \(Tx\).

Integral operators

Example. If \(K\in C([0,1]^2)\), the operator \(Tf(s)=\int_0^1K(s,t)f(t)\,\mathrm{d}t\) maps \(C[0,1]\) compactly into itself. Bounded sets are sent to uniformly bounded and equicontinuous families; Arzela-Ascoli gives relative compactness.
