2. Power series and analytic functions

Radius of convergence

Definition. A complex power series centered at \(a\) has the form \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(z-a)^n\). Its radius is \(R=(\limsup |c_n|^{1/n})^{-1}\), with the usual conventions. It converges absolutely for \(|z-a|R\).

Termwise differentiation

Theorem. Inside the disc of convergence, a power series defines a holomorphic function and may be differentiated term by term: \[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}z}\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(z-a)^n=\sum_{n=1}^\infty n c_n(z-a)^{n-1}. \] The differentiated series has the same radius of convergence.

Identity of coefficients

Proposition. If two power series agree on a set with an accumulation point inside the common disc of convergence, then all their coefficients agree. In particular, if \(f(z)=\sum c_n(z-a)^n\), then \(c_n=f^{(n)}(a)/n!\).

Analytic continuation warning

Remark. A power series gives a local representation. The same holomorphic function may require different power series around different centers, and singularities determine how far each local expansion can reach.

Geometric series model

Example. The function \((1-z)^{-1}\) equals \(\sum_{n\ge0}z^n\) on \(|z|<1\). Around \(a\ne1\), it has a different expansion whose radius is exactly \(|1-a|\), the distance to the nearest singularity.
