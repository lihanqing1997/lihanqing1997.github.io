11. Argument principle and Rouché theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/residue-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/conformal-maps/" %}

Argument principle and Rouché theorem records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the complex analysis note.

Definition

Definition. The argument principle counts zeros and poles by the winding of \(f(\gamma)\) around the origin. Rouché's theorem compares zero counts of nearby holomorphic functions.

Argument principle

Theorem. For meromorphic \(f\), \((2\pi i)^{-1}\int_\gamma f'(z)/f(z)\,\mathrm{d}z\) equals the number of zeros minus poles inside \(\gamma\), counted with multiplicity.

Proof

Proof. Near a zero or pole, write \(f(z)=(z-a)^m g(z)\) with \(g(a)\ne0\). Then \(f'/f=m/(z-a)+g'/g\), so residues count multiplicities.

Remark

Remark. Counting roots by contour deformation is a signature technique of complex analysis. It is robust under perturbations that avoid crossing zero.
