Contour strategy

Definition. Residue computations choose a meromorphic extension, a contour, and a limiting argument. The desired real integral appears as one part of the contour integral, while the remaining parts either vanish or are explicitly controlled.

Rational integrals on the real line

Proposition. If \(R(z)\) is rational, has no real poles, and decays like \(|z|^{-2}\) or faster, then \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}R(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=2\pi i\) times the sum of residues of \(R\) in the upper half-plane.

Jordan lemma type estimates

Lemma. For oscillatory integrals involving \(e^{iaz}\) with \(a>0\), upper-half-plane semicircles are controlled because \(|e^{iaz}|=e^{-a\operatorname{Im}z}\). This decay can overcome rational factors on large arcs.

Trigonometric integrals

Proposition. Integrals over \([0,2\pi]\) of rational functions of \(\sin t\) and \(\cos t\) can be converted by \(z=e^{it}\), \(\mathrm{d}t=\mathrm{d}z/(iz)\), \(\cos t=(z+z^{-1})/2\), and \(\sin t=(z-z^{-1})/(2i)\).

Principal value integrals

Remark. When poles lie on the contour, one must specify indentation or Cauchy principal value. Small semicircles around real poles contribute half-residue terms depending on orientation.

Basic real-line integral

Example. For \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}(x^2+1)^{-1}\,\mathrm{d}x\), the upper-half-plane pole at \(i\) has residue \((2i)^{-1}\), giving \(2\pi i(2i)^{-1}=\pi\).
