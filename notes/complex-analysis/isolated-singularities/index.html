8. Isolated singularities

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/zeros-and-identity-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/laurent-series/" %}

Isolated singularities records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the complex analysis note.

Definition

Definition. An isolated singularity is a point where a function is holomorphic in a punctured neighborhood but not initially defined or holomorphic at the point. It is removable, a pole, or essential.

Proposition

Proposition. A bounded isolated singularity is removable. A pole is characterized by \(|f(z)|\to\infty\), while an essential singularity has a Laurent series with infinitely many negative terms.

Proof

Proof. For removability, define the missing value by a Cauchy integral or use boundedness to remove the principal part. Pole and essential cases are read from the Laurent expansion.

Remark

Remark. The trichotomy of isolated singularities organizes all local failures of holomorphicity in one complex variable.
