10. Isolated singularities

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/zeros-and-identity-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/laurent-series/" %}

Three types

Definition. Let \(f\) be holomorphic on \(0<|z-a|

Removable singularities

Theorem. A singularity is removable if and only if \(f\) is bounded near it. Equivalently, \((z-a)f(z)\to0\). The extension value is the limit forced by Cauchy's formula.

Poles and principal parts

Proposition. The point \(a\) is a pole of order \(m\) if and only if \(f(z)=(z-a)^{-m}g(z)\) with \(g\) holomorphic and \(g(a)\ne0\). The finite sum of negative powers in the Laurent expansion is the principal part.

Casorati-Weierstrass theorem

Theorem. Near an essential singularity, the image of every punctured neighborhood is dense in \(\mathbb C\). Essential singularities are therefore maximally unstable compared with removable singularities and poles.

Classification by reciprocal

Example. The singularity of \(1/f\) at a zero of order \(m\) of \(f\) is a pole of order \(m\). The function \(e^{1/z}\) has an essential singularity at \(0\), with infinitely many negative Laurent coefficients.

Local algebra

Remark. The classification is algebraic once Laurent series exist: removable means no negative powers, pole means finitely many negative powers, essential means infinitely many negative powers.
