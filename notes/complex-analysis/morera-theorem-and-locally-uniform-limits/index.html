7. Morera theorem and locally uniform limits

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/cauchy-estimates-and-analyticity/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/maximum-principle/" %}

Morera theorem

Theorem. If \(f\) is continuous on a domain \(U\) and \(\int_{\partial T}f(z)\,\mathrm{d}z=0\) for every triangle \(T\subset U\), then \(f\) is holomorphic. The vanishing triangle integrals construct local primitives, whose derivatives recover \(f\).

Locally uniform convergence

Definition. A sequence \(f_n\) converges locally uniformly to \(f\) if convergence is uniform on every compact subset of the domain. This is the natural convergence for holomorphic functions because contour integrals live on compact curves.

Weierstrass convergence theorem

Theorem. If \(f_n\) are holomorphic on \(U\) and \(f_n\to f\) locally uniformly, then \(f\) is holomorphic and \(f_n^{(k)}\to f^{(k)}\) locally uniformly for every \(k\). Cauchy's integral formula permits the limit to pass through the integral.

Proof by Morera

Proof. Continuity follows from locally uniform convergence. For every triangle, \(\int f\,\mathrm{d}z=\lim_n\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}z=0\), since the convergence is uniform on the triangle boundary. Morera's theorem then gives holomorphicity.

Normal convergence of series

Corollary. If \(\sum f_n\) converges uniformly on compact subsets and each \(f_n\) is holomorphic, then the sum is holomorphic and may be differentiated term by term locally uniformly.

Why uniformity is needed

Remark. Pointwise limits of holomorphic functions need not be holomorphic. Local uniform control prevents singular behavior from forming invisibly on compact sets.
