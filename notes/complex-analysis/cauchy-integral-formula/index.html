Derivative formula

Corollary. Under the same hypotheses, \[ f^{(n)}(z)=\frac{n!}{2\pi i}\int_{|\zeta-a|=r}\frac{f(\zeta)}{(\zeta-z)^{n+1}}\,\mathrm{d}\zeta. \] Thus holomorphic functions are automatically infinitely complex differentiable.

Proof mechanism

Proof. Subtract \(f(z)\) inside the numerator: \((f(\zeta)-f(z))/(\zeta-z)\) is holomorphic near \(z\), so its integral vanishes by Cauchy's theorem. The remaining integral is \(f(z)\int(\zeta-z)^{-1}\,\mathrm{d}\zeta=2\pi i f(z)\).

Mean value property

Corollary. For a circle centered at \(a\), Cauchy's formula implies \(f(a)=(2\pi)^{-1}\int_0^{2\pi}f(a+re^{it})\,\mathrm{d}t\). Holomorphic functions equal their circular averages.

Analyticity follows

Theorem. Expanding \((\zeta-z)^{-1}\) as a geometric series inside the contour gives a convergent power series for \(f\). Hence every holomorphic function is analytic.

Boundary dependence

Remark. The formula is local: the contour must lie in the domain. Singularities between the point and the contour change the representation by adding residue contributions.
