7. Zeros and identity theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/maximum-principle/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/isolated-singularities/" %}

Zeros and identity theorem records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the complex analysis note.

Definition

Definition. Zeros of a holomorphic function are isolated unless the function is identically zero on a connected component. The order of a zero is the first nonzero term in the local power series.

Identity theorem

Theorem. If two holomorphic functions agree on a set with an accumulation point inside a connected domain, then they agree everywhere on that domain.

Proof

Proof. Apply the power series expansion at an accumulation point. If zeros accumulate, every coefficient must vanish; connectedness propagates the equality through overlapping disks.

Remark

Remark. The identity theorem is why analytic continuation is possible and dangerous: local agreement determines global behavior on connected domains.
