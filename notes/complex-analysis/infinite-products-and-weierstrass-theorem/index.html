23. Infinite products and Weierstrass theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/entire-functions-and-liouville-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/mittag-leffler-theorem-and-meromorphic-functions/" %}

Infinite products

Definition. An infinite product \(\prod_n(1+a_n)\) converges to a nonzero limit if the partial products converge and the limit is nonzero. Absolute convergence of \(\sum |a_n|\) is a basic sufficient condition.

Canonical factors

Definition. Weierstrass factors \(E_p(w)=(1-w)\exp(w+w^2/2+\cdots+w^p/p)\) make products converge while preserving a prescribed zero at \(w=1\). The exponential correction controls behavior away from the zero.

Weierstrass factorization theorem

Theorem. Given a discrete sequence \((a_n)\) in \(\mathbb C\) with no finite accumulation point and assigned positive multiplicities, there exists an entire function whose zeros are exactly those points with those multiplicities.

Proof strategy

Proof. Choose sufficiently high canonical factors so that the logarithms of the factors converge normally on compact sets. Normal convergence makes the product holomorphic, and the zero set is controlled factor by factor.

Sine product

Example. The classical product \(\sin\pi z=\pi z\prod_{n=1}^{\infty}(1-z^2/n^2)\) realizes the integer zeros of sine and illustrates how zero sets determine entire functions up to nonvanishing entire factors.

Nonuniqueness

Remark. If two entire functions have the same zeros with the same multiplicities, their quotient is a nowhere-zero entire function, hence locally an exponential. Zero data alone does not determine the function uniquely.
