15. Conformal maps

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/evaluation-of-integrals-by-residues/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/mobius-transformations/" %}

Angle preservation

Definition. A holomorphic map \(f\) is conformal at \(a\) if \(f'(a)\ne0\). Its derivative is multiplication by the complex number \(f'(a)\), so infinitesimal lengths are scaled by \(|f'(a)|\) and angles are rotated by \(\arg f'(a)\).

Local inverse theorem

Theorem. If \(f\) is holomorphic near \(a\) and \(f'(a)\ne0\), then \(f\) is biholomorphic between neighborhoods of \(a\) and \(f(a)\). The inverse is holomorphic and satisfies \((f^{-1})'(f(a))=1/f'(a)\).

Critical points

Remark. If \(f'(a)=0\) and \(f(z)-f(a)=(z-a)^m g(z)\), \(m\ge2\), then locally the map multiplies angles by \(m\). Critical points are precisely where conformality fails.

Biholomorphic equivalence

Definition. Two domains are conformally equivalent if there is a bijective holomorphic map between them whose inverse is holomorphic. Such maps preserve analytic structure but not necessarily Euclidean size or shape.

Mapping boundaries

Example. The exponential map sends horizontal strips to annuli or punctured planes depending on the strip width. The map \(z\mapsto z^2\) sends a half-plane angle to a full-plane angle, illustrating angle multiplication at a critical point.

Geometry from analysis

Remark. Conformal mapping theory is where complex differentiability becomes geometry: analytic formulas classify and transform planar shapes.
