Maximum modulus principle

Theorem. If \(f\) is holomorphic and nonconstant on a connected domain \(U\), then \(|f|\) has no local maximum in \(U\). If \(|f|\) attains an interior maximum, the mean value property forces \(f\) to be constant on a small disc and hence everywhere by identity.

Minimum principle

Corollary. If \(f\) is holomorphic and nowhere zero, then \(|f|\) has no interior local minimum unless \(f\) is constant. Apply the maximum principle to \(1/f\).

Boundary maximum on compact sets

Corollary. If \(f\) is continuous on \(\overline U\), holomorphic on bounded \(U\), and \(U\) has compact closure, then \(\max_{\overline U}|f|\) is achieved on \(\partial U\), unless \(f\) is constant.

Open mapping theorem

Theorem. A nonconstant holomorphic function maps open sets to open sets. Near a zero of order \(m\), one can write \(f(z)-f(a)=(z-a)^m g(z)\) with \(g(a)\ne0\), and the image contains a small disc around \(f(a)\).

Schwarz-type estimate

Example. If \(|f|\le1\) on the unit disc and \(f(0)=0\), the maximum principle applied to \(f(z)/z\) gives \(|f(z)|\le |z|\). This is the core estimate behind Schwarz's lemma.

Harmonic parallel

Remark. The real and imaginary parts of holomorphic functions are harmonic. The maximum principle for holomorphic functions mirrors the maximum principle for harmonic functions.
