12. Residue theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/laurent-series/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/argument-principle-and-rouche-theorem/" %}

Residue theorem

Theorem. Let \(f\) be holomorphic on \(U\) except for finitely many isolated singularities \(a_1,\dots,a_N\), and let \(\gamma\) be a closed contour avoiding them. Then \[ \int_\gamma f(z)\,\mathrm{d}z=2\pi i\sum_{j=1}^N n(\gamma,a_j)\operatorname{Res}(f,a_j), \] where \(n(\gamma,a_j)\) is the winding number.

Simple pole residue

Proposition. If \(f=g/h\), where \(g,h\) are holomorphic, \(h(a)=0\), and \(h'(a)\ne0\), then \(a\) is a simple pole and \(\operatorname{Res}(f,a)=g(a)/h'(a)\).

Higher-order pole formula

Proposition. If \(f\) has a pole of order \(m\) at \(a\), then \[ \operatorname{Res}(f,a)=\frac{1}{(m-1)!}\left[\frac{\mathrm{d}^{m-1}}{\mathrm{d}z^{m-1}}(z-a)^m f(z)\right]_{z=a}. \]

Proof by puncturing

Proof. Remove small circles around each singularity. Cauchy's theorem applies on the punctured region. Each small boundary circle contributes \(2\pi i\) times the local \((z-a_j)^{-1}\) coefficient, with sign determined by orientation and winding number.

Residue at infinity

Definition. For a meromorphic function near infinity, define \(\operatorname{Res}(f,\infty)=-\operatorname{Res}(z^{-2}f(1/z),0)\). On the Riemann sphere, the sum of all residues, including infinity, is zero.

Computational principle

Remark. Residues convert global contour integrals into local coefficient computations. The art is choosing a contour whose extra pieces vanish or combine into the desired integral.
