21. Normal families

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/analytic-continuation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/entire-functions-and-liouville-theorem/" %}

Normal family

Definition. A family \(\mathcal F\) of holomorphic functions on \(U\) is normal if every sequence in \(\mathcal F\) has a subsequence converging locally uniformly, possibly to \(\infty\) in the spherical metric when meromorphic functions are allowed.

Montel theorem

Theorem. A locally uniformly bounded family of holomorphic functions is normal. Cauchy's estimates give local equicontinuity, and Arzelà-Ascoli gives subsequential compactness on an exhaustion by compact subsets.

Derivative bounds

Lemma. If \(|f|\le M\) on \(D(a,R)\), then \(|f'|\le C M/R\) on smaller discs. These uniform derivative bounds are the equicontinuity input in Montel's theorem.

Vitali theorem

Theorem. If a locally bounded sequence of holomorphic functions converges pointwise on a set with an accumulation point, then it converges locally uniformly to a holomorphic function. Normality supplies subsequential limits, and identity forces all limits to agree.

Use in Riemann mapping

Example. The extremal proof of the Riemann mapping theorem uses Montel's theorem to extract a holomorphic limit from a maximizing sequence of normalized maps.

Compactness philosophy

Remark. Normal families are compactness for holomorphic functions. Cauchy estimates turn boundedness into enough regularity for subsequential limits.
