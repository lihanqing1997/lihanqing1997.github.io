Statement

Theorem. Every nonempty simply connected proper domain \(U\subsetneq\mathbb C\) is biholomorphic to the unit disc. After choosing \(a\in U\), there is a unique map \(f:U\to\mathbb D\) with \(f(a)=0\) and \(f'(a)>0\).

Why proper matters

Remark. The whole plane is simply connected but not biholomorphic to \(\mathbb D\), because a bounded entire biholomorphic image would contradict Liouville's theorem. The theorem is exactly about proper simply connected plane domains.

Extremal construction

Proof. Normalize maps \(f:U\to\mathbb D\) with \(f(a)=0\), and maximize \(|f'(a)|\). Normal families give a locally uniform limit of a maximizing sequence. One then proves the extremal map is injective and onto; otherwise a square-root or automorphism modification improves the derivative.

Uniqueness

Proposition. If two normalized maps satisfy \(f(a)=g(a)=0\) and positive real derivative at \(a\), then \(g\circ f^{-1}\) is an automorphism of \(\mathbb D\) fixing \(0\), hence a rotation. The derivative normalization forces the rotation to be the identity.

Boundary behavior

Remark. The theorem is an interior conformal classification. Extending the map continuously or smoothly to the boundary requires additional hypotheses such as Jordan boundary or smooth boundary.

Consequences

Example. Simply connected domains such as half-planes, strips, and slit planes are all conformally equivalent to the disc, although explicit maps may require exponentials, square roots, or more delicate constructions.
