22. Entire functions and Liouville theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/normal-families/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/infinite-products-and-weierstrass-theorem/" %}

Entire functions

Definition. An entire function is holomorphic on all of \(\mathbb C\). Polynomials, exponentials, and trigonometric functions are entire; rational functions with finite poles are not.

Liouville theorem

Theorem. Every bounded entire function is constant. Cauchy's estimate gives \(|f'(a)|\le M/R\) on every circle of radius \(R\), and letting \(R\to\infty\) yields \(f'(a)=0\).

Fundamental theorem of algebra

Corollary. Every nonconstant complex polynomial has a root. If a polynomial \(p\) had no zeros, then \(1/p\) would be bounded entire, hence constant, forcing \(p\) constant.

Growth and degree

Proposition. If an entire function satisfies \(|f(z)|\le C(1+|z|)^m\), then \(f\) is a polynomial of degree at most \(m\). Cauchy's estimates force all derivatives of order greater than \(m\) to vanish.

Entire functions with omitted values

Remark. Picard's theorems refine Liouville dramatically: an entire non-polynomial function cannot omit too many complex values. This sits beyond the basic note but explains the strength of entire-function theory.

Examples

Example. The exponential function omits \(0\) but is unbounded. The sine function is entire and surjective onto \(\mathbb C\). Polynomials are exactly entire functions with a pole, rather than an essential singularity, at infinity.
