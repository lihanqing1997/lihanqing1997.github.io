4. Cauchy theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/contour-integrals/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/cauchy-integral-formula/" %}

Statement on simply connected domains

Theorem. If \(U\) is simply connected and \(f\) is holomorphic on \(U\), then \(\int_\gamma f(z)\,\mathrm{d}z=0\) for every closed piecewise \(C^1\) contour \(\gamma\) in \(U\). Equivalently, holomorphic functions on simply connected domains have primitives.

Triangle version

Theorem. If \(f\) is holomorphic on a domain containing a triangle and its interior, then the integral of \(f\) around the triangle is zero. The proof subdivides the triangle repeatedly and uses differentiability at the limiting point.

Homotopy form

Proposition. If two closed contours are homotopic in \(U\) and \(f\) is holomorphic on \(U\), then their integrals are equal. Cauchy's theorem is therefore a statement about topology as well as analysis.

Goursat proof idea

Proof. Goursat's proof avoids assuming continuity of \(f'\). Repeatedly divide a triangle into four smaller triangles and choose one where the integral is at least one fourth of the previous absolute integral. The nested triangles shrink to a point, where complex differentiability makes the remaining integral negligible.

Holes and obstructions

Example. The function \(1/z\) is holomorphic on \(\mathbb C\setminus\{0\}\), but \(\int_{|z|=1}z^{-1}\,\mathrm{d}z=2\pi i\). The puncture is a topological obstruction to a global primitive.

Why it matters

Remark. Cauchy's theorem is the engine behind Cauchy's integral formula, analyticity of holomorphic functions, residue calculus, and the deformation of contours.
