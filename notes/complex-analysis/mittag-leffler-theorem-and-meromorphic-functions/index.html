24. Mittag-Leffler theorem and meromorphic functions

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/complex-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/complex-analysis/infinite-products-and-weierstrass-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/complex-analysis/meromorphic-functions-on-compact-surfaces/" %}

Meromorphic functions

Definition. A function is meromorphic on a domain if it is holomorphic except at isolated poles. Equivalently, locally it is a quotient of holomorphic functions with nonzero denominator not identically zero.

Prescribed principal parts

Theorem. The Mittag-Leffler theorem says that for a discrete set of points in a domain and prescribed finite principal parts at those points, there exists a meromorphic function having exactly those principal parts.

Convergence correction

Proof. One cannot simply sum all principal parts globally because the series may diverge. Subtract suitable Taylor polynomials from the local principal parts so the corrected series converges normally on compact subsets away from the poles.

Partial fractions

Example. Classical partial fraction expansions, such as expansions for \(\pi\cot\pi z\), are Mittag-Leffler constructions where the poles and principal parts are prescribed at the integers.

Relation to Weierstrass products

Remark. Weierstrass builds holomorphic functions with prescribed zeros; Mittag-Leffler builds meromorphic functions with prescribed poles. Together they describe the flexible global construction side of one-variable complex analysis.

Quotient representation

Corollary. On the complex plane, meromorphic functions can be represented as quotients of entire functions. Use Weierstrass products to create a denominator with the prescribed poles, then remove the poles from the product.
