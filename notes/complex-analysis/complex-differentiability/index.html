Complex derivative

Definition. Let \(U\subset\mathbb C\) be open. A function \(f:U\to\mathbb C\) is complex differentiable at \(z_0\in U\) if \[ f'(z_0)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)}{h} \] exists with \(h\in\mathbb C\). The key point is that the limit must be independent of every complex direction.

Cauchy-Riemann equations

Theorem. Write \(f=u+iv\) with \(u,v\) real-valued. If \(f\) is complex differentiable and \(u,v\) are real differentiable at \((x,y)\), then \(u_x=v_y\) and \(u_y=-v_x\). Conversely, if the partial derivatives are continuous near the point and satisfy these equations, then \(f\) is holomorphic there.

Real-linear proof

Proof. The derivative of \(f\) as a real map has matrix \(\begin{pmatrix}u_x&u_y\\ v_x&v_y\end{pmatrix}\). Complex differentiability means this real-linear map commutes with multiplication by \(i\), whose matrix is \(\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\). Equating the two products gives the Cauchy-Riemann equations.

Holomorphic functions

Definition. A function is holomorphic on \(U\) if it is complex differentiable at every point of \(U\). It is analytic if it has a convergent power-series expansion near every point. Cauchy's theory will prove that these two notions coincide.

Basic examples

Example. Polynomials, rational functions away from poles, \(e^z\), \(\sin z\), and \(\cos z\) are holomorphic. The conjugation map \(z\mapsto\overline z\) is real differentiable but not complex differentiable at any point, since the real and imaginary directional quotients disagree.

Local rigidity

Remark. One complex derivative imposes two real first-order equations. This overdetermined nature is why holomorphic functions are much more rigid than general smooth functions of two real variables.
