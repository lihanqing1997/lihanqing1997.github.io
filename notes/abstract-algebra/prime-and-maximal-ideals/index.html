7. Prime and maximal ideals

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/rings-ideals-and-quotient-rings/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/euclidean-domains-pids-and-ufds/" %}

Prime and maximal ideals records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the abstract algebra note.

Definition

Definition. An ideal \(\mathfrak p\) is prime if \(ab\in\mathfrak p\) implies \(a\in\mathfrak p\) or \(b\in\mathfrak p\). An ideal \(\mathfrak m\) is maximal if no proper ideal lies strictly between it and the ring.

Proposition

Proposition. For a commutative ring, \(R/\mathfrak p\) is an integral domain exactly when \(\mathfrak p\) is prime, and \(R/\mathfrak m\) is a field exactly when \(\mathfrak m\) is maximal.

Proof

Proof. Translate products and inverses in the quotient into statements about representatives. Domain means no zero divisors; field means every nonzero coset has a multiplicative inverse.

Remark

Remark. Prime ideals are generalized points; maximal ideals are closed points in many geometric settings. This is the beginning of algebraic geometry language.
