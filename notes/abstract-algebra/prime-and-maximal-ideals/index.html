7. Prime and maximal ideals

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/rings-ideals-and-quotient-rings/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/euclidean-domains-pids-and-ufds/" %}

Prime and maximal ideals generalize prime numbers and points. Their quotient characterizations are the fastest way to use them.

Integral domains and fields through quotients

Definition. A commutative ring \(R\) is an integral domain if \(ab=0\) implies \(a=0\) or \(b=0\). It is a field if every nonzero element is invertible.

Example. \(\mathbb Z\) and \(F[x]\) are integral domains. The ring \(\mathbb Z/6\mathbb Z\) is not, because \(2\cdot3=0\). The quotient \(\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z\) is a field exactly when \(p\) is prime.

Prime ideals

Definition. A proper ideal \(\mathfrak p\subset R\) is prime if \(ab\in\mathfrak p\) implies \(a\in\mathfrak p\) or \(b\in\mathfrak p\).

Proposition. An ideal \(\mathfrak p\) is prime iff \(R/\mathfrak p\) is an integral domain.

Proof. In \(R/\mathfrak p\), the product \((a+\mathfrak p)(b+\mathfrak p)\) is zero iff \(ab\in\mathfrak p\). The absence of zero divisors is exactly the defining implication for a prime ideal.

Maximal ideals

Definition. A proper ideal \(\mathfrak m\subset R\) is maximal if there is no ideal strictly between \(\mathfrak m\) and \(R\).

Proposition. An ideal \(\mathfrak m\) is maximal iff \(R/\mathfrak m\) is a field. Hence every maximal ideal is prime.

Proof. Ideals of \(R/\mathfrak m\) correspond to ideals of \(R\) containing \(\mathfrak m\). Thus \(\mathfrak m\) is maximal iff the quotient has only the zero ideal and the whole ring, which is equivalent to being a field.

Examples in polynomial rings

Example. In \(F[x]\), a nonzero prime ideal is generated by an irreducible polynomial. Since \(F[x]\) is a PID, such ideals are also maximal. Thus \(F[x]/(f)\) is a field exactly when \(f\) is irreducible.

Counterexample. In \(F[x,y]\), the ideal \((x)\) is prime because \(F[x,y]/(x)\cong F[y]\) is a domain, but it is not maximal because \(F[y]\) is not a field.

Radicals and nilpotents

Definition. The radical of an ideal \(I\) is \[ \sqrt I=\{r\in R:r^n\in I\text{ for some }n\ge1\}. \] An ideal is radical if \(I=\sqrt I\).

Proposition. The nilpotent elements of \(R/I\) correspond exactly to \(\sqrt I/I\). Thus \(I\) is radical iff \(R/I\) has no nonzero nilpotents.

Geometric intuition

Remark. In algebraic geometry, prime ideals behave like irreducible pieces and maximal ideals behave like points. Even when one is not doing geometry, this intuition helps organize quotient rings.
