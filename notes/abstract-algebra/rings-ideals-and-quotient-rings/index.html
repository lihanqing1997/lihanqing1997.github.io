6. Rings, ideals, and quotient rings

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/sylow-theory/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/prime-and-maximal-ideals/" %}

Rings, ideals, and quotient rings records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the abstract algebra note.

Definition

Definition. A ring has addition, multiplication, distributivity, and usually a multiplicative identity. An ideal \(I\) absorbs multiplication by ring elements, allowing quotient multiplication on \(R/I\).

Proposition

Proposition. The kernel of a ring homomorphism is an ideal, and every ideal is the kernel of the quotient map \(R\to R/I\).

Proof

Proof. Absorption follows because homomorphisms preserve products. Conversely, quotienting by an ideal makes two elements equal exactly when their difference lies in the ideal.

Remark

Remark. Ideals are the ring-theoretic analogue of normal subgroups. They encode equations, congruences, and algebraic sets.
