16. Solvable groups and radicals

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/finite-fields/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/group-representations/" %}

Solvable groups measure how far a group is from being built by abelian pieces. The classical link to radicals explains why group theory controls formulas for polynomial roots.

Commutators and derived series

Definition. The commutator of \(g,h\in G\) is \([g,h]=ghg^{-1}h^{-1}\). The commutator subgroup \([G,G]\) is generated by all commutators. The derived series is \[ G^{(0)}=G, \qquad G^{(n+1)}=[G^{(n)},G^{(n)}]. \]

Proposition. The quotient \(G/[G,G]\) is abelian, and every homomorphism from \(G\) to an abelian group factors uniquely through \(G/[G,G]\).

Solvable groups

Definition. A group \(G\) is solvable if \(G^{(n)}=1\) for some \(n\). Equivalently, \(G\) has a subnormal series whose successive quotients are abelian.

Example. Every abelian group is solvable. The symmetric group \(S_3\) is solvable because \(1\trianglelefteq A_3\trianglelefteq S_3\), with quotients \(A_3\cong C_3\) and \(S_3/A_3\cong C_2\).

Simple groups and obstruction

Proposition. A nonabelian simple group is not solvable.

Proof. If \(G\) is nonabelian simple, then \([G,G]\) is a nontrivial normal subgroup. Hence \([G,G]=G\), so the derived series never decreases.

Theorem. The alternating group \(A_5\) is simple and nonabelian. Therefore \(A_5\) and \(S_5\) are not solvable.

Extensions of solvable groups

Theorem. If \(N\trianglelefteq G\), and both \(N\) and \(G/N\) are solvable, then \(G\) is solvable.

Remark. This theorem is the main reason solvability is stable under building groups by layers. It also explains why composition factors matter.

Radical extensions

Definition. A radical extension is built by repeatedly adjoining elements \(\alpha\) such that \(\alpha^n\) lies in the previous field. A polynomial is solvable by radicals if its roots lie in such an extension of the base field.

Theorem. Over a field of characteristic zero, a polynomial is solvable by radicals only if its Galois group is solvable. Under standard hypotheses, the converse also holds.

The quintic boundary

Example. The general polynomial of degree \(5\) has Galois group \(S_5\). Since \(S_5\) is not solvable, there is no formula by radicals for the general quintic.

Remark. This statement does not mean every quintic is unsolvable by radicals. Special quintics may have smaller solvable Galois groups.
