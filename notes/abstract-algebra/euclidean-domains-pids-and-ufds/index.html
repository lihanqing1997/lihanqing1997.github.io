8. Euclidean domains, PIDs, and UFDs

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/prime-and-maximal-ideals/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/modules-and-exact-sequences/" %}

Euclidean domains, PIDs, and UFDs records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the abstract algebra note.

Definition

Definition. A Euclidean domain has a division algorithm measured by a Euclidean function. A principal ideal domain has every ideal generated by one element, and a unique factorization domain has factorization into irreducibles uniquely up to units and order.

Proposition

Proposition. Every Euclidean domain is a PID, and every PID is a UFD. The implications explain why division algorithms imply gcds and unique factorization.

Proof

Proof. The Euclidean algorithm finds generators of ideals. In a PID, irreducibles are prime, and prime factorization follows by induction using divisibility.

Remark

Remark. These classes isolate what makes integer arithmetic work. Many failures in algebraic number theory come from leaving the UFD world.
