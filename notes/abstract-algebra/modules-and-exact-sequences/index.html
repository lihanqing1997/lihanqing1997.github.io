Modules are linear algebra over rings. They keep the language of vectors and linear maps, but bases and dimension may fail, so exact sequences become essential bookkeeping.

Modules and module homomorphisms

Definition. Let \(R\) be a ring. A left \(R\)-module \(M\) is an abelian group with scalar multiplication \(R\times M\to M\) satisfying the usual associativity, distributivity, and identity axioms. An \(R\)-linear map \(f:M\to N\) preserves addition and scalar multiplication.

Example. Vector spaces are modules over fields. Abelian groups are exactly \(\mathbb Z\)-modules. Ideals of a ring \(R\) are \(R\)-submodules of \(R\).

Submodules and quotient modules

Definition. A submodule \(N\subset M\) is an additive subgroup closed under scalar multiplication. The quotient abelian group \(M/N\) becomes an \(R\)-module by \(r(m+N)=rm+N\).

Proposition. Kernels and images of module homomorphisms are submodules, and \(M/\ker f\cong\operatorname{im}f\).

Proof. The subgroup and scalar-closure checks are direct from linearity. The isomorphism theorem is proved exactly as for groups: send \(m+\ker f\) to \(f(m)\).

Exact sequences

Definition. A sequence of modules and linear maps \[ \cdots\to M_{i-1}\xrightarrow{f_{i-1}}M_i\xrightarrow{f_i}M_{i+1}\to\cdots \] is exact at \(M_i\) if \(\operatorname{im}f_{i-1}=\ker f_i\). It is exact if it is exact at every term.

Example. For a submodule \(N\subset M\), the sequence \[ 0\to N\to M\to M/N\to0 \] is exact. It says that \(N\) is embedded in \(M\), and quotienting by its image gives \(M/N\).

Short exact sequences and splitting

Definition. A short exact sequence is an exact sequence \(0\to A\xrightarrow{i}B\xrightarrow{p}C\to0\). It splits if there exists \(s:C\to B\) with \(p\circ s=\operatorname{id}_C\), or equivalently \(r:B\to A\) with \(r\circ i=\operatorname{id}_A\).

Theorem. A short exact sequence \(0\to A\to B\to C\to0\) splits iff \(B\cong A\oplus C\) in a way compatible with the maps.

Counterexample. The sequence \[ 0\to\mathbb Z\xrightarrow{\times 2}\mathbb Z\to\mathbb Z/2\mathbb Z\to0 \] is exact as a sequence of \(\mathbb Z\)-modules but does not split, because \(\mathbb Z\) has no element of order \(2\).

Free, torsion, and finitely generated modules

Definition. An \(R\)-module is free if it has a basis, finitely generated if finitely many elements span it, and torsion over a domain if every element is killed by some nonzero scalar.

Remark. Over a field, every module is free. Over a general ring this is false: \(\mathbb Z/2\mathbb Z\) is a finitely generated \(\mathbb Z\)-module, but it is not free.
