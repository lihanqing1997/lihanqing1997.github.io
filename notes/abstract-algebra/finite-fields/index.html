15. Finite fields

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/galois-groups-and-the-fundamental-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/abstract-algebra/solvable-groups-and-radicals/" %}

Finite fields records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the abstract algebra note.

Definition

Definition. A finite field has \(p^n\) elements for some prime \(p\). Its multiplicative group of nonzero elements is finite and cyclic.

Proposition

Proposition. For each prime power \(q=p^n\) there is a field \(\mathbb F_q\), unique up to isomorphism, and it is the splitting field of \(x^q-x\) over \(\mathbb F_p\).

Proof

Proof. The roots of \(x^q-x\) form a field because they are closed under addition, multiplication, and inverses in characteristic \(p\). Uniqueness follows because every field of size \(q\) consists of these roots.

Remark

Remark. Finite fields are where algebra, number theory, coding theory, and finite geometry meet. Their structure is unusually rigid and computable.
