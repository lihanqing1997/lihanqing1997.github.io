2. Infinitesimals, calculus, and rigor

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/irrational-magnitudes-and-the-first-crisis/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/dedekind-cantor-and-arithmetization/" %}

Calculus was powerful before its foundations were secure. The crisis was to explain why infinitesimal computations worked and which limiting operations were legitimate.

Infinitesimal reasoning

Historical note. Newton and Leibniz used infinitesimal and fluxional reasoning to compute derivatives, integrals, and series. The procedures worked, but their logical status was contested.

Limits replace infinitesimals

Definition. The modern derivative \(f^\prime(x)=\lim_{h\to0}(f(x+h)-f(x))/h\) replaces an infinitesimal quotient by a quantified limit.

Cauchy and Weierstrass

Historical note. Cauchy emphasized limits and convergence; Weierstrass sharpened the epsilon-delta formulation. Uniform convergence became necessary after failures of termwise limiting arguments.

Pathological functions

Example. Continuous nowhere differentiable functions showed that visual intuition about curves was unreliable. Analysis had to distinguish continuity, differentiability, bounded variation, and absolute continuity.
