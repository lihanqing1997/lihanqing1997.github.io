6. Logicism, formalism, and intuitionism

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/zermelo-fraenkel-set-theory-and-choice/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/godel-incompleteness-and-hilberts-program/" %}

After the paradoxes, mathematicians disagreed about what foundations should provide: reduction to logic, formal consistency, or constructive meaning.

Logicism

Historical note. Logicism sought to reduce mathematics to logic. Frege, Russell, and Whitehead pursued systems where arithmetic and analysis would follow from logical principles.

Formalism

Historical note. Hilbert's formalism emphasized axioms, symbolic rules, and consistency proofs. Mathematics was to be secured by studying formal systems themselves.

Intuitionism

Historical note. Brouwer's intuitionism rejected nonconstructive existence proofs and unrestricted excluded middle over infinite domains. Existence should mean construction.

Constructive contrast

Example. A classical proof may establish \(\exists x P(x)\) by contradiction. A constructive proof must provide a method for obtaining such an \(x\).
