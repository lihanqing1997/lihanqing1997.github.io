7. Gödel incompleteness and Hilbert’s program

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/logicism-formalism-and-intuitionism/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/continuum-problem-and-independence/" %}

Gödel showed that strong formal systems cannot be both complete and capable of proving their own consistency in the desired way.

Formal systems

Definition. A formal system has a language, axioms, and inference rules. It is consistent if it proves no contradiction and complete if every sentence or its negation is provable.

First incompleteness theorem

Theorem. Every consistent effectively axiomatized system strong enough for arithmetic has true arithmetic statements it cannot prove.

Second incompleteness theorem

Theorem. Such a system cannot prove its own consistency, provided consistency is formalized in the standard way and the system is in fact consistent.

Hilbert program

Historical note. Hilbert hoped for finitistic consistency proofs for mathematics. Gödel's theorems showed that this original program could not succeed as stated.
