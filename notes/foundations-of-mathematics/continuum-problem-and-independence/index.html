8. Continuum problem and independence

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/godel-incompleteness-and-hilberts-program/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/modern-foundations-and-structural-viewpoints/" %}

The continuum problem asks whether any cardinality lies strictly between the countable infinity of \(\mathbb N\) and the cardinality of \(\mathbb R\).

Continuum hypothesis

Definition. CH states that every infinite subset of \(\mathbb R\) is either countable or has the same cardinality as \(\mathbb R\).

Independence

Theorem. Gödel showed CH cannot be disproved from ZFC if ZFC is consistent; Cohen showed it cannot be proved from ZFC if ZFC is consistent. Thus CH is independent of ZFC.

Forcing

Remark. Forcing constructs extensions of set-theoretic universes while preserving ZFC. It became the central method for proving independence results.

Analysis connection

Remark. Most analysis used in statistics is settled in ZFC, but advanced measure, category, and functional-analysis questions can depend on additional set-theoretic axioms.
