3. Dedekind, Cantor, and arithmetization of analysis

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/infinitesimals-calculus-and-rigor/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/naive-set-theory-and-paradoxes/" %}

Nineteenth-century analysis rebuilt the continuum from arithmetic and set-theoretic ideas. This transformed geometric intuition into precise constructions.

Dedekind cuts

Construction. Dedekind represented real numbers by cuts in \(\mathbb Q\). Supremum becomes union of cuts, so completeness is built into the construction.

Cantor sequences and sets

Historical note. Cantor studied Cauchy sequences, point sets, derived sets, and different sizes of infinity. Problems in Fourier series helped motivate this set-theoretic language.

Cardinality

Definition. Two sets have the same cardinality if there is a bijection between them. Cantor proved \(\mathbb Q\) is countable while \(\mathbb R\) is uncountable.

Continuum lesson

Remark. Completeness and uncountability are different ideas. The real line is analytically useful because it is complete, not merely because it is large.
