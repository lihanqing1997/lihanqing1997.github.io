1. Irrational magnitudes and the first crisis

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/foundations-of-mathematics/infinitesimals-calculus-and-rigor/" %}

The discovery of incommensurable magnitudes showed that rational numbers could not exhaust geometry. This is the first foundational pressure behind the later construction of \(\mathbb R\).

Commensurability

Definition. Two magnitudes are commensurable if their ratio is rational. In modern notation, lengths \(a\) and \(b\) are commensurable when \(a/b\in\mathbb Q\).

Diagonal of the square

Theorem. \(\sqrt2\) is irrational. If \(\sqrt2=m/n\) in lowest terms, then \(m^2=2n^2\), so both \(m\) and \(n\) are even, a contradiction.

Greek response

Historical note. Greek mathematics separated arithmetic of whole numbers from geometry of magnitudes. Euclid's theory of proportion handled ratios without reducing every magnitude to a rational number.

Analytic resolution

Remark. Modern analysis resolves the crisis by constructing a complete ordered field. Dedekind cuts and Cauchy completion are two precise repairs.
