8. Diagonalization and triangularization

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/linear-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/linear-algebra/eigenvalues-and-invariant-subspaces/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/linear-algebra/jordan-form-and-minimal-polynomials/" %}

Diagonalization and triangularization records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the linear algebra note.

Definition

Definition. An operator is diagonalizable if some basis consists of eigenvectors. It is triangularizable if some basis puts its matrix in upper triangular form.

Triangularization theorem

Theorem. If \(V\) has a basis of eigenvectors, then the matrix of \(T\) in that basis is diagonal. Over an algebraically closed field every operator is triangularizable.

Proof

Proof. For triangularization, choose one eigenvector, quotient by its span, triangularize the induced map by induction, then lift a basis back to \(V\).

Remark

Remark. Diagonalization separates independent modes. Triangularization is weaker but always exposes eigenvalues on the diagonal and supports many spectral arguments.
