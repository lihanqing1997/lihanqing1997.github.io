7. Eigenvalues and invariant subspaces

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/linear-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/linear-algebra/determinants-and-volume/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/linear-algebra/diagonalization-and-triangularization/" %}

Eigenvalues and invariant subspaces records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the linear algebra note.

Definition

Definition. An eigenvector of \(T\) is a nonzero vector \(v\) with \(Tv=\lambda v\). An invariant subspace \(U\) satisfies \(T(U)\subset U\), so the operator restricts to \(U\).

Proposition

Proposition. Eigenvalues are roots of the characteristic polynomial \(\det(\lambda I-A)\). Over an algebraically closed field every finite-dimensional operator has at least one eigenvalue.

Proof

Proof. The equation \(Av=\lambda v\) is equivalent to singularity of \(\lambda I-A\). Existence over algebraically closed fields follows because a nonconstant polynomial has a root.

Remark

Remark. Invariant subspaces are more fundamental than individual eigenvectors: they are the pieces from which canonical decompositions are assembled.
