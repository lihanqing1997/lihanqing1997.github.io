10. Inner products and orthogonality

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/linear-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/linear-algebra/jordan-form-and-minimal-polynomials/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/linear-algebra/orthogonal-projections-and-least-squares/" %}

An inner product adds metric geometry to vector spaces: lengths, angles, orthogonality, best approximation, and adjoints. Many later decompositions are algebraic consequences of orthogonality.

Inner products and norms

Definition. An inner product on a real vector space is a map \(\langle\cdot, \cdot\rangle:V\times V\to\mathbb R\) that is bilinear, symmetric, and positive definite. On a complex vector space we use the convention that it is linear in the first variable, conjugate-linear in the second, Hermitian symmetric, and positive definite. The associated norm is \[ \|v\|=\sqrt{\langle v,v\rangle}. \]

Example. With this convention, the standard inner product on \(F^n\) is \(\langle x,y\rangle=y^*x\). On \(C[a,b]\), one uses \(\langle f,g\rangle=\int_a^b f(t)\overline{g(t)}\,\mathrm{d}t\). On \(M_{m,n}(F)\), the Frobenius inner product is \(\langle A,B\rangle=\operatorname{tr}(B^*A)\).

Cauchy-Schwarz and triangle inequality

Theorem. For any vectors \(u,v\), \[ |\langle u,v\rangle|\le \|u\|\|v\|. \] Consequently, \(\|u+v\|\le \|u\|+ \|v\|\).

Proof. If \(v=0\) the result is clear. Otherwise choose \(a=\langle u,v\rangle/\langle v,v\rangle\). Positivity of \(\|u-av\|^2\) gives \[ 0\le \|u\|^2-\frac{|\langle u,v\rangle|^2}{\|v\|^2}, \] which is Cauchy-Schwarz. The triangle inequality follows by expanding \(\|u+v\|^2\) and bounding the cross term by Cauchy-Schwarz.

Orthogonal complements

Definition. For \(S\subset V\), the orthogonal complement is \[ S^\perp=\{v\in V:\langle v,s\rangle=0\text{ for all }s\in S\}. \] It is always a subspace, even when \(S\) is not.

Theorem. If \(U\) is a finite-dimensional subspace of an inner product space \(V\), then \[ V=U\oplus U^\perp \] whenever \(V\) is finite-dimensional. More generally, every \(v\in V\) has a unique best approximation from \(U\), namely its orthogonal projection.

Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization

Theorem. Every linearly independent list \((v_1, \ldots,v_n)\) in an inner product space can be converted into an orthonormal list \((q_1, \ldots,q_n)\) with the same nested spans: \[ \operatorname{span}(q_1, \ldots,q_k)=\operatorname{span}(v_1, \ldots,v_k). \]

Calculation. With the linear-in-the-first convention, the projection of \(v_k\) onto the unit vector \(q_j\) is \[ \operatorname{proj}_{q_j}(v_k)=\langle v_k,q_j\rangle q_j. \] Gram-Schmidt removes all previous orthonormal components and then normalizes: \[ w_k=v_k-\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\langle v_k,q_j\rangle q_j, \qquad q_k=\frac{w_k}{\|w_k\|}. \] Independence ensures \(w_k\ne0\). If one uses the opposite convention, with the inner product linear in the second variable, the coefficient becomes \(\langle q_j,v_k\rangle\).

For instance, in \(\mathbb R^2\), take \(v_1=(1,1)\) and \(v_2=(1,0)\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} q_1&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1),\\ \langle v_2,q_1\rangle&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\\ w_2 &=v_2-\langle v_2,q_1\rangle q_1\\ &=(1,0)-\frac{1}{2}(1,1)\\ &=\frac{1}{2}(1,-1),\\ q_2&=\frac{w_2}{\|w_2\|} =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,-1). \end{aligned} \] This gives an orthonormal list \((q_1,q_2)\) with the same nested spans as \((v_1,v_2)\).

Orthogonal matrices and unitary matrices

Proposition. A square real matrix \(Q\) is orthogonal, or a complex matrix is unitary, if \(Q^*Q=I\). Its columns form an orthonormal basis, \(Q^{-1}=Q^*\), and \(\|Qx\|=\|x\|\) for every \(x\).

Remark. Orthogonality is the algebraic form of no interference. When subspaces are orthogonal, dimensions add cleanly, projections are stable, and computations avoid the amplification that appears in nearly dependent coordinate systems.
