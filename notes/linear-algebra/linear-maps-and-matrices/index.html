4. Linear maps and matrices

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/linear-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/linear-algebra/bases-and-dimension/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/linear-algebra/rank-nullity-and-quotient-spaces/" %}

Linear maps and matrices records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the linear algebra note.

Definition

Definition. A map \(T:V\to W\) is linear if \(T(av+bv')=aT(v)+bT(v')\). After choosing bases, \(T\) is represented by a matrix whose columns are the coordinate vectors of the images of basis elements.

Proposition

Proposition. Composition of linear maps corresponds to matrix multiplication, and change of basis corresponds to conjugation or left-right multiplication by invertible matrices.

Proof

Proof. Evaluate both sides on basis vectors. Since a linear map is determined by its values on a basis, equality on basis vectors proves equality everywhere.

Remark

Remark. Matrices are coordinate shadows of linear maps. Many confusions disappear once one separates intrinsic statements from statements depending on a chosen basis.
