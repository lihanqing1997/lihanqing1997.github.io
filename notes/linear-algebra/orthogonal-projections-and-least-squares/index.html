11. Orthogonal projections and least squares

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/linear-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/linear-algebra/inner-products-and-orthogonality/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/linear-algebra/spectral-theorem-for-normal-operators/" %}

Orthogonal projection turns geometry into an optimization principle: the best approximation is characterized by an error vector perpendicular to the approximating subspace.

The projection theorem

Theorem. Let \(U\) be a finite-dimensional subspace of an inner product space \(V\). For every \(v\in V\), there is a unique decomposition \[ v=u+r, \qquad u\in U, \qquad r\in U^\perp. \] The vector \(u\) is the unique element of \(U\) minimizing \(\|v-w\|\) over \(w\in U\).

Proof. Choose an orthonormal basis \(q_1, \ldots,q_k\) of \(U\) and set \(u=\sum_j\langle v,q_j\rangle q_j\). Then \(v-u\) is orthogonal to each \(q_j\), hence to \(U\). If \(w\in U\), then \(v-w=(v-u)+(u-w)\) with orthogonal summands, so \[ \|v-w\|^2=\|v-u\|^2+\|u-w\|^2, \] minimized uniquely at \(w=u\).

Projection matrices

Proposition. If the columns of \(Q\in F^{m\times k}\) are orthonormal, then the orthogonal projection onto \(\operatorname{col}Q\) is \[ P=QQ^*. \] The matrix \(P\) satisfies \(P^2=P\) and \(P^*=P\). Conversely, any self-adjoint idempotent matrix is an orthogonal projection.

Least squares and normal equations

Theorem. For \(A\in F^{m\times n}\) and \(b\in F^m\), a vector \(\hat x\) minimizes \(\|Ax-b\|\) if and only if \[ A^*(A\hat x-b)=0, \] equivalently \[ A^*A\hat x=A^*b. \] These are the normal equations.

Proof. The set \(\{Ax:x\in F^n\}\) is the column space of \(A\). The closest point \(A\hat x\) to \(b\) is the projection of \(b\) onto that column space. The residual \(b-A\hat x\) must therefore be orthogonal to every column of \(A\), which is exactly \(A^*(b-A\hat x)=0\).

Full column rank formula

Corollary. If \(A\) has full column rank, then \(A^*A\) is invertible and the least-squares solution is unique: \[ \hat x=(A^*A)^{-1}A^*b. \] The projection matrix onto \(\operatorname{col}A\) is \(P=A(A^*A)^{-1}A^*\).

QR factorization

Proposition. If \(A\in F^{m\times n}\) has linearly independent columns, Gram-Schmidt gives \(A=QR\), where \(Q\) has orthonormal columns and \(R\) is upper triangular with nonzero diagonal entries. Then least squares reduces to \[ R\hat x=Q^*b. \]

Example. Fitting a line \(y=\alpha+ \beta t\) to data points \((t_i,y_i)\) is the least-squares problem \(Ax\approx y\) with rows \((1,t_i)\). The residual vector is orthogonal to the columns \((1, \ldots,1)^\top\) and \((t_1, \ldots,t_m)^\top\), giving the classical normal equations for linear regression.
