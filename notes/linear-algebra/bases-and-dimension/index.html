3. Bases and dimension

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/linear-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/linear-algebra/linear-combinations-span-and-independence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/linear-algebra/linear-maps-and-matrices/" %}

Bases and dimension records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the linear algebra note.

Definition

Definition. A basis of \(V\) is a linearly independent spanning set. In a basis every vector has a unique coordinate representation, so \(V\) becomes algebraically identifiable with \(F^n\).

Dimension theorem

Theorem. Any two bases of a finite-dimensional vector space have the same cardinality. This common cardinality is the dimension of the space.

Proof

Proof. The exchange lemma replaces vectors in one basis by vectors from another while preserving spanning. Iterating the replacement shows neither basis can be longer than the other.

Remark

Remark. Dimension is invariant under isomorphism. It is not just a count of coordinates; it is the obstruction to compressing a vector space linearly without losing information.
