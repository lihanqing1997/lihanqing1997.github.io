12. Spectral theorem for normal operators

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/linear-algebra/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/linear-algebra/orthogonal-projections-and-least-squares/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/linear-algebra/singular-value-decomposition/" %}

Spectral theorem for normal operators records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the linear algebra note.

Definition

Definition. An operator on a complex inner-product space is normal if \(TT^*=T^*T\). Self-adjoint, unitary, and skew-adjoint operators are the main special cases.

Spectral theorem

Theorem. A finite-dimensional complex operator is normal exactly when it admits an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors. Self-adjoint operators have real eigenvalues, and unitary operators have eigenvalues on the unit circle.

Proof

Proof. Triangularize the operator using Schur form. Normality forces the triangular matrix to be diagonal by comparing row and column norms.

Remark

Remark. The spectral theorem is the cleanest expression of compatibility between algebra and metric geometry. It powers quadratic forms, functional calculus, and many statistical decompositions.
