12. Markov chain Monte Carlo

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/monte-carlo-integration-simulation-error/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/hamiltonian-monte-carlo-diagnostics/" %}

Markov chain Monte Carlo replaces independent posterior draws by a Markov chain whose invariant law is the posterior. Correctness is about invariance and ergodic averages, not about visual randomness of the path.

Invariant distributions

The chain must preserve the posterior distribution.

Definition. A Markov kernel \(K\) has invariant distribution \(\Pi\) if \[ \int \Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta)K(\theta,A)=\Pi(A) \] for all measurable \(A\).

Proposition. If \(K\) satisfies detailed balance, \[ \Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta)K(\theta,\mathrm{d}\vartheta) =\Pi(\mathrm{d}\vartheta)K(\vartheta,\mathrm{d}\theta), \] then \(\Pi\) is invariant.

Proof. Integrate both sides of detailed balance over \(\theta\) and over \(\vartheta\in A\). The right side becomes \(\Pi(A)\), proving invariance.

Metropolis-Hastings

Metropolis-Hastings builds a reversible kernel from a proposal and an accept-reject step.

Definition. Given proposal density \(q(\theta,\vartheta)\), accept the proposed \(\vartheta\) with probability \[ \alpha(\theta,\vartheta)=1\wedge \frac{\pi(\vartheta\mid X)q(\vartheta,\theta)} {\pi(\theta\mid X)q(\theta,\vartheta)}. \]

Proof. For \(\theta\ne\vartheta\), the transition density is \(q(\theta,\vartheta)\alpha(\theta,\vartheta)\). Multiplying by \(\pi(\theta\mid X)\) gives the minimum of \(\pi(\theta)q(\theta,\vartheta)\) and \(\pi(\vartheta)q(\vartheta,\theta)\), which is symmetric in \(\theta,\vartheta\). The holding probability handles the diagonal.

Ergodic averages

After invariance, the question becomes whether time averages converge to posterior expectations.

Theorem. For a Harris ergodic Markov chain with invariant law \(\Pi\), and integrable \(g\), \[ \frac1N\sum_{s=1}^N g(\theta_s)\to\int g\,\mathrm{d}\Pi \] almost surely.

Proof. Harris ergodicity gives convergence of the transition probabilities to \(\Pi\) and recurrence strong enough for the Markov-chain ergodic theorem. Applying that theorem to the integrable function \(g\) yields almost-sure convergence of time averages to the invariant expectation. In the stationary case this is the pointwise ergodic theorem; the general Harris case follows by regeneration or small-set approximation.

Remark. Autocorrelation changes Monte Carlo variance but not the invariant target. Poor mixing is an efficiency problem unless the chain has not reached the relevant region in practical time.

Trace plots and autocorrelation

MCMC diagnostics should be interpreted as diagnostics of computation, not diagnostics of the statistical model.

Remark. Multiple chains, rank plots, effective sample size, and autocorrelation estimates all try to detect whether the simulated averages are reliable approximations to posterior integrals.
