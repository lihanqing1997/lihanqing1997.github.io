Nonparametric Bayes places priors on functions or measures. Random series priors make this concrete by randomizing coefficients in a basis or frame.

Random series priors

A function-space prior is a probability law on an infinite-dimensional space.

Definition. Let \(\{\phi_j\}\) be basis functions and let \(\xi_j\) be random coefficients. A random series prior has the form \[ f=\sum_{j=1}^{\infty} a_j\xi_j\phi_j \] whenever the series converges in the chosen function norm.

Proposition. If \(\sum_j |a_j|\|\phi_j\|<\infty\) almost surely after weighting by \(|\xi_j|\), then the series converges absolutely in the Banach space.

Proof. The Weierstrass criterion in a Banach space says that absolute summability of the norms implies convergence of the series, because the partial sums are Cauchy.

Support through approximation

Support is determined by which deterministic functions can be approximated by likely coefficient sequences.

Proposition. If every coefficient neighborhood around a target sequence has positive prior probability and the basis expansion of \(f_0\) is approximable by finite truncations, then \(f_0\) lies in the support of the prior.

Proof. Choose a finite truncation close to \(f_0\). Positive prior mass in a small neighborhood of the first finitely many coefficients and sufficiently small tail gives positive probability of a ball around \(f_0\).

Regularity and coefficient decay

Smoothness is encoded through coefficient decay.

Remark. Gaussian, Laplace, and spike-and-slab coefficient priors induce different regularity and sparsity behavior. The prior scale \(a_j\) controls how much mass is placed on smooth or rough functions.

Posterior contraction

Random series priors contract by balancing approximation error and stochastic estimation error.

Proposition. For a truncation level \(J\), a typical rate calculation balances bias from \(\sum_{j>J}a_j\xi_j\phi_j\) with variance of estimating \(J\) effective coefficients.

Proof. The posterior cannot contract faster than the prior can approximate the truth, and the likelihood cannot estimate more than the effective finite-dimensional complexity allowed by the sample size. Equating the two terms gives the usual bias-variance rate heuristic.
