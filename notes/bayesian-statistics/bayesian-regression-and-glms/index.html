7. Bayesian regression and generalized linear models

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/hierarchical-models-exchangeability/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/robust-models-missing-data-data-collection/" %}

Regression models combine likelihood geometry with prior regularization. In linear Gaussian models everything is explicit; in generalized linear models the same ideas appear through curvature and approximation.

Gaussian linear regression

Conjugate Gaussian regression is the algebraic model behind ridge-like Bayesian regularization.

Definition. Let \(Y\mid\beta,\sigma^2\sim N(X\beta,\sigma^2 I_n)\) and \(\beta\sim N(b_0,V_0)\), with \(\sigma^2\) known. The posterior precision and mean are \[ V_n^{-1}=V_0^{-1}+\sigma^{-2}X^\top X,\qquad b_n=V_n(V_0^{-1}b_0+\sigma^{-2}X^\top Y). \]

Proof. The log posterior is the sum of two quadratic forms: \[ -\frac{1}{2}(\beta-b_0)^\top V_0^{-1}(\beta-b_0) -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|Y-X\beta\|^2. \] Collecting the quadratic and linear terms in \(\beta\) gives the displayed normal posterior.

Ridge interpretation

The posterior mode solves a penalized least-squares problem.

Proposition. If \(b_0=0\) and \(V_0=\tau^2 I\), then the posterior mode minimizes \[ \|Y-X\beta\|^2+\frac{\sigma^2}{\tau^2}\|\beta\|^2. \]

Proof. Multiplying the negative log posterior by \(2\sigma^2\) removes constants and yields the squared-error term plus the quadratic prior penalty.

Generalized linear models

For GLMs, the likelihood is no longer quadratic, but local curvature still drives posterior concentration.

Definition. In a canonical GLM, \[ p_\beta(y_i)=\exp\{y_i x_i^\top\beta-b(x_i^\top\beta)+c(y_i)\}. \] The log posterior is \(\ell_n(\beta)+\log\pi(\beta)\).

Remark. Laplace approximation uses the Hessian of \(-\ell_n(\beta)-\log\pi(\beta)\) at the posterior mode. This connects Bayesian GLMs to iteratively reweighted least squares and asymptotic normality.

Separation and prior regularization

In logistic regression, separation can destroy the maximum likelihood estimator but a proper prior can still yield a proper posterior.

Proposition. A proper prior with sufficiently light tails can regularize separated logistic regression by making the posterior normalizing integral finite.

Proof. The likelihood is bounded above by \(1\). Posterior propriety therefore follows if the prior is proper. The prior tail then determines whether posterior moments are finite.
