The Bernstein-von Mises theorem says that, in regular finite-dimensional models, the posterior is asymptotically Gaussian and prior effects disappear at first order.

Total variation formulation

The strongest common formulation compares the whole posterior law, not only means and variances.

Definition. Let \(\hat\theta_n\) be an efficient estimator. A Bernstein-von Mises theorem states \[ \left\|\Pi\left(\sqrt n(\theta-\hat\theta_n)\in\cdot\mid X^{(n)}\right) -N(0,I_{\theta_0}^{-1})\right\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\to0 \] in \(P_{\theta_0}\)-probability.

Remark. Centering at \(\hat\theta_n\) removes the random first-order shift. Centering at \(\theta_0\) gives a Gaussian with random mean \(I_{\theta_0}^{-1}\Delta_n\).

Proof mechanism

The proof has three conceptual parts: localization, LAN, and prior flatness.

Theorem. Assume posterior localization at \(n^{-1/2}\)-scale, LAN at \(\theta_0\), nonsingular Fisher information, and a prior density continuous and positive at \(\theta_0\). Then the finite-dimensional BvM conclusion holds.

Proof. Write the posterior in local coordinates \(h=\sqrt n(\theta-\theta_0)\). Outside large compact sets, localization makes the posterior mass negligible. On compact sets, LAN and prior continuity make the posterior density uniformly close to a Gaussian kernel. Normalizing constants converge by dominated convergence on compact sets plus the tail bound, yielding total variation convergence.

Credible ellipsoids

BvM connects Bayesian credible regions with frequentist confidence regions.

Corollary. Let \(C_n=\{\theta:n(\theta-\hat\theta_n)^\top I_{\theta_0}(\theta-\hat\theta_n)\le\chi^2_{d,1-\alpha}\}\). Under BvM and efficient centering, \(C_n\) has asymptotic posterior probability \(1-\alpha\) and frequentist coverage \(1-\alpha\).

Proof. Posterior credibility follows from convergence of the local posterior to the standard chi-square radius. Frequentist coverage follows because \(\sqrt n(\hat\theta_n-\theta_0)\Rightarrow N(0,I_{\theta_0}^{-1})\).

Where BvM fails

BvM is not automatic in nonregular or infinite-dimensional problems.

Remark. Boundary parameters, weak identifiability, growing dimension, nonparametric models, and irregular functionals can preserve prior influence or produce non-Gaussian limits.
