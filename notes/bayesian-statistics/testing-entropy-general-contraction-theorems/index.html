General contraction theorems turn posterior rate proofs into three verifications: prior mass near the truth, entropy of a sieve, and tests outside the target ball.

Prior mass condition

The posterior denominator is controlled by prior mass in KL-type neighborhoods.

Definition. A KL-variance neighborhood is \[ B_n(P_0,\varepsilon)=\{P:K(P_0,P)\le\varepsilon^2,\; V(P_0,P)\le\varepsilon^2\}, \] where \(V(P_0,P)=P_0[\log(p_0/p)-K(P_0,P)]^2\).

Proposition. If \(\Pi(B_n(P_0,\varepsilon_n))\ge \exp(-C n\varepsilon_n^2)\), then the posterior denominator is not smaller than \(\exp(-(C+c)n\varepsilon_n^2)\) with high \(P_0\)-probability.

Proof. Integrate the likelihood ratio over the KL-variance ball. The mean and variance bounds for log likelihood ratios imply, through exponential or Chebyshev-type control, that the integrated likelihood is not exponentially smaller than its prior mass on that ball.

Entropy of sieves

A sieve restricts attention to a high-prior-mass subset of manageable complexity.

Definition. The covering number \(N(\varepsilon,\mathcal F,d)\) is the smallest number of \(d\)-balls of radius \(\varepsilon\) needed to cover \(\mathcal F\). Its logarithm is metric entropy.

Proposition. If \(\log N(\varepsilon_n,\mathcal F_n,d)\lesssim n\varepsilon_n^2\), then tests can often be union-bounded over an \(\varepsilon_n\)-net of \(\mathcal F_n\).

Proof. A test for one separated ball has exponentially small type-II error. Applying the test to each net point and taking the maximum multiplies the error by the covering number. The entropy condition keeps the product exponentially small.

General contraction theorem

The theorem combines the denominator, sieve, and testing bounds.

Theorem. If the prior mass, sieve entropy, sieve complement mass, and testing conditions all hold at \(\varepsilon_n\), then the posterior contracts at rate \(\varepsilon_n\) up to a sufficiently large constant.

Proof. Decompose the posterior outside the target ball into the part inside the sieve and the sieve complement. Tests control the expected numerator on the sieve. The prior controls the sieve complement. The KL mass lower bound controls the denominator. Markov's inequality converts expected bounds into convergence in probability.

Choice of metric

The metric must match both testing and the scientific target.

Remark. Hellinger distance is natural for density testing; \(L^2\) is natural in Gaussian white noise; weaker metrics may yield faster rates but less inferential content.
