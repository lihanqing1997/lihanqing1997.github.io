41. Bayesian model averaging

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/entropic-variational-inference/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/dilution-priors-dpp-projection-predictive/" %}

Model index

Bayesian model averaging treats the model itself as an unknown quantity. Predictions and summaries should average over posterior model uncertainty rather than condition on a single selected model.

Definition. Let \(M\in\mathcal M\) be a model index with prior \(\pi(M)\), parameter prior \(\pi_M(\theta_M)\), and marginal likelihood \[ m_M(x)=\int p_M(x\mid\theta_M)\pi_M(\theta_M)\,\mathrm{d}\theta_M. \] Then \[ \Pi(M\mid x)={\pi(M)m_M(x)\over\sum_{M'}\pi(M')m_{M'}(x)}. \]

Proposition. For a future observation \(Y\), the BMA predictive distribution is \[ p(y\mid x)=\sum_{M\in\mathcal M}\Pi(M\mid x)\int p_M(y\mid x,\theta_M)\Pi_M(\mathrm{d}\theta_M\mid x). \]

Proof. Apply the law of total probability with respect to the posterior distribution of \(M\), then integrate over the posterior of parameters inside each model.

Occam factor

Remark. The marginal likelihood averages likelihood over prior volume. A complex model is rewarded only when enough of its prior mass predicts the observed data well, producing the Bayesian Occam effect.

Bayes factors and posterior odds

Proposition. For two models \(M_1,M_0\), \[ {\Pi(M_1\mid x)\over\Pi(M_0\mid x)} ={\pi(M_1)\over\pi(M_0)}\,{m_1(x)\over m_0(x)}. \] The second factor is the Bayes factor.

Proof. Divide the two posterior model probabilities. The common normalizing denominator over all models cancels.

Prediction versus selection

Remark. BMA can have excellent predictive behavior even when posterior mass does not concentrate on a single model. This distinction matters when predictors are redundant or when several models approximate the truth equally well.
