A prior on probability measures treats the sampling distribution itself as unknown. The central questions are support, conjugacy, and weak convergence.

Random probability measures

The parameter is now a probability measure \(P\), not a finite-dimensional vector.

Definition. A random probability measure is a measurable map \(P:\Omega\to\mathcal P(\mathcal X)\). A prior on \(\mathcal P(\mathcal X)\) induces a law for iid observations \(X_i\mid P\sim P\).

Definition. Weak neighborhoods of \(P_0\) are determined by finitely many bounded continuous test functions: \[ \left|\int f_j\,\mathrm{d}P-\int f_j\,\mathrm{d}P_0\right|<\varepsilon,\quad j=1,\ldots,k. \]

Weak support

A prior is useful only if it gives positive mass near plausible sampling laws.

Proposition. If every weak neighborhood of \(P_0\) has positive prior probability, then \(P_0\) belongs to the weak support of the prior.

Proof. This is the definition of support for a probability measure on a topological space: a point is in the support if all open neighborhoods of it have positive probability.

Posterior on measures

Bayes formula applies in the space of probability measures just as in parametric models.

Proposition. For iid observations and prior \(\Pi\) on \(\mathcal P(\mathcal X)\), the posterior is \[ \Pi(B\mid X_1,\ldots,X_n) =\frac{\int_B \prod_{i=1}^n p(X_i)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}P)} {\int \prod_{i=1}^n p(X_i)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}P)} \] when the measures have densities \(p\) relative to a common dominating measure.

Proof. This is ordinary Bayes formula with parameter \(P\). The likelihood is the product density of the iid sample under \(P\).

Discrete and continuous random measures

Some priors, such as the Dirichlet process, generate discrete probability measures almost surely even when the base measure is continuous.

Remark. Discreteness of the random measure does not prevent it from being useful for mixture modeling, because mixtures can smooth the induced sampling density.
