2. Posterior prediction and marginal likelihood

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/bayesian-models-priors-posteriors/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/posterior-and-decision/" %}

Prediction and marginal likelihood are obtained by integrating out the unknown parameter. The same normalizing constant that makes the posterior a probability measure also scores the model's predictive fit.

Prior predictive and posterior predictive laws

Before observing data, uncertainty is averaged with the prior. After observing data, the averaging measure becomes the posterior.

Definition. For a future observation \(Y\) with conditional law \(Q_\theta\), the prior predictive and posterior predictive laws are \[ Q_\Pi(C)=\int Q_\theta(C)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta),\qquad Q(C\mid X)=\int Q_\theta(C)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta\mid X). \]

Proposition. If \(Y\perp X\mid\theta\), then \(Q(C\mid X)=\mathbb P(Y\in C\mid X)\).

Proof. Under conditional independence, the joint law factors as \(P_\theta(\mathrm{d}x)Q_\theta(\mathrm{d}y)\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta)\). Disintegrating first with respect to \(X\) leaves the conditional distribution of \(\theta\) given \(X\), and integrating \(Q_\theta(C)\) against this conditional distribution gives the displayed formula.

Marginal likelihood as a normalizing constant

The marginal likelihood is both a predictive density for the observed data and the denominator in Bayes formula.

Definition. In a dominated model, the marginal likelihood is \[ m(x)=\int_\Theta p_\theta(x)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta). \] For independent observations \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\), \(m_n(x^{(n)})=\int\prod_{i=1}^n p_\theta(x_i)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta)\).

Proposition. The sequential predictive factorization is \[ m_n(X_1,\ldots,X_n)=\prod_{i=1}^n p(X_i\mid X_1,\ldots,X_{i-1}), \] where the factor is the posterior predictive density based on the previous observations.

Proof. Write \(m_i(x_{1:i})/m_{i-1}(x_{1:i-1})\). Cancelling the common integral over the first \(i-1\) likelihood factors shows that this ratio is \(\int p_\theta(x_i)\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta\mid x_{1:i-1})\). Multiplying the ratios gives the product formula.

Posterior predictive expectations

Predictive means are posterior means of conditional means, and predictive variances split into noise and parameter uncertainty.

Proposition. If \(Y\mid\theta\) has conditional mean \(a(\theta)\) and variance \(v(\theta)\), then \[ \mathbb E(Y\mid X)=\mathbb E(a(\theta)\mid X), \quad \operatorname{Var}(Y\mid X)=\mathbb E(v(\theta)\mid X)+\operatorname{Var}(a(\theta)\mid X). \]

Proof. These are the tower property and the conditional variance identity applied with the intermediate sigma-field generated by \(\theta\) and \(X\).

Model evidence and Occam factors

A model with a large parameter space is not automatically favored, because predictive mass is averaged over the prior rather than maximized.

Remark. The marginal likelihood rewards models that put high prior predictive mass near the observed data. Concentrated likelihood at a small prior volume can produce an Occam penalty, which is why Bayes factors differ from likelihood-ratio tests based only on maximized likelihoods.
