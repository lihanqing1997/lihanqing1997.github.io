1. Bayesian models, priors, and posteriors

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/posterior-prediction-marginal-likelihood/" %}

Bayesian inference starts from a joint probability law on parameters and observations. The posterior is not an informal update rule; it is a regular conditional distribution obtained by disintegrating that joint law.

Bayesian experiments and joint laws

The model, the prior, and the observation law should be treated as one probability space. This viewpoint keeps the distinction between the sampling distribution \(P_\theta\), the prior \(\Pi\), and the posterior kernel precise.

Definition. Let \((\mathcal X,\mathcal A)\) be the sample space and \((\Theta,\mathcal T)\) the parameter space. A Bayesian experiment consists of a Markov kernel \(\theta\mapsto P_\theta\) from \(\Theta\) to \(\mathcal X\) and a prior probability \(\Pi\) on \(\Theta\). The joint law is \[ M(\mathrm{d}\theta,\mathrm{d}x)=P_\theta(\mathrm{d}x)\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta). \] The marginal law of the data is \(P_\Pi(A)=\int P_\theta(A)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta)\).

Proposition. If \(\Theta\) and \(\mathcal X\) are standard Borel spaces, then there exists a posterior kernel \(x\mapsto\Pi(\cdot\mid x)\) satisfying \[ M(B\times A)=\int_A \Pi(B\mid x)\,P_\Pi(\mathrm{d}x) \] for all \(B\in\mathcal T\) and \(A\in\mathcal A\).

Proof. This is the regular conditional probability theorem for probability measures on standard Borel spaces. Applying it to the coordinate projection \((\theta,x)\mapsto x\) disintegrates the joint law \(M\) with respect to its \(x\)-marginal \(P_\Pi\). The resulting conditional law of the first coordinate is exactly the posterior kernel.

Bayes formula in dominated models

Most calculations use a dominated version of the preceding disintegration. The likelihood appears only after choosing a common dominating measure.

Theorem. Suppose \(P_\theta\ll\mu\) with density \(p_\theta\), and define \[ m(x)=\int_\Theta p_\theta(x)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta). \] For \(P_\Pi\)-almost every \(x\) with \(m(x)>0\), \[ \Pi(B\mid x)=\frac{\int_B p_\theta(x)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta)}{m(x)}. \]

Proof. For rectangles \(B\times A\), \[ M(B\times A)=\int_B\int_A p_\theta(x)\,\mu(\mathrm{d}x)\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta) =\int_A\int_B p_\theta(x)\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta)\,\mu(\mathrm{d}x). \] Also \(P_\Pi(\mathrm{d}x)=m(x)\mu(\mathrm{d}x)\). The displayed ratio gives a probability kernel and reproduces the joint law on rectangles, hence by a monotone class argument it is a version of the posterior.

Support and identifiability

The prior is part of the statistical model. No amount of data can give posterior mass to a region that has prior mass zero.

Proposition. If \(\Pi(B)=0\), then \(\Pi(B\mid X)=0\) for \(P_\Pi\)-almost every \(X\). If two parameters induce the same sampling law, \(P_{\theta_1}=P_{\theta_2}\), then the likelihood cannot distinguish them.

Proof. The first claim follows from Bayes formula, or directly from \(M(B\times\mathcal X)=0\). For the second claim, \(p_{\theta_1}(X)/p_{\theta_2}(X)=1\) \(P_{\theta_1}\)-almost surely under domination, so posterior odds between the two points are changed only by prior odds and not by data.

Likelihood ratios and posterior odds

Posterior odds are the cleanest finite-dimensional expression of Bayesian updating.

Example. For two simple hypotheses \(\theta_0,\theta_1\), \[ \frac{\Pi(\theta_1\mid X)}{\Pi(\theta_0\mid X)} =\frac{\Pi(\theta_1)}{\Pi(\theta_0)} \frac{p_{\theta_1}(X)}{p_{\theta_0}(X)}. \] Thus the posterior odds equal prior odds times the likelihood ratio.
