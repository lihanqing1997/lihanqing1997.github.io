1. Bayesian models, priors, and posteriors

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/posterior-prediction-marginal-likelihood/" %}

This page records the main definitions, propositions, and proof ideas for Bayesian models, priors, and posteriors.

Definition

Definition. A Bayesian experiment consists of a sample law \(P_\theta\), a prior \(\Pi\) on \(\Theta\), the prior predictive law \(P_\Pi(A)=\int P_\theta(A)\,\mathrm{d}\Pi(\theta)\), and a posterior kernel \(\Pi(\cdot\mid X)\).

Proposition

Proposition. In a dominated model with density \(p_\theta\), \[ \Pi(B\mid X)=\frac{\int_B p_\theta(X)\,\mathrm{d}\Pi(\theta)}{\int_\Theta p_\theta(X)\,\mathrm{d}\Pi(\theta)}. \]

Proof

Proof. Write the joint law as \(p_\theta(x)\,\mu(\mathrm{d}x)\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta)\). Conditioning this joint measure on \(x=X\) gives the normalized density above.

Remarks

The prior support is part of the model: posterior mass cannot recover values that the prior assigns zero local mass.
