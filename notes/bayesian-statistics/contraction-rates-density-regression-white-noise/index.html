30. Contraction rates in density, regression, and white-noise models

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/testing-entropy-general-contraction-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/bayesian-statistics/adaptive-bayes-empirical-bayes-hierarchical-bayes/" %}

Contraction rates become concrete in density estimation, nonparametric regression, and Gaussian white noise. Each model has its own testing geometry but the same bias-complexity-prior-mass logic.

White-noise sequence model

The Gaussian sequence model makes the bias-variance calculation explicit.

Definition. In the sequence form of white noise, \[ Y_j=\theta_j+n^{-1/2}Z_j,\qquad Z_j\stackrel{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim}N(0,1). \]

Proposition. If \(\theta_0\) has Sobolev smoothness \(\beta\), the minimax \(L^2\)-rate is \(n^{-\beta/(2\beta+1)}\).

Proof. Estimate the first \(J\) coefficients and ignore the rest. Variance is \(J/n\); squared bias is \(J^{-2\beta}\). Balancing \(J/n\asymp J^{-2\beta}\) gives \(J\asymp n^{1/(2\beta+1)}\) and squared error \(n^{-2\beta/(2\beta+1)}\).

Density estimation

For densities, Hellinger testing often drives the rate theorem.

Remark. Mixture priors and log-density priors require separate approximation estimates, entropy bounds, and prior small-ball estimates. Smoothness of the true density determines the approximation term.

Regression

Nonparametric regression rates resemble white-noise rates when the design is regular and errors are Gaussian.

Proposition. For fixed regular design and Gaussian errors, posterior contraction in empirical \(L^2\) norm follows from the same testing and entropy structure as Gaussian sequence models.

Proof. The likelihood ratio is Gaussian with squared empirical norm as its natural distance. Tests between regression functions are built from normal likelihood ratios, with errors controlled by \(\exp(-c n\|f-g\|_n^2)\).

Adaptive rates

Adaptation requires the prior to put enough mass near each smoothness class without knowing the smoothness in advance.

Remark. Hierarchical priors on truncation, bandwidth, or scale can adapt if their hyperprior mass matches the optimal bias-variance balance over a range of regularities.
