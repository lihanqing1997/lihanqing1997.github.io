Exponential-family form

Bayesian GLMs combine prior regularization with likelihoods whose curvature depends on the link and variance function. In high dimension, the local likelihood geometry must be controlled on sparse cones.

Definition. A one-parameter exponential-family GLM has density \[ p_\beta(y_i\mid x_i)=\exp\left\{ {y_i\theta_i-b(\theta_i)\over a(\phi)}+c(y_i,\phi)\right\}, \] with mean \(\mu_i=b'(\theta_i)\), variance \(a(\phi)b''(\theta_i)\), and link \(g(\mu_i)=x_i^\top\beta\). The canonical link is \(g=(b')^{-1}\), so \(\theta_i=x_i^\top\beta\).

Proposition. For canonical links, the score and Hessian are \[ \dot\ell_n(\beta)=\sum_i x_i\{y_i-b'(x_i^\top\beta)\}/a(\phi),\quad -\ddot\ell_n(\beta)=\sum_i x_ix_i^\top b''(x_i^\top\beta)/a(\phi). \]

Proof. Differentiate the log likelihood with respect to \(x_i^\top\beta\), then multiply by \(x_i\). Differentiating once more gives the weighted Gram matrix with weights \(b''(x_i^\top\beta)/a(\phi)\).

High-dimensional curvature

Definition. A sparse GLM restricted curvature condition requires \[ \Delta^\top\{-n^{-1}\ddot\ell_n(\beta)\}\Delta\ge\kappa\|\Delta_S\|_2^2 \] for \(\Delta\) in a cone \(\|\Delta_{S^c}\|_1\le c\|\Delta_S\|_1\) and for \(\beta\) in a sparse local neighborhood.

Remark. Logistic regression can lose curvature under separation; Poisson regression can have exploding curvature if \(x_i^\top\beta\) is uncontrolled. Sparse BvM arguments must therefore combine design conditions with parameter localization.

Logistic and Poisson local curvature

Proposition. In logistic regression the weight is \(w_i(\beta)=p_i(\beta)\{1-p_i(\beta)\}\le1/4\). In Poisson regression the weight is \(w_i(\beta)=\exp(x_i^\top\beta)\). Hence logistic curvature can vanish under separation, while Poisson curvature can explode without localization.

Proof. The Hessian of a canonical GLM is \(X^\top W X\), with \(W_{ii}=b''(x_i^\top\beta)\). Substitute \(b(\eta)=\log(1+e^\eta)\) and \(b(\eta)=e^\eta\) for logistic and Poisson regression.

Posterior concentration before normality

Remark. A BvM theorem is a second-order statement. It normally requires a first-order contraction theorem first, because the posterior must lie in the region where sparse curvature and Taylor expansion are valid.
