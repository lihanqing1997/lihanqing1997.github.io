Oracle posterior normality

Sparse BvM theory tries to recover the fixed-dimensional BvM theorem after the posterior has learned a low-dimensional active set. The target is usually an oracle Gaussian law on the true or selected coordinates.

Definition. Let \(S_0\) be the true support with \(|S_0|=s_0\). An oracle sparse BvM statement has the form \[ \left\|\Pi\left(\sqrt n(\beta_{S_0}-\hat\beta_{S_0})\in\cdot,\ \beta_{S_0^c}=0\mid X^{(n)}\right)-N(0,I_{S_0}^{-1})\otimes\delta_0\right\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\to0. \]

Theorem. A typical sparse BvM proof requires: posterior concentration on models of size \(O(s_0)\), negligible posterior mass on wrong supports or negligible effect of wrong supports on the target functional, a uniform LAN expansion over sparse neighborhoods, and prior flatness on local \(n^{-1/2}\)-balls inside the active coordinates.

Proof. Decompose the posterior by support. On the oracle support, change variables \(h=\sqrt n(\beta_{S_0}-\beta_{0,S_0})\) and use LAN plus prior flatness. On nonoracle supports, use selection consistency or show their total contribution to the marginal law is negligible. Uniform concentration restricts the argument to sparse local sets where the quadratic approximation is valid.

Dimension growth

Remark. The phrase sparse BvM hides the most delicate point: \(s_0\) may grow. Gaussian approximation, determinant terms, and local prior flatness must remain uniform over dimensions increasing with \(n\).

Uniform LAN on sparse sets

Definition. Sparse LAN means that uniformly over supports \(|S|\le Cs_0\) and bounded \(h\in\mathbb R^S\), \[ \ell_n(\beta_0+h/\sqrt n)-\ell_n(\beta_0) =h^\top\Delta_{n,S}-{1\over2}h^\top I_S h+r_n(S,h), \] with \(\sup |r_n(S,h)|\to0\) in probability on the posterior local set.

Proposition. The main rate restrictions in sparse BvM usually come from controlling the LAN remainder, model-counting terms, and posterior leakage to large or wrong supports.

Proof. The Gaussian change of variables is local and supportwise. Summing over many supports introduces combinatorial factors, while Taylor remainders scale with sparse radius and dimension. The stated restrictions are exactly the places where dimension growth enters the fixed-dimensional BvM proof.
