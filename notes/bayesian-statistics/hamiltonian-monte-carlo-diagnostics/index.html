Hamiltonian Monte Carlo uses gradient information to propose distant moves with high acceptance probability. Its diagnostics reflect failures of numerical geometry.

Hamiltonian augmentation

HMC augments the parameter with momentum so that posterior simulation becomes approximate Hamiltonian flow.

Definition. Let \(U(q)=-\log\pi(q\mid X)\), choose mass matrix \(M\), and introduce momentum \(p\sim N(0,M)\). The Hamiltonian is \[ H(q,p)=U(q)+\frac12p^\top M^{-1}p. \]

Proposition. The joint density proportional to \(\exp\{-H(q,p)\}\) has marginal density \(\pi(q\mid X)\).

Proof. The joint density factors into \(\pi(q\mid X)\) times the Gaussian momentum density. Integrating out \(p\) leaves the posterior density of \(q\).

Leapfrog reversibility and volume preservation

The leapfrog integrator is used because it preserves the geometric properties needed for a Metropolis correction.

Proposition. Leapfrog updates are reversible and volume preserving. Therefore a Metropolis correction using the Hamiltonian error leaves the joint target invariant.

Proof. Each half-step momentum update and full-step position update is a shear map with determinant one. The symmetric composition is reversible after momentum negation. Volume preservation and reversibility are exactly the conditions used in the Metropolis acceptance proof.

Divergences

Divergences indicate that the numerical trajectory has encountered geometry too sharp for the chosen step size and parameterization.

Remark. Divergences often occur near funnels, boundaries, or strong posterior curvature. Increasing target acceptance may help, but reparameterization is often the mathematical fix.

Energy and effective sample size

HMC diagnostics are geometric diagnostics of exploration.

Definition. Energy Bayesian fraction of missing information and related energy diagnostics compare variation in the Hamiltonian across transitions with marginal energy variation. Low energy exploration suggests poor movement across posterior level sets.

Remark. Large effective sample size for some scalar summaries does not guarantee that all posterior geometry has been explored. HMC should be checked on the quantities that matter for the inferential question.
