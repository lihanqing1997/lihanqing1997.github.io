Bayesian inference becomes decision theory once a loss function is specified. The posterior distribution is then used through posterior risk, not only through point summaries.

Posterior risk

A Bayes action minimizes the conditional expected loss after seeing the data.

Definition. Let \(a\in\mathcal A\) be an action and \(L(\theta,a)\) a loss. The posterior risk is \[ \rho(a\mid X)=\int_\Theta L(\theta,a)\,\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta\mid X). \] A Bayes action is any measurable minimizer \(\delta(X)\in\arg\min_a\rho(a\mid X)\).

Theorem. If a measurable posterior-risk minimizer exists, then it minimizes the global Bayes risk \(r(\delta)=\int R(\theta,\delta)\Pi(\mathrm{d}\theta)\).

Proof. By Fubini and the definition of posterior, \[ r(\delta)=\mathbb E_\Pi L(\theta,\delta(X)) =\mathbb E_\Pi\{\rho(\delta(X)\mid X)\}. \] If \(\delta(X)\) minimizes the integrand for each \(X\), then the expectation is no larger than for any other decision rule.

Quadratic and absolute losses

Classical posterior summaries arise as Bayes actions for different losses.

Proposition. Under squared-error loss \(L(\theta,a)=\|\theta-a\|^2\), the Bayes action is \(a=\mathbb E(\theta\mid X)\), whenever the posterior second moment is finite. Under one-dimensional absolute loss, any posterior median is a Bayes action.

Proof. For squared loss, \[ \mathbb E\|\theta-a\|^2=\mathbb E\|\theta-\bar\theta\|^2+\|a-\bar\theta\|^2 \] with \(\bar\theta=\mathbb E(\theta\mid X)\). For absolute loss, a subgradient of \(a\mapsto\mathbb E|\theta-a|\) is \(\Pi(\theta\le a\mid X)-\Pi(\theta\ge a\mid X)\), so zero belongs to the subgradient exactly at a median.

Credible decisions

Credible sets become decision procedures only after one states what loss or coverage target they are intended to serve.

Definition. A \(1-\alpha\) credible set is a measurable set \(C(X)\) such that \(\Pi(C(X)\mid X)\ge 1-\alpha\). A highest posterior density set has the form \(\{\theta:p(\theta\mid X)\ge c_X\}\) when a posterior density exists.

Remark. Posterior credibility is a conditional probability statement under the Bayesian joint law. Frequentist coverage is a different repeated-sampling property and requires separate analysis.

Randomized actions

For convex losses, randomization is usually unnecessary; for nonconvex losses it can be part of the decision space.

Proposition. If \(L(\theta,a)\) is convex in \(a\), then replacing a randomized action by its conditional mean cannot increase posterior risk.

Proof. Condition on the data and on the posterior draw of \(\theta\). Jensen's inequality gives \(L(\theta,\mathbb E A)\le \mathbb E\{L(\theta,A)\}\). Integrating over the posterior proves the claim.
