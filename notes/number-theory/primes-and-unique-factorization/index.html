2. Primes and unique factorization

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/number-theory/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/number-theory/divisibility-and-euclidean-algorithm/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/number-theory/congruences-and-modular-arithmetic/" %}

Primes and unique factorization records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the number theory note.

Definition

Definition. A prime integer \(p\) has only trivial positive divisors. Unique factorization states that every nonzero integer factors into primes uniquely up to order and signs.

Proposition

Proposition. Euclid's lemma, \(p\mid ab\Rightarrow p\mid a\) or \(p\mid b\), implies unique prime factorization in \(\mathbb Z\).

Proof

Proof. Euclid's lemma follows from Bezout identity for \(p\) and an integer not divisible by \(p\). Existence is by descent; uniqueness follows by repeatedly matching prime divisors.

Remark

Remark. Unique factorization is the arithmetic baseline. Much of algebraic number theory studies what survives when it fails.
