Regularization restricts the effective size of the predictor chosen by empirical risk minimization. It can be written as a penalty, a constraint, or an algorithmic bias.

Penalized empirical risk

A penalty expresses a preference among predictors with similar empirical fit.

Definition. Given a penalty \(J(f)\ge0\), a penalized ERM solves \[ \hat f_\lambda\in\operatorname*{argmin}_{f\in\mathcal F} \left\{P_n\ell_f+\lambda J(f)\right\}. \]

Proposition. For every \(f\in\mathcal F\), \[ P_n\ell_{\hat f_\lambda}+\lambda J(\hat f_\lambda) \le P_n\ell_f+\lambda J(f). \] Consequently, after adding and subtracting population risks, the excess risk is controlled by empirical-process terms plus \(\lambda\{J(f)-J(\hat f_\lambda)\}\).

Proof. The first inequality is the definition of the minimizer. Subtract \(P\ell_{\hat f_\lambda}\) and \(P\ell_f\) on the two sides to express the population risk difference through \((P_n-P)(\ell_f-\ell_{\hat f_\lambda})\) and the penalty difference.

Strong convexity and stability

Strongly convex regularization can make the algorithm stable, and stability implies generalization.

Theorem. If the objective is \(\lambda\)-strongly convex and each loss is \(L\)-Lipschitz in the predictor norm, then changing one observation changes the regularized solution by \(O(L/(\lambda n))\) under standard boundedness assumptions.

Proof. Let \(\hat f\) and \(\hat f^{(i)}\) be solutions trained on samples differing in one point. Strong convexity gives quadratic growth of each objective away from its minimizer. Adding the two optimality inequalities cancels all common sample terms, leaving only the two changed losses. Lipschitzness bounds these by \(L\|\hat f-\hat f^{(i)}\|/n\), which yields \(\|\hat f-\hat f^{(i)}\|=O(L/(\lambda n))\).

Implicit regularization

Some algorithms regularize without an explicit penalty.
