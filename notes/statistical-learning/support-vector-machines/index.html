Support vector machines combine a convex margin loss with norm regularization. Their geometry is separation by a maximum-margin hyperplane in a feature space.

Hard-margin geometry

When the data are linearly separable, the SVM chooses the separating hyperplane with largest geometric margin.

Definition. For \(y_i\in\{-1,1\}\), the hard-margin problem is \[ \min_{w,b}\frac12\|w\|_2^2 \quad\text{subject to}\quad y_i(\langle w,x_i\rangle+b)\ge1,\ i=1,\ldots,n. \] The geometric margin is \(1/\|w\|_2\).

Proposition. Minimizing \(\|w\|_2\) under the normalized constraints maximizes the minimum geometric margin.

Proof. The constraints force the functional margin to be at least \(1\). The corresponding geometric margin is at least \(1/\|w\|_2\). Rescaling \((w,b)\) changes functional margins and \(\|w\|\) by the same factor, so the normalization is harmless and minimizing \(\|w\|\) maximizes the normalized geometric margin.

Soft-margin objective

Nonseparable data require slack or, equivalently, hinge loss.

Definition. The soft-margin SVM solves \[ \min_{w,b}\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n(1-y_i(\langle w,x_i\rangle+b))_+ +\lambda\|w\|_2^2. \]

Remark. The hinge loss is zero beyond margin \(1\), linear for margin violations, and convex. This shape makes only some observations active in the optimum.

Dual problem and support vectors

The dual reveals the kernelization and the role of support vectors.

Theorem. For the hard-margin linear SVM, the dual is \[ \max_{\alpha_i\ge0}\sum_i\alpha_i-\frac12\sum_{i,j}\alpha_i\alpha_jy_iy_j\langle x_i,x_j\rangle \quad\text{subject to}\quad \sum_i\alpha_iy_i=0. \] At optimum, \(w=\sum_i\alpha_iy_ix_i\).

Proof. Form the Lagrangian \[ L(w,b,\alpha)=\frac12\|w\|^2-\sum_i\alpha_i\{y_i(\langle w,x_i\rangle+b)-1\}. \] Stationarity in \(w\) gives \(w=\sum_i\alpha_iy_ix_i\); stationarity in \(b\) gives \(\sum_i\alpha_iy_i=0\). Substituting these conditions into the Lagrangian gives the dual objective.

Generalization through norm and margin

The SVM bound is a margin bound for a norm-bounded linear class.

Proposition. If \(\|x\|_2\le R\) and \(\|w\|_2\le B\), the Rademacher complexity of linear scores \(x\mapsto\langle w,x\rangle\) is at most \(BR/\sqrt n\).

Proof. By the linear-class formula, \[ \widehat{\mathfrak R}_n\le \frac{B}{n}\mathbb E_\varepsilon\left\|\sum_i\varepsilon_ix_i\right\|_2. \] Jensen's inequality and independence of the signs give \[ \mathbb E_\varepsilon\left\|\sum_i\varepsilon_ix_i\right\|_2 \le \left(\sum_i\|x_i\|_2^2\right)^{1/2}\le R\sqrt n. \]
