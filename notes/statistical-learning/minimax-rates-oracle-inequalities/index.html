23. Minimax rates and oracle inequalities

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/statistical-learning/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/statistical-learning/nonconvex-sparse-penalties/" %}

Sparse minimax rate

Theorem. In Gaussian sparse linear regression with \(s\)-sparse \(\beta^*\), the prediction and estimation rates contain the complexity term \(s\log(p/s)\). A canonical squared-error rate is \[ {\sigma^2s\log(p/s)\over n} \] up to design constants.

Proof. The upper bound comes from penalized estimators or Bayesian contraction: estimate \(s\) coefficients and pay a model-search price \(\log {p\choose s}\asymp s\log(p/s)\). The lower bound follows by packing many \(s\)-sparse vectors separated in loss and applying Fano's inequality.

Oracle inequalities

Definition. An oracle inequality compares an estimator with the best candidate in a class: \[ R(\hat f)\le C\inf_{m\in\mathcal M}\{R(f_m)+\operatorname{pen}(m)\}+r_n. \]

Proposition. For Lasso-type estimators, the oracle term balances approximation by a sparse vector and stochastic complexity of the chosen support.

Remark. Minimax rates answer what no method can uniformly beat. Oracle inequalities answer whether an adaptive method behaves nearly as well as the best sparse approximation chosen with hindsight.

Fano lower bound template

Theorem. If one can construct an \(s\)-sparse packing \(\{\beta^1,\ldots,\beta^M\}\) with pairwise loss separation \(2\delta\) and average KL divergence at most \(\alpha\log M\) for \(\alpha\lt1\), then every estimator has worst-case risk at least a constant multiple of \(\delta\).

Proof. Estimating \(\beta\) would identify the packing index up to small error. Fano's inequality lower-bounds the probability of misidentifying that index when the KL information is smaller than the logarithmic packing size. Loss separation converts identification error into estimation risk.
