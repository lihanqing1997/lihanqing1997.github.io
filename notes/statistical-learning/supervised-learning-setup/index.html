Statistical learning formalizes prediction as risk minimization under an unknown data-generating law. The central object is not the fitted model itself, but the excess risk it incurs relative to the best available rule.

Learning problems and losses

A learning problem begins with a sample space, an action space, and a loss that tells us what prediction quality means.

Definition. Let \((\mathcal X,\mathcal A)\) be the feature space, \((\mathcal Y,\mathcal B)\) the response space, and \(\mathcal A_0\) an action space. A predictor is a measurable map \(f:\mathcal X\to\mathcal A_0\). Given a loss \(\ell:\mathcal A_0\times\mathcal Y\to[0,\infty)\) and a distribution \(P\) on \(\mathcal X\times\mathcal Y\), the risk is \[ R(f)=P\ell_f=\int \ell(f(x),y)\,P(\mathrm{d}x,\mathrm{d}y). \] The training sample is usually \(Z_i=(X_i,Y_i)\), \(i=1,\ldots,n\), drawn i.i.d. from \(P\).

Remark. The word model can mean a probabilistic family for \(P\), a class of predictors \(\mathcal F\), or an algorithm. Statistical learning separates these: the distribution generates data, the class restricts candidates, and the algorithm chooses from the class.

Population and empirical risk

The empirical risk is observable; the population risk is the target. The theory is the study of how close these two optimization problems are.

Definition. For a class \(\mathcal F\), define \[ R_n(f)=P_n\ell_f=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(f(X_i),Y_i), \qquad f_{\mathcal F}^*\in\operatorname*{argmin}_{f\in\mathcal F}R(f). \] An empirical risk minimizer satisfies \(R_n(\hat f_n)\le\inf_{f\in\mathcal F}R_n(f)+\eta_n\), where \(\eta_n\ge0\) is optimization error.

Proposition. For any approximate ERM \(\hat f_n\), \[ R(\hat f_n)-R(f_{\mathcal F}^*)\le 2\sup_{f\in\mathcal F}|(P_n-P)\ell_f|+\eta_n. \]

Proof. Let \(f^*=f_{\mathcal F}^*\). Add and subtract empirical risks: \[ R(\hat f_n)-R(f^*)\le (P-P_n)\ell_{\hat f_n} +(P_n\ell_{\hat f_n}-P_n\ell_{f^*})+(P_n-P)\ell_{f^*}. \] The middle term is at most \(\eta_n\) by approximate empirical optimality, and the two remaining terms are bounded by the same uniform deviation.

Excess risk decomposition

Risk decomposes into statistical, approximation, and algorithmic pieces. Keeping the pieces separate prevents one from blaming estimation for a misspecified class or optimization for insufficient data.

Proposition. Let \(f^*\) be the unrestricted Bayes rule, \(f_{\mathcal F}^*\) the best rule in \(\mathcal F\), and \(\hat f_n\) an approximate estimator in \(\mathcal F\). Then \[ R(\hat f_n)-R(f^*)= \{R(\hat f_n)-R(f_{\mathcal F}^*)\} +\{R(f_{\mathcal F}^*)-R(f^*)\}. \] The first term is estimation plus optimization error; the second is approximation error.

Proof. Insert and subtract \(R(f_{\mathcal F}^*)\). No inequality is hidden here: the equality is useful because different assumptions control the two terms.

Remark. Modern learning theory often sharpens the first term by replacing a global complexity of \(\mathcal F\) with a localized complexity near \(f_{\mathcal F}^*\).

Measurability and random classes

The empirical-process notation \(P_n f\) silently assumes enough measurability to take suprema. This is a technical point, but it matters in proofs.

Definition. A class \(\mathcal G\) of measurable functions is \(P\)-Glivenko-Cantelli if \[ \sup_{g\in\mathcal G}|P_ng-Pg|\to0 \] in probability or almost surely. It is \(P\)-Donsker if the empirical process \(\sqrt n(P_n-P)\) indexed by \(\mathcal G\) converges weakly in \(\ell^\infty(\mathcal G)\).

Remark. Learning bounds usually need Glivenko-Cantelli behavior. Distributional refinements, uncertainty quantification, and localized asymptotics need Donsker or related empirical-process control.
