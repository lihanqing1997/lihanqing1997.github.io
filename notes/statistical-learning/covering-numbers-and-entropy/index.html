Covering numbers measure metric size. In learning theory they quantify how many finite representatives are needed to approximate a loss class at a chosen accuracy.

Metric entropy

Entropy replaces an infinite class by finite nets at multiple scales.

Definition. For a metric space \((T,d)\), the covering number \(N(\varepsilon,T,d)\) is the smallest number of \(d\)-balls of radius \(\varepsilon\) needed to cover \(T\). The metric entropy is \(\log N(\varepsilon,T,d)\).

Example. The Euclidean ball \(B_2^p(R)\) satisfies \[ N(\varepsilon,B_2^p(R),\|\cdot\|_2)\le \left(1+\frac{2R}{\varepsilon}\right)^p. \] This is a volume comparison between a large ball and disjoint small balls centered at a maximal separated set.

Bracketing

For function classes, brackets often give stronger integral bounds than ordinary covers.

Definition. For functions on \((\mathcal Z,P)\), an \(L^r(P)\)-bracket \([l,u]\) is the set of \(g\) satisfying \(l\le g\le u\) and \(\|u-l\|_{L^r(P)}\le\varepsilon\). The bracketing number \(N_{[]}(\varepsilon,\mathcal G,L^r(P))\) is the minimum number of such brackets covering \(\mathcal G\).

Remark. Brackets control both approximation and order. This is why monotone and bounded-variation classes often have clean bracketing entropy bounds.

Dudley entropy integral

Chaining converts entropy at all scales into an expected supremum bound.

Theorem. For a sub-Gaussian process \((X_t:t\in T)\) with canonical metric \(d(s,t)^2=\mathbb E(X_s-X_t)^2\), \[ \mathbb E\sup_{t\in T}(X_t-X_{t_0}) \le C\int_0^{\operatorname{diam}(T)}\sqrt{\log N(\varepsilon,T,d)}\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon. \]

Proof. Choose nested \(2^{-k}\)-nets and write each \(X_t-X_{t_0}\) as a telescoping sum of increments between successive net projections. At each scale, the maximum over finitely many sub-Gaussian increments is bounded by the scale times the square root of the log covering number. Summing over scales and passing to the integral gives Dudley's bound.

Entropy and uniform convergence

Entropy bounds become learning bounds after applying them to the loss class.

Proposition. If \(\mathcal G\) is uniformly bounded and \[ \int_0^1\sqrt{\log N(\varepsilon,\mathcal G,L^2(P_n))}\,\mathrm{d}\varepsilon \] is bounded in probability, then \(\mathcal G\) has controlled Rademacher complexity and hence uniform deviations of order \(n^{-1/2}\) up to the entropy integral.

Proof. Conditional on the data, the Rademacher process indexed by \(\mathcal G\) is sub-Gaussian under the empirical \(L^2(P_n)\) metric. Dudley's bound controls its expected supremum. Symmetrization then transfers this control to \(\sup_g|(P_n-P)g|\).
