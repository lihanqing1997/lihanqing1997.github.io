2. Bayes rules and excess risk

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/statistical-learning/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/statistical-learning/supervised-learning-setup/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/statistical-learning/empirical-risk-minimization/" %}

Bayes rules identify the irreducible part of prediction. Excess risk is the mathematical scale on which a learned rule is compared with this optimal decision.

Conditional risk and Bayes rules

The Bayes rule minimizes conditional risk pointwise. This converts a global optimization problem into a family of ordinary optimization problems indexed by \(x\).

Definition. For a predictor \(a=f(x)\), the conditional risk is \[ L(a,x)=\mathbb E\{\ell(a,Y)\mid X=x\}. \] A Bayes rule is any measurable \(f^*\) satisfying \(f^*(x)\in\operatorname*{argmin}_{a\in\mathcal A_0}L(a,x)\) for \(P_X\)-almost every \(x\).

Theorem. If a measurable pointwise minimizer \(f^*\) exists, then \(f^*\) minimizes \(R(f)\) over all measurable predictors.

Proof. By the tower property, \[ R(f)=\mathbb E\,L(f(X),X). \] For almost every \(x\), \(L(f^*(x),x)\le L(f(x),x)\). Integrating over \(P_X\) gives \(R(f^*)\le R(f)\).

Regression under squared loss

Under squared loss, the Bayes rule is the regression function and excess risk is an \(L^2(P_X)\) distance.

Theorem. For \(\ell(a,y)=(a-y)^2\), the Bayes rule is \(m(x)=\mathbb E(Y\mid X=x)\), and \[ R(f)-R(m)=\mathbb E\{f(X)-m(X)\}^2. \]

Proof. Write \[ Y-f(X)=Y-m(X)+m(X)-f(X). \] After squaring and conditioning on \(X\), the cross term vanishes because \(\mathbb E\{Y-m(X)\mid X\}=0\). This yields the displayed identity.

Remark. This identity explains why nonparametric regression theory is often \(L^2(P_X)\) approximation theory plus stochastic control.

Classification under zero-one loss

In binary classification, only the sign relative to \(1/2\) matters.

Definition. Let \(Y\in\{0,1\}\) and \(\eta(x)=P(Y=1\mid X=x)\). For zero-one loss, the Bayes classifier is \[ g^*(x)=\mathbf 1\{\eta(x)\ge1/2\}. \]

Theorem. For any classifier \(g\), \[ R(g)-R(g^*)=\mathbb E\left[|2\eta(X)-1|\mathbf 1\{g(X)\ne g^*(X)\}\right]. \]

Proof. Condition on \(X=x\). The conditional error of predicting \(1\) is \(1-\eta(x)\), and the conditional error of predicting \(0\) is \(\eta(x)\). Choosing the smaller gives the Bayes error. If \(g\) disagrees with \(g^*\), it chooses the larger conditional error, and the increase is \(|(1-\eta(x))-\eta(x)|=|2\eta(x)-1|\). Integrate over \(X\).

Margin and noise

Classification is easier when \(\eta(X)\) is usually far from \(1/2\).

Definition. The Tsybakov margin condition with exponent \(\alpha>0\) is \[ P_X(0<|\eta(X)-1/2|\le t)\le C t^\alpha,\qquad t>0. \]

Remark. The condition says there is little probability mass near the decision boundary. It is one source of fast rates because classification mistakes near the boundary are the only mistakes that can have small excess-risk cost.
