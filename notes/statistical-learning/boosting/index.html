Boosting builds a strong predictor by adding weak learners sequentially. Its mathematical forms include margin maximization, functional gradient descent, and additive modeling.

Additive models

Boosting searches in the linear span of a weak learner class.

Definition. Given weak learners \(\mathcal H\), an additive predictor has the form \[ F_T(x)=\sum_{t=1}^T\alpha_t h_t(x),\qquad h_t\in\mathcal H. \] For classification \(Y\in\{-1,1\}\), prediction is \(\operatorname{sign}F_T(x)\).

Remark. The number of boosting rounds, shrinkage, and weak learner complexity all act as regularization parameters.

AdaBoost and exponential loss

AdaBoost can be derived as greedy minimization of empirical exponential loss.

Proposition. At round \(t\), AdaBoost chooses a weak learner using weights proportional to \(\exp(-Y_iF_{t-1}(X_i))\). If its weighted error is \(e_t<1/2\), the optimal step for exponential loss along that learner is \[ \alpha_t=\frac12\log\frac{1-e_t}{e_t}. \]

Proof. The empirical exponential loss after adding \(\alpha h\) is \[ \sum_i w_i e^{-\alpha Y_ih(X_i)} =e^{-\alpha}\sum_{Y_ih=1}w_i+e^\alpha\sum_{Y_ih=-1}w_i. \] With normalized weights, this equals \(e^{-\alpha}(1-e_t)+e^\alpha e_t\). Differentiating and setting the derivative to zero gives the displayed \(\alpha_t\).

Training-error decay

Weak learning gives multiplicative decay of exponential loss.

Theorem. If each weak learner has weighted error \(e_t=1/2-\gamma_t\), then AdaBoost's empirical zero-one error is bounded by \[ \prod_{t=1}^T 2\sqrt{e_t(1-e_t)} \le \exp\left(-2\sum_{t=1}^T\gamma_t^2\right). \]

Proof. Zero-one loss is bounded by exponential loss. At each step, the normalized exponential loss is multiplied by \(2\sqrt{e_t(1-e_t)}\). Since \(e_t(1-e_t)=1/4-\gamma_t^2\) and \(\sqrt{1-4\gamma_t^2}\le e^{-2\gamma_t^2}\), the result follows.

Margins and overfitting

Boosting often continues to improve margins after training error reaches zero.

Remark. The empirical margin distribution can explain why more rounds need not immediately overfit. Generalization, however, still depends on weak learner complexity, shrinkage, sample size, and noise.
