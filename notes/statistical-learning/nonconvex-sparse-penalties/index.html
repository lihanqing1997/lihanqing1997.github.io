22. Nonconvex sparse penalties

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/statistical-learning/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/statistical-learning/compatibility-irrepresentability-support-recovery/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/statistical-learning/minimax-rates-oracle-inequalities/" %}

Folded-concave penalties

SCAD and MCP are designed to reduce the large-coefficient bias of the Lasso while keeping sparsity-inducing behavior near zero.

Definition. A separable penalized estimator minimizes \[ {1\over 2n}\|y-X\beta\|_2^2+\sum_{j=1}^p p_\lambda(|\beta_j|). \] SCAD and MCP choose \(p_\lambda\) concave on \([0,\infty)\), with derivative near zero equal to \(\lambda\) and derivative eventually zero for large coefficients.

Proposition. For an orthogonal design, a folded-concave penalty yields thresholding rules that set small coordinates to zero but apply less shrinkage to large coordinates than soft-thresholding.

Proof. Under orthogonality the objective separates coordinatewise. The derivative equation is \(\beta_j-z_j+p_\lambda'(|\beta_j|)\operatorname{sign}(\beta_j)=0\). Since \(p_\lambda'\) decreases to zero for large \(|\beta_j|\), large coordinates solve approximately \(\beta_j=z_j\), unlike Lasso where the shrinkage remains \(\lambda\).

Oracle property

Theorem. Under beta-min, local concavity, and suitable design conditions, folded-concave penalized estimators can have the oracle property: they recover the true support and estimate active coefficients as if the support were known.

Remark. The price is nonconvexity. Computation, initialization, and local minima become part of the statistical theorem rather than merely implementation details.

SCAD and MCP derivatives

Definition. SCAD and MCP are specified by decreasing derivatives. MCP has \(p'_\lambda(t)=(\lambda-t/a)_+\). SCAD has derivative \(\lambda\) for \(t\le\lambda\), \((a\lambda-t)/(a-1)\) for \(\lambda

Proposition. The derivative becoming zero for large coefficients is the source of reduced asymptotic bias compared with Lasso.

Proof. The KKT equation on a nonzero coordinate is score plus \(p'_\lambda(|\beta_j|)\operatorname{sign}(\beta_j)=0\). If \(p'_\lambda\) vanishes for large signals, the large-coordinate equation matches the unpenalized score equation.
