Margin theory explains why classification can generalize well even when the classifier class is large: confident correct predictions are less sensitive to small perturbations.

Functional and geometric margins

For linear classifiers, the same separating rule can be rescaled, so the meaningful quantity is normalized margin.

Definition. For labels \(Y\in\{-1,1\}\) and score \(f\), the functional margin is \(Yf(X)\). For a linear score \(f_w(x)=\langle w,x\rangle+b\), the geometric margin of \((x,y)\) is \[ \frac{y(\langle w,x\rangle+b)}{\|w\|_2}. \]

Remark. The sign of the margin determines correctness; its magnitude measures confidence. Large-margin methods control the norm of the scoring function while forcing margins to be positive.

Margin losses

Margin losses upper bound zero-one error and are easier to optimize.

Definition. A margin loss has the form \(\phi(yf(x))\), where \(\phi:\mathbb R\to[0,\infty)\). Common choices include the hinge loss \((1-u)_+\), logistic loss \(\log(1+e^{-u})\), and exponential loss \(e^{-u}\).

Proposition. If \(\phi(u)\ge \mathbf 1\{u\le0\}\), then \[ P(Yf(X)\le0)\le P\phi(Yf(X)). \]

Proof. The pointwise inequality \(\mathbf 1\{Yf(X)\le0\}\le \phi(Yf(X))\) holds for every observation. Taking expectations gives the result.

Margin bounds

Generalization can be controlled by the complexity of the real-valued score class and the inverse margin.

Theorem. Let \(\mathcal F\) be a class of scores bounded by \(B\). With probability at least \(1-\delta\), for every \(f\in\mathcal F\) and every \(\gamma>0\), \[ P(Yf(X)\le0) \le P_n(Yf(X)\le\gamma) +\frac{2}{\gamma}\widehat{\mathfrak R}_n(\mathcal F_Y) +3\sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\delta)}{2n}}, \] where \(\mathcal F_Y=\{(x,y)\mapsto yf(x):f\in\mathcal F\}\).

Proof. Upper bound the zero-one loss by the ramp loss that equals \(1\) on \((-\infty,0]\), equals \(0\) on \([\gamma,\infty)\), and is \(1/\gamma\)-Lipschitz. Apply the Rademacher generalization bound to this loss class and use contraction to obtain the complexity term. The empirical ramp loss is bounded by \(P_n(Yf(X)\le\gamma)\).

Low-noise fast rates

Margins in the distribution can improve rates beyond the slow \(n^{-1/2}\) scale.

Definition. A low-noise or margin condition controls the mass near the decision boundary, for example \[ P_X(|\eta(X)-1/2|\le t)\le Ct^\alpha. \]

Remark. When little mass lies near the boundary, disagreement with the Bayes classifier has excess risk comparable to a stronger distance. This curvature is what enables fast-rate classification bounds.
