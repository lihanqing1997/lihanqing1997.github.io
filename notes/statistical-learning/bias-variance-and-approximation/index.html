5. Bias, variance, and approximation

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/statistical-learning/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/statistical-learning/concentration-inequalities/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/statistical-learning/vc-dimension/" %}

Bias-variance analysis is a useful finite-dimensional identity, but in learning theory it is only one piece of the larger approximation-estimation tradeoff.

Squared-error decomposition

For regression estimators, the mean squared error splits into noise, squared bias, and variance.

Theorem. Let \(Y=m(X)+\varepsilon\), \(\mathbb E(\varepsilon\mid X)=0\), and let \(\hat m(x)\) be a random estimator trained on data independent of a fresh \((X,Y)\). Then \[ \mathbb E\{Y-\hat m(X)\}^2 =\mathbb E\sigma^2(X) +\mathbb E\{\mathbb E\hat m(X)-m(X)\}^2 +\mathbb E\{\hat m(X)-\mathbb E\hat m(X)\}^2, \] where \(\sigma^2(X)=\mathbb E(\varepsilon^2\mid X)\).

Proof. Condition on \(X\). Write \[ Y-\hat m(X)=\varepsilon+m(X)-\mathbb E\hat m(X)+\mathbb E\hat m(X)-\hat m(X). \] The three components have zero cross terms after taking expectation: the noise has conditional mean zero, and the centered estimator has mean zero over the training randomness. The identity follows.

Approximation and estimation

In function-class learning, approximation error is deterministic and estimation error is stochastic.

Definition. For a class \(\mathcal F_k\) indexed by complexity \(k\), define \[ A(k)=\inf_{f\in\mathcal F_k}R(f)-R(f^*), \qquad E_n(k)=R(\hat f_{k,n})-\inf_{f\in\mathcal F_k}R(f). \] The risk is controlled by \(A(k)+E_n(k)\).

Proposition. If \(A(k)\lesssim k^{-2s/d}\) and \(E_n(k)\lesssim k/n\), then the optimized rate is \[ \inf_k\{A(k)+E_n(k)\}\lesssim n^{-2s/(2s+d)}. \]

Proof. Balance \(k^{-2s/d}\) with \(k/n\). The equality \(k^{-2s/d}\asymp k/n\) gives \(k\asymp n^{d/(2s+d)}\). Substituting gives \(n^{-2s/(2s+d)}\).

Smoothing parameters

A smoothing parameter controls bias by restricting roughness and variance by averaging observations.

Example. For a kernel smoother with bandwidth \(h\) in \(d\) dimensions, a typical heuristic risk is \[ h^{2s}+\frac{1}{nh^d}. \] The first term is squared bias for \(s\)-smooth functions; the second is variance from an effective sample size \(nh^d\).

Proposition. Optimizing \(h^{2s}+1/(nh^d)\) yields \(h\asymp n^{-1/(2s+d)}\) and risk \(n^{-2s/(2s+d)}\).

Proof. Set \(h^{2s}\asymp 1/(nh^d)\), hence \(h^{2s+d}\asymp n^{-1}\). The claimed bandwidth and risk follow.

Beyond the classical identity

Modern learning often has interpolation, implicit regularization, and nonconvex optimization. Bias-variance language remains useful only when tied back to risk.

Remark. A method can have zero training error and still generalize when the algorithmic bias selects a low-complexity interpolating solution. In such settings, norm, margin, stability, or compression may be more informative than the classical parameter-count variance.
