7. Rademacher complexity

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/statistical-learning/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/statistical-learning/vc-dimension/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/statistical-learning/covering-numbers-and-entropy/" %}

Rademacher complexity measures the ability of a real-valued class to correlate with pure noise on the observed sample. It is a data-dependent and geometry-sensitive alternative to pure counting.

Empirical complexity

The empirical complexity is computed on the realized design points and then averaged over random signs.

Definition. For functions \(\mathcal G\) on \(\mathcal Z\), the empirical Rademacher complexity is \[ \widehat{\mathfrak R}_n(\mathcal G) =\mathbb E_\varepsilon\sup_{g\in\mathcal G}\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n\varepsilon_i g(Z_i), \] where \(\varepsilon_i\) are independent signs with \(P(\varepsilon_i=1)=P(\varepsilon_i=-1)=1/2\).

Remark. If a class can fit random signs well, it can also fit noise in the sample. This is why \(\widehat{\mathfrak R}_n\) appears naturally in generalization bounds.

Symmetrization

Symmetrization is the main bridge from unknown expectations to observable random fluctuations.

Theorem. For any class \(\mathcal G\) with integrable envelope, \[ \mathbb E\sup_{g\in\mathcal G}|(P_n-P)g| \le 2\,\mathbb E\widehat{\mathfrak R}_n(\mathcal G). \]

Proof. Let \(Z_1',\ldots,Z_n'\) be an independent ghost sample. Jensen's inequality gives \[ \mathbb E\sup_g(P-P_n)g \le \mathbb E\sup_g(P_n'-P_n)g. \] Conditioning on both samples, the distribution of \(\sum_i(g(Z_i')-g(Z_i))\) is unchanged after multiplying each paired difference by an independent sign. The supremum of the signed difference is bounded by the sum of two signed empirical suprema, giving the factor \(2\). Apply the same argument to the negative class for absolute values.

Contraction

Lipschitz transformations of a class do not increase Rademacher complexity beyond the Lipschitz factor.

Theorem. If \(\phi_i:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R\) are \(L\)-Lipschitz and \(\phi_i(0)=0\), then \[ \mathbb E_\varepsilon\sup_{g\in\mathcal G}\frac1n\sum_i\varepsilon_i\phi_i(g(Z_i)) \le L\,\mathbb E_\varepsilon\sup_{g\in\mathcal G}\frac1n\sum_i\varepsilon_i g(Z_i). \]

Proof. This is the Ledoux-Talagrand contraction inequality. The proof replaces coordinates one at a time and uses the fact that, conditional on the other signs, the two-point oscillation of \(\phi_i\circ g\) is at most \(L\) times the oscillation of \(g\). Iterating over coordinates gives the result.

Linear classes

For linear prediction, complexity is controlled by the dual norm of the design.

Proposition. Let \(\mathcal G=\{z\mapsto\langle w,z\rangle:\|w\|\le B\}\) for a norm with dual \(\|\cdot\|_*\). Then \[ \widehat{\mathfrak R}_n(\mathcal G) =\frac{B}{n}\mathbb E_\varepsilon\left\|\sum_{i=1}^n\varepsilon_iZ_i\right\|_*. \]

Proof. By duality, \[ \sup_{\|w\|\le B}\left\langle w,\sum_i\varepsilon_iZ_i\right\rangle =B\left\|\sum_i\varepsilon_iZ_i\right\|_*. \] Divide by \(n\) and take the conditional expectation over the signs.
