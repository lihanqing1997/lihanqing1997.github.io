14. High-dimensional sparsity

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/statistical-learning/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/statistical-learning/support-vector-machines/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/statistical-learning/model-selection-and-aggregation/" %}

High-dimensional learning is possible when the target has low effective complexity, such as sparsity, even if the ambient dimension exceeds the sample size.

Sparse linear model

The canonical example is linear regression with many covariates but few active coefficients.

Definition. In the linear model \(y=X\beta^*+\xi\), the vector \(\beta^*\in\mathbb R^p\) is \(s\)-sparse if \(|S|=s\) for \(S=\{j:\beta_j^*\ne0\}\). The Lasso estimator solves \[ \hat\beta\in\operatorname*{argmin}_{\beta\in\mathbb R^p} \left\{\frac{1}{2n}\|y-X\beta\|_2^2+\lambda\|\beta\|_1\right\}. \]

Remark. The \(\ell^1\) norm is convex but has corners aligned with coordinate sparsity. That geometry is why it can select sparse solutions.

Basic inequality

Most Lasso theory begins with one deterministic inequality.

Lemma. Let \(\Delta=\hat\beta-\beta^*\). If \(\lambda\ge 2\|X^\top\xi/n\|_\infty\), then \[ \frac1{2n}\|X\Delta\|_2^2+\frac{\lambda}{2}\|\Delta_{S^c}\|_1 \le \frac{3\lambda}{2}\|\Delta_S\|_1. \]

Proof. Optimality of \(\hat\beta\) compared with \(\beta^*\) gives \[ \frac1{2n}\|X\Delta\|_2^2 \le \frac{\xi^\top X\Delta}{n}+\lambda(\|\beta^*\|_1-\|\hat\beta\|_1). \] The stochastic term is bounded by \((\lambda/2)\|\Delta\|_1\). Since \(\beta^*_{S^c}=0\), \[ \|\beta^*\|_1-\|\hat\beta\|_1\le \|\Delta_S\|_1-\|\Delta_{S^c}\|_1. \] Combining terms gives the displayed inequality.

Restricted eigenvalues

Prediction and estimation rates require curvature on sparse cones, not on all of \(\mathbb R^p\).

Definition. A restricted eigenvalue condition holds with constant \(\kappa>0\) if \[ \frac{\|X\Delta\|_2}{\sqrt n}\ge \kappa\|\Delta_S\|_2 \] for all \(\Delta\) satisfying \(\|\Delta_{S^c}\|_1\le3\|\Delta_S\|_1\).

Theorem. Under the basic-inequality event and the restricted eigenvalue condition, \[ \frac1n\|X(\hat\beta-\beta^*)\|_2^2\lesssim \frac{\lambda^2s}{\kappa^2}, \qquad \|\hat\beta-\beta^*\|_1\lesssim \frac{\lambda s}{\kappa^2}. \]

Proof. The basic inequality implies the cone condition \(\|\Delta_{S^c}\|_1\le3\|\Delta_S\|_1\). Then \(\|\Delta_S\|_1\le\sqrt s\|\Delta_S\|_2\le \sqrt s\,\|X\Delta\|_2/(\sqrt n\kappa)\). Substitute this into the basic inequality and solve for \(\|X\Delta\|_2^2/n\). The \(\ell^1\) bound follows by combining the cone condition with the resulting \(\ell^2\) bound on \(\Delta_S\).

Choice of penalty

The penalty level must dominate noise correlations with all covariates.

Proposition. If columns of \(X\) have empirical norm at most \(1\) and \(\xi_i\) are sub-Gaussian with scale \(\sigma\), then \[ \left\|X^\top\xi/n\right\|_\infty =O_P\left(\sigma\sqrt{\frac{\log p}{n}}\right). \]

Proof. For each coordinate, \(X_j^\top\xi/n\) is sub-Gaussian with scale at most \(\sigma/\sqrt n\). A union bound over \(p\) coordinates gives the \(\sqrt{\log p/n}\) maximum scale.
