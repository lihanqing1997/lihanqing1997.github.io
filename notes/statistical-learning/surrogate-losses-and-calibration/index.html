10. Surrogate losses and calibration

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/statistical-learning/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/statistical-learning/margins-in-classification/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/statistical-learning/regularization/" %}

Surrogate losses replace discontinuous decision losses by convex or smooth objectives. Calibration is the condition that optimizing the surrogate also optimizes the target decision problem.

Surrogate risk

A surrogate is useful only if its risk remembers the original task.

Definition. For binary \(Y\in\{-1,1\}\), a margin surrogate has risk \[ R_\phi(f)=\mathbb E\,\phi(Yf(X)). \] It is classification-calibrated if every sequence with \(R_\phi(f_n)\to\inf_f R_\phi(f)\) also has zero-one risk approaching the Bayes risk.

Remark. Convexity helps computation; calibration is the statistical condition that prevents the convex problem from solving the wrong task.

Conditional calibration

Calibration can be checked pointwise through the conditional risk.

Theorem. Let \(\eta=P(Y=1\mid X=x)\). For a convex differentiable margin loss \(\phi\), classification calibration holds if \(\phi'(0)<0\).

Proof. The conditional surrogate risk is \[ C_\eta(t)=\eta\phi(t)+(1-\eta)\phi(-t). \] At \(t=0\), \(C_\eta'(0)=(2\eta-1)\phi'(0)\). If \(\eta>1/2\), then \(C_\eta'(0)<0\), so a minimizer must prefer positive scores; if \(\eta<1/2\), the sign reverses. Thus the surrogate minimizer has the Bayes sign.

Excess-risk transfer

A calibrated surrogate gives a quantitative link between surrogate excess risk and classification excess risk.

Theorem. For every classification-calibrated convex margin loss, there exists a nondecreasing function \(\psi\) with \(\psi(0)=0\) such that \[ \psi(R(g_f)-R^*)\le R_\phi(f)-R_\phi^*. \]

Proof. Define the best conditional surrogate risk under the correct sign and under the wrong sign. The gap between these two conditional optima is positive away from \(\eta=1/2\) by calibration. Taking the convex lower envelope of the resulting conditional gap yields \(\psi\). Integrating the conditional inequality gives the displayed bound.

Logistic and hinge losses

The most common surrogates have different geometry but the same calibrated direction.

Example. The logistic loss \(\log(1+e^{-u})\) is smooth and strictly convex in the margin. The hinge loss \((1-u)_+\) is nonsmooth and produces sparse support-vector solutions in linear RKHS formulations.

Remark. The logistic surrogate estimates conditional probabilities naturally through log-odds, while hinge loss focuses on the decision boundary and margin.
