Stability theory studies how much an algorithm changes when one training point is replaced. Stable algorithms generalize because no single observation can dominate the fitted rule.

Uniform stability

Uniform stability is an algorithmic condition, not a property of the function class alone.

Definition. An algorithm \(A\) has uniform stability \(\beta_n\) with respect to loss \(\ell\) if for any two samples \(S,S'\) differing in one observation, \[ \sup_z |\ell(A(S),z)-\ell(A(S'),z)|\le\beta_n. \]

Remark. This definition is deliberately worst-case. Weaker notions such as hypothesis stability or on-average stability can give sharper but more delicate results.

Expected generalization gap

Uniform stability directly controls the expected difference between population and empirical risk.

Theorem. If \(A\) has uniform stability \(\beta_n\), then \[ \left|\mathbb E\{R(A(S))-R_n(A(S))\}\right|\le\beta_n. \]

Proof. Let \(S^{(i)}\) replace \(Z_i\) by an independent copy \(Z_i'\). By exchangeability, \[ \mathbb E R(A(S))=\frac1n\sum_i\mathbb E\,\ell(A(S^{(i)}),Z_i). \] Compare this with \(n^{-1}\sum_i\mathbb E\,\ell(A(S),Z_i)\). Each term differs by at most \(\beta_n\) by stability.

High-probability stability

Stability also gives concentration when the loss is bounded.

Proposition. If \(0\le\ell\le M\) and \(A\) has stability \(\beta_n\), then the generalization gap concentrates at a scale controlled by \(\beta_n\) and \(M/\sqrt n\).

Proof. Changing one data point changes the generalization gap by at most a constant multiple of \(\beta_n+M/n\). Apply the bounded-differences inequality to the gap and combine it with the expected stability bound.

Regularized ERM

Strong convexity is a common route from optimization geometry to stability.

Theorem. For \(L\)-Lipschitz losses and \(\lambda\)-strongly convex regularization, regularized ERM has stability of order \(L^2/(\lambda n)\).

Proof. Compare the two regularized objectives for samples differing in one point. Strong convexity controls the distance between minimizers, while Lipschitzness converts that distance into a difference in losses. Since only one empirical term changes, the factor \(1/n\) appears.
