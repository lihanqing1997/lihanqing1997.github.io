15. Model selection and aggregation

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/statistical-learning/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/statistical-learning/high-dimensional-sparsity/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/statistical-learning/stability-and-generalization/" %}

Model selection chooses among candidate procedures; aggregation combines them. Both aim to mimic the best candidate while paying only a controlled complexity price.

Penalized selection

A penalty should compensate for searching over many models.

Definition. Given models \(\mathcal F_m\), a penalized selector chooses \[ \hat m\in\operatorname*{argmin}_m\left\{\inf_{f\in\mathcal F_m}P_n\ell_f+\operatorname{pen}(m)\right\}. \]

Proposition. If \(\operatorname{pen}(m)\) dominates the stochastic fluctuation of model \(m\), then the selected estimator satisfies an oracle inequality of the form \[ R(\hat f_{\hat m})\le C\inf_m\{\inf_{f\in\mathcal F_m}R(f)+\operatorname{pen}(m)\} \] with high probability.

Proof. The penalized optimality inequality compares \(\hat m\) with an arbitrary \(m\). Add and subtract population risks in both selected and comparison models. The penalty domination absorbs the empirical-process fluctuations, leaving the population oracle expression.

Holdout and cross-validation

Sample splitting estimates risk on data not used for fitting.

Definition. In holdout selection, train candidate estimators \(\hat f_m^{\mathrm{tr}}\) on a training split and select the one minimizing validation risk on an independent validation split.

Proposition. Conditional on the training data, finite-class concentration applies to validation losses and yields an oracle inequality over the trained candidates.

Proof. Once the training data are fixed, the candidates are fixed functions of the validation observations. Apply the finite-class Hoeffding union bound to their validation risks and then compare the validation minimizer with the best candidate.

Exponential weights

Aggregation can average predictors instead of selecting a single one.

Definition. For candidate predictors \(f_1,\ldots,f_M\), exponential weights take \[ w_j=\frac{\exp(-\eta nR_n(f_j))}{\sum_{k=1}^M\exp(-\eta nR_n(f_k))} \] and aggregate by \(\bar f=\sum_jw_jf_j\) when the prediction space is convex.

Theorem. For convex losses, exponential weighting satisfies oracle bounds of order \((\log M)/n\) under exp-concavity or related curvature conditions.

Proof. The log-sum-exp variational identity gives \[ -\eta^{-1}n^{-1}\log\sum_j\pi_j e^{-\eta nR_n(f_j)} =\inf_Q\left\{Q R_n+\frac{\operatorname{KL}(Q\|\pi)}{\eta n}\right\}. \] Concentration or exp-concavity transfers this empirical variational inequality to population risk, producing the \(\log M/n\) term for point-mass \(Q\).

AIC, BIC, and prediction

Information criteria encode different asymptotic goals.

Remark. AIC estimates predictive risk and tends to favor models with good out-of-sample prediction. BIC approximates marginal likelihood under fixed-dimensional parametric assumptions and is oriented toward model identification when the true model is among the candidates.
