Distribution shift separates training performance from deployment performance. The central problem is to compare risks under two laws, usually called source and target.

Source and target risks

The same predictor can have different risks under different feature-response laws.

Definition. Let \(P\) be the source law and \(Q\) the target law. The source and target risks are \[ R_P(f)=P\ell_f,\qquad R_Q(f)=Q\ell_f. \] Domain adaptation asks for small \(R_Q(f)\) using mostly or only data from \(P\), plus structural information linking \(P\) and \(Q\).

Remark. Without assumptions connecting \(P\) and \(Q\), source data alone cannot guarantee target performance.

Covariate shift

Under covariate shift, the conditional law of labels is stable and only the feature distribution changes.

Definition. Covariate shift assumes \(P(Y\mid X)=Q(Y\mid X)\) but \(P_X\ne Q_X\). If \(Q_X\ll P_X\), let \(w(x)=\mathrm{d}Q_X/\mathrm{d}P_X\).

Proposition. Under covariate shift, \[ R_Q(f)=\mathbb E_P\left[w(X)\ell(f(X),Y)\right]. \]

Proof. Disintegrate both laws into conditional label laws and feature marginals. The conditional parts agree, while \(Q_X(\mathrm{d}x)=w(x)P_X(\mathrm{d}x)\). Substituting into the target risk gives the weighted source expectation.

Divergence-based comparison

Target risk can be bounded by source risk plus a discrepancy between distributions.

Proposition. For bounded losses \(0\le\ell_f\le M\), \[ |R_Q(f)-R_P(f)|\le M\|Q-P\|_{\mathrm{TV}}. \]

Proof. The signed measure \(Q-P\) applied to \(\ell_f\) has absolute value at most \(\|\ell_f\|_\infty\|Q-P\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\), with the convention that total variation is the supremum over bounded functions by norm \(1\).

Representation and invariant prediction

A representation can make domains closer, but invariance must preserve predictive information.

Remark. If a map \(\Phi(X)\) removes all domain information but also removes label information, adaptation fails. Useful invariant representations balance small domain discrepancy with small conditional or approximation error in the representation.
