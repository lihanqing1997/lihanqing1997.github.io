Axiomatic set theory was the attempt to keep Cantor's universe while preventing the paradoxes. The guiding idea is simple and powerful: sets are built by permitted operations, not summoned by arbitrary descriptions.

The cumulative picture

Modern set theory imagines a hierarchy. Start with the empty set, form power sets, collect earlier stages, and continue through ordinal time. A set appears only when it is formed at some stage. This picture makes it harder for the universe of all sets to masquerade as one more set.

Definition. ZF consists of axioms including extensionality, empty set, pairing, union, power set, infinity, separation, replacement, foundation, and choice if one passes to ZFC.

Separation and replacement

Separation says one may carve a subset out of an existing set. Replacement says the image of a set under a definable function is again a set. Together they let ordinary mathematics proceed without allowing a set of all sets.

Proposition. In ZF, Russell's class \(\{x:x\notin x\}\) may be described as a class, but it is not forced to be a set; hence the contradiction is blocked.

Choice and equivalent forms

Theorem. Over ZF, the axiom of choice is equivalent to the well-ordering theorem and to Zorn's lemma.

The axiom of choice says that for every family of nonempty sets there is a function choosing one element from each. It is indispensable in much of algebra, topology, and functional analysis, yet it can produce nonconstructive objects such as nonmeasurable sets.

What axioms changed

Axioms changed the emotional tone of mathematics. Instead of asking whether a totality is intuitively legitimate, mathematicians ask what follows from specified rules. This shift made independence results possible: some questions are not settled by the chosen axioms.

Choice as a fork in the road

Choice is historically important because it makes the tension between existence and construction visible. With choice, every vector space has a basis and every product of compact spaces is compact. Without choice, some of these familiar statements can fail or require separate assumptions.

The Banach-Tarski paradox and nonmeasurable sets show the cost of such general existence principles. The lesson is not that choice is wrong, but that foundational assumptions carry mathematical personality: they decide which objects are available and which proofs count as acceptable.

Choice, replacement, and the cumulative universe

Zermelo introduced choice and the first modern set axioms; Fraenkel and Skolem strengthened them through replacement and formal precision; von Neumann clarified the cumulative hierarchy and ordinals; Hausdorff and Kuratowski made set-theoretic methods central in topology.
