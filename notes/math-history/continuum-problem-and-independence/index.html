Cantor discovered that \(\mathbb N\) and \(\mathbb R\) have different sizes. The continuum problem asks whether there is a size strictly between countable infinity and the size of the real line. It became Hilbert's first problem and later a model case for independence.

Continuum hypothesis

Definition. The continuum hypothesis states that every infinite subset of \(\mathbb R\) is either countable or has the same cardinality as \(\mathbb R\). Equivalently, \(2^{\aleph_0}=\aleph_1\).

Gödel's constructible universe

Gödel constructed the inner model \(L\), the universe of constructible sets. Inside \(L\), the axioms of ZFC hold and the continuum hypothesis holds. Therefore, if ZFC is consistent, ZFC cannot disprove CH.

Theorem. If ZF is consistent, then ZFC plus the continuum hypothesis is consistent relative to ZF.

Cohen's forcing

Paul Cohen invented forcing to build models in which new sets are added in a controlled way. He showed that CH can fail while the axioms of ZFC remain true. This completed the independence result.

Theorem. If ZFC is consistent, then ZFC plus the negation of the continuum hypothesis is also consistent.

Meaning of independence

Independence does not mean that the question is meaningless. It means that the chosen axioms do not settle it. Set theorists may then study stronger axioms, forcing axioms, large cardinals, or structural criteria for deciding which extensions of ZFC are mathematically fruitful.

Models as mathematical worlds

The independence of CH changed the meaning of foundational work. Axioms are no longer only rules for deduction; they also describe possible universes. One model can satisfy CH, another can satisfy \(\neg\mathrm{CH}\), and both can satisfy ZFC if the background consistency assumptions hold.

This made model construction a central method. Gödel's \(L\) shows how a thin, definable universe behaves; forcing shows how to extend a universe by adding generic objects. Together they teach that the set-theoretic universe has a geometry of possible extensions.

Cardinality, constructibility, and forcing

Cantor posed the continuum problem through his theory of cardinality; Hilbert placed it first on his 1900 list; Gödel proved relative consistency with CH; Cohen proved relative consistency with not-CH and introduced forcing, one of the great methods of modern logic.
