After the paradoxes, mathematicians did not agree on what mathematics fundamentally is. Three programs gave different answers: logicism tried to reduce mathematics to logic, formalism treated mathematics as rule-governed symbolic practice, and intuitionism grounded mathematics in constructive mental acts.

Logicism

Frege wanted arithmetic to follow from logic alone. Russell and Whitehead carried the project into Principia Mathematica, replacing naive comprehension by type distinctions. Logicism made explicit how much ordinary mathematics depends on quantification, identity, and classes.

Proposition. Logicism succeeds in revealing the logical form of large parts of mathematics, but the need for type theory, reducibility principles, or set-theoretic axioms shows that arithmetic is not obtained from completely neutral logic without further commitments.

Formalism

Hilbert's formalism asked for axioms and proof rules to be written explicitly, then sought finitary proofs that these formal systems are consistent. The slogan was not that symbols are meaningless, but that reliability should be certified by a precise metamathematics.

Definition. A formal system consists of a formal language, axioms, and inference rules. A proof is a finite sequence of formulas, each an axiom or following from earlier formulas by a rule.

Intuitionism

Brouwer rejected the unrestricted use of classical existence proofs. To assert \(\exists x P(x)\) should mean that one can construct such an \(x\). The law of excluded middle is not accepted as a universal principle for infinite mathematical domains.

Constructive principle. A constructive proof of \(\exists x P(x)\) supplies a witness \(x\) and a proof of \(P(x)\). A proof by contradiction may show \(\neg\neg\exists x P(x)\) without producing such a witness.

What the debate taught

The three programs turned foundations into a study of mathematical meaning. Do proofs discover objects, manipulate symbols, or construct possibilities? Modern mathematics often uses all three temperaments: logical clarity, formal proof, and constructive content.

Legacy in modern practice

The debate survives in ordinary mathematical choices. A classical analyst may prove existence by contradiction; a constructive analyst asks for an algorithm or witness; a proof theorist studies the formal strength needed for the argument; a logician asks which axioms are doing the work. These are not merely philosophical moods but different ways of controlling information in a proof.

Proof assistants make this visible again. Lean, Coq, Agda, and related systems require definitions, inference rules, and dependencies to be explicit. In that sense, the old foundational programs return as working infrastructure for checking modern mathematics.

Logicism, formalism, and intuitionism

Frege gave logicism its first great system; Russell and Whitehead rebuilt it through type theory; Hilbert shaped formalism and proof theory; Brouwer founded intuitionism; Heyting gave intuitionistic logic a formal calculus.
