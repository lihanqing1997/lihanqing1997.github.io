34. Lebesgue decomposition and singularity

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/radon-nikodym-derivatives/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/distribution-functions-and-lebesgue-stieltjes-measures/" %}

Measures can split into a part visible to another measure and a singular part living on a null set. This explains densities, atoms, and singular laws in one theorem.

Mutual singularity

Definition. Measures \(\nu\) and \(\mu\) are mutually singular if some measurable set carries all of \(\nu\) and none of \(\mu\). Write \(\nu\perp\mu\).

Lebesgue decomposition

Theorem. For sigma-finite measures, \(\nu=\nu_a+\nu_s\) where \(\nu_a\ll\mu\) and \(\nu_s\perp\mu\). The decomposition is unique.

Three parts on the line

Proposition. A probability law on \(\mathbb R\) may have atomic, absolutely continuous, and singular continuous components. Distribution functions decompose into jumps, density-integral parts, and singular continuous parts.

Cantor distribution

Example. The Cantor distribution is continuous but singular with respect to Lebesgue measure. Its mass lives on the Cantor set, which has Lebesgue measure zero.

Testing interpretation

Remark. If \(P\perp Q\), some measurable set separates the hypotheses perfectly. Absolute continuity prevents this exact separation and forces likelihood-ratio analysis instead.
