34. Lebesgue decomposition and singularity

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/radon-nikodym-derivatives/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/distribution-functions-and-lebesgue-stieltjes-measures/" %}

Lebesgue decomposition separates a measure into the part visible through a reference measure and the part living on a reference-null set.

Mutual singularity

Singular measures occupy disjoint measurable supports.

Definition. Measures \(\nu\) and \(\mu\) are mutually singular, written \(\nu\perp\mu\), if there is a measurable set \(N\) such that \(\mu(N)=0\) and \(\nu(X\setminus N)=0\).

Example. A point mass \(\delta_x\) is singular with respect to Lebesgue measure, because it is concentrated on the Lebesgue-null set \(\{x\}\).

Lebesgue decomposition theorem

Every sigma-finite measure splits into absolutely continuous and singular parts.

Theorem. If \(\nu\) and \(\mu\) are sigma-finite, then there are unique measures \(\nu_a,\nu_s\) such that \(\nu=\nu_a+\nu_s\), \(\nu_a\ll\mu\), and \(\nu_s\perp\mu\).

Proof. Apply the Radon-Nikodym theorem to the absolutely continuous part obtained by maximizing \(\rho\le\nu\) among measures dominated by \(\mu\). The remaining measure cannot contain a nonzero absolutely continuous submeasure; by Hahn decomposition applied to \(\nu-\lambda\mu\) over increasing \(\lambda\), this forces singularity. Uniqueness follows because a measure both absolutely continuous and singular with respect to \(\mu\) must be zero.

Decomposition of distribution functions

On the line, finite measures correspond to increasing functions.

Proposition. A distribution function decomposes into an absolutely continuous part, a pure jump part, and a singular continuous part.

Proof. Apply Lebesgue decomposition to the associated Lebesgue-Stieltjes measure relative to Lebesgue measure. The singular part further splits into its atoms and its atomless singular remainder. Translating these measures back into cumulative functions gives the stated decomposition.

Cantor singular measure

Singularity need not mean discreteness.

Example. The Cantor distribution is continuous and singular with respect to Lebesgue measure. It assigns full mass to the Cantor set, which has Lebesgue measure zero, but has no atoms.

Detecting singularity

Singular and absolutely continuous parts never overlap.

Corollary. If \(\nu\ll\mu\) and \(\nu\perp\mu\), then \(\nu=0\).

Proof. Singularity gives a set \(N\) with \(\mu(N)=0\) and \(\nu(X\setminus N)=0\). Absolute continuity gives \(\nu(N)=0\). Hence \(\nu(X)=0\).
