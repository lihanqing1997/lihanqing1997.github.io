7. Mean value theorems

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/differentiability-and-taylor-expansion/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/riemann-integration/" %}

Mean value theorems records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the real analysis note.

Definition

Definition. Mean value theorems connect derivative information to finite increments of a function. They require continuity on a closed interval and differentiability inside.

Mean value theorem

Theorem. If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\), then \(f(b)-f(a)=f'(c)(b-a)\) for some \(c\in(a,b)\).

Proof

Proof. Subtract the secant line from \(f\) so the endpoints agree. Rolle's theorem gives an interior point where the derivative of the adjusted function vanishes.

Remark

Remark. The mean value theorem is the engine behind monotonicity tests, inverse function estimates, Taylor's theorem, and ODE uniqueness.
