16. Mean value theorems and consequences

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/differentiability-and-taylor-expansion/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/convex-functions-and-monotone-functions/" %}

Mean value theorems turn local derivative information into global finite-difference information. They are the engine behind monotonicity, inverse estimates, and Taylor remainders.

Rolle's theorem

Rolle's theorem is the zero-slope principle between equal endpoint values.

Theorem. If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\), differentiable on \((a,b)\), and \(f(a)=f(b)\), then some \(c\in(a,b)\) satisfies \(f'(c)=0\).

Proof. By compactness, \(f\) attains a maximum and minimum. If both occur only at the endpoints, the function is constant and any interior point works. Otherwise an interior extremum occurs, and the derivative there is zero by comparing difference quotients from the left and right.

Lagrange mean value theorem

The average slope is realized somewhere inside the interval.

Theorem. If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and differentiable on \((a,b)\), then there is \(c\in(a,b)\) such that \(f(b)-f(a)=f'(c)(b-a)\).

Proof. Apply Rolle's theorem to \(g(x)=f(x)-\ell(x)\), where \(\ell\) is the secant line joining \((a,f(a))\) and \((b,f(b))\). Then \(g(a)=g(b)=0\), so \(g'(c)=0\), which gives the formula.

Cauchy mean value theorem

Cauchy's form compares two functions and underlies l'Hopital-type arguments.

Theorem. If \(f,g\) are continuous on \([a,b]\), differentiable on \((a,b)\), and \(g'\) does not vanish, then for some \(c\in(a,b)\), \[ \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}=\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}. \]

Proof. Since \(g'\) never vanishes, Rolle's theorem implies \(g(b)\ne g(a)\). Apply Rolle's theorem to \(h(x)=(f(b)-f(a))(g(x)-g(a))-(g(b)-g(a))(f(x)-f(a))\). Then \(h(a)=h(b)=0\), and \(h'(c)=0\) gives the identity.

Monotonicity and derivative sign

Derivative sign controls global order.

Proposition. If \(f'\ge0\) on an interval, then \(f\) is increasing. If \(f'=0\) on an interval, then \(f\) is constant.

Proof. For \(x\lt y\), the mean value theorem gives \(f(y)-f(x)=f'(c)(y-x)\) for some \(c\in(x,y)\). The sign of \(f'\) gives the conclusion.

Derivative bounds

Uniform derivative bounds give Lipschitz estimates.

Corollary. If \(|f'|\le M\) on an interval, then \(|f(x)-f(y)|\le M|x-y|\) for all \(x,y\) in the interval.

Proof. Apply the mean value theorem on the interval between \(x\) and \(y\): \(|f(x)-f(y)|=|f'(c)||x-y|\le M|x-y|\).
