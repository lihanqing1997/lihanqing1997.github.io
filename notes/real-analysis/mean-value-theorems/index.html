16. Mean value theorems and consequences

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/differentiability-and-taylor-expansion/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/convex-functions-and-monotone-functions/" %}

Mean value theorems turn local derivative information into global finite-increment information. They are the workhorse behind monotonicity, inverse behavior, and error bounds.

Rolle theorem

Theorem. If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\), differentiable on \((a,b)\), and \(f(a)=f(b)\), then some \(c\in(a,b)\) satisfies \(f^\prime(c)=0\).

Lagrange mean value theorem

Theorem. Under the same regularity without equal endpoints, some \(c\in(a,b)\) satisfies \(f(b)-f(a)=f^\prime(c)(b-a)\). This compares average and instantaneous slopes.

Cauchy mean value theorem

Theorem. For differentiable \(f\) and \(g\), there is \(c\) with \((f(b)-f(a))g^\prime(c)=(g(b)-g(a))f^\prime(c)\). Ratio estimates and l'Hospital-type rules come from this form.

Monotonicity and Lipschitz bounds

Corollary. If \(f^\prime\ge0\) on an interval, then \(f\) is increasing. If \(|f^\prime|\le L\), then \(|f(x)-f(y)|\le L|x-y|\).

Use in asymptotic expansions

Remark. Many remainder estimates are mean-value estimates in disguise: one expands around a target point and bounds a derivative on the segment between the estimator and the target.
