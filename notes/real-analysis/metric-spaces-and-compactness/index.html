8. Metric spaces, compactness, and total boundedness

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/series-and-absolute-convergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/complete-metric-spaces-and-baire-category/" %}

Metric spaces isolate distance-based analysis. Compactness is the mechanism that turns local estimates into finite global estimates.

Metric topology

Definition. A metric \(d\) defines balls \(B(x,r)\), open sets, closed sets, and convergence. A map between metric spaces is continuous exactly when it preserves convergent sequences.

Compactness and sequential compactness

Theorem. In metric spaces, open-cover compactness is equivalent to sequential compactness. Thus every sequence in a compact metric space has a subsequence converging to a point of the space.

Total boundedness

Definition. A metric space is totally bounded if every \(\varepsilon>0\) admits a finite \(\varepsilon\)-net. Compact metric spaces are complete and totally bounded, and complete totally bounded spaces are compact.

Heine-Borel theorem

Theorem. A subset of \(\mathbb R^d\) is compact if and only if it is closed and bounded. This finite-dimensional theorem fails in infinite-dimensional normed spaces, where closed bounded sets usually need not be compact.

Covering numbers

Remark. Total boundedness is the deterministic ancestor of entropy estimates. Covering numbers later measure the size of function classes in empirical process theory.
