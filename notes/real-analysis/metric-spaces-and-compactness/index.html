4. Metric spaces and compactness

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/series-and-absolute-convergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/continuity-and-uniform-continuity/" %}

Metric spaces isolate the part of real analysis that depends only on distance. Compactness is the finiteness principle that makes many local or sequential arguments global.

Metric topology

Definition. A metric on \(X\) is a function \(d:X\times X\to[0,\infty)\) satisfying positivity, symmetry, and the triangle inequality. Open balls \(B(x,r)=\{y:d(x,y)

Completeness and total boundedness

Definition. A metric space is complete if every Cauchy sequence converges in the space. It is totally bounded if for every \(\varepsilon>0\), finitely many \(\varepsilon\)-balls cover the space. Boundedness alone is weaker than total boundedness outside finite-dimensional Euclidean spaces.

Compactness criteria in metric spaces

Theorem. For a metric space, compactness, sequential compactness, and completeness plus total boundedness are equivalent. In \(\mathbb R^d\), this specializes to the Heine-Borel theorem: a set is compact if and only if it is closed and bounded.

Proof mechanism

Proof. Total boundedness lets one choose a finite cover by balls of radius \(1\), then one ball containing infinitely many terms of a sequence, then a finite cover of that ball by balls of radius \(1/2\), and so on. A diagonal selection gives a Cauchy subsequence. Completeness supplies its limit; closedness keeps limits inside subsets.

Continuous images and extreme values

Corollary. If \(K\) is compact and \(f:K\to Y\) is continuous, then \(f(K)\) is compact. If \(f:K\to\mathbb R\), then \(f\) attains its maximum and minimum. This is the compactness engine behind many existence theorems.

Noncompact examples

Example. The interval \((0,1)\) is bounded but not compact because \(1/n\) has no limit in the set. The real line is complete but not totally bounded. An infinite-dimensional unit ball in many normed spaces is bounded but not compact, which is one reason functional analysis is subtler than Euclidean analysis.
