Metric spaces and compactness records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the real analysis note.

Definition

Definition. A metric space is a set with a distance satisfying positivity, symmetry, and the triangle inequality. Compactness can be expressed through open covers or, in metric spaces, through sequences.

Metric compactness theorem

Theorem. For metric spaces, compactness is equivalent to sequential compactness and to completeness plus total boundedness.

Proof

Proof. Total boundedness creates finite nets at every scale; a diagonal choice gives Cauchy subsequences. Conversely, failure of total boundedness or completeness constructs a sequence with no convergent subsequence.

Remark

Remark. Metric compactness is where analysis and topology meet. It converts infinite problems into finite approximations plus limiting arguments.
