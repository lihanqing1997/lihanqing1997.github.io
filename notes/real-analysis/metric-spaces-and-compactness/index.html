8. Metric spaces, compactness, and total boundedness

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/series-and-absolute-convergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/complete-metric-spaces-and-baire-category/" %}

Metric spaces isolate the part of real analysis depending only on distance. Compactness is the finiteness principle that makes local control uniform and turns sequences into convergent subsequences.

Metric topology

Open sets, closed sets, and convergence are all described by balls.

Definition. A metric on \(X\) is a function \(d:X\times X\to[0,\infty)\) that separates points, is symmetric, and satisfies the triangle inequality. The open ball \(B(x,r)\) is \(\{y:d(x,y)\lt r\}\).

Proposition. A set \(F\subset X\) is closed if and only if every convergent sequence in \(F\) has its limit in \(F\).

Proof. If \(F\) is closed and \(x_n\in F\), \(x_n\to x\notin F\), then the open complement contains a ball around \(x\), eventually excluding \(x_n\), contradiction. Conversely, if \(F\) is not closed, some \(x\in\overline F\setminus F\). Choose \(x_n\in F\cap B(x,1/n)\); then \(x_n\to x\notin F\).

Sequential compactness

In metric spaces, compactness can be tested by sequences.

Definition. A metric space is sequentially compact if every sequence has a convergent subsequence whose limit lies in the space.

Theorem. In metric spaces, compactness by open covers is equivalent to sequential compactness.

Proof. Compactness implies sequential compactness by covering the space with finitely many balls of radius \(1\), choosing one containing infinitely many terms, then repeating at radii \(1/2,1/3,\ldots\) and diagonalizing. Conversely, if an open cover has no finite subcover, one constructs a sequence escaping every finite initial subcover; sequential compactness gives a limit point, and an open set in the cover containing that limit eventually contains the sequence, contradiction.

Total boundedness and completeness

Compactness is exactly completeness plus finite approximability at every scale.

Definition. A metric space is totally bounded if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) it can be covered by finitely many balls of radius \(\varepsilon\).

Theorem. A metric space is compact if and only if it is complete and totally bounded.

Proof. Compact spaces are totally bounded by applying a finite subcover to all \(\varepsilon\)-balls, and complete because Cauchy sequences have convergent subsequences whose limit then attracts the whole sequence. Conversely, total boundedness lets one extract a Cauchy subsequence by repeatedly choosing balls of radius \(2^{-k}\) containing infinitely many terms; completeness gives a limit, proving sequential compactness.

Continuous images

Compactness survives continuous maps and produces extrema.

Proposition. If \(f:X\to Y\) is continuous and \(K\subset X\) is compact, then \(f(K)\) is compact. If \(Y=\mathbb R\), then \(f\) attains its maximum and minimum on \(K\).

Proof. An open cover of \(f(K)\) pulls back to an open cover of \(K\), which has a finite subcover; pushing forward gives a finite subcover of \(f(K)\). A compact subset of \(\mathbb R\) is closed and bounded, so its supremum and infimum belong to it.

Heine-Borel in Euclidean space

Euclidean compactness has a simple closed-and-bounded form.

Theorem. A subset of \(\mathbb R^d\) is compact if and only if it is closed and bounded.

Proof. Closed subsets of compact spaces are compact, and boxes \([-M,M]^d\) are compact by repeated bisection or by product compactness. Thus closed bounded sets are compact. Conversely, compact sets are bounded by finitely many unit balls and closed because metric compact sets contain limits of their convergent sequences.
