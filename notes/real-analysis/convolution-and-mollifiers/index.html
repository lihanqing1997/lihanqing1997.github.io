Convolution averages a function against translated kernels. Mollifiers use this operation to turn rough functions into smooth approximations.

Convolution definition

Convolution combines translation and integration.

Definition. For suitable functions \(f,g\) on \(\mathbb R^d\), define \[ (f*g)(x)=\int_{\mathbb R^d} f(x-y)g(y)\,\mathrm{d}y. \]

Proposition. If \(f\in L^p\) and \(g\in L^1\), then \(f*g\) is defined almost everywhere and \(\|f*g\|_p\le\|f\|_p\|g\|_1\).

Proof. Minkowski's integral inequality gives \(\|f*g\|_p\le\int |g(y)|\|f(\cdot-y)\|_p\,\mathrm{d}y=\|f\|_p\|g\|_1\). The \(p=\infty\) case is direct from the essential supremum.

Smoothness by convolution

Smooth kernels transfer smoothness to the convolution.

Proposition. If \(g\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R^d)\) and \(f\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}\), then \(f*g\in C^\infty\), and \(\partial^\alpha(f*g)=f*(\partial^\alpha g)\).

Proof. Difference quotients may be passed under the integral because the support of \(g\) is compact and derivatives of \(g\) are bounded. Dominated convergence justifies differentiating under the integral, and iteration gives all multi-index derivatives.

Approximate identities

A mollifier concentrates mass near the origin while preserving total mass one.

Definition. A standard mollifier is \(\rho_\varepsilon(x)=\varepsilon^{-d}\rho(x/\varepsilon)\), where \(\rho\in C_c^\infty\), \(\rho\ge0\), and \(\int\rho=1\).

Theorem. If \(f\in L^p(\mathbb R^d)\), \(1\le p\lt \infty\), then \(\rho_\varepsilon*f\to f\) in \(L^p\). If \(f\) is bounded and uniformly continuous, convergence is uniform.

Proof. Write \(\rho_\varepsilon*f-f=\int\rho_\varepsilon(y)(f(\cdot-y)-f)\,\mathrm{d}y\). For \(L^p\), use Minkowski and continuity of translations in \(L^p\). For uniform convergence, use the modulus of continuity and the fact that \(\rho_\varepsilon\) is supported near zero.

Support and boundary effects

Convolution expands support by the support of the kernel.

Proposition. \(\operatorname{supp}(f*g)\subset \operatorname{supp}f+\operatorname{supp}g\) whenever the convolution is defined and the supports are closed in the usual sense.

Proof. If \(x\) is outside the Minkowski sum of the supports, then for every \(y\), either \(y\notin\operatorname{supp}g\) or \(x-y\notin\operatorname{supp}f\). Thus the integrand vanishes in a neighborhood sense almost everywhere, so \((f*g)(x)=0\).

Mollifiers as local averages

Mollification is a controlled local blur.

Remark. Mollifiers preserve constants, commute with derivatives in the weak sense, and provide the standard path from \(L^p\) functions to smooth test functions.
