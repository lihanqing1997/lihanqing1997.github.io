29. Lebesgue integral

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/lebesgue-measurable-functions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/convergence-theorems/" %}

The Lebesgue integral is built from simple functions and monotone limits. Its power is that it interacts cleanly with almost-everywhere convergence.

Simple functions

Definition. For \(s=\sum_{k=1}^m a_k\mathbf 1_{A_k}\) with disjoint measurable sets and \(a_k\ge0\), define \(\int s\,\mathrm{d}\mu=\sum a_k\mu(A_k)\). Refinement of partitions shows this is well-defined.

Nonnegative functions

Definition. For \(f\ge0\), define \(\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu=\sup{\int s\,\mathrm{d}\mu:0\le s\le f, s \text{ simple}}\). The value may be infinite.

Integrable functions

Definition. A real or complex measurable function is integrable if \(\int |f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu<\infty\). For real functions, \(f=f^+-f^-\) and the integral is the difference of the positive and negative parts.

Linearity and order

Proposition. The integral is linear on \(L^1\), monotone on nonnegative functions, and satisfies \(|\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu|\le \int |f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Layer-cake formula

Theorem. For \(f\ge0\), \(\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu=\int_0^\infty \mu(f>t)\,\mathrm{d}t\). This turns integrals into tail-area calculations and is especially useful for moment bounds.
