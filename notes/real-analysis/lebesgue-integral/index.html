29. Lebesgue integral

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/lebesgue-measurable-functions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/convergence-theorems/" %}

The Lebesgue integral is built from simple functions and monotone limits. Its strength is that limiting theorems are built into the construction rather than added afterward.

Simple functions

Simple functions are finite measurable step functions.

Definition. For \(s=\sum_{k=1}^m a_k\mathbf 1_{A_k}\) with \(a_k\ge0\) and \(A_k\) disjoint measurable sets, define \(\int s\,\mathrm{d}\mu=\sum_{k=1}^m a_k\mu(A_k)\).

Proposition. The integral of a nonnegative simple function is independent of the chosen disjoint representation.

Proof. Given two representations, refine them by all intersections \(A_i\cap B_j\). On this common partition the function has the same value cell by cell, and finite additivity of \(\mu\) makes both sums equal to the refined sum.

Nonnegative measurable functions

General nonnegative integrals are suprema of simple lower approximations.

Definition. For measurable \(f\ge0\), define \[ \int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu=\sup\left\{\int s\,\mathrm{d}\mu:0\le s\le f,\ s\text{ simple}\right\}. \]

Theorem. If \(0\le f_n\uparrow f\), then \(\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\uparrow\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proof. Monotonicity gives the limit \(L\le\int f\). For a simple \(s\le f\) and \(0\lt c\lt 1\), the sets \(A_n=\{f_n\ge cs\}\) increase to \(X\) on \(\{s>0\}\). Thus \(\int f_n\ge c\int_{A_n}s\to c\int s\). Taking \(n\to\infty\), then \(c\uparrow1\), and then the supremum over \(s\), gives \(L\ge\int f\).

Integrable functions

Signed and complex functions are integrable when their absolute value is integrable.

Definition. A measurable real or complex function \(f\) is integrable if \(\int |f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\lt \infty\). For real \(f\), write \(f=f^+-f^-\), and define \(\int f=\int f^+-\int f^-\).

Proposition. The integral is linear on \(L^1\), monotone on real functions, and satisfies \(|\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu|\le\int|f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proof. Linearity for nonnegative functions follows first from simple functions and monotone convergence, then extends by positive and negative parts. Monotonicity is immediate from the nonnegative construction. The absolute-value inequality follows from \(-|f|\le f\le |f|\) in the real case and by rotating a complex integral to make it nonnegative real.

Layer-cake representation

Nonnegative integrals can be computed from tail measures.

Theorem. If \(f\ge0\), then \[ \int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu=\int_0^\infty \mu(f>t)\,\mathrm{d}t. \]

Proof. For each \(x\), \(f(x)=\int_0^\infty \mathbf 1_{\{t\lt f(x)\}}\,\mathrm{d}t\). Tonelli's theorem applies to the nonnegative function \(\mathbf 1_{\{t\lt f(x)\}}\) on \(X\times[0,\infty)\), allowing the order of integration to be switched.

Absolute continuity of the integral

Integrable functions have uniformly small integral over small-measure sets.

Proposition. If \(f\in L^1(\mu)\), then for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(\delta>0\) such that \(\mu(A)\lt \delta\Rightarrow\int_A |f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\lt \varepsilon\).

Proof. Choose \(M\) so large that \(\int_{\{|f|>M\}}|f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\lt \varepsilon/2\). Then \(\int_A|f|\le M\mu(A)+\varepsilon/2\). Take \(\delta=\varepsilon/(2M)\), with the trivial modification if \(M=0\).
