18. Radon-Nikodym derivatives

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/signed-measures-and-decompositions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/lp-spaces/" %}

The Radon-Nikodym theorem explains when one measure has a density with respect to another. It is the invariant form of density, likelihood ratio, and change of measure.

Absolute continuity of measures

Definition. A measure \(\nu\) is absolutely continuous with respect to \(\mu\), written \(\nu\ll\mu\), if \(\mu(A)=0\) implies \(\nu(A)=0\). This says \(\nu\) puts no mass on sets invisible to \(\mu\).

Radon-Nikodym theorem

Theorem. If \(\nu\) and \(\mu\) are \(\sigma\)-finite measures and \(\nu\ll\mu\), then there exists a measurable \(f\ge0\) such that \(\nu(A)=\int_A f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\) for every measurable \(A\). The function \(f\), written \(\mathrm{d}\nu/\mathrm{d}\mu\), is unique up to \(\mu\)-null sets.

Uniqueness proof

Proof. If \(f\) and \(g\) both represent \(\nu\), then \(\int_A(f-g)\,\mathrm{d}\mu=0\) for every measurable \(A\). Taking \(A=\{f>g\}\) and \(A=\{g>f\}\) gives \(f=g\) almost everywhere.

Lebesgue decomposition theorem

Theorem. If \(\nu\) and \(\mu\) are \(\sigma\)-finite, then \(\nu=\nu_{\mathrm{ac}}+\nu_s\), where \(\nu_{\mathrm{ac}}\ll\mu\) and \(\nu_s\perp\mu\). The absolutely continuous part has a Radon-Nikodym density, while the singular part lives on a \(\mu\)-null set.

Chain rule

Proposition. If \(\lambda\ll\nu\ll\mu\), then \[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\lambda}{\mathrm{d}\mu}=\frac{\mathrm{d}\lambda}{\mathrm{d}\nu}\frac{\mathrm{d}\nu}{\mathrm{d}\mu} \quad \mu\text{-a.e.} \] If \(\nu\ll\mu\), then \(\int h\,\mathrm{d}\nu=\int h(\mathrm{d}\nu/\mathrm{d}\mu)\,\mathrm{d}\mu\) for nonnegative or integrable \(h\).

Statistical interpretation

Remark. Likelihood ratios are Radon-Nikodym derivatives. If \(P\ll Q\), then \(\mathrm{d}P/\mathrm{d}Q\) is the density of model \(P\) relative to reference law \(Q\). Change-of-measure identities in probability are integral identities from the theorem.
