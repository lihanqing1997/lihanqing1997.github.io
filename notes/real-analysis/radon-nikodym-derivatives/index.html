The Radon-Nikodym theorem is the measure-theoretic derivative theorem. It tells when one measure has a density with respect to another.

Absolute continuity of measures

One measure is absolutely continuous with respect to another if it ignores the same null sets.

Definition. For measures \(\nu,\mu\), write \(\nu\ll\mu\) if \(\mu(A)=0\Rightarrow \nu(A)=0\). A density of \(\nu\) with respect to \(\mu\) is a measurable \(f\ge0\) such that \(\nu(A)=\int_A f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proposition. If \(\nu(A)=\int_A f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\), then \(\nu\ll\mu\), and \(f\) is unique \(\mu\)-almost everywhere.

Proof. If \(\mu(A)=0\), then \(\int_A f\,\mathrm{d}\mu=0\). If \(f,g\) give the same measure, then \(\int_A(f-g)\,\mathrm{d}\mu=0\) for all \(A\). Taking \(A=\{f>g\}\) and \(A=\{g>f\}\) gives \(f=g\) almost everywhere.

Radon-Nikodym theorem

Absolute continuity is also sufficient for the existence of a density under sigma-finiteness.

Theorem. If \(\nu\) and \(\mu\) are sigma-finite measures and \(\nu\ll\mu\), then there exists a measurable \(f\ge0\) such that \(\nu(A)=\int_A f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\) for all \(A\). Write \(f=\mathrm{d}\nu/\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proof. In the finite case, first prove the result for a bounded positive functional \(T(g)=\int g\,\mathrm{d}\nu\) on \(L^2(\mu+\nu)\). Riesz representation gives a function \(h\), and algebraic manipulation yields a density \(f=h/(1-h)\) on the nontrivial part. Sigma-finite measures are handled by applying the finite result on countable finite-measure pieces and pasting the densities.

Chain rule and change of measure

Radon-Nikodym derivatives behave like ordinary derivatives.

Proposition. If \(\lambda\ll\nu\ll\mu\), then \[ \frac{\mathrm{d}\lambda}{\mathrm{d}\mu} =\frac{\mathrm{d}\lambda}{\mathrm{d}\nu}\frac{\mathrm{d}\nu}{\mathrm{d}\mu} \quad \mu\text{-a.e.} \]

Proof. For every measurable \(A\), \[ \lambda(A)=\int_A \frac{\mathrm{d}\lambda}{\mathrm{d}\nu}\,\mathrm{d}\nu =\int_A \frac{\mathrm{d}\lambda}{\mathrm{d}\nu}\frac{\mathrm{d}\nu}{\mathrm{d}\mu}\,\mathrm{d}\mu. \] Uniqueness of Radon-Nikodym derivatives gives the identity.

Lebesgue differentiation viewpoint

Densities are local ratios of masses in Euclidean spaces.

Theorem. If \(\nu\ll m\) on \(\mathbb R^d\) with density \(f\), then for \(m\)-almost every \(x\), \[ f(x)=\lim_{r\downarrow0}\frac{\nu(B(x,r))}{m(B(x,r))}. \]

Proof. Since \(\nu(B(x,r))=\int_{B(x,r)}f\,\mathrm{d}m\), the ratio is the average of \(f\) over the ball. The Lebesgue differentiation theorem says these averages converge to \(f(x)\) almost everywhere.

Statistical densities

Likelihood ratios are Radon-Nikodym derivatives.

Remark. If \(P_\theta\ll P_{\theta_0}\), the likelihood ratio is \(\mathrm{d}P_\theta/\mathrm{d}P_{\theta_0}\). This measure-theoretic notation is what keeps dominated and nondominated statistical models conceptually clean.
