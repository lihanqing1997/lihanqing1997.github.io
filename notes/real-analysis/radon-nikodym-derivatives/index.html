33. Radon-Nikodym derivatives

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/signed-measures-and-decompositions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/lebesgue-decomposition-and-singularity/" %}

The Radon-Nikodym theorem says when one measure has a density with respect to another. Likelihood ratios are precisely such derivatives.

Absolute continuity of measures

Definition. Write \(\nu\ll\mu\) if \(\mu(A)=0\) implies \(\nu(A)=0\). Then \(\nu\) assigns no mass to sets that \(\mu\) regards as null.

Radon-Nikodym theorem

Theorem. If \(\nu\ll\mu\) and the measures are sigma-finite, then there exists measurable \(f\ge0\) such that \(\nu(A)=\int_A f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\). The function is unique \(\mu\)-a.e. and is written \(\mathrm{d}\nu/\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Chain rule

Proposition. If \(\lambda\ll\nu\ll\mu\), then \(\mathrm{d}\lambda/\mathrm{d}\mu=(\mathrm{d}\lambda/\mathrm{d}\nu)(\mathrm{d}\nu/\mathrm{d}\mu)\) a.e. This is the abstract product rule for likelihood ratios.

Change of measure

Corollary. If \(\nu\) has density \(f\) with respect to \(\mu\), then \(\int g\,\mathrm{d}\nu=\int gf\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Conditional expectation

Remark. Conditional expectation is a Radon-Nikodym derivative on a sub-sigma-algebra. This is why it is characterized by integrals over all conditioning events.
