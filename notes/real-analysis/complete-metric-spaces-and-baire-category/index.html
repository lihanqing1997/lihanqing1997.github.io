Completeness is not only about limits of sequences; it also prevents a space from being built out of too many thin pieces. Baire category is one of the deepest consequences of completeness.

Completion of a metric space

Every metric space can be embedded densely into a complete one.

Theorem. Every metric space \(X\) has a completion \(\widehat X\): an isometric embedding \(i:X\to\widehat X\) with dense image, where \(\widehat X\) is complete. It is unique up to unique isometry fixing \(X\).

Proof. Let \(\widehat X\) be equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences in \(X\), identifying two sequences when their mutual distance tends to zero. Define the distance by \(\widehat d([(x_n)],[(y_n)])=\lim_n d(x_n,y_n)\), which exists because the real sequence is Cauchy. Constant sequences embed \(X\). Completeness follows by a diagonal choice of representatives for Cauchy sequences of classes; uniqueness follows by extending the identity from the dense copy of \(X\).

Contraction principle

Complete spaces make iterative fixed-point schemes converge.

Theorem. If \(X\) is complete and \(T:X\to X\) satisfies \(d(Tx,Ty)\le c d(x,y)\) for some \(0\lt c\lt 1\), then \(T\) has a unique fixed point and \(T^nx_0\) converges to it for every \(x_0\in X\).

Proof. The iterates satisfy \(d(x_{n+1},x_n)\le c^n d(x_1,x_0)\), so the tail distance is bounded by a geometric series and is Cauchy. Let \(x_n\to x\). Continuity of \(T\) gives \(Tx=\lim Tx_n=\lim x_{n+1}=x\). If \(x,y\) are fixed, \(d(x,y)=d(Tx,Ty)\le c d(x,y)\), so \(x=y\).

Nowhere dense and meagre sets

Category measures topological smallness rather than measure-theoretic size.

Definition. A set \(A\) is nowhere dense if \(\operatorname{int}\overline A=\varnothing\). A set is meagre if it is a countable union of nowhere dense sets. A residual set contains the complement of a meagre set.

Remark. A dense set can be meagre, as \(\mathbb Q\) is in \(\mathbb R\). Category and measure are different notions of largeness.

Baire category theorem

A complete metric space cannot be exhausted by countably many closed sets with empty interior.

Theorem. In a complete metric space, the intersection of countably many dense open sets is dense. Equivalently, a nonempty complete metric space is not meagre in itself.

Proof. Let \(G_n\) be dense open sets and let \(U\) be a nonempty open set. Choose a closed ball \(\overline B_1\subset U\cap G_1\) of radius at most \(1\). Inductively choose \(\overline B_{n+1}\subset B_n\cap G_{n+1}\) with radius at most \(2^{-n}\). The centers form a Cauchy sequence, hence converge to some \(x\). The nested closed balls force \(x\in U\cap\bigcap_nG_n\).

Functional-analytic consequences

Many theorems in functional analysis are category arguments in disguise.

Example. The uniform boundedness principle, open mapping theorem, and closed graph theorem all rely on Baire category. Completeness is therefore not a technical hypothesis; it is the engine that turns pointwise information into uniform information.
