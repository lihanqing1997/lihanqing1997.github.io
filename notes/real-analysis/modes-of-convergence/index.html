38. Modes of convergence for functions

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/differentiation-of-measures-and-density-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/uniform-integrability-and-vitali-theorem/" %}

Different convergence modes preserve different operations. Mastering their implication diagram prevents illegal limit interchanges.

Almost-everywhere convergence

Definition. Almost-everywhere convergence means \(f_n(x)\to f(x)\) outside a null set. It is pointwise convergence after ignoring a set invisible to the measure.

Almost uniform convergence

Definition. Almost uniform convergence means that outside a set of arbitrarily small measure, the convergence is uniform. It is stronger than convergence in measure and close to a.e. convergence on finite spaces.

Egorov theorem

Theorem. On a finite measure space, almost-everywhere convergence implies almost uniform convergence. Finiteness is essential because one must remove a set of small total measure.

Convergence in measure and Lp

Proposition. \(L^p\) convergence implies convergence in measure under standard finite-measure or localization assumptions. Convergence in measure has almost-everywhere convergent subsequences.

Implication failures

Counterexample. Convergence in measure need not imply almost-everywhere convergence of the full sequence. Almost-everywhere convergence need not imply \(L^1\) convergence without domination or uniform integrability.
