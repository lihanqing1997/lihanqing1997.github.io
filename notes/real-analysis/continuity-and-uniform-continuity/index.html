11. Continuity, uniform continuity, and compact domains

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/connectedness-and-components/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/semicontinuity-and-extreme-values/" %}

Continuity is local preservation of limits. Uniform continuity strengthens the quantifiers so one modulus works over the whole domain.

Pointwise continuity

Definition. A map \(f:X\to Y\) is continuous at \(x\) if every output tolerance is guaranteed by a sufficiently small input tolerance around \(x\). In metric spaces, this is equivalent to \(x_n\to x\) implying \(f(x_n)\to f(x)\).

Uniform continuity

Definition. Uniform continuity requires one \(\delta\) to work for all points in the domain. It preserves Cauchy sequences, which is why uniformly continuous maps extend to completions.

Heine-Cantor theorem

Theorem. Every continuous map from a compact metric space to a metric space is uniformly continuous. Compactness converts pointwise neighborhoods into a finite subcover.

Extreme values

Theorem. A continuous real-valued function on a compact set attains its maximum and minimum. This is the basic existence theorem behind many finite-dimensional optimization and estimation arguments.

Moduli of continuity

Definition. The modulus \(\omega_f(\delta)=\sup_{d(x,y)\le\delta}d(f(x),f(y))\) records uniform oscillation. Lipschitz and Hölder functions have explicit moduli, which are essential for quantitative bounds.
