Continuity is local preservation of limits. Uniform continuity is a global version that makes one choice of scale work everywhere on the domain.

Pointwise continuity and sequences

In metric spaces, continuity can be read from convergent sequences.

Definition. A map \(f:X\to Y\) between metric spaces is continuous at \(x\) if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(\delta>0\) such that \(d_X(x,z)\lt \delta\Rightarrow d_Y(f(x),f(z))\lt \varepsilon\).

Theorem. \(f\) is continuous at \(x\) if and only if \(x_n\to x\) implies \(f(x_n)\to f(x)\).

Proof. The epsilon-delta definition directly gives the sequential implication. Conversely, if continuity fails, there is \(\varepsilon>0\) such that for every \(n\) one can choose \(x_n\) with \(d(x_n,x)\lt 1/n\) but \(d(f(x_n),f(x))\ge\varepsilon\), contradicting sequential continuity.

Uniform continuity

Uniform continuity removes dependence of \(\delta\) on the base point.

Definition. \(f:X\to Y\) is uniformly continuous if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(\delta>0\) such that \(d_X(x,z)\lt \delta\Rightarrow d_Y(f(x),f(z))\lt \varepsilon\) for all \(x,z\in X\).

Theorem. Every continuous map from a compact metric space to a metric space is uniformly continuous.

Proof. For each \(x\), choose \(r_x>0\) such that \(d(x,z)\lt 2r_x\Rightarrow d(f(x),f(z))\lt \varepsilon/2\). Finitely many balls \(B(x_i,r_{x_i})\) cover compact \(X\). Let \(\delta=\min_i r_{x_i}\). If \(d(y,z)\lt \delta\) and \(y\in B(x_i,r_{x_i})\), then both \(y\) and \(z\) lie within \(2r_{x_i}\) of \(x_i\), so \(d(f(y),f(z))\lt \varepsilon\).

Continuous extension from dense sets

Uniform continuity is exactly what allows extension from approximating domains.

Theorem. If \(A\) is dense in a metric space \(X\), \(Y\) is complete, and \(f:A\to Y\) is uniformly continuous, then \(f\) extends uniquely to a continuous map \(\overline f:X\to Y\).

Proof. For \(x\in X\), choose \(a_n\in A\) with \(a_n\to x\). Uniform continuity makes \(f(a_n)\) Cauchy in \(Y\), so define \(\overline f(x)=\lim f(a_n)\). The value is independent of the approximating sequence by applying uniform continuity to interlaced sequences. Density gives uniqueness, and the same epsilon-delta estimate gives continuity.

Moduli of continuity

Quantitative continuity is often more useful than qualitative continuity.

Definition. A modulus of continuity for \(f\) is a function \(\omega:[0,\infty)\to[0,\infty)\) with \(\omega(t)\downarrow0\) as \(t\downarrow0\) and \(d(f(x),f(y))\le\omega(d(x,y))\).

Example. Lipschitz maps have \(\omega(t)=Lt\), and Hölder maps have \(\omega(t)=Ct^\alpha\). These estimates are central in approximation, empirical process bounds, and regularity theory.

Uniform limits

Uniform convergence preserves continuity because the error can be controlled before taking the point limit.

Theorem. If \(f_n:X\to Y\) are continuous, \(Y\) is metric, and \(f_n\to f\) uniformly, then \(f\) is continuous.

Proof. Fix \(x\) and \(\varepsilon>0\). Choose \(n\) such that \(\sup_z d(f_n(z),f(z))\lt \varepsilon/3\). By continuity of \(f_n\) at \(x\), choose \(\delta\) such that \(d(x,y)\lt \delta\Rightarrow d(f_n(x),f_n(y))\lt \varepsilon/3\). The triangle inequality gives \(d(f(x),f(y))\lt \varepsilon\).
