20. Bounded variation and absolute continuity

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/riemann-stieltjes-integration/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/fundamental-theorem-of-calculus-and-change-of-variables/" %}

Bounded variation controls total oscillation. Absolute continuity is stronger: it says all variation is accumulated by integrating an ordinary derivative.

Total variation

Variation measures the total amount a function moves.

Definition. The total variation of \(f:[a,b]\to\mathbb R\) is \(V_a^b(f)=\sup_P\sum_i |f(x_i)-f(x_{i-1})|\), where the supremum is over finite partitions.

Proposition. \(f\) has bounded variation if and only if \(f=g-h\) for two increasing functions \(g,h\).

Proof. If \(f\) has bounded variation, set \(g(x)=V_a^x(f)\) and \(h(x)=g(x)-f(x)\). Both are increasing because variation over a larger interval increases and dominates increments of \(f\). Conversely, the variation of \(g-h\) is at most the variation of \(g\) plus the variation of \(h\), finite on \([a,b]\).

Absolute continuity

Absolute continuity is uniform smallness of image variation over collections of short intervals.

Definition. \(f\) is absolutely continuous on \([a,b]\) if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(\delta>0\) such that disjoint intervals \((a_k,b_k)\) with \(\sum_k(b_k-a_k)\lt \delta\) satisfy \(\sum_k|f(b_k)-f(a_k)|\lt \varepsilon\).

Proposition. Absolute continuity implies uniform continuity and bounded variation.

Proof. Uniform continuity follows by applying the definition to one interval. For bounded variation, choose \(\delta\) for \(\varepsilon=1\), split any partition into groups whose total interval length is below \(\delta\), and sum the corresponding variation bounds over finitely many groups controlled by \(b-a\).

Fundamental characterization

Absolutely continuous functions are exactly indefinite integrals of \(L^1\) functions.

Theorem. \(f\) is absolutely continuous on \([a,b]\) if and only if there is \(g\in L^1([a,b])\) such that \(f(x)=f(a)+\int_a^x g(t)\,\mathrm{d}t\). In that case \(f'=g\) almost everywhere.

Proof. If \(f(x)=f(a)+\int_a^x g\), absolute continuity follows from absolute continuity of the Lebesgue integral. Conversely, an absolutely continuous function has bounded variation, hence is differentiable almost everywhere, and the signed measure induced by its increments is absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure. The Radon-Nikodym derivative is \(f'\), giving the integral representation.

Singular bounded variation

Bounded variation allows movement on sets of Lebesgue measure zero.

Example. The Cantor function is continuous, increasing, and of bounded variation, with derivative \(0\) almost everywhere, but it is not constant. Hence it is not absolutely continuous.

Variation measures

Bounded-variation functions are distribution functions of signed measures.

Remark. The decomposition of a bounded-variation function into absolutely continuous, jump, and singular continuous parts is the one-dimensional version of Lebesgue decomposition for measures.
