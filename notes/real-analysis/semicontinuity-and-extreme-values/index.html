12. Semicontinuity and variational arguments

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/continuity-and-uniform-continuity/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/sequences-of-functions/" %}

Semicontinuity is weaker than continuity but often exactly what minimization requires. It is the natural regularity condition for existence of minimizers.

Lower semicontinuity

Definition. A function \(f:X\to(-\infty,\infty]\) is lower semicontinuous if its sublevel sets \(\{x:f(x)\le a\}\) are closed. Sequentially, \(f(x)\le \liminf f(x_n)\) whenever \(x_n\to x\).

Upper semicontinuity

Definition. Upper semicontinuity is lower semicontinuity of \(-f\). It is suited to maximization, while lower semicontinuity is suited to minimization.

Compact minimization

Theorem. If \(K\) is compact and \(f:K\to(-\infty,\infty]\) is lower semicontinuous and not identically infinite, then \(f\) attains its minimum. A minimizing sequence has a convergent subsequence, and lower semicontinuity passes to the limit.

Epigraph criterion

Proposition. A function is lower semicontinuous if and only if its epigraph \({(x,t):f(x)\le t}\) is closed. This geometric viewpoint is central in convex analysis.

Estimator existence

Remark. Many estimators minimize contrasts. Compactness or coercivity gives limiting subsequences, and lower semicontinuity ensures the limit still minimizes the objective.
