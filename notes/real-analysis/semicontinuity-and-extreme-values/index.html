12. Semicontinuity and variational arguments

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/continuity-and-uniform-continuity/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/sequences-of-functions/" %}

Semicontinuity is the right continuity notion for optimization and variational arguments. It is weak enough to survive limits, but strong enough to guarantee minimizers on compact sets.

Lower and upper semicontinuity

Lower semicontinuity prevents sudden downward jumps; upper semicontinuity prevents sudden upward jumps.

Definition. A function \(f:X\to[-\infty,\infty]\) is lower semicontinuous if \(f(x)\le\liminf f(x_n)\) whenever \(x_n\to x\). It is upper semicontinuous if \(-f\) is lower semicontinuous.

Proposition. \(f\) is lower semicontinuous if and only if every sublevel set \(\{x:f(x)\le a\}\) is closed.

Proof. If \(x_n\in\{f\le a\}\) and \(x_n\to x\), lower semicontinuity gives \(f(x)\le\liminf f(x_n)\le a\), so the sublevel set is closed. Conversely, if all sublevel sets are closed and \(x_n\to x\), then for any \(a>\liminf f(x_n)\), infinitely many \(x_n\) lie in \(\{f\le a\}\), forcing \(x\in\{f\le a\}\). Let \(a\downarrow\liminf f(x_n)\).

Extreme value principle

Compactness turns semicontinuity into existence of optimizers.

Theorem. If \(K\) is compact and \(f:K\to(-\infty,\infty]\) is lower semicontinuous, then \(f\) attains its minimum provided it is not identically \(+\infty\).

Proof. Let \(m=\inf_K f\), and choose \(x_n\in K\) with \(f(x_n)\downarrow m\). Compactness gives a subsequence \(x_{n_k}\to x\in K\). Lower semicontinuity gives \(f(x)\le\liminf f(x_{n_k})=m\), hence \(f(x)=m\).

Dini-type monotone convergence

Monotonicity plus compactness can upgrade pointwise convergence to uniform convergence.

Theorem. If \(K\) is compact, \(f_n\) are continuous, \(f_n\downarrow0\) pointwise, then \(f_n\to0\) uniformly.

Proof. For \(\varepsilon>0\), the sets \(U_n=\{x:f_n(x)\lt \varepsilon\}\) are open and increasing, and pointwise convergence gives \(\bigcup_n U_n=K\). Compactness gives \(K=U_N\) for some \(N\), and monotonicity gives \(f_n\lt \varepsilon\) on \(K\) for all \(n\ge N\).

Direct method in compact form

The direct method is the template behind many existence proofs.

Proposition. If \(K\) is compact and \(f\) is lower semicontinuous, every minimizing sequence has a subsequence converging to a minimizer.

Proof. Any minimizing sequence lies in compact \(K\), so it has a convergent subsequence. Lower semicontinuity at the subsequential limit gives a value no larger than the limiting infimum, hence equal to the minimum.

Coercive replacement for compactness

On noncompact spaces, compact sublevel sets often replace compact domains.

Remark. If all sublevel sets \(\{f\le a\}\) are compact and \(f\) is lower semicontinuous, then the same minimizing-sequence proof works. This is the common form used for convex analysis, variational problems, and estimators defined by objective minimization.
