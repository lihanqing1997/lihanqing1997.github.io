11. Sigma-algebras and measures

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/power-series-on-the-real-line/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/outer-measures-and-lebesgue-measure/" %}

Measure theory begins by deciding which sets are measurable and how size behaves under countable operations. Countability is the key algebraic constraint that makes limiting arguments possible.

Sigma-algebras

Definition. A \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal A\) on \(X\) is a collection of subsets containing \(X\), closed under complements, and closed under countable unions. It is also closed under countable intersections and set differences. The pair \((X,\mathcal A)\) is a measurable space.

Generated sigma-algebras and Borel sets

Definition. Given a collection \(\mathcal C\) of subsets, \(\sigma(\mathcal C)\) is the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra containing \(\mathcal C\). On a metric space, the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal B(X)\) is generated by open sets. This makes topological information measurable.

Measures and countable additivity

Definition. A measure \(\mu:\mathcal A\to[0,\infty]\) satisfies \(\mu(\varnothing)=0\) and countable additivity on disjoint measurable sets: \(\mu(\bigcup_n A_n)=\sum_n\mu(A_n)\). A probability measure is a measure with total mass \(1\).

Continuity of measure

Proposition. If \(A_n\uparrow A\), then \(\mu(A_n)\uparrow\mu(A)\). If \(A_n\downarrow A\) and \(\mu(A_1)<\infty\), then \(\mu(A_n)\downarrow\mu(A)\). The finite initial mass condition in the decreasing case is essential.

Proof by disjointization

Proof. For increasing sets, write \(A= A_1\cup\bigcup_{n\ge2}(A_n\setminus A_{n-1})\) as a disjoint union and use countable additivity. For decreasing sets, apply the increasing result to \(A_1\setminus A_n\), which increases to \(A_1\setminus A\), and subtract from \(\mu(A_1)\).

Why countable additivity matters

Remark. Finite additivity alone does not control limits. Countable additivity is exactly what lets one pass from finite approximations to limiting sets, and later from simple functions to measurable functions and integrals.
