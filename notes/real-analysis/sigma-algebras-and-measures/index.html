25. Sigma-algebras and measures

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/inverse-and-implicit-function-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/outer-measures-and-lebesgue-measure/" %}

Measure theory begins by deciding which sets are observable and how size is assigned to countable unions. Sigma-algebras and measures encode those two decisions.

Sigma-algebras

A sigma-algebra is the domain on which countable set operations are legitimate.

Definition. A sigma-algebra \(\mathcal A\) on \(X\) is a collection of subsets containing \(X\), closed under complements, and closed under countable unions. The pair \((X,\mathcal A)\) is a measurable space.

Proposition. Arbitrary intersections of sigma-algebras are sigma-algebras. Therefore every collection \(\mathcal C\) generates a smallest sigma-algebra \(\sigma(\mathcal C)\).

Proof. Membership in an intersection means membership in every sigma-algebra in the family. The closure properties hold in each member, hence hold in the intersection. Taking the intersection of all sigma-algebras containing \(\mathcal C\) gives \(\sigma(\mathcal C)\).

Measures and continuity

Countable additivity is often used through monotone continuity.

Definition. A measure is a map \(\mu:\mathcal A\to[0,\infty]\) with \(\mu(\varnothing)=0\) and \(\mu(\bigcup_n A_n)=\sum_n\mu(A_n)\) for pairwise disjoint measurable sets \(A_n\).

Theorem. If \(A_n\uparrow A\), then \(\mu(A_n)\uparrow\mu(A)\). If \(A_n\downarrow A\) and \(\mu(A_1)\lt \infty\), then \(\mu(A_n)\downarrow\mu(A)\).

Proof. For increasing sets, decompose \(A\) into disjoint increments \(A_1\) and \(A_n\setminus A_{n-1}\). Countable additivity gives the limit. For decreasing sets, apply the increasing result to \(A_1\setminus A_n\uparrow A_1\setminus A\), using finiteness of \(\mu(A_1)\) to subtract.

Pi-lambda uniqueness

Measures are often determined by their values on a smaller generating class.

Theorem. If two finite measures agree on a pi-system \(\mathcal P\), then they agree on \(\sigma(\mathcal P)\).

Proof. Let \(\mathcal D=\{A:\mu(A)=\nu(A)\}\). Finiteness makes \(\mathcal D\) a lambda-system: it contains \(X\), is closed under complements, and under disjoint countable unions. Since \(\mathcal P\subset\mathcal D\), Dynkin's pi-lambda theorem gives \(\sigma(\mathcal P)\subset\mathcal D\).

Completion

Completeness adds all subsets of null sets.

Proposition. Every measure space has a completion \((X,\overline{\mathcal A},\overline\mu)\), where \(\overline{\mathcal A}\) contains all subsets of \(\mu\)-null measurable sets.

Proof. Let \(\overline{\mathcal A}\) consist of sets \(A\cup N\), with \(A\in\mathcal A\) and \(N\subset Z\) for some \(Z\in\mathcal A\) with \(\mu(Z)=0\). Define \(\overline\mu(A\cup N)=\mu(A)\). Symmetric differences of two representations lie in null sets, so the value is well-defined, and countable additivity follows by comparison with measurable representatives.

Measurable maps

Measurability is the condition that inverse images preserve observable sets.

Definition. A map \(f:(X,\mathcal A)\to(Y,\mathcal B)\) is measurable if \(f^{-1}(B)\in\mathcal A\) for every \(B\in\mathcal B\).

Remark. To check measurability into a generated sigma-algebra, it suffices to check inverse images of a generating class closed enough to generate \(\mathcal B\).
