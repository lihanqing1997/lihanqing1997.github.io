11. Sigma-algebras and measures

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/power-series-on-the-real-line/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/lebesgue-measurable-functions/" %}

Sigma-algebras and measures records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the real analysis note.

Definition

Definition. A \(\sigma\)-algebra is closed under complements and countable unions. A measure assigns nonnegative size to measurable sets and is countably additive on disjoint unions.

Proposition

Proposition. A measure is continuous from below for increasing sets and from above for decreasing sets of finite initial measure.

Proof

Proof. For increasing sets, write the union as a disjoint union of successive differences. For decreasing sets, apply the increasing result to complements inside the first set.

Remark

Remark. Countable additivity is the key difference between measure and finite additivity. It is what makes limiting operations compatible with size.
