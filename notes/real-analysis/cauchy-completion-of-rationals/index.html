4. Cauchy completion of the rationals

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/dedekind-cuts-and-real-line/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/completeness-principles/" %}

The Cauchy construction builds the real line by adding limits to rational Cauchy sequences. It is the metric counterpart to Dedekind's order-theoretic construction.

Rational Cauchy sequences

Definition. A rational sequence \((q_n)\) is Cauchy if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(N\) such that \(|q_n-q_m|<\varepsilon\) whenever \(m,n\ge N\). This definition mentions only rational distances, not an external limit.

Equivalence of approximations

Definition. Two rational Cauchy sequences are equivalent when their difference tends to \(0\). A real number is an equivalence class of such sequences. Constant sequences embed \(\mathbb Q\) into the completion.

Operations by representatives

Construction. Addition and multiplication are defined termwise: \([(q_n)]+[(r_n)]=[(q_n+r_n)]\) and \([(q_n)][(r_n)]=[(q_nr_n)]\). Cauchy estimates show these definitions do not depend on the chosen representatives.

Completeness of the completion

Theorem. Every Cauchy sequence of equivalence classes has a limit represented by a carefully chosen diagonal sequence of rational approximants. Thus the completion is complete as a metric space and also becomes a complete ordered field.

Comparison with cuts

Proposition. A Cauchy real determines the cut of rationals below it, and a cut can be approached by rational sequences from inside. These maps preserve order and arithmetic, so the two constructions give isomorphic real lines.
