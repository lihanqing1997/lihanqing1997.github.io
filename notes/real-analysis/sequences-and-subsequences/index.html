2. Sequences and subsequences

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/ordered-fields-and-completeness/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/series-and-absolute-convergence/" %}

Sequences are the basic laboratory for limits. They encode convergence, divergence, compactness, and the behavior of functions through simple indexed families.

Limits and eventual behavior

Definition. A sequence \((x_n)\) in a metric space converges to \(x\), written \(x_n\to x\), if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there exists \(N\) such that \(d(x_n,x)<\varepsilon\) whenever \(n\ge N\). The definition ignores finitely many initial terms; convergence is an eventual property.

Cauchy criterion on the real line

Theorem. A real sequence converges in \(\mathbb R\) if and only if it is Cauchy: for every \(\varepsilon>0\), there is \(N\) such that \(|x_n-x_m|<\varepsilon\) for all \(m,n\ge N\). The forward direction follows from the triangle inequality. The reverse direction uses completeness of \(\mathbb R\).

Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem

Theorem. Every bounded real sequence has a convergent subsequence. More generally, every bounded sequence in \(\mathbb R^d\) has a convergent subsequence. This is the sequential form of compactness for closed bounded subsets of Euclidean space.

Interval bisection proof

Proof. Place the bounded sequence in a closed interval. Bisect the interval and choose a half containing infinitely many terms; repeat. This gives nested intervals whose lengths tend to zero. Choose \(n_k\) successively so that \(x_{n_k}\) lies in the \(k\)-th interval. The nested interval theorem gives a unique point, and the selected subsequence converges to it.

Limsup and liminf

Definition. For a real sequence, \(\limsup x_n=\lim_{n\to\infty}\sup_{k\ge n}x_k\) and \(\liminf x_n=\lim_{n\to\infty}\inf_{k\ge n}x_k\), with extended real values allowed. The sequence converges in \(\mathbb R\) exactly when these two quantities are finite and equal.

Oscillation example

Example. For \(x_n=(-1)^n+1/n\), the even subsequence converges to \(1\) and the odd subsequence converges to \(-1\). Thus \(\limsup x_n=1\), \(\liminf x_n=-1\), and the full sequence does not converge.
