17. Convex functions and monotone functions

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/mean-value-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/riemann-integration/" %}

Convex and monotone functions are one-dimensional models of order, curvature, and optimization. They have strong regularity even when not differentiable.

Convexity by chords

Convexity means the graph lies below its chords.

Definition. A function \(f:I\to\mathbb R\) on an interval is convex if \(f((1-\lambda)x+\lambda y)\le(1-\lambda)f(x)+\lambda f(y)\) for \(0\le\lambda\le1\).

Proposition. For convex \(f\), slopes of secant lines are monotone: if \(x\lt y\lt z\), then \[ \frac{f(y)-f(x)}{y-x}\le \frac{f(z)-f(x)}{z-x}\le \frac{f(z)-f(y)}{z-y}. \]

Proof. Write \(y=(1-\lambda)x+\lambda z\). Convexity gives \(f(y)\le(1-\lambda)f(x)+\lambda f(z)\), which rearranges to the first inequality. Writing \(y\) between \(x\) and \(z\) and rearranging symmetrically gives the second.

One-sided derivatives

Convex functions have controlled directional slopes.

Theorem. A convex function on an open interval has finite left and right derivatives at every point, and these one-sided derivatives are increasing.

Proof. The monotonicity of secant slopes shows that the limits of \((f(x+h)-f(x))/h\) as \(h\downarrow0\) and \(h\uparrow0\) exist as monotone limits bounded by nearby secant slopes. Comparing secant slopes at different base points gives monotonicity of the one-sided derivatives.

Jensen inequality

Convexity converts averages of inputs into bounds on averages of outputs.

Theorem. If \(f\) is convex and \(\lambda_i\ge0\), \(\sum_i\lambda_i=1\), then \[ f\left(\sum_i\lambda_i x_i\right)\le \sum_i\lambda_i f(x_i). \]

Proof. The case of two points is the definition. The finite case follows by induction: combine the first \(n-1\) points into their weighted average, then apply the two-point inequality to that average and \(x_n\).

Monotone functions

Monotone functions can jump, but only in a controlled way.

Theorem. A monotone real function on an interval has left and right limits at every point and has at most countably many discontinuities.

Proof. For increasing \(f\), the left limit is \(\sup_{y\lt x}f(y)\) and the right limit is \(\inf_{y>x}f(y)\). A discontinuity has a positive jump interval \((f(x-),f(x+))\). For each \(m\), the jumps of size at least \(1/m\) are finite on bounded subintervals, because their disjoint jump intervals fit inside the total variation. Taking a countable union over \(m\) and bounded intervals gives countability.

Subgradients

Subgradients replace derivatives at corners.

Definition. A number \(p\) is a subgradient of a convex \(f\) at \(x\) if \(f(y)\ge f(x)+p(y-x)\) for all \(y\).

Remark. At differentiability points, the only subgradient is \(f'(x)\). At nondifferentiable points, the subdifferential is the interval between the left and right derivatives.
