16. Product measures and Fubini theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/convergence-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/signed-measures-and-decompositions/" %}

Product measure gives a rigorous meaning to area, volume, and integration over product spaces. Tonelli and Fubini explain when one may compute a product integral by iterated integration.

Product sigma-algebra

Definition. For measurable spaces \((X,\mathcal A)\) and \((Y,\mathcal B)\), the product \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal A\otimes\mathcal B\) is generated by measurable rectangles \(A\times B\). It is the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra making the coordinate projections measurable.

Product measure

Theorem. If \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are \(\sigma\)-finite measures, there is a unique measure \(\mu\otimes\nu\) on \(\mathcal A\otimes\mathcal B\) such that \((\mu\otimes\nu)(A\times B)=\mu(A)\nu(B)\). The \(\sigma\)-finiteness hypothesis ensures uniqueness from rectangles.

Sections of measurable sets and functions

Proposition. If \(E\in\mathcal A\otimes\mathcal B\), then the sections \(E_x=\{y:(x,y)\in E\}\) and \(E^y=\{x:(x,y)\in E\}\) are measurable. If \(f\) is product-measurable, then \(f_x(y)=f(x,y)\) and \(f^y(x)=f(x,y)\) are measurable sections.

Tonelli theorem

Theorem. If \(f\ge0\) is measurable on \(X\times Y\), then \[ \int f\,\mathrm{d}(\mu\otimes\nu)=\int_X\left(\int_Y f(x,y)\,\mathrm{d}\nu(y)\right)\mathrm{d}\mu(x), \] and the same value is obtained by integrating in the opposite order, allowing \(+\infty\).

Fubini theorem

Theorem. If \(f\) is integrable on \(X\times Y\), then for almost every \(x\), the section \(f_x\) is integrable; for almost every \(y\), the section \(f^y\) is integrable; and the iterated integrals exist and equal \(\int f\,\mathrm{d}(\mu\otimes\nu)\).

Integrability is essential

Remark. Tonelli works for nonnegative functions without prior integrability. Fubini for signed functions requires integrability of \(|f|\). Without this hypothesis, the two iterated integrals may exist but disagree, or one may fail to exist.
