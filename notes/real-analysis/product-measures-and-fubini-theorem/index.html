31. Product measures and Fubini theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/convergence-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/signed-measures-and-decompositions/" %}

Product measures allow integration over several variables. Tonelli and Fubini are the precise rules for changing the order of integration.

Product sigma-algebra

Definition. The product sigma-algebra \(\mathcal A\otimes\mathcal B\) is generated by measurable rectangles \(A\times B\). It is the smallest sigma-algebra making coordinate projections measurable.

Product measure

Theorem. For sigma-finite measures \(\mu\) and \(\nu\), there is a unique measure \(\mu\otimes\nu\) satisfying \((\mu\otimes\nu)(A\times B)=\mu(A)\nu(B)\).

Tonelli theorem

Theorem. For nonnegative measurable \(f\), the product integral equals either iterated integral, allowing value \(\infty\). Nonnegativity is enough because no cancellation is involved.

Fubini theorem

Theorem. If \(f\in L^1(\mu\otimes\nu)\), then almost all sections are integrable and the two iterated integrals agree with the product integral.

Independence

Example. Independence of random variables means the joint law is a product measure. Expectations of functions of independent variables are therefore governed by Tonelli and Fubini.
