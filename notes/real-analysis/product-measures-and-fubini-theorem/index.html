31. Product measures and Fubini theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/convergence-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/signed-measures-and-decompositions/" %}

Product measures make integration over several variables precise. Tonelli and Fubini are the theorems that justify iterated integration.

Product sigma-algebras

Rectangles generate the measurable structure on products.

Definition. For measurable spaces \((X,\mathcal A)\) and \((Y,\mathcal B)\), the product sigma-algebra \(\mathcal A\otimes\mathcal B\) is generated by measurable rectangles \(A\times B\).

Proposition. If \(E\in\mathcal A\otimes\mathcal B\), then sections \(E_x=\{y:(x,y)\in E\}\) and \(E^y=\{x:(x,y)\in E\}\) are measurable.

Proof. The class of sets with measurable sections is a sigma-algebra. It contains rectangles because rectangle sections are either \(B\) or \(\varnothing\), and either \(A\) or \(\varnothing\). Hence it contains the generated sigma-algebra.

Existence of product measure

A product measure is determined by its values on rectangles.

Theorem. If \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are sigma-finite measures, there is a unique measure \(\mu\otimes\nu\) on \(\mathcal A\otimes\mathcal B\) such that \((\mu\otimes\nu)(A\times B)=\mu(A)\nu(B)\).

Proof. Define the premeasure on finite disjoint unions of rectangles. Sigma-finiteness ensures consistency and countable additivity on the generating algebra after decomposing into finite-measure pieces. Caratheodory extension gives existence, and uniqueness follows from the pi-lambda theorem on rectangles.

Tonelli theorem

Nonnegative functions can always be integrated in either order.

Theorem. If \(f\ge0\) is \(\mathcal A\otimes\mathcal B\)-measurable, then \[ \int f\,\mathrm{d}(\mu\otimes\nu)=\int\left(\int f(x,y)\,\mathrm{d}\nu(y)\right)\mathrm{d}\mu(x) \] with values in \([0,\infty]\), and similarly with the order reversed.

Proof. Prove first for indicators of measurable rectangles, then for indicators of product-measurable sets by a monotone class argument. Extend to nonnegative simple functions by linearity, and then to arbitrary nonnegative measurable functions by monotone convergence.

Fubini theorem

Integrable functions have finite iterated integrals almost everywhere and matching values.

Theorem. If \(f\in L^1(\mu\otimes\nu)\), then for almost every \(x\), \(f(x,\cdot)\in L^1(\nu)\), the function \(x\mapsto\int f(x,y)\,\mathrm{d}\nu(y)\) is integrable, and the product integral equals the iterated integral.

Proof. Apply Tonelli to \(|f|\) to see that the iterated integral of \(|f|\) is finite. Hence the sections are integrable almost everywhere. Apply Tonelli separately to \(f^+\) and \(f^-\), both having finite total integral, and subtract.

Changing order safely

Absolute integrability is the safe hypothesis for exchanging integration order.

Remark. Conditional integrability can make iterated integrals depend on the order. In applications, checking \(\int |f|\lt \infty\) is often the cleanest way to avoid ambiguity.
