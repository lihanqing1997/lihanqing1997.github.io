17. Signed measures and decompositions

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/product-measures-and-fubini-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/radon-nikodym-derivatives/" %}

Signed measures allow cancellation while keeping countable additivity. Their structure is controlled by positive and negative parts, just as an integrable function is controlled by its positive and negative parts.

Signed measures

Definition. A signed measure \(\nu\) on \((X,\mathcal A)\) is a countably additive set function taking values in \((-\infty,\infty]\) or \([-\infty,\infty)\), but not both infinities. Countable additivity is required whenever the series is meaningful.

Positive and negative sets

Definition. A measurable set \(P\) is positive for \(\nu\) if \(\nu(A)\ge0\) for every measurable \(A\subset P\). A set \(N\) is negative if \(\nu(A)\le0\) for every measurable \(A\subset N\). These are stronger conditions than merely \(\nu(P)\ge0\) or \(\nu(N)\le0\).

Hahn decomposition theorem

Theorem. For every signed measure \(\nu\), there are disjoint measurable sets \(P,N\) with \(X=P\cup N\), such that \(P\) is positive and \(N\) is negative. The decomposition is unique up to \(\nu\)-null sets in the natural sense.

Jordan decomposition theorem

Theorem. Every signed measure has a unique decomposition \(\nu=\nu^+-\nu^-\), where \(\nu^+\) and \(\nu^-\) are mutually singular positive measures. The total variation measure is \(|\nu|=\nu^++\nu^-\).

Integral representation example

Example. If \(f\) is integrable and \(\nu(A)=\int_A f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\), then \(\nu^+(A)=\int_A f^+\,\mathrm{d}\mu\), \(\nu^-(A)=\int_A f^-\,\mathrm{d}\mu\), and \(|\nu|(A)=\int_A |f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\). Thus signed-measure decomposition generalizes positive and negative parts of a function.

Mutual singularity

Definition. Two positive measures \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are mutually singular, written \(\mu\perp\nu\), if there is a measurable set \(A\) such that \(\mu\) is concentrated on \(A\) and \(\nu\) is concentrated on \(A^c\). Singularity is a geometric separation of mass.
