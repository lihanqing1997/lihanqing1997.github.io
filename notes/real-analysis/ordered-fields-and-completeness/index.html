Ordered-field algebra lets one manipulate inequalities; completeness lets one take limits of bounded approximations. Real analysis is the study of what becomes possible when both structures coexist.

Order-compatible field operations

The order must interact with addition and multiplication strongly enough to justify algebraic inequality arguments.

Definition. An ordered field is a field \(F\) with a total order \(\le\) such that \(x\le y\Rightarrow x+z\le y+z\), and \(0\le x,0\le y\Rightarrow 0\le xy\).

Proposition. In an ordered field, \(x^2\ge0\), \(1>0\), multiplication by a positive element preserves inequalities, and taking reciprocals reverses inequalities among positive elements.

Proof. Since either \(x\ge0\) or \(-x\ge0\), the product rule gives \(x^2\ge0\). If \(1\lt 0\), then \((-1)(-1)>0\) gives \(1>0\), contradiction, so \(1>0\). The remaining claims follow by adding \(-ay\) or comparing \(1/x\) and \(1/y\) after multiplying by the positive product \(xy\).

Least upper bounds

Suprema are the formal language for saying that a bounded increasing approximation has an endpoint.

Definition. If \(A\subset F\), an upper bound is \(u\in F\) with \(a\le u\) for all \(a\in A\). A supremum \(\sup A\) is an upper bound \(s\) such that every upper bound \(u\) satisfies \(s\le u\).

Lemma. If \(s=\sup A\), then for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(a\in A\) with \(s-\varepsilon\lt a\le s\).

Proof. If no such \(a\) existed, then \(s-\varepsilon\) would still be an upper bound for \(A\), contradicting the minimality of \(s\) among upper bounds.

Archimedean order

Completeness rules out real infinitesimals and infinitely large real numbers.

Theorem. Every complete ordered field is Archimedean: for every \(x\in F\), there exists \(n\in\mathbb N\) such that \(x\lt n\).

Proof. If \(\mathbb N\) were bounded above, let \(s=\sup\mathbb N\). By the approximation property for suprema, there is \(n\in\mathbb N\) with \(s-1\lt n\). Then \(s\lt n+1\), contradicting that \(s\) is an upper bound for \(\mathbb N\).

Density of rational numbers

Archimedean order makes rational grids arbitrarily fine.

Proposition. If \(x\lt y\) in a complete ordered field containing \(\mathbb Q\), then there is \(q\in\mathbb Q\) with \(x\lt q\lt y\).

Proof. Choose \(n\in\mathbb N\) so large that \(1/n\lt y-x\). By Archimedeanness choose \(m\in\mathbb Z\) with \(m-1\le nx\lt m\). Then \(x\lt m/n\le x+1/n\lt y\).

Equivalent faces of completeness

Different areas of analysis prefer different formulations of the same no-gap principle.

Theorem. On an Archimedean ordered field, the least-upper-bound property, monotone convergence for bounded monotone sequences, Cauchy completeness, nested closed interval completeness, and Bolzano-Weierstrass are equivalent.

Proof. The least-upper-bound property gives monotone convergence by taking the supremum of the range. Monotone convergence gives nested interval completeness by applying it to the left endpoints. Nested intervals give Cauchy completeness by trapping a Cauchy sequence in intervals of shrinking length. Cauchy completeness gives Bolzano-Weierstrass by bisection of a bounded interval. Bolzano-Weierstrass gives least upper bounds by applying it to a sequence in \(A\) approaching the infimum of the upper bounds.
