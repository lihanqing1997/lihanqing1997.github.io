49. Tightness and Prokhorov theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/weak-convergence-of-measures/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/parameter-dependent-integrals/" %}

Tightness is compactness for probability measures. It prevents mass from escaping to infinity.

Tight families

Definition. A family \(\mathcal P\) of probability measures is tight if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is compact \(K\) with \(P(K)\ge1-\varepsilon\) for all \(P\in\mathcal P\).

Prokhorov theorem

Theorem. On a Polish space, a family of probability measures is relatively compact for weak convergence if and only if it is tight.

Moment criterion

Proposition. On \(\mathbb R^d\), a uniform bound on \(\int |x|^p\,\mathrm{d}P(x)\) implies tightness. Markov inequality controls the tails outside large balls.

Compactness plus uniqueness

Theorem. If every subsequence has a further weakly convergent subsequence and every possible subsequential limit is \(P\), then the whole sequence converges weakly to \(P\).

Why Polish spaces appear

Remark. Polish spaces provide separability, regularity, tightness criteria, and enough countable structure for probability and statistics on general spaces.
