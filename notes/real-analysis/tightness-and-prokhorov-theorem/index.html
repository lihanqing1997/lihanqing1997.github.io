49. Tightness and Prokhorov theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/weak-convergence-of-measures/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/parameter-dependent-integrals/" %}

Tightness prevents mass from escaping to infinity. Prokhorov's theorem identifies tightness as the compactness criterion for probability measures on Polish spaces.

Tight families

Tightness says most mass lies in one compact set, uniformly over the family.

Definition. A family \(\mathcal P\) of probability measures on a metric space \(S\) is tight if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is compact \(K\subset S\) such that \(P(K)\ge1-\varepsilon\) for all \(P\in\mathcal P\).

Proposition. On \(\mathbb R^d\), a family is tight if and only if \(\lim_{M\to\infty}\sup_{P\in\mathcal P}P(\|x\|>M)=0\).

Proof. Closed balls are compact in \(\mathbb R^d\), so the tail condition gives tightness. Conversely, if \(K\) is compact, it is contained in some closed ball \(\{\|x\|\le M\}\), so mass outside that ball is at most mass outside \(K\).

Relative compactness

Tightness is designed to produce weakly convergent subsequences.

Theorem. Every tight sequence of probability measures on \(\mathbb R^d\) has a weakly convergent subsequence.

Proof. Use tightness to restrict attention to large compact balls. On each ball, approximate bounded Lipschitz test functions by values on a finite net. A diagonal argument extracts a subsequence on which integrals of a countable determining class converge. The limiting functional defines a probability measure by Riesz representation, and tightness prevents loss of mass.

Prokhorov theorem

Polish spaces admit the same compactness criterion.

Theorem. On a Polish space, a family of probability measures is relatively compact for weak convergence if and only if it is tight.

Proof. Relative compactness implies tightness by covering compact subsets of the weak closure and using regularity of each probability measure. Tightness implies relative compactness by embedding the argument above into a countable base of the Polish topology and using Prokhorov's metric or a countable convergence-determining class.

Moment criteria

Moment bounds imply tightness in Euclidean spaces.

Proposition. If \(\sup_{P\in\mathcal P}\int \|x\|^p\,\mathrm{d}P(x)\lt \infty\) for some \(p>0\), then \(\mathcal P\) is tight on \(\mathbb R^d\).

Proof. Markov's inequality gives \(P(\|x\|>M)\le M^{-p}\int\|x\|^p\,\mathrm{d}P\). Taking the supremum over \(P\) and sending \(M\to\infty\) gives the tail criterion for tightness.

From real analysis to probability limits

Tightness plus identification of subsequential limits is a standard convergence proof.

Remark. Many weak convergence proofs have two steps: prove tightness, then show every subsequential weak limit must equal the desired law by a determining class of test functions.
