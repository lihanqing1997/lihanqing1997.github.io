48. Weak convergence of measures

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/fourier-transform-on-euclidean-space/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/tightness-and-prokhorov-theorem/" %}

Weak convergence tests measures against bounded continuous functions. It is the analytic form of convergence in distribution.

Weak convergence

Definition. Probability measures \(P_n\) on a metric space converge weakly to \(P\) if \(\int f\,\mathrm{d}P_n\to \int f\,\mathrm{d}P\) for every bounded continuous \(f\).

Portmanteau theorem

Theorem. Weak convergence is equivalent to closed-set upper bounds, open-set lower bounds, and convergence of probabilities of continuity sets. These forms are often easier to verify than test-function convergence.

Bounded-Lipschitz metric

Proposition. On many metric spaces, bounded Lipschitz test functions metrize weak convergence. This gives a countable and quantitative way to check distributional convergence.

Continuous mapping theorem

Theorem. If \(X_n\Rightarrow X\) and \(g\) is continuous at \(X\) almost surely, then \(g(X_n)\Rightarrow g(X)\). Discontinuity sets must be null under the limit law.

Total variation comparison

Remark. Total variation convergence implies weak convergence, but weak convergence is much weaker. Moving point masses by small distances can converge weakly while remaining singular in total variation.
