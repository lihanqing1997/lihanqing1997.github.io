50. Parameter-dependent integrals and differentiation under the integral sign

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/tightness-and-prokhorov-theorem/" %}

Parameter-dependent integrals are controlled by convergence theorems. Continuity and differentiability under the integral sign require uniform integrable bounds.

Continuity under the integral sign

Dominated convergence gives the basic continuity theorem.

Theorem. Let \(f:\Theta\times X\to\mathbb R\) be measurable in \(x\), continuous in \(\theta\) at \(\theta_0\) for almost every \(x\), and satisfy \(|f(\theta,x)|\le g(x)\in L^1\) near \(\theta_0\). Then \(F(\theta)=\int f(\theta,x)\,\mathrm{d}\mu(x)\) is continuous at \(\theta_0\).

Proof. If \(\theta_n\to\theta_0\), then \(f(\theta_n,x)\to f(\theta_0,x)\) almost everywhere and is dominated by \(g\). Dominated convergence gives \(F(\theta_n)\to F(\theta_0)\), which is sequential continuity.

Differentiation under the integral sign

A dominated derivative permits exchanging derivative and integral.

Theorem. Suppose \(\partial_\theta f(\theta,x)\) exists near \(\theta_0\), \(f(\theta_0,\cdot)\in L^1\), and \(|\partial_\theta f(\theta,x)|\le g(x)\in L^1\). Then \(F(\theta)=\int f(\theta,x)\,\mathrm{d}\mu(x)\) is differentiable at \(\theta_0\) and \[ F'(\theta_0)=\int \partial_\theta f(\theta_0,x)\,\mathrm{d}\mu(x). \]

Proof. The difference quotient equals \(\int_0^1\partial_\theta f(\theta_0+th,x)\,\mathrm{d}t\) for almost every \(x\). It converges pointwise to \(\partial_\theta f(\theta_0,x)\) as \(h\to0\) and is dominated by \(g(x)\). Dominated convergence applies.

Vector parameters

Gradients are handled coordinatewise or by linearization.

Proposition. If \(\nabla_\theta f(\theta,x)\) exists near \(\theta_0\), is pointwise continuous at \(\theta_0\), and \(\|\nabla_\theta f(\theta,x)\|\le g(x)\in L^1\), then \(\nabla F(\theta_0)=\int\nabla_\theta f(\theta_0,x)\,\mathrm{d}\mu(x)\).

Proof. Apply the one-dimensional differentiation theorem to each coordinate direction. The uniform \(L^1\) bound also gives the Frechet expansion by writing the increment as an integral of the gradient along the line segment from \(\theta_0\) to \(\theta_0+h\).

Leibniz rule with moving endpoints

Boundary motion contributes endpoint terms.

Theorem. If \(F(\theta)=\int_{a(\theta)}^{b(\theta)} f(\theta,x)\,\mathrm{d}x\), with \(a,b\) differentiable and \(f,\partial_\theta f\) continuous, then \[ F'(\theta)=f(\theta,b(\theta))b'(\theta)-f(\theta,a(\theta))a'(\theta)+\int_{a(\theta)}^{b(\theta)}\partial_\theta f(\theta,x)\,\mathrm{d}x. \]

Proof. Rewrite the integral over a fixed reference interval by an affine change of variables from \([0,1]\) to \([a(\theta),b(\theta)]\). Differentiate under the integral sign using the previous theorem and simplify the derivative of the transformed integrand.

Statistical objectives

Differentiating expectations needs hypotheses, not just formal algebra.

Remark. Scores, Hessians, risk gradients, and estimating equations all involve parameter-dependent integrals. Dominating envelopes or uniform integrability assumptions are what make the formal interchange valid.
