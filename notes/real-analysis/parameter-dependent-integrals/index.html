50. Parameter-dependent integrals and differentiation under the integral sign

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/tightness-and-prokhorov-theorem/" %}

Statistical criteria often have the form of an integral depending on a parameter. Continuity and differentiability of such criteria are analytic theorems, not formal manipulations.

Continuity under domination

Theorem. If \(f(\theta,x)\) is a.e. continuous in \(\theta\) at \(\theta_0\) and dominated near \(\theta_0\) by an integrable \(g\), then \(\theta\mapsto \int f(\theta,x)\,\mathrm{d}\mu(x)\) is continuous at \(\theta_0\).

Differentiation under the integral

Theorem. If \(\partial_\theta f(\theta,x)\) exists near \(\theta_0\) and is dominated by an integrable function, then the derivative of the integral equals the integral of the derivative.

Vector parameters

Proposition. For \(\theta\in\mathbb R^d\), domination of partial derivatives gives a gradient by integrating the pointwise gradient. Higher-order differentiation requires analogous domination of higher derivatives.

Score identity

Example. If \(p_\theta\) is a density and differentiation is justified, then \(\int \partial_\theta p_\theta\,\mathrm{d}\mu=0\), hence \(\mathbb E_\theta \partial_\theta \log p_\theta(X)=0\).

Neighborhood domination

Remark. Dominating only at one parameter value is usually not enough. Uniform statements require domination on neighborhoods or compact parameter sets.
