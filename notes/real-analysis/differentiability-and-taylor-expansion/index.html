6. Differentiability and Taylor expansion

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/continuity-and-uniform-continuity/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/mean-value-theorems/" %}

Differentiability is the existence of a first-order linear approximation. Taylor expansion asks how much information is contained in higher derivatives and how large the remainder is.

Derivative as linear approximation

Definition. For \(f:I\to\mathbb R\), the derivative at \(a\) is the number \(f'(a)\) such that \(f(a+h)=f(a)+f'(a)h+o(h)\) as \(h\to0\). This linear approximation viewpoint is more structural than the quotient formula, though the two are equivalent in one dimension.

Rules and inverse behavior

Proposition. Sums, products, quotients, and compositions of differentiable functions are differentiable under the usual hypotheses, with the standard derivative rules. If \(f\) is strictly monotone, continuous, differentiable near \(a\), and \(f'(a)\ne0\), then the inverse is differentiable at \(f(a)\) with derivative \((f^{-1})'(f(a))=1/f'(a)\).

Taylor polynomial

Definition. If \(f\) has derivatives up to order \(n\) at \(a\), its degree \(n\) Taylor polynomial is \(P_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n f^{(k)}(a)(x-a)^k/k!\). Taylor's theorem is not just the polynomial; it is the statement that \(f(x)-P_n(x)\) has a controlled remainder.

Taylor theorem with remainders

Theorem. If \(f\) has \(n+1\) continuous derivatives on an interval containing \(a\) and \(x\), then \[ f(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k+\frac{f^{(n+1)}(\xi)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1} \] for some \(\xi\) between \(a\) and \(x\). If \(f^{(n+1)}\) is integrable, one also has the integral remainder \(\int_a^x f^{(n+1)}(t)(x-t)^n/n!\,\mathrm{d}t\).

Peano form and local order

Corollary. If \(f\) has \(n\) derivatives at \(a\), then under suitable local differentiability assumptions \(f(x)=P_n(x)+o((x-a)^n)\). The Peano form is often the cleanest way to compare local orders without estimating a global bound for a derivative.

Taylor series versus Taylor theorem

Counterexample. The function \(f(x)=e^{-1/x^2}\) for \(x\ne0\), \(f(0)=0\), is \(C^\infty\) and all derivatives at \(0\) vanish, so its Taylor series at \(0\) is identically zero. The function is not identically zero near \(0\). Infinite differentiability alone does not imply analyticity.
