15. Differentiability and Taylor expansion

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/equicontinuity-and-arzela-ascoli/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/mean-value-theorems/" %}

Differentiability linearizes a function near a point. Taylor expansion measures the error of replacing a function by its polynomial approximation.

Derivative as linear approximation

Definition. For one real variable, \(f^\prime(x)=\lim_{h\to0}(f(x+h)-f(x))/h\) when the limit exists. Differentiability implies continuity, but continuity alone gives no derivative.

Rules of differentiation

Proposition. Linearity, product, quotient, and chain rules follow from algebra of difference quotients. The chain rule is the analytic core of transformations and delta-method arguments.

Taylor formula

Theorem. If \(f\) is sufficiently smooth near \(a\), then \(f(a+h)=\sum_{j=0}^k f^{(j)}(a)h^j/j!+o(h^k)\). Integral or Lagrange remainders give sharper quantitative bounds when higher derivatives are controlled.

Quadratic approximation

Example. If \(f^\prime(a)=0\) and \(f^{\prime\prime}(a)>0\), then \(f(a+h)=f(a)+\frac12 f^{\prime\prime}(a)h^2+o(h^2)\). Likelihood and risk expansions often have this quadratic form.

Pathologies

Counterexample. The function \(|x|\) is continuous but not differentiable at \(0\). Smoothness assumptions are not decoration; they determine which expansions are valid.
