15. Differentiability and Taylor expansion

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/equicontinuity-and-arzela-ascoli/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/mean-value-theorems/" %}

Differentiability is linear approximation at small scales. Taylor's theorem refines this approximation by recording higher-order local information.

Derivative as best linear approximation

In one dimension, the derivative is the coefficient of the first-order local model.

Definition. A function \(f:I\to\mathbb R\) is differentiable at \(x\in I\) if the limit \(f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}(f(x+h)-f(x))/h\) exists.

Proposition. If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x\), then \(f(x+h)=f(x)+f'(x)h+o(h)\) as \(h\to0\), and \(f\) is continuous at \(x\).

Proof. The definition says \((f(x+h)-f(x))/h=f'(x)+r(h)\) with \(r(h)\to0\). Multiplying by \(h\) gives the expansion. Since \(f(x+h)-f(x)=h(f'(x)+r(h))\to0\), continuity follows.

Algebra and chain rules

Differentiability is stable under the usual algebraic operations.

Theorem. If \(f,g\) are differentiable at \(x\), then \(f+g\), \(fg\), and \(f/g\) where \(g(x)\ne0\), are differentiable at \(x\). If \(g\) is differentiable at \(x\) and \(f\) is differentiable at \(g(x)\), then \((f\circ g)'(x)=f'(g(x))g'(x)\).

Proof. Write first-order expansions \(f(x+h)=f(x)+f'(x)h+o(h)\) and \(g(x+h)=g(x)+g'(x)h+o(h)\). Multiplying expansions gives the product rule, and applying the product rule to \(g^{-1}\) gives the quotient rule. For the chain rule, use \(g(x+h)-g(x)=g'(x)h+o(h)\) inside the first-order expansion of \(f\) at \(g(x)\).

Taylor theorem with remainder

Higher derivatives give polynomial approximations and quantitative errors.

Theorem. If \(f\in C^{n+1}\) on an interval containing \(a\) and \(x\), then \[ f(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k+\frac{f^{(n+1)}(\xi)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1} \] for some \(\xi\) between \(a\) and \(x\).

Proof. Subtract the degree \(n\) Taylor polynomial and choose \(C\) so that the remainder equals \(C(x-a)^{n+1}\) at the endpoint. Repeatedly apply Rolle's theorem to the function \(f(t)-T_n(t)-C(t-a)^{n+1}\), which has enough vanishing derivatives at \(a\) and vanishes at \(x\). After \(n+1\) applications, \(C=f^{(n+1)}(\xi)/(n+1)!\).

Peano form

The Peano remainder is often cleaner for asymptotics.

Theorem. If \(f\) has \(n\) derivatives near \(a\) and \(f^{(n)}\) is continuous at \(a\), then \[ f(a+h)=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}h^k+o(h^n). \]

Proof. Apply Taylor's formula with integral or repeated mean-value remainder to \(f^{(n-1)}\), using continuity of \(f^{(n)}\) at \(a\). The error is bounded by \(|h|^n\) times the oscillation of \(f^{(n)}\) near \(a\), which tends to zero.

Taylor expansion as local geometry

The first derivative gives slope; the second derivative gives curvature.

Remark. In asymptotic statistics, Taylor expansion of criteria and log-likelihoods is the deterministic core behind consistency rates, asymptotic normality, Laplace approximation, and local asymptotic quadraticity.
