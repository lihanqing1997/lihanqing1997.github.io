6. Differentiability and Taylor expansion

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/continuity-and-uniform-continuity/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/mean-value-theorems/" %}

Differentiability and Taylor expansion records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the real analysis note.

Definition

Definition. A real function is differentiable at \(x\) if it has a linear first-order approximation there. Higher derivatives describe higher-order local approximations.

Taylor theorem

Theorem. If \(f\) has \(n+1\) continuous derivatives near \(a\), then Taylor's formula writes \(f(x)\) as its degree \(n\) Taylor polynomial plus a remainder controlled by \(f^{(n+1)}\).

Proof

Proof. Apply the mean value theorem repeatedly to the difference between \(f\) and its Taylor polynomial. Integral and Lagrange remainders are different ways of encoding the same leftover term.

Remark

Remark. Taylor expansion is local, not global magic. Its accuracy depends on smoothness and the size of the neighborhood.
