44. Approximation by simple, continuous, and smooth functions

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/egorov-lusin-and-approximation-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/convolution-and-mollifiers/" %}

Approximation is the method that lets one prove a theorem for simple or smooth functions and then pass to rough measurable functions by density.

Simple functions in \(L^p\)

Simple functions are the first dense class in \(L^p\).

Theorem. If \(1\le p\lt \infty\), simple functions are dense in \(L^p(\mu)\).

Proof. For \(f\ge0\), dyadic simple approximations of \(f\wedge n\) converge pointwise to \(f\) and are dominated by \(f\). Dominated convergence gives \(L^p\) convergence after applying the construction to \(|f|^p\). Decompose general real or complex \(f\) into positive and negative real and imaginary parts.

Continuous approximation on \(\mathbb R^d\)

Regularity upgrades measurable approximations to continuous ones on Euclidean spaces.

Theorem. For \(1\le p\lt \infty\), \(C_c(\mathbb R^d)\) is dense in \(L^p(\mathbb R^d)\).

Proof. Approximate \(f\) by a simple function supported on a set of finite measure. Use regularity of Lebesgue measure to approximate each measurable set by compact sets inside and open sets outside with small measure difference. Urysohn-type continuous functions between those compact and open sets approximate the indicators in \(L^p\). Combine linearly.

Smooth approximation by mollifiers

Convolution with smooth approximate identities produces smooth functions.

Theorem. If \(f\in L^p(\mathbb R^d)\), \(1\le p\lt \infty\), and \(\rho_\varepsilon(x)=\varepsilon^{-d}\rho(x/\varepsilon)\) is a standard mollifier, then \(\rho_\varepsilon*f\to f\) in \(L^p\).

Proof. Translations are continuous in \(L^p\): \(\|f(\cdot-h)-f\|_p\to0\). Then \(\rho_\varepsilon*f-f=\int \rho_\varepsilon(h)(f(\cdot-h)-f)\,\mathrm{d}h\). Minkowski's integral inequality bounds the \(L^p\) norm by the average of translation errors over \(|h|=O(\varepsilon)\), which tends to zero.

Approximation with support control

Support matters in PDE and local analysis.

Proposition. If \(f\in L^p(\Omega)\) and \(\Omega\subset\mathbb R^d\) is open, then functions in \(C_c^\infty(\Omega)\) approximate \(f\) in \(L^p\) when \(f\) is supported away from the boundary; general \(f\) is handled by cutoff exhaustion.

Proof. For support away from the boundary, extend \(f\) by zero and mollify with \(\varepsilon\) smaller than the distance to the boundary. For general \(f\), multiply by cutoffs \(\chi_k\) supported in \(\Omega\) and equal to \(1\) on an increasing exhaustion; \(\chi_k f\to f\) in \(L^p\), then mollify each cutoff version.

Proof strategy

Density arguments must keep the norm and topology visible.

Remark. A dense class is useful only relative to a topology. Simple functions are dense in \(L^p\), continuous functions may be dense on regular spaces, and smooth compactly supported functions are dense in Euclidean \(L^p\), but not in \(L^\infty\) under the essential sup norm.
