22. Approximation by smooth and simple functions

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/egorov-lusin-and-approximation-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/absolute-continuity-and-bounded-variation/" %}

Approximation theorems let one prove results for simple, continuous, or smooth functions and then pass to rough functions by density. This is how abstract measure theory becomes calculable.

Density of simple functions

Theorem. For \(1\le p<\infty\), simple functions with finite-measure support are dense in \(L^p\). The proof truncates \(f\) in size and support, then approximates the truncated function by dyadic simple functions.

Continuous compact support

Theorem. For Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb R^d\), \(C_c(\mathbb R^d)\) is dense in \(L^p(\mathbb R^d)\) for \(1\le p<\infty\). The key ingredient is regularity of Lebesgue measure: finite-measure sets can be squeezed between compact and open sets with small measure difference.

Mollifiers

Definition. A mollifier is a nonnegative \(C_c^\infty\) function \(\rho\) with \(\int\rho\,\mathrm{d}x=1\). Define \(\rho_\varepsilon(x)=\varepsilon^{-d}\rho(x/\varepsilon)\). Convolution \(f*\rho_\varepsilon\) smooths \(f\) by averaging it over a ball of radius \(\varepsilon\).

Smooth approximation in \(L^p\)

Theorem. For \(1\le p<\infty\), \(C_c^\infty(\mathbb R^d)\) is dense in \(L^p(\mathbb R^d)\). Approximate first by continuous compactly supported functions, then convolve with mollifiers. Uniform continuity on compact support controls the error as \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\).

Approximate identities

Proposition. The family \(\rho_\varepsilon\) is an approximate identity: for \(f\in L^p\), \(f*\rho_\varepsilon\to f\) in \(L^p\) for \(1\le p<\infty\). If \(f\) is continuous at \(x\), then \(f*\rho_\varepsilon(x)\to f(x)\).

Why p infinity is different

Remark. Smooth compactly supported functions are not dense in \(L^\infty(\mathbb R^d)\) under the essential sup norm. Sup-norm approximation remembers discontinuities and behavior at infinity much more rigidly than \(L^p\) approximation for finite \(p\).
