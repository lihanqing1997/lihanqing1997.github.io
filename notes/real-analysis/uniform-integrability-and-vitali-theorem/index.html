39. Uniform integrability and Vitali convergence

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/modes-of-convergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/lp-spaces/" %}

Uniform integrability prevents mass from hiding in rare high spikes. It is the exact condition that often upgrades convergence in measure to convergence of integrals.

Uniform integrability

Definition. A family \(\mathcal F\subset L^1\) is uniformly integrable if \(\sup_{f\in\mathcal F}\int_{|f|>M}|f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\to0\) as \(M\to\infty\).

de la Vallee Poussin criterion

Theorem. Uniform integrability is equivalent to the existence of a convex increasing \(\Phi\) with \(\Phi(t)/t\to\infty\) and \(\sup_f \int \Phi(|f|)\,\mathrm{d}\mu<\infty\).

Vitali theorem

Theorem. On a finite measure space, convergence in measure plus uniform integrability implies \(L^1\) convergence. Conversely, \(L^1\) convergence gives convergence in measure and uniform integrability of the sequence.

Truncation proof

Proof. Split \(|f_n-f|\) into bounded and tail parts. Convergence in measure handles the bounded part, while uniform integrability makes the tails small uniformly.

Spike example

Counterexample. \(f_n=n\mathbf 1_{(0,1/n)}\) converges to zero in measure but has integral \(1\). This is the canonical failure of uniform integrability.
