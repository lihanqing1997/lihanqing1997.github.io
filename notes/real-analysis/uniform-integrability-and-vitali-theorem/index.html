Uniform integrability is the condition that prevents mass from escaping into rare large values. It is the right replacement for domination in many limit theorems.

Uniform integrability

Uniform integrability controls tails uniformly over a family.

Definition. A family \(\mathcal F\subset L^1(\mu)\) is uniformly integrable if \[ \sup_{f\in\mathcal F}\int_{\{|f|>M\}}|f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\to0 \] as \(M\to\infty\), and the integrals are uniformly absolutely continuous over small-measure sets.

Proposition. If \(|f|\le g\in L^1\) for all \(f\in\mathcal F\), then \(\mathcal F\) is uniformly integrable.

Proof. The tail integral is bounded by \(\int_{\{g>M\}}g\,\mathrm{d}\mu\), which tends to zero. Absolute continuity over small sets follows from the absolute continuity of the integral of \(g\).

de la Vallee-Poussin criterion

Convex superlinear bounds characterize uniform integrability.

Theorem. A family \(\mathcal F\subset L^1\) is uniformly integrable if and only if there is an increasing convex \(\Phi\) with \(\Phi(t)/t\to\infty\) and \(\sup_{f\in\mathcal F}\int\Phi(|f|)\,\mathrm{d}\mu\lt \infty\).

Proof. If such \(\Phi\) exists, then on \(\{|f|>M\}\), \(|f|\le [M/\Phi(M)]\Phi(|f|)\) after monotone adjustment, giving uniform tail decay. Conversely, uniform tail decay lets one build a piecewise linear convex \(\Phi\) with slopes increasing slowly enough that the \(\Phi\)-integrals remain uniformly bounded but fast enough that \(\Phi(t)/t\to\infty\).

Vitali convergence theorem

Uniform integrability plus convergence in measure is exactly \(L^1\) convergence.

Theorem. If \(f_n\to f\) in measure and \(\{f_n\}\) is uniformly integrable, then \(f\in L^1\) and \(\|f_n-f\|_1\to0\). Conversely, \(L^1\) convergence implies uniform integrability of \(\{f_n\}\cup\{f\}\).

Proof. Use uniform integrability to choose \(M\) so tails of \(f_n\) are uniformly small. Truncated variables are bounded, and convergence in measure implies \(L^1\) convergence for bounded functions on finite-measure pieces. The tails complete the estimate. Conversely, \(L^1\)-convergent sequences are close to one integrable function, and finite \(L^1\)-neighborhoods of a uniformly integrable singleton are uniformly integrable.

Weak compactness in \(L^1\)

Uniform integrability is also a compactness condition.

Theorem. On a finite measure space, a bounded subset of \(L^1\) is relatively weakly compact if and only if it is uniformly integrable.

Proof. This is the Dunford-Pettis theorem. One direction follows because weak compactness prevents concentration on shrinking sets when tested against bounded indicators. The other direction uses uniform integrability to approximate by bounded functions on finite partitions, then diagonal compactness and duality with \(L^\infty\).

Statistical meaning

Uniform integrability upgrades distributional or probabilistic convergence to convergence of expectations.

Remark. For random variables, uniform integrability is the missing hypothesis in statements like \(X_n\to X\) in probability and \(\mathbb E X_n\to\mathbb E X\). It rules out rare but large contributions.
