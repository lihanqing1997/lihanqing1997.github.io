1. Natural numbers, integers, rationals, and induction

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/ordered-fields-and-completeness/" %}

Real analysis begins before limits: it begins with the algebraic and order structures that make limiting arguments meaningful. The natural numbers provide induction, the integers provide additive inverses, and the rationals provide the first ordered field, but the rationals still have gaps.

Induction and recursion on natural numbers

Induction is the proof principle; recursion is the construction principle. Together they make definitions by finite iteration legitimate.

Definition. The natural numbers are a set \(\mathbb N\) with a distinguished element \(0\) and successor map \(S\) satisfying the Peano axioms: \(0\) is not a successor, \(S\) is injective, and every set containing \(0\) and closed under \(S\) is all of \(\mathbb N\).

Theorem. If \(A\subseteq\mathbb N\), \(0\in A\), and \(n\in A\Rightarrow S(n)\in A\), then \(A=\mathbb N\). Equivalently, if a statement \(P(n)\) holds at \(0\) and \(P(n)\Rightarrow P(n+1)\), then \(P(n)\) holds for all \(n\).

Proof. Apply the Peano induction axiom to the set \(A=\{n:P(n)\text{ is true}\}\). The hypotheses say exactly that \(A\) contains \(0\) and is closed under successor, hence \(A=\mathbb N\).

Integers and cancellation

Passing from \(\mathbb N\) to \(\mathbb Z\) is the algebraic operation of adding formal additive inverses.

Proposition. The integers may be constructed as equivalence classes of pairs \((a,b)\in\mathbb N^2\), with \((a,b)\sim(c,d)\) if \(a+d=b+c\). The class of \((a,b)\) represents \(a-b\), and addition and multiplication are well-defined.

Proof. The relation is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive by associativity and commutativity in \(\mathbb N\). Define \((a,b)+(c,d)=(a+c,b+d)\) and \((a,b)(c,d)=(ac+bd,ad+bc)\). If representatives are changed by the relation, the defining equalities preserve these formulas after expanding both sides, so the operations descend to equivalence classes.

Rationals as the smallest ordered field

The rationals add multiplicative inverses for nonzero integers while preserving the order inherited from \(\mathbb Z\).

Definition. The rational numbers are equivalence classes of pairs \((m,n)\in\mathbb Z\times(\mathbb Z\setminus\{0\})\), with \((m,n)\sim(p,q)\) if \(mq=np\). The class of \((m,n)\) is written \(m/n\).

Proposition. \(\mathbb Q\) is an ordered field, and for any ordered field \(F\), there is a unique order-preserving field embedding \(\mathbb Q\hookrightarrow F\).

Proof. The field operations are inherited from cross-multiplication. In an ordered field \(F\), the image of \(n\in\mathbb N\) is \(1+\cdots+1\), and the images of integers and quotients are forced by additive and multiplicative inverses. Positivity is preserved because squares and sums of positive elements are positive.

Density and countability of rational points

The rationals are algebraically rich and order-dense, yet still too small for analysis.

Theorem. \(\mathbb Q\) is countable and order-dense: if \(a,b\in\mathbb R\) with \(a\lt b\), then some \(q\in\mathbb Q\) satisfies \(a\lt q\lt b\).

Proof. Countability follows by listing fractions \(m/n\) by increasing \(|m|+|n|\). For density, choose \(n\in\mathbb N\) with \(n(b-a)>1\) by the Archimedean property, and then choose \(m\in\mathbb Z\) with \(na\lt m\lt nb\). Then \(q=m/n\) lies strictly between \(a\) and \(b\).

The first gap

The rationals already support limits of many sequences, but not all Cauchy sequences converge rationally.

Example. The set \(\{q\in\mathbb Q:q^2\lt 2\}\) is nonempty and bounded above in \(\mathbb Q\), but has no rational least upper bound. This is the gap repaired by Dedekind cuts or Cauchy completion.
