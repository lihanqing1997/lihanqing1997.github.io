Inequalities are the working estimates of analysis. In \(L^p\) spaces they control products, averages, convolutions, interpolation, and tail probabilities.

Jensen inequality

Convexity moves through integration.

Theorem. If \(\mu\) is a probability measure, \(\phi\) is convex, and \(f,\phi(f)\in L^1\), then \(\phi(\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu)\le\int\phi(f)\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proof. For any point \(x_0\) in the interior of the domain of \(\phi\), convexity gives a supporting affine function \(\ell\) with \(\ell\le\phi\) and \(\ell(x_0)=\phi(x_0)\). Apply this with \(x_0=\int f\). Then \(\phi(x_0)=\ell(\int f)=\int \ell(f)\le\int\phi(f)\).

Holder and generalized Holder

Products are integrable when reciprocal exponents add to one.

Theorem. If \(p_i\in[1,\infty]\) and \(\sum_i1/p_i=1\), then \(\|\prod_i f_i\|_1\le\prod_i\|f_i\|_{p_i}\).

Proof. The two-factor case is Holder. The finite-factor case follows by induction: combine the first \(n-1\) factors into one function with exponent \(r\) satisfying \(1/r=\sum_{i\lt n}1/p_i\), then apply the two-factor inequality to that product and \(f_n\).

Interpolation

Intermediate norms are controlled by endpoint norms.

Theorem. If \(1\le p\lt r\lt q\le\infty\) and \(1/r=\theta/p+(1-\theta)/q\), then \(\|f\|_r\le\|f\|_p^\theta\|f\|_q^{1-\theta}\).

Proof. For \(q\lt \infty\), write \(|f|^r=|f|^{\theta r}|f|^{(1-\theta)r}\) and apply Holder with exponents \(p/(\theta r)\) and \(q/((1-\theta)r)\). The \(q=\infty\) case follows directly from \(\int |f|^r\le\|f\|_\infty^{r-p}\int|f|^p\).

Young convolution inequality

Convolution smooths while obeying norm bounds.

Theorem. On \(\mathbb R^d\), if \(1+1/r=1/p+1/q\), then \(\|f*g\|_r\le\|f\|_p\|g\|_q\) for the admissible Young range.

Proof. The endpoint \(\|f*g\|_\infty\le\|f\|_p\|g\|_q\) is Holder, and \(\|f*g\|_p\le\|f\|_p\|g\|_1\) follows from Minkowski's integral inequality. The general case follows by interpolation between these endpoints.

Markov and Chebyshev inequalities

Integral bounds imply tail bounds.

Proposition. If \(f\ge0\), then \(\mu(f>\lambda)\le\lambda^{-1}\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\). More generally, \(\mu(|X|>\lambda)\le\lambda^{-p}\|X\|_p^p\).

Proof. On the set \(\{f>\lambda\}\), one has \(f\ge\lambda\mathbf 1_{\{f>\lambda\}}\). Integrating gives Markov's inequality. Apply it to \(f=|X|^p\) for the \(L^p\) form.
