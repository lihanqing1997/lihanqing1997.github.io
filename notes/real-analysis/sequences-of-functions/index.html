9. Sequences of functions

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/riemann-integration/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/power-series-on-the-real-line/" %}

Sequences of functions introduce a new layer of limiting behavior: one can converge at each point, uniformly over a domain, in integral norm, or in measure. The theorem determines which mode is strong enough.

Pointwise and uniform convergence

Definition. A sequence \(f_n:X\to Y\) converges pointwise to \(f\) if \(f_n(x)\to f(x)\) for each fixed \(x\). It converges uniformly if \(\sup_{x\in X}d(f_n(x),f(x))\to0\). Uniform convergence controls all points with one tail estimate.

Uniform Cauchy criterion

Theorem. If \(Y\) is complete, then \(f_n\) converges uniformly to a function \(f:X\to Y\) if and only if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(N\) such that \(d(f_n(x),f_m(x))<\varepsilon\) for all \(x\) and all \(m,n\ge N\). This reduces uniform convergence to Cauchy estimates.

Uniform limit theorem

Theorem. A uniform limit of continuous functions is continuous. More generally, if \(f_n\) are continuous on a metric space and converge uniformly to \(f\), then preimages of open sets can be controlled through a three-term \(\varepsilon/3\) argument.

Interchanging limits and integrals

Proposition. If \(f_n\to f\) uniformly on \([a,b]\) and each \(f_n\) is Riemann integrable, then \(f\) is Riemann integrable and \(\int_a^b f_n\,\mathrm{d}x\to\int_a^b f\,\mathrm{d}x\). The proof bounds the integral of \(|f_n-f|\) by \((b-a)\|f_n-f\|_\infty\).

Interchanging limits and derivatives

Theorem. Suppose \(f_n\) are continuously differentiable on \([a,b]\), \(f_n(x_0)\) converges at one point, and \(f_n'\) converges uniformly to \(g\). Then \(f_n\) converges uniformly to a differentiable function \(f\), and \(f'=g\). Derivatives require stronger hypotheses than integrals.

Pointwise convergence can lose continuity

Counterexample. On \([0,1]\), the functions \(f_n(x)=x^n\) are continuous and converge pointwise to \(0\) for \(0\le x<1\) and to \(1\) at \(x=1\). The limit is discontinuous, so convergence cannot be uniform.
