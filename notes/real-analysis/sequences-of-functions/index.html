9. Sequences of functions

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/riemann-integration/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/power-series-on-the-real-line/" %}

Sequences of functions records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the real analysis note.

Definition

Definition. A sequence \(f_n\) converges pointwise to \(f\) if \(f_n(x)\to f(x)\) for each fixed \(x\). It converges uniformly if \(\sup_x |f_n(x)-f(x)|\to0\).

Uniform limit theorem

Theorem. Uniform limits of continuous functions are continuous, and uniform convergence permits interchange of limit and integration on compact intervals.

Proof

Proof. Given a point and an error tolerance, split \(|f(x)-f(y)|\) into three terms involving \(f_N\). Uniform convergence controls the outer terms, continuity of \(f_N\) controls the middle term.

Remark

Remark. Pointwise convergence preserves far less structure than uniform convergence. This is the first warning that the mode of convergence is part of the theorem.
