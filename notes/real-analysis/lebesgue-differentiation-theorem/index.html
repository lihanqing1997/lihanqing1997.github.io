36. Lebesgue differentiation theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/distribution-functions-and-lebesgue-stieltjes-measures/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/differentiation-of-measures-and-density-theorems/" %}

Lebesgue differentiation recovers an integrable function from its local averages almost everywhere. It is the rigorous foundation of density recovery.

Maximal function

Definition. For locally integrable \(f\) on \(\mathbb R^d\), the Hardy-Littlewood maximal function is \(Mf(x)=\sup_{r>0}|B(x,r)|^{-1}\int_{B(x,r)}|f(y)|\,\mathrm{d}y\).

Weak maximal inequality

Theorem. There is a constant \(C_d\) such that \(\lambda(Mf>\alpha)\le C_d\|f\|_1/\alpha\). Covering lemmas are the geometric heart of the proof.

Differentiation theorem

Theorem. If \(f\in L^1_{loc}(\mathbb R^d)\), then averages of \(f\) over balls shrinking to \(x\) converge to \(f(x)\) for almost every \(x\).

Lebesgue points

Definition. A point \(x\) is a Lebesgue point if the average of \(|f(y)-f(x)|\) over shrinking balls tends to zero. Almost every point is a Lebesgue point.

Density theorem

Corollary. For measurable \(A\), almost every point of \(A\) has density \(1\) in \(A\), and almost every point of its complement has density \(0\). Apply differentiation to \(\mathbf 1_A\).
