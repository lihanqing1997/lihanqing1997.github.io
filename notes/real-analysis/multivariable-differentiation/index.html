23. Multivariable differentiation and total derivatives

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/power-series-on-the-real-line/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/inverse-and-implicit-function-theorems/" %}

In several variables, differentiability means approximation by a linear map, not merely existence of partial derivatives. This distinction is central for asymptotic expansions.

Total derivative

Definition. For \(f:U\subset\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^m\), differentiability at \(x\) means \(f(x+h)=f(x)+Df(x)h+o(|h|)\). The derivative \(Df(x)\) is a linear map, represented by the Jacobian matrix in coordinates.

Continuous partials imply differentiability

Proposition. If all partial derivatives exist near \(x\) and are continuous at \(x\), then \(f\) is differentiable at \(x\). Existence of partial derivatives at a single point alone is not sufficient.

Chain rule

Theorem. If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x\) and \(g\) is differentiable at \(f(x)\), then \(D(g\circ f)(x)=Dg(f(x))Df(x)\). Jacobians multiply in the order of composition.

Hessian and Taylor formula

Theorem. For scalar twice differentiable \(f\), \(f(x+h)=f(x)+Df(x)h+\frac12 h^\top D^2f(x)h+o(|h|^2)\). This is the deterministic core of quadratic likelihood approximations.

Delta-method bridge

Remark. If an estimator has a first-order limit and \(g\) is differentiable at the target, the expansion of \(g\) transfers the limit through its derivative. Probability supplies the mode of convergence for the remainder.
