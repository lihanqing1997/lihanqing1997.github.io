23. Multivariable differentiation and total derivatives

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/power-series-on-the-real-line/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/inverse-and-implicit-function-theorems/" %}

In several variables, differentiability means approximation by a linear map, not merely the existence of partial derivatives.

Frechet derivative

The derivative is the linear map that captures first-order behavior.

Definition. A map \(f:U\subset\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^m\) is differentiable at \(x\) if there is a linear map \(Df(x)\) such that \[ f(x+h)=f(x)+Df(x)h+o(\|h\|). \]

Proposition. If \(f\) is differentiable at \(x\), then all directional derivatives exist and equal \(Df(x)v\).

Proof. Substitute \(h=tv\) into the Frechet expansion and divide by \(t\). The error \(o(|t|\|v\|)/t\) tends to zero as \(t\to0\).

Partial derivatives are not enough

Partial derivatives see only coordinate directions.

Remark. Existence of all partial derivatives at a point does not imply differentiability. A common sufficient condition is existence of partial derivatives in a neighborhood together with continuity of those partial derivatives at the point.

Theorem. If all first partial derivatives of \(f\) exist in a neighborhood of \(x\) and are continuous at \(x\), then \(f\) is differentiable at \(x\).

Proof. Write \(f(x+h)-f(x)\) as a telescoping sum changing one coordinate at a time. The one-dimensional mean value theorem expresses each difference using the corresponding partial derivative at an intermediate point. Continuity of the partial derivatives at \(x\) turns the sum into \(\sum_j \partial_j f(x)h_j+o(\|h\|)\).

Chain rule

Linear approximations compose by matrix multiplication.

Theorem. If \(g\) is differentiable at \(x\) and \(f\) is differentiable at \(g(x)\), then \(f\circ g\) is differentiable at \(x\) and \(D(f\circ g)(x)=Df(g(x))Dg(x)\).

Proof. Use \(g(x+h)=g(x)+Dg(x)h+r(h)\), where \(\|r(h)\|=o(\|h\|)\), and apply the expansion of \(f\) at \(g(x)\). Since \(\|Dg(x)h+r(h)\|=O(\|h\|)\), the remainder from \(f\) is \(o(\|h\|)\).

Mean value inequality

Derivative bounds imply global Lipschitz bounds on convex domains.

Proposition. If \(U\) is convex and \(\|Df(z)\|\le M\) for all \(z\in U\), then \(\|f(x)-f(y)\|\le M\|x-y\|\) for \(x,y\in U\).

Proof. Define \(\gamma(t)=f(y+t(x-y))\). Then \(\gamma'(t)=Df(y+t(x-y))(x-y)\), so \(\|\gamma'(t)\|\le M\|x-y\|\). Integrating \(\gamma'\) from \(0\) to \(1\) gives the estimate.

Second-order expansion

Hessians encode local curvature.

Theorem. If \(f\in C^2\) near \(x\), then \[ f(x+h)=f(x)+\nabla f(x)^\top h+\frac12 h^\top \nabla^2 f(x)h+o(\|h\|^2). \]

Proof. Apply the one-dimensional Taylor theorem to \(\phi(t)=f(x+th)\) at \(t=0\). Then \(\phi'(0)=\nabla f(x)^\top h\) and \(\phi''(0)=h^\top\nabla^2 f(x)h\), while continuity of the Hessian gives the Peano remainder.
