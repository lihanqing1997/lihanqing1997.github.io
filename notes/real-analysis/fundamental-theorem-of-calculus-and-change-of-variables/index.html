21. Fundamental theorem of calculus and change of variables

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/absolute-continuity-and-bounded-variation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/power-series-on-the-real-line/" %}

The fundamental theorem identifies differentiation and integration as inverse operations, provided the correct regularity is used. In modern analysis, absolute continuity is the natural hypothesis.

Integral functions are absolutely continuous

Integrating an \(L^1\) function produces an absolutely continuous primitive.

Theorem. If \(g\in L^1([a,b])\) and \(F(x)=\int_a^x g(t)\,\mathrm{d}t\), then \(F\) is absolutely continuous and \(F'=g\) almost everywhere.

Proof. Absolute continuity follows because \(\int_E |g|\,\mathrm{d}t\) is small when \(m(E)\) is small. For the derivative, the difference quotient is the average of \(g\) over shrinking intervals; the Lebesgue differentiation theorem says these averages converge to \(g(x)\) for almost every \(x\).

Recovering the function from its derivative

Absolute continuity is the exact condition needed for the derivative to reconstruct the function.

Theorem. If \(F\) is absolutely continuous on \([a,b]\), then \(F'\in L^1\) and \[ F(x)-F(a)=\int_a^x F'(t)\,\mathrm{d}t. \]

Proof. Absolute continuity gives a signed measure \(\nu((s,t])=F(t)-F(s)\) absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure. By Radon-Nikodym, \(\nu(E)=\int_E h\,\mathrm{d}t\) for some \(h\in L^1\). Differentiating interval averages shows \(h=F'\) almost everywhere.

Classical substitution

Change of variables transforms integrals by the local stretching factor.

Theorem. If \(\phi:[\alpha,\beta]\to[a,b]\) is \(C^1\) and \(f\) is continuous, then \[ \int_{\phi(\alpha)}^{\phi(\beta)} f(u)\,\mathrm{d}u=\int_\alpha^\beta f(\phi(t))\phi'(t)\,\mathrm{d}t \] when \(\phi\) is monotone, with orientation included.

Proof. Let \(F(x)=\int_{\phi(\alpha)}^x f(u)\,\mathrm{d}u\). Then \(F'=f\), and by the chain rule \((F\circ\phi)'=f(\phi(t))\phi'(t)\). Integrating from \(\alpha\) to \(\beta\) gives the identity.

Lebesgue change of variables in one dimension

Monotone absolutely continuous maps have a measure-theoretic substitution formula.

Proposition. If \(\phi\) is increasing and absolutely continuous, then for nonnegative measurable \(f\), \[ \int_{\phi(a)}^{\phi(b)} f(y)N(y)\,\mathrm{d}y=\int_a^b f(\phi(x))\phi'(x)\,\mathrm{d}x, \] where \(N(y)\) counts preimages when needed; for strictly increasing \(\phi\), \(N=1\) on the image.

Proof. First prove the identity for indicators of intervals using absolute continuity of \(\phi\). Extend to simple functions by linearity, then to nonnegative measurable functions by monotone convergence.

Where formulas fail

Without absolute continuity, derivatives may miss singular variation.

Remark. The Cantor function has derivative zero almost everywhere but is not constant. Thus the formula \(F(b)-F(a)=\int_a^bF'\) requires absolute continuity, not merely differentiability almost everywhere.
