21. Fundamental theorem of calculus and change of variables

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/absolute-continuity-and-bounded-variation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/power-series-on-the-real-line/" %}

The fundamental theorem identifies integration with antidifferentiation under the correct hypotheses. Change of variables explains how integrals transform under smooth maps.

Indefinite Lebesgue integrals

Theorem. If \(g\in L^1[a,b]\) and \(F(x)=\int_a^x g(t)\,\mathrm{d}t\), then \(F\) is absolutely continuous and \(F^\prime=g\) almost everywhere.

Recovering increments

Theorem. If \(F\) is absolutely continuous, then \(F(b)-F(a)=\int_a^b F^\prime(x)\,\mathrm{d}x\). Bounded variation alone is not enough because singular continuous parts may have zero derivative a.e.

One-dimensional substitution

Theorem. For a continuously differentiable one-to-one map \(\phi\), \(\int f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=\int f(\phi(t))|\phi^\prime(t)|\,\mathrm{d}t\) under the usual nonnegative or integrable hypotheses.

Densities under transformations

Example. If \(Y=\phi(X)\) and \(\phi\) is increasing and differentiable, then \(f_Y(y)=f_X(\phi^{-1}(y))/\phi^\prime(\phi^{-1}(y))\) where densities exist.

Differentiation under the integral

Remark. Interchanging derivatives and integrals requires domination or uniform integrability. In likelihood theory, score identities are analytic consequences of justified differentiation under the integral sign.
