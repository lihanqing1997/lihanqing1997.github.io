22. Power series on the real line

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/fundamental-theorem-of-calculus-and-change-of-variables/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/multivariable-differentiation/" %}

Power series define functions from infinite coefficient sequences. Their convergence is local, but inside the radius of convergence they behave like polynomials.

Radius of convergence

Theorem. For \(\sum a_n(x-c)^n\) there is \(R\in[0,\infty]\) such that the series converges absolutely for \(|x-c|R\). The Cauchy-Hadamard formula is \(1/R=\limsup |a_n|^{1/n}\).

Compact-uniform convergence

Proposition. Inside the radius, convergence is uniform on every compact subinterval. This follows from the Weierstrass test after choosing \(r

Termwise operations

Theorem. Power series may be differentiated and integrated term by term inside their radius of convergence, and the resulting series have the same radius.

Analytic versus smooth

Counterexample. A smooth function need not equal its Taylor series. The standard function \(e^{-1/x^2}\) for positive \(x\), extended by zero at the origin, is smooth but flat at \(0\).

Moment generating functions

Example. If \(M(t)=\mathbb E e^{tX}\) is finite near \(0\), then its derivatives at zero recover moments. The neighborhood condition supplies the domination needed for termwise differentiation.
