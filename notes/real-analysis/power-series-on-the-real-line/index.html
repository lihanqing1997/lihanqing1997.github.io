Power series are functions defined by infinite algebraic data. Inside their radius of convergence they behave like polynomials: continuous, differentiable, and integrable term by term.

Radius of convergence

The convergence set of a power series is controlled by one number.

Theorem. For \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-c)^n\), there is \(R\in[0,\infty]\) such that the series converges absolutely for \(|x-c|\lt R\) and diverges for \(|x-c|>R\). Moreover \(1/R=\limsup |a_n|^{1/n}\).

Proof. Apply the root test to terms \(a_n(x-c)^n\). The limsup of the \(n\)-th roots is \(|x-c|\limsup |a_n|^{1/n}\). It is below \(1\) inside the radius and above \(1\) outside.

Uniform convergence on compact subintervals

Inside the radius, convergence is locally uniform.

Proposition. If \(0\lt r\lt R\), then \(\sum a_n(x-c)^n\) converges uniformly and absolutely on \(|x-c|\le r\).

Proof. Choose \(\rho\) with \(r\lt \rho\lt R\). Since \(\sum |a_n|\rho^n\) converges, \(|a_n(x-c)^n|\le |a_n|r^n\le |a_n|\rho^n\) on \(|x-c|\le r\). The Weierstrass test gives uniform absolute convergence.

Termwise differentiation

The derivative series has the same radius of convergence.

Theorem. Inside \(|x-c|\lt R\), the sum \(f(x)=\sum a_n(x-c)^n\) is differentiable and \[ f'(x)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty n a_n(x-c)^{n-1}. \]

Proof. The differentiated series has the same radius because \(\lim n^{1/n}=1\). On every compact subinterval inside the radius, the derivative series converges uniformly, and the original series converges at \(c\). The theorem on differentiating uniformly convergent derivative series applies.

Termwise integration

Power series can be integrated coefficient by coefficient.

Proposition. For \(|x-c|\lt R\), \[ \int_c^x\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(t-c)^n\,\mathrm{d}t =\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{a_n}{n+1}(x-c)^{n+1}. \]

Proof. On the interval between \(c\) and \(x\), choose a compact subinterval strictly inside the radius. Uniform convergence permits interchanging the Riemann or Lebesgue integral with the infinite sum.

Identity principle on intervals

Coefficients are uniquely determined by the local function.

Theorem. If a power series centered at \(c\) vanishes on a nonempty open interval inside its radius, then every coefficient is zero.

Proof. Since the sum is infinitely differentiable and derivatives are obtained termwise, \(a_n=f^{(n)}(c)/n!\). If the function vanishes on a neighborhood of \(c\), all derivatives at \(c\) vanish, hence all coefficients vanish. If the open interval does not contain \(c\), recenter at a point in the interval and continue by overlapping intervals.
