10. Power series on the real line

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/sequences-of-functions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/sigma-algebras-and-measures/" %}

Power series are series of functions with unusually strong convergence and differentiability properties inside their interval of convergence. They are the real-analysis entrance to analytic functions.

Radius of convergence

Definition. A power series centered at \(c\) has the form \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-c)^n\). Its radius of convergence is \(R=1/\limsup |a_n|^{1/n}\), with the usual conventions. The series converges absolutely for \(|x-c|R\).

Termwise differentiation and integration

Theorem. Inside \((c-R,c+R)\), a power series may be differentiated and integrated term by term: \[ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-c)^n=\sum_{n=1}^\infty n a_n(x-c)^{n-1}. \] The differentiated and integrated series have the same radius of convergence as the original series.

Analytic identity principle on intervals

Proposition. If a power series centered at \(c\) is zero on a nontrivial interval around \(c\), then all its coefficients are zero. Repeated differentiation at \(c\) gives \(a_n=f^{(n)}(c)/n!\). Thus a power series is determined by its values on any interval with an accumulation point inside the radius.

Endpoint behavior

Example. The series \(\sum x^n\) has radius \(1\), diverges at \(x=1\), and converges at no endpoint beyond \(-1\) except conditionally for related examples. The series \(\sum x^n/n\) has radius \(1\), diverges at \(1\), and converges at \(-1\). Endpoint behavior is not determined by the radius alone.

Smooth is not always analytic

Remark. Power series define smooth functions, but not every smooth function is represented by its Taylor series. The function \(e^{-1/x^2}\) extended by \(0\) at \(0\) is the standard warning that \(C^\infty\) and analytic are different notions.
