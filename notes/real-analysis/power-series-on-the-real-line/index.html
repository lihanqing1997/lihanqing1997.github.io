10. Power series on the real line

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/sequences-of-functions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/sigma-algebras-and-measures/" %}

Power series on the real line records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the real analysis note.

Definition

Definition. A power series has the form \(\sum_{n\ge0} a_n(x-c)^n\). Its radius of convergence separates points where the series converges absolutely from points where it diverges.

Termwise differentiation theorem

Theorem. Inside its radius of convergence, a power series may be differentiated and integrated term-by-term, and the resulting series have the same radius of convergence.

Proof

Proof. Uniform convergence on compact subintervals inside the radius follows from comparison with a geometric series. Termwise operations are then justified by uniform convergence.

Remark

Remark. Power series are the real-analysis entrance to analytic functions. Complex analysis later reveals why their behavior is even more rigid over \(\mathbb C\).
