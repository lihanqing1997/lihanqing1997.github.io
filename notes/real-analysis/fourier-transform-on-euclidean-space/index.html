47. Fourier transform on Euclidean space

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/fourier-series-and-orthogonality/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/weak-convergence-of-measures/" %}

The Fourier transform decomposes functions on \(\mathbb R^d\) into frequencies. It turns translation into modulation, convolution into multiplication, and differentiation into polynomial weights.

Fourier transform on \(L^1\)

Integrable functions have bounded continuous transforms.

Definition. For \(f\in L^1(\mathbb R^d)\), define \(\widehat f(\xi)=\int_{\mathbb R^d}e^{-ix\cdot\xi}f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x\).

Proposition. \(\widehat f\) is bounded and uniformly continuous, with \(\|\widehat f\|_\infty\le\|f\|_1\).

Proof. The bound is immediate from \(|e^{-ix\cdot\xi}|=1\). For continuity, \(\widehat f(\xi+h)-\widehat f(\xi)=\int e^{-ix\cdot\xi}(e^{-ix\cdot h}-1)f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x\), and dominated convergence applies.

Convolution theorem

Fourier transform turns convolution into multiplication.

Theorem. If \(f,g\in L^1(\mathbb R^d)\), then \(\widehat{f*g}=\widehat f\,\widehat g\).

Proof. Use Fubini: \[ \widehat{f*g}(\xi)=\int e^{-ix\cdot\xi}\int f(x-y)g(y)\,\mathrm{d}y\,\mathrm{d}x. \] Set \(u=x-y\). The integral factors into \(\int e^{-iu\cdot\xi}f(u)\,\mathrm{d}u\int e^{-iy\cdot\xi}g(y)\,\mathrm{d}y\).

Inversion on Schwartz functions

For rapidly decreasing smooth functions, inversion is exact.

Theorem. If \(f\) is a Schwartz function, then \[ f(x)=(2\pi)^{-d}\int_{\mathbb R^d}e^{ix\cdot\xi}\widehat f(\xi)\,\mathrm{d}\xi. \]

Proof. Insert a Gaussian cutoff \(e^{-\varepsilon|\xi|^2}\), whose inverse transform is an approximate identity. The regularized inverse equals convolution of \(f\) with a Gaussian kernel. As \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\), the approximate identity converges to \(f\) uniformly for Schwartz \(f\).

Plancherel theorem

Fourier transform extends from \(L^1\cap L^2\) to a unitary operator on \(L^2\).

Theorem. With the standard normalization, \(\|\widehat f\|_2=(2\pi)^{d/2}\|f\|_2\) for \(f\in L^2\), after defining the transform by density.

Proof. Prove the identity first for Schwartz functions using inversion and Fubini. Since Schwartz functions are dense in \(L^2\), the transform extends uniquely by continuity to all \(L^2\). The norm identity passes to the limit.

Differentiation and decay

Smoothness in one domain corresponds to decay in the other.

Proposition. For Schwartz \(f\), \(\widehat{\partial^\alpha f}(\xi)=(i\xi)^\alpha\widehat f(\xi)\) and \(\widehat{x^\alpha f}=i^{|\alpha|}\partial^\alpha_\xi\widehat f\), up to the chosen sign convention.

Proof. The derivative identity follows by integration by parts, with boundary terms vanishing because \(f\) is rapidly decreasing. The multiplication identity follows by differentiating under the integral sign with respect to \(\xi\).
