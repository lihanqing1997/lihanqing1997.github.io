47. Fourier transform on Euclidean space

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/fourier-series-and-orthogonality/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/weak-convergence-of-measures/" %}

The Fourier transform decomposes functions on Euclidean space into frequencies. It turns convolution into multiplication and differentiation into polynomial weights.

Fourier transform on L1

Definition. For \(f\in L^1(\mathbb R^d)\), define \(\hat f(\xi)=\int e^{-ix\cdot\xi}f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x\). Then \(\hat f\) is bounded and uniformly continuous.

Inversion

Theorem. When \(f\) and \(\hat f\) are integrable, \(f(x)=(2\pi)^{-d}\int e^{ix\cdot\xi}\hat f(\xi)\,\mathrm{d}\xi\) at continuity points. General inversion follows by approximation.

Convolution theorem

Proposition. Under suitable assumptions, \(\widehat{f*g}=\hat f\hat g\). This is why convolution operators become multipliers in frequency space.

Plancherel theorem

Theorem. The Fourier transform extends to a unitary operator on \(L^2\) after normalization. Thus frequency analysis preserves Hilbert-space energy.

Characteristic functions

Example. For a probability law, \(\varphi_X(t)=\mathbb E e^{itX}\) is its Fourier transform. Products of characteristic functions correspond to sums of independent variables.
