Differentiation of measures studies local ratios of measures on shrinking balls. It generalizes differentiation of functions and is a key bridge to geometry and statistics.

Measure ratios

Local densities compare a measure to a reference measure at small scales.

Definition. For Radon measures \(\nu,\mu\) on \(\mathbb R^d\), a derivative of \(\nu\) with respect to \(\mu\) at \(x\) is a limit, when it exists, \[ \lim_{r\downarrow0}\frac{\nu(B(x,r))}{\mu(B(x,r))}. \]

Remark. When \(\mu\) is Lebesgue measure and \(\nu=f\mu\), this derivative is \(f(x)\) at Lebesgue points of \(f\).

Vitali covering lemma

Covering lemmas make local estimates global.

Lemma. From a Vitali family of balls covering a set \(E\subset\mathbb R^d\), one can choose a disjoint countable subfamily whose union covers \(E\) up to a null set.

Proof. Choose balls greedily by scales: at each stage take a maximal disjoint subcollection among balls with radii in a dyadic range. Disjointness is built in, and any omitted ball intersects a selected ball of comparable or larger radius, so a fixed enlargement of selected balls covers the original family. Letting scales shrink gives coverage up to null sets.

Besicovitch differentiation for Radon measures

Radon-Nikodym derivatives can be recovered from ball ratios.

Theorem. If \(\nu\ll\mu\) are Radon measures on \(\mathbb R^d\) and \(f=\mathrm{d}\nu/\mathrm{d}\mu\), then \[ \frac{\nu(B(x,r))}{\mu(B(x,r))}\to f(x) \] for \(\mu\)-almost every \(x\) with \(\mu(B(x,r))>0\).

Proof. Write \(\nu=f\mu\). The ratio is the \(\mu\)-average of \(f\) over the ball. The differentiation theorem for Radon measures, proved with the Vitali or Besicovitch covering theorem and maximal inequalities, says these averages converge to \(f\) at \(\mu\)-Lebesgue points.

Density of measurable sets

A measurable set is locally almost all or almost nothing at almost every point.

Theorem. For measurable \(E\subset\mathbb R^d\), almost every \(x\in E\) has density \(1\) and almost every \(x\notin E\) has density \(0\).

Proof. Apply the differentiation theorem to \(\mathbf 1_E\). The averages over balls are exactly the relative densities \(m(E\cap B(x,r))/m(B(x,r))\), and they converge almost everywhere to \(\mathbf 1_E(x)\).

Singular measures

Singular measures have infinite or zero local ratios on the wrong reference scale.

Proposition. If \(\nu\perp\mu\), then the absolutely continuous density of \(\nu\) with respect to \(\mu\) is zero \(\mu\)-almost everywhere, and local \(\nu/\mu\) ratios cannot represent a finite positive \(\mu\)-density.

Proof. Singularity gives a set \(N\) with \(\mu(N)=0\) and \(\nu(X\setminus N)=0\). The Radon-Nikodym derivative of the absolutely continuous part of \(\nu\) relative to \(\mu\) is therefore zero. Any finite positive density would define a nonzero absolutely continuous component, contradicting singularity.
