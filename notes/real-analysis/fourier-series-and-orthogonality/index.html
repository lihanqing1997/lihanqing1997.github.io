25. Fourier series and orthogonality

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/lebesgue-differentiation-theorem/" %}

Fourier series express periodic functions through orthogonal trigonometric modes. In real analysis, the cleanest foundation is the Hilbert-space geometry of \(L^2\).

Trigonometric system

Definition. On \([-\pi,\pi]\), the complex exponentials \(e_n(x)=e^{inx}\), \(n\in\mathbb Z\), form an orthogonal system in \(L^2\) with \(\langle f,g\rangle=(2\pi)^{-1}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\overline{g(x)}\,\mathrm{d}x\).

Fourier coefficients

Definition. For \(f\in L^1([-\pi,\pi])\), define \(\widehat f(n)=(2\pi)^{-1}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)e^{-inx}\,\mathrm{d}x\). The formal Fourier series is \(\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}\widehat f(n)e^{inx}\).

Bessel inequality and Parseval identity

Theorem. If \(f\in L^2\), then \(\sum_{n\in\mathbb Z}|\widehat f(n)|^2\le \|f\|_2^2\). Completeness of the trigonometric system gives Parseval's identity: \(\sum_n|\widehat f(n)|^2=\|f\|_2^2\).

\(L^2\) convergence of Fourier series

Theorem. For every \(f\in L^2([-\pi,\pi])\), the symmetric Fourier partial sums converge to \(f\) in \(L^2\). This follows because trigonometric polynomials are dense in \(L^2\), and partial sums are orthogonal projections onto finite-dimensional subspaces.

Dirichlet kernel and pointwise convergence

Proposition. The \(N\)-th partial sum can be written as convolution with the Dirichlet kernel \(D_N\). Pointwise convergence is subtler than \(L^2\) convergence because \(\|D_N\|_1\) grows. Additional smoothness or bounded variation gives pointwise convergence to the midpoint of one-sided limits.

Orthogonality viewpoint

Remark. Fourier analysis is linear algebra in infinite dimension: coefficients are inner products, partial sums are orthogonal projections, and Parseval is Pythagoras. The main new issue is convergence, because infinite-dimensional bases require topology.
