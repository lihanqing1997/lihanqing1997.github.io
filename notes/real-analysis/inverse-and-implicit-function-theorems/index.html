24. Inverse and implicit function theorems

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/multivariable-differentiation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/sigma-algebras-and-measures/" %}

The inverse and implicit function theorems say that a nonlinear map with invertible linearization behaves locally like that linearization.

Local linear invertibility

The key hypothesis is invertibility of the derivative at the base point.

Definition. A \(C^1\) map \(F:U\subset\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^d\) has nonsingular derivative at \(x_0\) if the matrix \(DF(x_0)\) is invertible.

Lemma. If \(DF(x_0)\) is invertible and \(DF\) is continuous, then \(DF(x)\) remains invertible for all \(x\) in a sufficiently small neighborhood of \(x_0\).

Proof. Write \(DF(x)=DF(x_0)(I+DF(x_0)^{-1}(DF(x)-DF(x_0)))\). For \(x\) close enough, the norm of the perturbation is below \(1\), so the inverse exists by the Neumann series.

Inverse function theorem

A \(C^1\) map with invertible derivative is locally a \(C^1\) coordinate change.

Theorem. If \(F\in C^1\) near \(x_0\) and \(DF(x_0)\) is invertible, then there are neighborhoods \(U_0\) of \(x_0\) and \(V_0\) of \(F(x_0)\) such that \(F:U_0\to V_0\) is bijective and \(F^{-1}\in C^1\).

Proof. After translating and applying the linear inverse, reduce to \(F(x)=x+R(x)\) with \(DR(0)\) small. For \(y\) near \(0\), solve \(x=y-R(x)\) by the contraction mapping theorem on a small ball. This gives existence and uniqueness. Differentiability of the inverse follows from the identity \(F^{-1}(F(x+h))-F^{-1}(F(x))=h\) and the expansion of \(F\), yielding \(D(F^{-1})(F(x))=DF(x)^{-1}\).

Implicit function theorem

Equations can be solved locally for variables whose derivative block is invertible.

Theorem. Let \(G:\mathbb R^p\times\mathbb R^q\to\mathbb R^q\) be \(C^1\), \(G(a,b)=0\), and \(D_yG(a,b)\) invertible. Then locally the equation \(G(x,y)=0\) is equivalent to \(y=\varphi(x)\) for a \(C^1\) function \(\varphi\).

Proof. Define \(F(x,y)=(x,G(x,y))\). Its derivative with respect to \((x,y)\) is block triangular with invertible diagonal blocks \(I\) and \(D_yG(a,b)\), hence invertible. Apply the inverse function theorem to \(F\). The inverse has form \((u,v)\mapsto (u,\psi(u,v))\), and setting \(v=0\) gives \(\varphi(u)=\psi(u,0)\).

Derivative of the implicit map

Differentiating the constraint gives the sensitivity formula.

Corollary. Under the implicit function theorem hypotheses, \[ D\varphi(x)=-[D_yG(x,\varphi(x))]^{-1}D_xG(x,\varphi(x)). \]

Proof. Differentiate the identity \(G(x,\varphi(x))=0\). The chain rule gives \(D_xG+D_yG\,D\varphi=0\). Since \(D_yG\) is invertible, solve for \(D\varphi\).

Use in local asymptotics

Many estimators are defined implicitly by first-order conditions.

Remark. The implicit function theorem is the deterministic mechanism behind differentiability of argmin and estimating-equation maps. It explains why a nonsingular Hessian or information matrix yields stable local solutions.
