24. Inverse and implicit function theorems

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/multivariable-differentiation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/sigma-algebras-and-measures/" %}

The inverse and implicit function theorems are local solvability theorems. A nonsingular derivative makes a nonlinear problem behave locally like a linear one.

Inverse function theorem

Theorem. If \(F:U\to\mathbb R^d\) is continuously differentiable and \(DF(x_0)\) is invertible, then \(F\) is a differentiable bijection between neighborhoods of \(x_0\) and \(F(x_0)\). The inverse derivative is \([DF(F^{-1}(y))]^{-1}\).

Implicit function theorem

Theorem. If \(F(a,b)=0\) and \(D_yF(a,b)\) is invertible, then near \((a,b)\) the equation \(F(x,y)=0\) has a unique solution \(y=g(x)\). Moreover \(Dg=-[D_yF]^{-1}D_xF\).

Contraction proof idea

Proof. After linear normalization, solving the nonlinear equation becomes a fixed-point problem. Continuity of the derivative makes the remainder small enough for the contraction mapping theorem on a small ball.

Regular level sets

Corollary. If \(DF\) has full rank on \(F^{-1}(c)\), then the level set is locally the graph of a differentiable map. This is the analytic beginning of submanifold theory.

Statistical reparameterization

Remark. Invertible Jacobians preserve local regularity of a statistical model. Singular Jacobians signal constraints, nonidentifiability, or nonregular asymptotics.
