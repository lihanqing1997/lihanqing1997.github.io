3. Series and absolute convergence

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/sequences-and-subsequences/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/metric-spaces-and-compactness/" %}

A series is a sequence of partial sums. The main questions are when the partial sums converge, whether convergence survives rearrangement, and how quantitative tests detect convergence.

Series and partial sums

Definition. For a sequence \((a_n)\), the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) means the limit of partial sums \(s_N=\sum_{n=1}^N a_n\), if that limit exists. A necessary condition is \(a_n\to0\), but this condition is far from sufficient.

Cauchy criterion for series

Theorem. The series \(\sum a_n\) converges in \(\mathbb R\) or \(\mathbb C\) if and only if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(N\) such that \(\left|\sum_{n=p}^q a_n\right|<\varepsilon\) whenever \(q\ge p\ge N\). This is simply the Cauchy criterion applied to the partial sums.

Absolute convergence

Proposition. If \(\sum |a_n|\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) converges. The proof is the triangle inequality applied to tails: \(\left|\sum_{n=p}^q a_n\right|\le\sum_{n=p}^q |a_n|\). Absolute convergence is therefore a robust form of convergence.

Comparison, ratio, and root tests

Theorem. If \(0\le a_n\le b_n\) eventually and \(\sum b_n\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) converges. If \(\limsup |a_n|^{1/n}<1\), the series converges absolutely; if \(\limsup |a_n|^{1/n}>1\), it diverges. The ratio test follows as a convenient sufficient condition from eventual geometric domination.

Alternating and conditional convergence

Theorem. If \(a_n\) decreases to \(0\), then \(\sum (-1)^{n-1}a_n\) converges, and the error after \(N\) terms has magnitude at most \(a_{N+1}\). Such convergence may be conditional: \(\sum (-1)^{n-1}/n\) converges but \(\sum 1/n\) diverges.

Rearrangement theorem

Remark. Absolutely convergent series can be rearranged without changing their sum. Conditionally convergent real series are unstable: by the Riemann rearrangement theorem, their terms can be rearranged to converge to any prescribed real number or to diverge. Conditional convergence is therefore order-sensitive.
