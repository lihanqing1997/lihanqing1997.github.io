3. Series and absolute convergence

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/sequences-and-subsequences/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/metric-spaces-and-compactness/" %}

Series and absolute convergence records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the real analysis note.

Definition

Definition. A series \(\sum a_n\) is the limit of its partial sums. Absolute convergence means \(\sum |a_n|\) converges; conditional convergence means convergence without absolute convergence.

Absolute convergence criterion

Theorem. Absolute convergence implies convergence in \(\mathbb R\) or \(\mathbb C\). Rearrangements preserve the sum under absolute convergence but can change sums under conditional convergence.

Proof

Proof. Cauchy criteria compare tails. Absolute convergence controls every tail by a positive convergent tail, while conditional rearrangements exploit separated positive and negative parts.

Remark

Remark. Series are a laboratory for convergence modes: pointwise, uniform, absolute, conditional, summability, and later integration.
