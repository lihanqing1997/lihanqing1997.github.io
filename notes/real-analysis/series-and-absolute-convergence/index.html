7. Series, absolute convergence, and rearrangements

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/sequences-and-subsequences/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/metric-spaces-and-compactness/" %}

A series is a sequence of partial sums. Absolute convergence is the condition that makes infinite addition stable under rearrangement, comparison, and many limiting operations.

Partial sums and convergence

Definition. The series \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n\) converges if \(s_N=\sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) converges. It converges absolutely if \(\sum |a_n|\) converges.

Cauchy criterion for series

Theorem. A scalar series converges if and only if every tail \(\sum_{k=m}^n a_k\) becomes arbitrarily small uniformly over \(n\ge m\) large. This criterion is often easier to verify than identifying the sum.

Comparison, ratio, and root tests

Proposition. Positive series are controlled by comparison. The ratio test uses \(\limsup |a_{n+1}/a_n|<1\), while the root test uses \(\limsup |a_n|^{1/n}<1\). Power series are naturally governed by the root test.

Conditional convergence

Example. The alternating harmonic series converges, but \(\sum 1/n\) diverges. Conditional convergence is unstable: rearranging terms can change the sum, unlike absolutely convergent series.

Unconditional convergence

Theorem. Every absolutely convergent scalar series is unconditionally convergent, meaning all rearrangements converge to the same value. In infinite-dimensional spaces, unconditional and absolute convergence separate, which leads toward functional analysis.
