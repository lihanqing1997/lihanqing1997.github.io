Infinite series are limits of partial sums. Their convergence is governed by Cauchy estimates, comparison, cancellation, and the distinction between absolute and conditional convergence.

Partial sums and the Cauchy criterion

A series converges precisely when its tails become small.

Definition. The series \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_n\) converges to \(s\) if the partial sums \(s_N=\sum_{n=1}^N a_n\) converge to \(s\).

Theorem. \(\sum a_n\) converges if and only if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(N\) such that \(m>n\ge N\Rightarrow |\sum_{k=n+1}^m a_k|\lt \varepsilon\).

Proof. This is exactly the Cauchy criterion for the sequence of partial sums, since \(s_m-s_n=\sum_{k=n+1}^m a_k\).

Absolute convergence and comparison

Absolute convergence removes dependence on cancellation.

Theorem. If \(\sum |a_n|\lt \infty\), then \(\sum a_n\) converges. If \(|a_n|\le b_n\) eventually and \(\sum b_n\lt \infty\), then \(\sum a_n\) converges absolutely.

Proof. For tails, \(|\sum_{k=n+1}^m a_k|\le\sum_{k=n+1}^m |a_k|\). The right side tends to zero by convergence of the nonnegative series. The comparison statement follows by applying this to \(\sum |a_n|\).

Root and ratio tests

Exponential rates decide many series.

Proposition. If \(\limsup |a_n|^{1/n}\lt 1\), then \(\sum a_n\) converges absolutely. If \(\liminf |a_n|^{1/n}>1\), then \(a_n\not\to0\) and the series diverges.

Proof. In the first case choose \(r\lt 1\) such that \(|a_n|^{1/n}\le r\) eventually. Then \(|a_n|\le r^n\), and comparison with a geometric series applies. In the second case \(|a_n|\) is eventually bounded below by \(r^n\) for some \(r>1\), so the terms cannot tend to zero.

Alternating and Dirichlet tests

Controlled oscillation can replace absolute summability.

Theorem. If \(b_n\downarrow0\), then \(\sum (-1)^n b_n\) converges. More generally, if partial sums of \(a_n\) are bounded and \(b_n\downarrow0\), then \(\sum a_nb_n\) converges.

Proof. The alternating test follows because even and odd partial sums are monotone and squeeze each other. For Dirichlet, summation by parts gives \(\sum_{k=m}^n a_kb_k=A_nb_n-A_{m-1}b_m+\sum_{k=m}^{n-1}A_k(b_k-b_{k+1})\), where \(A_k=\sum_{j=m}^k a_j\). Boundedness of \(A_k\) and \(b_k\to0\) make the tail small.

Rearrangements

Conditional convergence is fragile because positive and negative masses are separately infinite.

Theorem. An absolutely convergent series has the same sum under every rearrangement.

Proof. Given \(\varepsilon>0\), choose \(N\) so that \(\sum_{n>N}|a_n|\lt \varepsilon\). Any rearranged partial sum containing all terms \(a_1,\ldots,a_N\) differs from the original sum only by a subseries of the absolute tail, hence by at most \(\varepsilon\).
