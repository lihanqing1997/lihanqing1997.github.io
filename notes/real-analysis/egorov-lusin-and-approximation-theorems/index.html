43. Egorov, Lusin, and measurable approximation

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/lp-duality-and-weak-convergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/approximation-by-smooth-and-simple-functions/" %}

Egorov and Lusin show that measurable behavior is nearly uniform or nearly continuous after removing a small set. They are approximation theorems for rough objects.

Egorov theorem

Theorem. On a finite measure space, almost-everywhere convergence implies almost uniform convergence. Thus pointwise convergence can be made uniform outside a set of arbitrarily small measure.

Lusin theorem

Theorem. A finite a.e. measurable function on a finite-measure subset of \(\mathbb R^d\) is continuous on a compact set whose complement has arbitrarily small measure.

Simple approximation

Proposition. Measurable functions can be approximated pointwise by simple functions, and integrable functions can be approximated in \(L^1\) by integrable simple functions.

Continuous approximation

Proposition. For Radon measures, many measurable functions can be approximated by continuous functions in measure or in \(L^p\). Regularity of the measure is the key input.

Methodological role

Remark. A common proof strategy is to prove a claim for simple or continuous functions, then extend by approximation using domination, tightness, or uniform integrability.
