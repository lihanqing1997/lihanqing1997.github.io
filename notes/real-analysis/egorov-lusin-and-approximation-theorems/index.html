43. Egorov, Lusin, and measurable approximation

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/lp-duality-and-weak-convergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/approximation-by-smooth-and-simple-functions/" %}

Egorov and Lusin convert measurable behavior into nearly uniform or nearly continuous behavior after discarding a set of small measure.

Egorov theorem

On finite measure spaces, almost-everywhere convergence is almost uniform convergence.

Theorem. If \(\mu(X)\lt \infty\) and \(f_n\to f\) almost everywhere, then for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(E\subset X\) with \(\mu(E)\lt \varepsilon\) such that \(f_n\to f\) uniformly on \(X\setminus E\).

Proof. For \(k,m\), let \(A_{k,m}=\bigcup_{n\ge m}\{|f_n-f|>1/k\}\). For each \(k\), \(A_{k,m}\downarrow\varnothing\) up to a null set, so choose \(m_k\) with \(\mu(A_{k,m_k})\lt \varepsilon2^{-k}\). Let \(E=\bigcup_kA_{k,m_k}\). Outside \(E\), for every \(k\), all \(n\ge m_k\) satisfy \(|f_n-f|\le1/k\), which is uniform convergence.

Lusin theorem

Measurable functions are continuous outside small exceptional sets on Radon spaces.

Theorem. If \(f\) is finite-valued and measurable on a finite-measure Borel subset of \(\mathbb R^d\), then for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is a compact \(K\) with \(\mu(X\setminus K)\lt \varepsilon\) such that \(f|_K\) is continuous.

Proof. First approximate \(f\) uniformly outside a small set by a simple function. Use regularity to choose compact subsets inside the level sets of that simple function, with total complement small. On the union of these compact pieces the simple function is continuous because the pieces are separated after shrinking. Then transfer the approximation to \(f\) and diagonalize over accuracy levels.

Almost uniform convergence

Almost uniform convergence sits between almost-everywhere and uniform convergence.

Definition. \(f_n\to f\) almost uniformly if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(E\) with \(\mu(E)\lt \varepsilon\) such that convergence is uniform on \(X\setminus E\).

Proposition. Almost uniform convergence implies almost-everywhere convergence.

Proof. For each \(j\), choose \(E_j\) with \(\mu(E_j)\lt 2^{-j}\) and uniform convergence on \(X\setminus E_j\). The exceptional limsup \(\bigcap_m\bigcup_{j\ge m}E_j\) has measure zero by continuity of measure and summability. Outside it, the point eventually lies outside some \(E_j\), where convergence holds.

Measurable approximation by simple functions

Simple functions are dense in measure and in \(L^p\).

Theorem. If \(f\) is measurable and finite almost everywhere, then there are simple functions \(s_n\) with \(s_n\to f\) almost everywhere. If \(f\in L^p\), one can choose \(s_n\to f\) in \(L^p\).

Proof. Use dyadic quantization of truncated functions. Pointwise convergence follows as the truncation level and dyadic resolution increase. For \(L^p\), the errors are dominated by \(2^p|f|^p\) after truncation, and dominated convergence gives norm convergence.

Why approximation matters

Approximation theorems let proofs start with nice objects.

Remark. Many measure-theoretic identities are proved first for indicators or simple functions, then extended by monotone convergence, dominated convergence, or \(L^p\)-density.
