3. Dedekind cuts and construction of the real line

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/ordered-fields-and-completeness/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/cauchy-completion-of-rationals/" %}

Dedekind cuts construct real numbers from rational order alone. A real number is represented by all rationals lying strictly to its left, so completeness becomes a theorem about unions of left sets.

Cuts in the rationals

Definition. A Dedekind cut is a set \(\alpha \subset \mathbb Q\) that is nonempty, not all of \(\mathbb Q\), downward closed, and has no largest element. A rational \(r\) corresponds to \(\{q \in \mathbb Q:q

Order by inclusion

Definition. Cuts are ordered by inclusion: \(\alpha\le\beta\) exactly when \(\alpha \subset \beta\). This extends the rational order, since the cut below \(r\) is included in the cut below \(s\) exactly when \(r\le s\).

Arithmetic of cuts

Construction. Addition and multiplication are defined so that rational cuts behave like rational numbers and order compatibility is preserved. For positive cuts, one starts from sums and products of positive rational representatives and then closes downward.

Suprema as unions

Theorem. If \(\mathcal A\) is a nonempty family of cuts bounded above, then \(\bigcup_{\alpha \in \mathcal A}\alpha\) is the least upper bound. This proves Dedekind completeness directly from the construction.

Dedekind in context

Historical note. Dedekind's construction is both technical and historical: it arithmetizes the continuity of the line. The foundations note records how this belongs to the nineteenth-century response to gaps, infinitesimals, and the need for rigor.
