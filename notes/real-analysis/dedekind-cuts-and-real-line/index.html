3. Dedekind cuts and construction of the real line

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/ordered-fields-and-completeness/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/cauchy-completion-of-rationals/" %}

Dedekind's construction replaces an intuitive continuum by an order-theoretic object. A real number is encoded by the rational numbers lying strictly to its left.

Cuts as left halves of rational order

A cut records all rational approximations below a would-be real number.

Definition. A Dedekind cut is a nonempty proper subset \(\alpha\subset\mathbb Q\) such that \(q\in\alpha\) and \(r\lt q\) imply \(r\in\alpha\), and such that \(\alpha\) has no greatest element.

Example. For \(a\in\mathbb Q\), the rational cut is \(\alpha_a=\{q\in\mathbb Q:q\lt a\}\). The irrational cut for \(\sqrt2\) is \(\{q:q\lt 0\}\cup\{q\ge0:q^2\lt 2\}\).

Order and rational embedding

The order of cuts is inclusion; larger cuts contain more rational numbers.

Proposition. The map \(a\mapsto\alpha_a\) embeds \(\mathbb Q\) into the set of cuts, and \(\alpha_a\subset\alpha_b\) if and only if \(a\le b\).

Proof. If \(a\le b\), then every rational below \(a\) is below \(b\). If \(\alpha_a\subset\alpha_b\) and \(b\lt a\), density of \(\mathbb Q\) gives \(q\) with \(b\lt q\lt a\); then \(q\in\alpha_a\) but \(q\notin\alpha_b\), contradiction.

Arithmetic of cuts

Addition and multiplication are first defined on positive cuts, then extended by signs.

Definition. For cuts \(\alpha,\beta\), define \(\alpha+\beta=\{r\in\mathbb Q:r\lt a+b\text{ for some }a\in\alpha,b\in\beta\}\). For positive cuts, define \(\alpha\beta=\{r:r\lt ab\text{ for some }a\in\alpha,b\in\beta,\ a,b>0\}\), with the usual sign extension.

Proposition. With these operations and inclusion order, Dedekind cuts form a complete ordered field.

Proof. Closure under the cut axioms follows from downward closure and rational density. The field identities reduce to the corresponding identities in \(\mathbb Q\), using approximation inside cuts to remove boundary problems. Positivity and compatibility of order follow because sums and products of positive rational approximants remain positive.

Suprema by unions

The crucial advantage of cuts is that bounded families have visibly defined suprema.

Theorem. If \(\mathcal A\) is a nonempty family of cuts bounded above by a cut \(\beta\), then \(\bigcup_{\alpha\in\mathcal A}\alpha\) is a cut and is \(\sup\mathcal A\).

Proof. The union is nonempty, downward closed, and proper because it is contained in \(\beta\). If it had a greatest rational \(q\), then \(q\in\alpha\) for some member \(\alpha\), contradicting that \(\alpha\) has no greatest element. Inclusion makes it an upper bound, and any other upper bound must contain every \(\alpha\), hence must contain the union.

Why cuts repair the rational gaps

The construction forces every bounded rational approximation process to have a target.

Remark. Dedekind completeness is order completeness. It is especially natural for monotone convergence, distribution functions, quantiles, and any argument where the endpoint is identified by what lies below it.
