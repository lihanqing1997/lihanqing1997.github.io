14. Convergence theorems

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/lebesgue-integral/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/product-measures-and-fubini-theorem/" %}

Convergence theorems records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the real analysis note.

Definition

Definition. The main convergence theorems describe when limits can pass through integrals. They are monotone convergence, Fatou's lemma, and dominated convergence.

Dominated convergence theorem

Theorem. If \(f_n\to f\) almost everywhere and \(|f_n|\le g\) for an integrable \(g\), then \(\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\to\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proof

Proof. Apply Fatou's lemma to \(g+f_n\) and \(g-f_n\), then combine the resulting inequalities. Fatou itself follows from monotone convergence applied to infima of tails.

Remark

Remark. Dominated convergence is the workhorse of probability, statistics, and analysis. The domination hypothesis is the price paid for interchanging limit and integral.
