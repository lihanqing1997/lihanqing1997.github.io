30. Monotone, Fatou, and dominated convergence

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/lebesgue-integral/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/product-measures-and-fubini-theorem/" %}

The convergence theorems identify when limits pass through integrals. They are the core analytic tools behind expectation limits and asymptotic approximations.

Monotone convergence

Theorem. If \(0\le f_n\uparrow f\), then \(\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\uparrow \int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\). The theorem follows by testing simple functions below \(f\).

Fatou lemma

Lemma. For nonnegative \(f_n\), \(\int \liminf f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu \le \liminf \int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\). Fatou is lower semicontinuity of the integral.

Dominated convergence

Theorem. If \(f_n\to f\) almost everywhere and \(|f_n|\le g\) for some \(g\in L^1\), then \(\int |f_n-f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\to0\). Hence integrals converge.

Scheffe lemma

Theorem. If \(f_n,f\ge0\), \(f_n\to f\) a.e., and \(\int f_n\to \int f<\infty\), then \(\int |f_n-f|\to0\). For densities this is total variation convergence.

Spike obstruction

Counterexample. The functions \(f_n=n\mathbf 1_{(0,1/n)}\) converge to zero a.e. on \([0,1]\), but \(\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}x=1\). Domination fails because mass concentrates in shrinking high spikes.
