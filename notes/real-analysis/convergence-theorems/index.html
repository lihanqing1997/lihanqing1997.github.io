The main convergence theorems explain when limits can pass through integrals. They are the daily tools of analysis, probability, and asymptotic statistics.

Monotone convergence

Monotone convergence is built into the definition of the integral.

Theorem. If \(0\le f_n\uparrow f\), then \(\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\uparrow\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proof. The inequality \(\lim\int f_n\le\int f\) follows from monotonicity. For a simple \(s\le f\) and \(0\lt c\lt 1\), the sets \(\{f_n\ge cs\}\) increase to \(X\) on the support of \(s\). Hence \(\lim\int f_n\ge c\int s\). Let \(c\uparrow1\) and take the supremum over simple \(s\le f\).

Fatou's lemma

Fatou gives a one-sided lower semicontinuity of the integral.

Theorem. If \(f_n\ge0\), then \(\int\liminf_n f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\le\liminf_n\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proof. Let \(g_k=\inf_{n\ge k}f_n\). Then \(g_k\uparrow\liminf f_n\). By monotone convergence, \(\int g_k\to\int\liminf f_n\). Since \(g_k\le f_n\) for all \(n\ge k\), \(\int g_k\le\inf_{n\ge k}\int f_n\). Let \(k\to\infty\).

Dominated convergence

Domination gives two-sided control around pointwise convergence.

Theorem. If \(f_n\to f\) almost everywhere and \(|f_n|\le g\in L^1\), then \(f\in L^1\) and \(\int |f_n-f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\to0\). In particular, \(\int f_n\to\int f\).

Proof. Since \(|f|\le g\) almost everywhere, \(f\in L^1\). Apply Fatou's lemma to \(2g-|f_n-f|\ge0\). This gives \(2\int g\le\liminf(2\int g-\int|f_n-f|)\), hence \(\limsup\int|f_n-f|\le0\).

Scheffe-type consequence

For nonnegative functions with matching masses, pointwise convergence implies \(L^1\) convergence.

Proposition. If \(f_n,f\ge0\), \(f_n\to f\) almost everywhere, and \(\int f_n\to\int f\lt \infty\), then \(\int|f_n-f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\to0\).

Proof. Use \(|f_n-f|=f_n+f-2(f_n\wedge f)\). By Fatou or dominated convergence applied through monotone truncations, \(\int(f_n\wedge f)\to\int f\). Combining with \(\int f_n\to\int f\) yields the result.

Vitali perspective

Domination is sufficient but not necessary.

Remark. Uniform integrability plus convergence in measure is the natural replacement for a fixed dominating function. This is the form most often used for families of random variables and statistical estimators.
