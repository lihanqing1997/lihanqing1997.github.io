15. Convergence theorems

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/lebesgue-integral/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/product-measures-and-fubini-theorem/" %}

The power of Lebesgue integration is the ability to pass limits through integrals under natural hypotheses. The three central tools are monotone convergence, Fatou's lemma, and dominated convergence.

Monotone convergence theorem

Theorem. If \(0\le f_n\uparrow f\) pointwise, then \[ \int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\uparrow\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu. \] The theorem is built into the definition of the integral and is the main reason nonnegative functions are treated before signed functions.

Fatou lemma

Theorem. For nonnegative measurable functions, \[ \int \liminf_{n\to\infty} f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\le \liminf_{n\to\infty}\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu. \] Apply monotone convergence to \(g_n=\inf_{k\ge n}f_k\), since \(g_n\uparrow\liminf f_n\).

Dominated convergence theorem

Theorem. If \(f_n\to f\) almost everywhere and \(|f_n|\le g\) for an integrable \(g\), then \(f\) is integrable and \(\int |f_n-f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\to0\). In particular \(\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\to\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proof of dominated convergence

Proof. Since \(|f_n-f|\le2g\), apply Fatou's lemma to \(2g-|f_n-f|\). This gives \(\int 2g\,\mathrm{d}\mu\le\liminf\int(2g-|f_n-f|)\,\mathrm{d}\mu\), hence \(\limsup\int|f_n-f|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\le0\).

Bounded convergence theorem

Corollary. On a finite measure space, if \(f_n\) are uniformly bounded and \(f_n\to f\) almost everywhere, then \(\int f_n\,\mathrm{d}\mu\to\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\). This is dominated convergence with \(g=M\) and \(\mu(X)<\infty\).

Need for domination

Counterexample. Let \(f_n=n\mathbf 1_{(0,1/n)}\) on \((0,1)\). Then \(f_n\to0\) almost everywhere but \(\int_0^1 f_n(x)\,\mathrm{d}x=1\) for every \(n\). Pointwise convergence alone does not control mass concentrating on shrinking sets.
