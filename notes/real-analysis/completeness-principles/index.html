5. Completeness principles for the real line

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/cauchy-completion-of-rationals/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/sequences-and-subsequences/" %}

Completeness appears under several disguises. A good analyst chooses the form that matches the proof: order completeness, Cauchy completeness, compactness, or monotone convergence.

Monotone numerical convergence

Theorem. Every increasing sequence bounded above converges to its supremum, and every decreasing sequence bounded below converges to its infimum. This is the one-dimensional prototype of monotone convergence for functions and measures.

Limsup and liminf

Definition. For a real sequence, \(\limsup x_n=\inf_n \sup_{k\ge n}x_k\) and \(\liminf x_n=\sup_n \inf_{k\ge n}x_k\) in the extended real line. The sequence converges exactly when the two are equal and finite.

Bolzano-Weierstrass

Theorem. Every bounded sequence in \(\mathbb R^d\) has a convergent subsequence. The theorem can be proved by nested intervals in one dimension and by coordinatewise extraction in finite dimension.

Cauchy criterion

Theorem. A sequence in \(\mathbb R^d\) converges if and only if it is Cauchy. This criterion is powerful because it proves convergence without first guessing the limiting point.

Asymptotic role

Remark. Statistical asymptotics repeatedly uses completeness in this form: estimators, empirical averages, and stochastic processes are shown to be tight, Cauchy, or subsequentially compact before their limits are identified.
