14. Equicontinuity and Arzela-Ascoli theorem

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/sequences-of-functions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/differentiability-and-taylor-expansion/" %}

Arzela-Ascoli is compactness for families of functions. It says that uniform boundedness and uniform continuity across a family produce convergent subsequences.

Equicontinuity

Equicontinuity asks for one continuity scale that works for the whole family.

Definition. A family \(\mathcal F\subset C(K)\) is equicontinuous if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is \(\delta>0\) such that \(d(x,y)\lt \delta\Rightarrow |f(x)-f(y)|\lt \varepsilon\) for every \(f\in\mathcal F\).

Example. A uniformly bounded family of Lipschitz functions with a common Lipschitz constant is equicontinuous. Uniform boundedness alone is not enough.

Finite nets and oscillation control

Compactness of the domain reduces uniform control to finitely many points.

Lemma. If \(K\) is compact and \(\mathcal F\) is equicontinuous, then for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there are points \(x_1,\ldots,x_m\in K\) such that every \(x\in K\) is within a \(\delta\)-ball of some \(x_i\), and \(|f(x)-f(x_i)|\lt \varepsilon\) for all \(f\in\mathcal F\).

Proof. Choose \(\delta\) from equicontinuity for \(\varepsilon\). Compactness gives a finite cover of \(K\) by \(\delta\)-balls centered at points \(x_i\). The stated oscillation bound is exactly the equicontinuity estimate.

Arzela-Ascoli theorem

Pointwise subsequential compactness plus equicontinuity gives uniform subsequential compactness.

Theorem. If \(K\) is compact metric and \(\mathcal F\subset C(K)\) is uniformly bounded and equicontinuous, then every sequence in \(\mathcal F\) has a uniformly convergent subsequence.

Proof. Choose a countable dense set \(D\subset K\). Uniform boundedness and diagonal extraction produce a subsequence converging at every point of \(D\). Equicontinuity extends this convergence uniformly: choose a finite \(\delta\)-net from the previous lemma, control convergence on the finitely many net points, and use equicontinuity to control the gaps. The uniform limit is continuous.

Compactness in \(C(K)\)

Arzela-Ascoli characterizes relatively compact subsets of the sup-norm space.

Corollary. A subset of \(C(K)\), with \(K\) compact metric, is relatively compact in \(\|\cdot\|_\infty\) if and only if it is uniformly bounded and equicontinuous.

Proof. The forward direction follows because compact sets in \(C(K)\) are totally bounded, and finite approximants are uniformly bounded and equicontinuous up to a small error. The reverse direction is exactly sequential compactness from Arzela-Ascoli, which is equivalent to compactness in the metric space \(C(K)\).

Why statisticians use it

The theorem is a compactness mechanism for deterministic approximations to random functions.

Remark. Equicontinuity estimates are the deterministic ancestor of stochastic equicontinuity. In empirical process theory, tightness of random functions is often proved by probabilistic analogues of finite-net and oscillation control.
