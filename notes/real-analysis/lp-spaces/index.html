40. L^p spaces and completeness

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/uniform-integrability-and-vitali-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/inequalities-in-lp-spaces/" %}

\(L^p\) spaces turn measurable functions modulo almost-everywhere equality into Banach spaces. They are the analytic bridge between measure theory and functional analysis.

Norms and equivalence classes

Functions equal almost everywhere represent the same \(L^p\) element.

Definition. For \(1\le p\lt \infty\), \(L^p(\mu)\) consists of measurable functions modulo almost-everywhere equality with \(\|f\|_p=(\int |f|^p\,\mathrm{d}\mu)^{1/p}\lt \infty\). \(L^\infty\) uses the essential supremum norm.

Proposition. \(\|f\|_p=0\) if and only if \(f=0\) almost everywhere.

Proof. If \(f=0\) almost everywhere, the integral is zero. Conversely, if \(\int |f|^p=0\), then for every \(n\), the set \(\{|f|>1/n\}\) has measure zero by Markov's inequality. The union over \(n\) is \(\{|f|>0\}\), so \(f=0\) almost everywhere.

Holder inequality

Holder controls products by dual norms.

Theorem. If \(1\lt p\lt \infty\), \(1/p+1/q=1\), then \(\int |fg|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\le\|f\|_p\|g\|_q\).

Proof. Normalize \(\|f\|_p=\|g\|_q=1\). Young's inequality \(ab\le a^p/p+b^q/q\) gives \(|fg|\le |f|^p/p+|g|^q/q\). Integrating yields the normalized result; scaling gives the general case.

Minkowski inequality

The triangle inequality is the nontrivial part of the \(L^p\) norm.

Theorem. For \(1\le p\le\infty\), \(\|f+g\|_p\le\|f\|_p+\|g\|_p\).

Proof. The cases \(p=1,\infty\) are direct. For \(1\lt p\lt \infty\), write \(|f+g|^p\le |f||f+g|^{p-1}+|g||f+g|^{p-1}\). Apply Holder to both terms with conjugate exponent \(q=p/(p-1)\), then divide by \(\|f+g\|_p^{p-1}\) when nonzero.

Completeness

\(L^p\) spaces are Banach spaces.

Theorem. For \(1\le p\le\infty\), \(L^p(\mu)\) is complete.

Proof. For \(p\lt \infty\), take a Cauchy sequence and choose a subsequence \(f_{n_k}\) with \(\|f_{n_{k+1}}-f_{n_k}\|_p\le2^{-k}\). The series of increments is summable in \(L^p\), and by monotone convergence its pointwise absolute sum is finite almost everywhere. Thus the subsequence converges almost everywhere to some \(f\), and Fatou gives \(f\in L^p\). The Cauchy property then upgrades subsequential convergence to full norm convergence. The \(p=\infty\) case follows from uniform essential bounds outside a null set.

Hilbert structure of \(L^2\)

\(L^2\) is special because its norm comes from an inner product.

Example. In \(L^2\), \(\langle f,g\rangle=\int f\overline g\,\mathrm{d}\mu\). Orthogonal projection, conditional expectation, Fourier series, and least-squares estimation all use this Hilbert-space structure.
