40. L^p spaces and completeness

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/uniform-integrability-and-vitali-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/inequalities-in-lp-spaces/" %}

The spaces \(L^p\) turn measurable functions into normed spaces. They are the bridge from measure theory to functional analysis.

Lp norms

Definition. For \(1\le p<\infty\), define \(\|f\|_p=(\int |f|^p\,\mathrm{d}\mu)^{1/p}\). For \(p=\infty\), use the essential supremum. Functions equal almost everywhere are identified.

Hölder inequality

Theorem. If \(1/p+1/q=1\), then \(\int |fg|\,\mathrm{d}\mu\le \|f\|_p\|g\|_q\). This is the central product estimate in \(L^p\) theory.

Minkowski inequality

Theorem. For \(1\le p\le\infty\), \(\|f+g\|_p\le\|f\|_p+\|g\|_p\). Thus \(L^p\) is a normed vector space.

Completeness

Theorem. For \(1\le p\le\infty\), \(L^p\) is complete. The proof extracts a subsequence with summable increments and uses Fatou-type estimates to obtain norm convergence.

Hilbert case

Example. \(L^2\) has inner product \(\langle f,g\rangle=\int f\overline g\,\mathrm{d}\mu\). Orthogonal projections, conditional expectation, and least squares live naturally in this structure.
