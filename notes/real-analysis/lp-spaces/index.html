19. \(L^p\) spaces

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/radon-nikodym-derivatives/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/modes-of-convergence/" %}

The spaces \(L^p\) organize functions by integrability size. They are both measure-theoretic objects and normed vector spaces, forming a bridge toward functional analysis and probability.

\(L^p\) seminorms and equivalence classes

Definition. For \(1\le p<\infty\), define \(\|f\|_p=(\int |f|^p\,\mathrm{d}\mu)^{1/p}\), and define \(\|f\|_\infty\) as the essential supremum. The space \(L^p\) consists of equivalence classes of measurable functions with finite \(p\)-norm, identifying functions equal almost everywhere.

Completeness of \(L^p\)

Theorem. For \(1\le p\le\infty\), \(L^p\) is complete. If \(f_n\) is Cauchy in \(L^p\), choose a subsequence with rapidly summable increments, show the subsequence converges almost everywhere to some \(f\), and then use Fatou and Cauchy estimates to prove \(f_n\to f\) in \(L^p\).

Inclusions on finite measure spaces

Proposition. If \(\mu(X)<\infty\) and \(1\le q\le p\le\infty\), then \(L^p\subset L^q\), with \(\|f\|_q\le \mu(X)^{1/q-1/p}\|f\|_p\). On infinite measure spaces, no general inclusion holds without additional hypotheses.

Hilbert space case

Example. The space \(L^2\) has inner product \(\langle f,g\rangle=\int f\overline g\,\mathrm{d}\mu\). Orthogonal projection, Fourier series, conditional expectation, and many statistical least-squares arguments live naturally in this Hilbert-space structure.
