28. Measurable functions and convergence in measure

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/real-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/real-analysis/borel-and-radon-measures/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/real-analysis/lebesgue-integral/" %}

Measurable functions are those whose level sets are measurable. They are stable under the limiting operations needed for integration and probability.

Measurability

Definition. A map \(f:(X,\mathcal A)\to(Y,\mathcal B)\) is measurable if inverse images of sets in \(\mathcal B\) lie in \(\mathcal A\). For real functions it suffices to check half-lines with rational thresholds.

Closure under limits

Proposition. Sums, products, compositions with Borel maps, pointwise suprema, infima, limsup, and liminf of measurable functions are measurable. This stability is why measurable functions form the right class for analysis.

Simple approximation

Theorem. Every nonnegative measurable function is the increasing pointwise limit of nonnegative simple functions. This theorem is the construction engine for the Lebesgue integral.

Almost-everywhere equality

Definition. Functions that agree outside a null set are equal almost everywhere. Integration and \(L^p\) norms identify such functions, since null-set modifications do not change their analytic value.

Convergence in measure

Definition. The sequence \(f_n\) converges to \(f\) in measure if \(\mu(|f_n-f|>\varepsilon)\to0\) for every \(\varepsilon>0\). On probability spaces this is convergence in probability.
