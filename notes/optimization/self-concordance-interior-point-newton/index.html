20. Self-concordance and interior-point Newton methods

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/optimization/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/optimization/line-search-trust-regions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/optimization/proximal-maps-moreau-envelopes/" %}

This page records the definitions, propositions, and proof mechanisms for Self-concordance and interior-point Newton methods.

Barrier method

Definition. For constraints \(g_i(x)<0\), a barrier method minimizes \(f(x)+t^{-1}\Phi(x)\), often with \(\Phi(x)=-\sum_i\log(-g_i(x))\), and follows the central path as \(t\to\infty\).

Self-concordance

Definition. A convex function is self-concordant if its third derivative is controlled by its second derivative. This condition makes Newton decrements quantitatively reliable.

Newton decrement

Theorem. For self-concordant barriers, the Newton decrement controls both distance to the local minimizer and the decrease obtained by a damped Newton step. This yields polynomial iteration complexity for interior-point methods.

Geometry

Remark. Interior-point methods replace hard constraints by a curved metric that becomes singular at the boundary, steering iterates through the feasible interior.
