5. Existence, coercivity, and error bounds

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/optimization/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/optimization/smoothness-strong-convexity-condition-numbers/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/optimization/lagrange-multipliers/" %}

This page records the definitions, propositions, and proof mechanisms for Existence, coercivity, and error bounds.

Weierstrass principle

Theorem. If \(C\) is compact and \(f:C\to\mathbb R\) is continuous, then \(f\) attains its minimum on \(C\). Compactness is often replaced by coercivity of \(f\).

Coercivity

Definition. A function \(f\) on \(\mathbb R^d\) is coercive if \(f(x)\to\infty\) whenever \(\|x\|\to\infty\). A lower semicontinuous coercive function has compact sublevel sets and hence minimizers when proper.

Quadratic growth

Definition. An objective has a quadratic growth error bound around \(X^\star\) if \(f(x)-f^\star\ge c\,\operatorname{dist}(x,X^\star)^2\). This controls parameter error by objective error.

Statistical relevance

Remark. Consistency proofs often combine stochastic uniform convergence with deterministic separation: outside every neighborhood of the target, the limiting objective is strictly worse.
