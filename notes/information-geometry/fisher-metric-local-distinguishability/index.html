4. Fisher metric and local distinguishability

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/information-geometry/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/information-geometry/fisher-information-metric/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/information-geometry/fisher-rao-length-distance/" %}

The Fisher metric is the second-order part of several statistical divergences. It therefore quantifies local distinguishability of nearby distributions.

Second-order KL expansion

Nearby models are separated quadratically by Fisher information.

Theorem. For a regular model, \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_\theta\|p_{\theta+\mathrm{d}\theta}) =\frac12 g_{ij}(\theta)\,\mathrm{d}\theta^i\mathrm{d}\theta^j+o(\|\mathrm{d}\theta\|^2). \]

Proof. Expand \(\ell_{\theta+\mathrm{d}\theta}\) around \(\theta\): \[ \ell_{\theta+\mathrm{d}\theta}=\ell_\theta+s_i\mathrm{d}\theta^i+\frac12\partial_i\partial_j\ell_\theta\,\mathrm{d}\theta^i\mathrm{d}\theta^j+o(\|\mathrm{d}\theta\|^2). \] Taking \(\mathbb E_\theta(\ell_\theta-\ell_{\theta+\mathrm{d}\theta})\), the first-order term vanishes because \(\mathbb E_\theta s_i=0\). The expected Hessian identity gives the quadratic term.

Hellinger and chi-square geometry

Different divergences share the same local metric up to scale.

Proposition. The squared Hellinger distance satisfies \[ H^2(p_\theta,p_{\theta+\mathrm{d}\theta}) =\frac18 g_{ij}(\theta)\,\mathrm{d}\theta^i\mathrm{d}\theta^j+o(\|\mathrm{d}\theta\|^2) \] under the convention \(H^2(p,q)=\frac12\int(\sqrt p-\sqrt q)^2\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proof. Differentiate \(\sqrt{p_\theta}\): \(\partial_i\sqrt p=\frac12s_i\sqrt p\). The squared \(L^2(\mu)\) norm of the first-order change is \(\frac14 g_{ij}\mathrm{d}\theta^i\mathrm{d}\theta^j\). The extra factor \(1/2\) in the Hellinger convention gives \(1/8\).

Cramer-Rao interpretation

The same metric controls local estimation lower bounds.

Theorem. For an unbiased estimator \(T\) of a scalar parameter \(\psi(\theta)\), \[ \operatorname{Var}_\theta(T)\ge \partial_i\psi\,g^{ij}\,\partial_j\psi. \]

Proof. Differentiate \(\mathbb E_\theta T=\psi(\theta)\) to obtain \(\partial_i\psi=\operatorname{Cov}_\theta(T,s_i)\). For any vector \(a\), \(a^i\partial_i\psi=\operatorname{Cov}(T,a^is_i)\). Cauchy-Schwarz gives \((a^i\partial_i\psi)^2\le\operatorname{Var}(T)a^ig_{ij}a^j\). Optimize over \(a\) to obtain the bound.

Local experiments

The Fisher metric is the quadratic geometry behind LAN and Gaussian local approximations.

Remark. For \(n\) i.i.d. observations, Fisher information scales by \(n\), so local alternatives of size \(n^{-1/2}\) have finite statistical separation. This is why Fisher geometry appears throughout asymptotic statistics.
