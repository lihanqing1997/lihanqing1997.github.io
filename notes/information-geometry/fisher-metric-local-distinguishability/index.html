4. Fisher metric and local distinguishability

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/information-geometry/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/information-geometry/fisher-information-metric/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/information-geometry/fisher-rao-length-distance/" %}

The Fisher metric is the second-order part of statistical distinguishability. Nearby distributions have KL divergence governed by the Fisher quadratic form.

Local KL expansion

Theorem. For a regular model and small displacement \(h\), \[ D(p_\theta\|p_{\theta+h})=\frac12 h^i h^j g_{ij}(\theta)+o(\|h\|^2). \] The same quadratic term appears for \(D(p_{\theta+h}\|p_\theta)\).

Proof by Taylor expansion

Proof. Expand \(\ell_{\theta+h}\) around \(\theta\). The first-order term has expectation zero because the score is centered. The second-order term is \(-\frac12 h^ih^j\mathbb E_\theta\partial_i\partial_j\ell_\theta\), which equals the Fisher quadratic form.

Local alternatives

Corollary. For \(n\) iid observations and \(h/\sqrt n\) local displacement, the accumulated local KL divergence is asymptotically \(\frac12 h^\top I_\theta h\). This is the geometric input behind LAN and local asymptotic minimax theory.

Interpretation

Remark. The metric makes directions with high likelihood sensitivity longer. A small Euclidean parameter change may be statistically large if the score variance in that direction is large.
