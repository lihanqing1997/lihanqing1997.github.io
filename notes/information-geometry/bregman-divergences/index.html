21. Bregman divergences

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/information-geometry/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/information-geometry/f-divergences-information-monotonicity/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/information-geometry/dually-flat-manifolds/" %}

Bregman divergences arise from convex potentials. They are the divergence side of dually flat geometry and mirror descent.

Convex potential

A Bregman divergence measures the gap between a convex function and its tangent plane.

Definition. For a differentiable strictly convex function \(\varphi\) on a convex domain, \[ D_\varphi(x,y)=\varphi(x)-\varphi(y)-\langle\nabla\varphi(y),x-y\rangle. \]

Proposition. \(D_\varphi(x,y)\ge0\), with equality iff \(x=y\) when \(\varphi\) is strictly convex.

Proof. Convexity gives \(\varphi(x)\ge\varphi(y)+\langle\nabla\varphi(y),x-y\rangle\). Strict convexity makes equality possible only at \(x=y\).

Metric and dual coordinates

The Hessian of the potential is the metric in primal affine coordinates.

Proposition. The local quadratic expansion is \[ D_\varphi(x,x+\mathrm{d}x) =\frac12\partial_i\partial_j\varphi(x)\,\mathrm{d}x^i\mathrm{d}x^j+o(\|\mathrm{d}x\|^2). \]

Proof. Taylor expand \(\varphi(x+\mathrm{d}x)\) and \(\nabla\varphi(x+\mathrm{d}x)\) in the definition. First-order terms cancel, leaving half the Hessian quadratic form.

Generalized Pythagorean theorem

Bregman projections satisfy an exact additive identity when the residual is dual-orthogonal.

Theorem. If \(y\) is the Bregman projection of \(x\) onto a convex affine set \(C\), then for \(z\in C\), \[ D_\varphi(z,x)=D_\varphi(z,y)+D_\varphi(y,x) \] when the first-order optimality condition gives the required orthogonality; in general the right side is a lower bound.

Proof. A direct three-point identity gives \[ D_\varphi(z,x)-D_\varphi(z,y)-D_\varphi(y,x) =\langle\nabla\varphi(y)-\nabla\varphi(x),z-y\rangle. \] For projection onto an affine set, the first-order condition makes this inner product zero for feasible \(z-y\).

Examples

KL on exponential families is a Bregman divergence of the log-partition function.

Remark. Squared Euclidean distance comes from \(\varphi(x)=\|x\|^2/2\). KL divergence between members of the same exponential family comes from the cumulant potential.
