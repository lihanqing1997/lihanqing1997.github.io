The probability simplex is the simplest full statistical manifold. Many information-geometric formulas become explicit there and reveal the meaning of Fisher geometry.

Interior simplex

The natural manifold is the strictly positive simplex, where support does not change.

Definition. The \(n\)-category probability simplex is \[ \Delta_n^\circ=\{p\in\mathbb R^n:p_i>0,\ \sum_i p_i=1\}. \] Its tangent space at \(p\) is \(T_p\Delta_n^\circ=\{u\in\mathbb R^n:\sum_i u_i=0\}\).

Proposition. The Fisher metric on the simplex is \[ g_p(u,v)=\sum_i\frac{u_iv_i}{p_i}. \]

Proof. For coordinate perturbation \(u\), the score component is \(u_i/p_i\) on category \(i\). Taking the expectation under \(p\) gives \(\sum_i p_i(u_i/p_i)(v_i/p_i)=\sum_i u_iv_i/p_i\).

Square-root sphere

The square-root map turns the simplex into a piece of a sphere.

Theorem. The map \(p\mapsto 2\sqrt p\) is an isometric embedding from \((\Delta_n^\circ,g)\) into the sphere of radius \(2\).

Proof. The differential sends \(u\) to \(u_i/\sqrt{p_i}\). The Euclidean inner product of two such differentials is \(\sum_i u_iv_i/p_i\), the Fisher metric. The image has squared norm \(4\sum_i p_i=4\).

The simplex carries two natural affine coordinate systems.

Boundary behavior

The Fisher metric blows up near the boundary in probability coordinates.

Remark. As \(p_i\downarrow0\), the term \(u_i^2/p_i\) becomes large. This reflects the statistical singularity of changing a nearly impossible category by a fixed absolute amount.
