Dually flat manifolds are the cleanest setting in information geometry: two dual affine coordinate systems, a Hessian metric, and a canonical divergence all fit together.

Dual affine coordinates

Flatness gives coordinate systems in which the two dual connections have zero coefficients.

Definition. A statistical manifold \((M,g,\nabla,\nabla^*)\) is dually flat if \(\nabla\) and \(\nabla^*\) are flat, torsion-free, and dual with respect to \(g\). Coordinates \(\theta\) affine for \(\nabla\) and \(\eta\) affine for \(\nabla^*\) are dual affine coordinates.

Remark. Exponential-family natural parameters and expectation parameters are the main example.

Hessian metric

In affine coordinates, the metric is the Hessian of a convex potential.

Theorem. On a dually flat manifold, locally there is a potential \(\psi(\theta)\) such that \[ g_{ij}=\partial_i\partial_j\psi(\theta), \qquad \eta_i=\partial_i\psi(\theta). \]

Proof. In \(\nabla\)-affine coordinates, dual flatness and metric duality imply that the metric tensor has symmetric first derivatives: \(\partial_kg_{ij}=\partial_ig_{kj}\). The Poincare lemma gives a local potential whose Hessian is \(g\). Defining \(\eta_i=\partial_i\psi\) yields dual affine coordinates.

Canonical divergence

The convex potential produces the divergence compatible with the dual geometry.

Definition. The canonical divergence in dual coordinates is \[ D(p,q)=\psi(\theta(p))+\psi^*(\eta(q))-\theta^i(p)\eta_i(q), \] where \(\psi^*\) is the Legendre dual of \(\psi\).

Proposition. This canonical divergence is the Bregman divergence generated by \(\psi\) in \(\theta\)-coordinates.

Proof. Since \(\psi^*(\eta(q))=\theta^i(q)\eta_i(q)-\psi(\theta(q))\), substitution gives \[ D(p,q)=\psi(\theta_p)-\psi(\theta_q)-\langle\nabla\psi(\theta_q),\theta_p-\theta_q\rangle. \]

Flatness and projections

Projection theorems become exact because primal and dual geodesics are affine lines.

Remark. This is the setting where generalized Pythagorean theorems, alternating projections, and maximum-entropy characterizations are most transparent.
