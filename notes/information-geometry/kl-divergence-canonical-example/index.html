18. KL divergence as canonical example

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/information-geometry/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/information-geometry/connections-from-divergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/information-geometry/forward-reverse-kl-projections/" %}

KL divergence is the central example because it is both statistically operational and geometrically generative. Its second and third derivatives recover Fisher geometry and dual connections.

Definition and asymmetry

KL divergence is directed: its two arguments have different statistical roles.

Definition. For \(P\ll Q\), \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|Q)=\int \log\frac{\mathrm{d}P}{\mathrm{d}Q}\,\mathrm{d}P. \] In densities, \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)=\int p\log(p/q)\,\mathrm{d}\mu\).

Proposition. KL divergence is nonnegative and equals zero iff \(P=Q\) almost surely.

Proof. Let \(Z=\mathrm{d}Q/\mathrm{d}P\). Since \(-\log\) is convex, \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|Q)=\mathbb E_P[-\log Z]\ge -\log\mathbb E_PZ=0. \] Equality in Jensen requires \(Z\) to be constant \(P\)-a.s.; normalization gives \(Z=1\).

Fisher metric from KL

The local quadratic term of KL is Fisher information.

Theorem. For a regular model, \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_\theta\|p_{\theta+\mathrm{d}\theta}) =\frac12g_{ij}\mathrm{d}\theta^i\mathrm{d}\theta^j+o(\|\mathrm{d}\theta\|^2). \]

Proof. Taylor expand \(\ell_{\theta+\mathrm{d}\theta}\) inside \(\mathbb E_\theta(\ell_\theta-\ell_{\theta+\mathrm{d}\theta})\). The linear score term has mean zero. The expected Hessian term is \(-g_{ij}\), producing the factor \(1/2\).

Chain rule

KL has an additive decomposition under joint distributions.

Proposition. For joint laws \(P_{XY},Q_{XY}\), \[ D(P_{XY}\|Q_{XY})=D(P_X\|Q_X)+\mathbb E_{P_X}D(P_{Y|X}\|Q_{Y|X}). \]

Proof. Factor the likelihood ratio as \[ \frac{\mathrm{d}P_{XY}}{\mathrm{d}Q_{XY}} =\frac{\mathrm{d}P_X}{\mathrm{d}Q_X}\frac{\mathrm{d}P_{Y|X}}{\mathrm{d}Q_{Y|X}}. \] Taking \(P\)-expectations of the logarithm gives the two terms.

Projection behavior

KL's asymmetry leads to different forward and reverse projection geometries.

Remark. Forward KL tends to cover mass under the first distribution, while reverse KL can be mode-seeking. In dually flat geometry these are tied to mixture and exponential projections.
