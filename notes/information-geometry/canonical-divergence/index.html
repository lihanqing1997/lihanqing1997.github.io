24. Canonical divergence

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/information-geometry/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/information-geometry/legendre-duality-potential-functions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/information-geometry/dual-geodesics-pythagorean-theorem/" %}

Every dually flat manifold has a canonical divergence. In affine coordinates it is exactly a Bregman divergence built from the potential.

Canonical form

Definition. For points \(P,Q\) with coordinates \((\theta(P),\eta(P))\) and \((\theta(Q),\eta(Q))\), the canonical divergence is \[ D(P:Q)=F(\theta(P))+F^\ast(\eta(Q))-\theta(P)\cdot\eta(Q). \]

Bregman identity

Proposition. The canonical divergence equals \[ D(P:Q)=B_F(\theta(P):\theta(Q))=B_{F^\ast}(\eta(Q):\eta(P)). \]

Proof by Young identity

Proof. Since \(F(\theta(Q))+F^\ast(\eta(Q))=\theta(Q)\cdot\eta(Q)\), substituting into the canonical form gives exactly the Bregman expression generated by \(F\).

Asymmetry

Remark. The divergence is generally asymmetric because the two arguments use different affine roles: the first is measured in primal coordinates and the second in dual coordinates.
