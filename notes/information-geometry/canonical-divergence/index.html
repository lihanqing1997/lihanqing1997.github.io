The canonical divergence of a dually flat manifold is the Bregman divergence determined by its potential. It is the global contrast compatible with the local dualistic geometry.

Definition in dual coordinates

The divergence pairs a point in primal coordinates with a point in dual coordinates.

Definition. For points \(p,q\) with primal coordinates \(\theta(p),\theta(q)\) and dual coordinates \(\eta(p),\eta(q)\), \[ D(p,q)=\psi(\theta(p))+\psi^*(\eta(q))-\theta^i(p)\eta_i(q). \]

Proposition. The canonical divergence is nonnegative and vanishes exactly on the diagonal.

Proof. By Fenchel's inequality, \(\psi(\theta)+\psi^*(\eta)\ge\theta^i\eta_i\). Equality holds exactly when \(\eta=\nabla\psi(\theta)\), which means the two coordinate descriptions refer to the same point.

Bregman form

In primal coordinates, the canonical divergence is a Bregman divergence.

Proposition. The canonical divergence can be written as \[ D(p,q)=\psi(\theta_p)-\psi(\theta_q)-(\theta_p^i-\theta_q^i)\partial_i\psi(\theta_q). \]

Proof. Use \(\psi^*(\eta_q)=\theta_q^i\eta_i(q)-\psi(\theta_q)\) and \(\eta_i(q)=\partial_i\psi(\theta_q)\) in the definition.

Metric and connections

The canonical divergence regenerates the Hessian metric and the dual flat connections.

Theorem. The second mixed derivatives of \(D\) give \(g\), and the third derivatives give the flat dual connections associated with \(\theta\) and \(\eta\).

Proof. In Bregman form, differentiating twice at the diagonal gives the Hessian \(\partial_i\partial_j\psi\). Third derivatives with respect to the first affine coordinate vanish for the flat primal connection, while the corresponding dual derivatives encode the dual affine structure through the Legendre transform.

Asymmetry

The canonical divergence is generally asymmetric because the two affine coordinate systems play different roles.

Remark. Symmetry occurs essentially in the quadratic Euclidean case. In statistical examples, asymmetry is a feature: it records the difference between exponential and mixture linearity.
