11. Affine connections and covariant derivatives

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/information-geometry/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/information-geometry/examples-fisher-geometry/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/information-geometry/geodesics-parallel-transport/" %}

Information geometry needs more than a Riemannian metric. Affine connections describe how tangent vectors are compared across nearby model points.

Affine connection

Definition. An affine connection \(\nabla\) assigns to vector fields \(X,Y\) another vector field \(\nabla_XY\), bilinear over constants, linear in \(X\) over functions, and satisfying the Leibniz rule \(\nabla_X(fY)=X(f)Y+f\nabla_XY\).

Christoffel symbols

Definition. In local coordinates, the connection is represented by coefficients \[ \nabla_{\partial_i}\partial_j=\Gamma^k_{ij}\partial_k. \] Lowered coefficients are \(\Gamma_{ij,k}=g_{k\ell}\Gamma^\ell_{ij}\).

Torsion-free condition

Definition. A connection is torsion-free when \(\nabla_XY-\nabla_YX=[X,Y]\). In coordinates this is \(\Gamma^k_{ij}=\Gamma^k_{ji}\).

Why connections matter

Remark. The metric measures lengths inside one tangent space. A connection tells us how to differentiate vector fields and which curves count as straight for a chosen affine structure.
