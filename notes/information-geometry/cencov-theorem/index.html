Cencov's theorem explains why the Fisher metric is canonical: under invariance and monotonicity requirements, it is essentially the unique Riemannian metric on finite probability simplices.

Statement on finite simplices

The theorem is a uniqueness result, not merely another formula for Fisher information.

Theorem. On finite positive probability simplices, any smooth Riemannian metric that is invariant under congruent Markov embeddings is a constant multiple of the Fisher metric \[ g_p(u,v)=c\sum_i\frac{u_iv_i}{p_i}. \]

Proof. The full theorem is proved by reducing the metric to its behavior under finite category refinements. Permutation invariance first removes preferred labels. Congruent embeddings then compare a point \(p\) with a refinement in which one mass \(p_i\) is split into \(ap_i\) and \((1-a)p_i\), while tangent vectors split in the same proportions. These comparisons force the metric to be diagonal in the coordinate tangent directions and force each diagonal coefficient to solve the splitting functional equation \(a(x)=c/x\). Summing over coordinates gives the displayed Fisher form, with a single constant \(c\) because permutations identify all coordinates.

Remark. Different formulations use invariance under sufficient statistics, congruent embeddings, or monotonicity under Markov morphisms. The conclusion is the same canonical Fisher tensor up to scale.

Idea of the proof

Permutation symmetry and splitting maps force the metric coefficients to have Fisher form.

Proposition. Permutation invariance makes the metric diagonal with coefficients depending only on the probability coordinate, and invariance under splitting a category forces that coefficient to be proportional to \(1/p_i\).

Proof. Permutations exchange categories, so cross terms and diagonal terms must obey strong symmetry. A congruent embedding that splits one category into two subcategories preserves statistical distinguishability. Comparing the length of a tangent perturbation before and after such a split yields a functional equation for the coefficient \(a(p)\), whose solution is \(a(p)=c/p\).

Why uniqueness matters

The theorem gives a structural reason that Fisher geometry appears in statistics, not only an algebraic accident.

Remark. Cencov's theorem is the information-geometric analogue of a representation theorem: if a metric respects stochastic transformations of experiments, it must be Fisher.

Beyond the classical theorem

Infinite-dimensional, quantum, and noncommutative settings have related but more delicate uniqueness statements.

Remark. In quantum information geometry, monotone metrics form a family indexed by operator-monotone functions, so the classical uniqueness of Fisher becomes a richer classification problem.
