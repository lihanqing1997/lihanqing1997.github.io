The Fisher-Rao length is the Riemannian length induced by Fisher information. It turns a statistical model into a metric space when the geodesic distance is finite and nondegenerate.

Length of statistical curves

A smooth curve of distributions has length obtained by integrating Fisher speed.

Definition. For a smooth curve \(t\mapsto\theta(t)\), \[ L(\theta)=\int_0^1\sqrt{g_{ij}(\theta(t))\dot\theta^i(t)\dot\theta^j(t)}\,\mathrm{d}t. \] The Fisher-Rao distance is the infimum of this length over smooth curves joining two model points.

Proposition. The length is invariant under smooth reparametrization of the statistical model.

Proof. Under coordinate change, tangent components and the Fisher tensor transform by inverse rules. The scalar \(g_{ij}\dot\theta^i\dot\theta^j\) is therefore unchanged. The integral over curve parameter is also unchanged under monotone reparametrization of \(t\).

Square-root embedding

For the full positive simplex, Fisher geometry becomes spherical geometry after taking square roots.

Theorem. On the probability simplex, the map \(p\mapsto 2\sqrt p\) embeds the simplex into the positive orthant of the sphere of radius \(2\), and the Fisher metric is the pullback of the Euclidean metric.

Proof. For a tangent vector \(u\) with \(\sum_i u_i=0\), the differential is \(u_i/\sqrt{p_i}\). Its Euclidean squared norm is \(\sum_i u_i^2/p_i\), which is the Fisher metric on the simplex. The image has norm squared \(4\sum_i p_i=4\).

Distance on the simplex

The spherical embedding gives an explicit Fisher-Rao distance.

Corollary. For strictly positive probability vectors \(p,q\), \[ d_{\mathrm{FR}}(p,q)=2\arccos\left(\sum_i\sqrt{p_iq_i}\right). \]

Proof. The Euclidean sphere of radius \(2\) has geodesic distance \(2\arccos(\langle x,y\rangle/4)\). With \(x=2\sqrt p\) and \(y=2\sqrt q\), \(\langle x,y\rangle/4=\sum_i\sqrt{p_iq_i}\).

Distance versus divergence

Fisher-Rao distance is intrinsic but harder to compute than divergences.

Remark. KL divergence is directed and not a metric; Fisher-Rao distance is symmetric and geodesic. Locally they agree to second order through the Fisher tensor, but globally they encode different geometry.
