The natural gradient is the Riemannian gradient under the Fisher metric. It turns an ordinary differential into an intrinsic vector field, correcting for parameter scale and statistical curvature.

Riemannian gradient

The gradient depends on the metric used to identify covectors and vectors.

Definition. For a smooth objective \(L\), the natural gradient \(\widetilde\nabla L\) is defined by \[ g_\theta(\widetilde\nabla L,v)=\mathrm{d}L_\theta(v) \] for all tangent vectors \(v\). In coordinates, \[ (\widetilde\nabla L)^i=g^{ij}\partial_jL. \]

Proposition. The natural gradient transforms as a vector field under reparametrization.

Proof. The differential \(\partial_jL\) is a covector and \(g^{ij}\) is the inverse metric tensor. Contracting them raises the index, so the result obeys the contravariant vector transformation law.

Steepest ascent

The natural gradient is steepest when step length is measured by Fisher distance.

Theorem. Among directions \(v\) satisfying \(g_\theta(v,v)\le\varepsilon^2\), the first-order gain \(\mathrm{d}L_\theta(v)\) is maximized by \(v\) proportional to \(\widetilde\nabla L\).

Proof. By Cauchy-Schwarz in the Fisher inner product, \[ \mathrm{d}L(v)=g(\widetilde\nabla L,v)\le \|\widetilde\nabla L\|_g\|v\|_g. \] Equality holds when \(v\) is a positive multiple of \(\widetilde\nabla L\).

KL trust region

Natural gradient is the infinitesimal solution of a KL-constrained optimization problem.

Proposition. For small steps, maximizing \(L(\theta+\Delta)\) subject to \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_\theta\|p_{\theta+\Delta})\le\varepsilon\) gives \(\Delta\propto g^{-1}\nabla L\) to first order.

Proof. Use the expansions \(L(\theta+\Delta)=L(\theta)+\partial_iL\Delta^i+o(\|\Delta\|)\) and \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}=\frac12g_{ij}\Delta^i\Delta^j+o(\|\Delta\|^2)\). The Lagrange multiplier equation for the quadratic constraint gives \(\partial_iL=\lambda g_{ij}\Delta^j\).

Relation with mirror descent

In dually flat coordinates, natural-gradient flow and mirror descent are two descriptions of the same geometry.

Remark. When Fisher geometry is Hessian, the metric potential defines a mirror map. Natural-gradient steps in primal coordinates correspond to ordinary gradient-like steps in dual coordinates up to discretization choices.
