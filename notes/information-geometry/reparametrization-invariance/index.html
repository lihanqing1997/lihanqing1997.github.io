Information geometry insists that statistical constructions should not depend on the accidental coordinates chosen for a model. Reparametrization invariance is the first test of whether a formula is geometric.

Coordinate changes

A parameter transformation changes components but not the underlying distribution.

Definition. A reparametrization is a diffeomorphism \(\phi=\phi(\theta)\). Scalars, vectors, covectors, and tensors transform by the usual rules: \[ v^a=\frac{\partial\phi^a}{\partial\theta^i}v^i,\qquad \omega_a=\frac{\partial\theta^i}{\partial\phi^a}\omega_i. \]

Remark. A scalar statistical objective such as KL divergence between two fixed distributions is invariant. Its coordinate gradient is not a vector until a metric is used to raise an index.

Fisher metric as a tensor

The Fisher information matrix has the pullback transformation law of a Riemannian metric.

Proposition. Under \(\theta=\theta(\phi)\), \[ g^{(\phi)}_{ab} =\frac{\partial\theta^i}{\partial\phi^a}\frac{\partial\theta^j}{\partial\phi^b}g^{(\theta)}_{ij}. \]

Proof. The score transforms by the chain rule. Taking score inner products gives exactly the pullback formula.

Ordinary gradient is not invariant

Euclidean gradients depend on the coordinate scale.

Proposition. The coordinate gradient \(\partial_i L\) is a covector, not an invariant search direction. The natural gradient \(g^{ij}\partial_jL\) transforms as a vector.

Proof. The differential \(\mathrm{d}L\) has components \(\partial_iL\), which transform covariantly. Multiplying by the inverse metric raises the index. Because \(g^{ij}\) transforms contravariantly in two indices, \(g^{ij}\partial_jL\) has the vector transformation law.

Invariant algorithms

Natural gradient and mirror-descent formulations are attempts to make optimization respect the statistical geometry.

Remark. Invariance does not automatically guarantee good numerical behavior, but it prevents the algorithm from changing merely because the same model was written in different coordinates.
