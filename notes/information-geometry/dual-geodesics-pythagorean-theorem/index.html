The generalized Pythagorean theorem is the geometric heart of information projection. It combines one primal geodesic, one dual geodesic, and Fisher orthogonality.

Dual geodesics

In a dually flat manifold, primal and dual geodesics are straight in different coordinate systems.

Definition. A \(\nabla\)-geodesic is affine in \(\theta\)-coordinates. A \(\nabla^*\)-geodesic is affine in \(\eta\)-coordinates. These two notions of straightness are dual through \(g\).

Remark. For exponential families, exponential geodesics are straight in natural parameters; mixture geodesics are straight in expectation or probability coordinates.

Three-point identity

The Pythagorean theorem follows from a Bregman three-point identity.

Lemma. For the canonical divergence, \[ D(p,r)-D(p,q)-D(q,r) =\langle \theta(q)-\theta(p),\,\eta(r)-\eta(q)\rangle. \]

Proof. Write each divergence in Bregman form and cancel the potential terms. The remaining linear terms collect into the displayed pairing between primal and dual coordinate differences.

Generalized Pythagorean theorem

Orthogonality of primal and dual geodesics makes the three-point term vanish.

Theorem. If the \(\nabla\)-geodesic from \(q\) to \(p\) and the \(\nabla^*\)-geodesic from \(q\) to \(r\) meet \(g\)-orthogonally at \(q\), then \[ D(p,r)=D(p,q)+D(q,r). \]

Proof. The tangent of the primal geodesic is represented by \(\theta(p)-\theta(q)\); the tangent of the dual geodesic is represented by \(\eta(r)-\eta(q)\). Orthogonality makes their pairing zero. The three-point identity then gives the equality.

Projection interpretation

Information projection is a Pythagorean footpoint in dual geometry.

Remark. Many maximum-likelihood, maximum-entropy, and variational projection statements are concrete versions of this theorem.
