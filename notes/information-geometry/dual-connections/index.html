Dual connections are paired through the metric. They let information geometry carry two compatible notions of straightness, typically mixture and exponential.

Metric duality

The definition of duality says that differentiating an inner product can be split between two connections.

Definition. Two affine connections \(\nabla\) and \(\nabla^*\) are dual with respect to \(g\) if \[ Xg(Y,Z)=g(\nabla_XY,Z)+g(Y,\nabla_X^*Z) \] for all vector fields \(X,Y,Z\).

Proposition. If \(\nabla\) is torsion-free, then the dual connection \(\nabla^*\), when it exists, is uniquely determined by \(g\) and \(\nabla\).

Proof. For fixed \(X,Z\), the defining identity determines the covector \(Y\mapsto g(Y,\nabla_X^*Z)\). Nondegeneracy of \(g\) identifies this covector with a unique vector \(\nabla_X^*Z\).

Average connection

The Levi-Civita connection is the midpoint of a dual pair.

Theorem. If \(\nabla\) and \(\nabla^*\) are torsion-free and dual, then \[ \nabla^{(0)}=\frac12(\nabla+\nabla^*) \] is the Levi-Civita connection of \(g\).

Proof. The average connection is torsion-free. Adding the duality identity to the same identity with \(\nabla\) and \(\nabla^*\) exchanged shows that \(\nabla^{(0)}g=0\). By uniqueness of the torsion-free metric connection, it is Levi-Civita.

Dual geodesic coordinates

Dual flatness supplies primal and dual affine coordinate systems.

Definition. A pair \((\nabla,\nabla^*)\) is dually flat if both connections are flat and dual with respect to \(g\). Coordinates affine for \(\nabla\) and \(\nabla^*\) are called dual affine coordinates.

Remark. Exponential-family natural parameters and expectation parameters are the basic statistical example.

Pythagorean geometry

Duality is the reason orthogonal projection identities involve one geodesic of each type.

Remark. The generalized Pythagorean theorem uses a \(\nabla\)-geodesic and a \(\nabla^*\)-geodesic meeting orthogonally under \(g\).
