A divergence is not a metric, but its derivatives near the diagonal generate a metric and affine connections. This is the main mechanism by which information geometry extracts local geometry from global contrast functions.

Divergence functions

A divergence vanishes on the diagonal and is positive away from it, at least locally.

Definition. A smooth divergence on a manifold \(M\) is a function \(D:M\times M\to[0,\infty)\) such that \(D(p,q)=0\) iff \(p=q\), and the first derivatives vanish on the diagonal: \[ \partial_iD(p,q)|_{q=p}=0,\qquad \partial_{j'}D(p,q)|_{q=p}=0. \]

Remark. The prime on an index indicates differentiation in the second argument. This convention is essential when deriving two dual connections from one function.

Metric from second derivatives

The Hessian of a divergence at the diagonal gives the Riemannian metric.

Theorem. Define \[ g_{ij}(p)=-\partial_i\partial_{j'}D(p,q)\big|_{q=p}. \] Under the usual positivity assumptions, \(g\) is a Riemannian metric.

Proof. Because the first derivatives vanish on the diagonal, the second-order Taylor expansion of \(D(p,p+\mathrm{d}p)\) is quadratic. The mixed Hessian gives the bilinear form controlling this quadratic term. Nonnegativity of \(D\) near the diagonal forces this form to be positive semidefinite; strict local separation gives positive definiteness.

Dual connections from third derivatives

Third derivatives distinguish the two arguments of the divergence and produce dual affine connections.

Definition. The divergence-induced connections are commonly defined by \[ \Gamma_{ijk}=-\partial_i\partial_j\partial_{k'}D|_{\mathrm{diag}}, \qquad \Gamma^*_{ijk}=-\partial_{i'}\partial_{j'}\partial_kD|_{\mathrm{diag}}. \]

Proposition. The two connections are dual with respect to the metric generated by the same divergence.

Proof. Differentiate the identity \(g_{ij}=-\partial_i\partial_{j'}D|_{\mathrm{diag}}\) along a coordinate direction. The derivative hits both arguments on the diagonal and splits into the two third-derivative expressions above. This is exactly the coordinate form of metric duality.

Local versus global information

Different divergences can generate the same local metric while differing globally.

Remark. KL, Hellinger, and many \(f\)-divergences all generate Fisher information locally, but their global projection behavior and asymmetry differ. Local geometry and global optimization should not be conflated.
