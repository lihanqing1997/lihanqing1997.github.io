10. Examples of Fisher geometry

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/information-geometry/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/information-geometry/probability-simplex/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/information-geometry/affine-connections-covariant-derivatives/" %}

Small parametric families make the abstract definitions concrete. Bernoulli, multinomial, and normal models show different faces of the Fisher metric.

Bernoulli family

Proposition. For \(X\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(p)\), the Fisher metric in coordinate \(p\in(0,1)\) is \[ g(p)=\frac{1}{p(1-p)}. \] The coordinate \(\varphi=2\arcsin\sqrt p\) makes the metric Euclidean on an interval.

Multinomial family

Proposition. The multinomial model inherits the simplex Fisher metric \(\sum_i u_iv_i/p_i\). Its square-root image lies on a sphere, so categorical Fisher geometry is spherical geometry in disguise.

Normal location-scale family

Proposition. For \(N(\mu,\sigma^2)\), the Fisher metric is \[ \mathrm{d}s^2=\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\,\mathrm{d}\mu^2+\frac{2}{\sigma^2}\,\mathrm{d}\sigma^2. \] It is a constant negative curvature geometry after a simple rescaling.

Lesson

Remark. The same metric definition yields interval, spherical, and hyperbolic-looking geometries depending on the statistical model.
