19. Forward and reverse KL projections

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/information-geometry/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/information-geometry/kl-divergence-canonical-example/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/information-geometry/f-divergences-information-monotonicity/" %}

Forward and reverse KL define different projections because KL divergence is directed, even though both directions recover the same Fisher metric locally.

Two KL directions

Definition. For a target \(p\) and an approximating family \(\mathcal Q\), forward KL minimizes \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p:q)\), while reverse KL minimizes \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q:p)\) over \(q\in\mathcal Q\).

Information and moment projections

Definition. In information geometry, minimizing \(D(p:q)\) is often called an I-projection in one convention, while minimizing the reversed direction is the dual projection. The essential point is the chosen affine structure: one direction is natural for mixture constraints, the other for exponential constraints.

Pythagorean identity

Theorem. On a dually flat manifold, if a projection is orthogonal in the dual affine sense, KL divergence decomposes as a Pythagorean identity: \(D(p:r)=D(p:q^\star)+D(q^\star:r)\), with direction determined by the projection geometry.

Approximation behavior

Proposition. Forward KL penalizes missing mass under \(p\), so it tends to cover the target support. Reverse KL penalizes placing mass where \(p\) is small, so it may concentrate on one high-density region. This heuristic depends on the family \(\mathcal Q\) and should be read through the exact optimization problem.

Local geometry

Remark. Both directions have the same second-order Fisher metric at \(p=q\), but their third-order terms interchange the exponential and mixture connections.
