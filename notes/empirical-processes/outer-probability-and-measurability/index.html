Large function classes may produce nonmeasurable suprema. Empirical-process theory therefore states many results with outer probability and outer expectation before separability assumptions remove the technical burden.

Definition. For a possibly nonmeasurable random variable \(Z\), \(E^*Z=\inf\{EY:Y\ge Z,\;Y\ \text{measurable}\}\), and \(P^*(A)=\inf\{P(B):A\subset B,\;B\ \text{measurable}\}\).

Outer convergence

  • Statements such as \(\|P_n-P\|_{\mathcal F}\to0\) are often interpreted as outer convergence unless measurability is known.
  • A countable subclass that approximates every \(f\in\mathcal F\) usually makes the supremum measurable.
  • Outer notation is not decoration: it keeps theorems valid for nonseparable spaces such as \(\ell^\infty(\mathcal F)\).

Separability

When applying the theory, first prove or cite separability; otherwise keep \(P^*\), \(E^*\), and measurable majorants in the statement.

Outer probability criterion

Definition. Outer convergence \(Z_n\to0\) in outer probability means \(P^*(|Z_n|>\varepsilon)\to0\) for every \(\varepsilon>0\). If the relevant supremum is measurable, this reduces to ordinary convergence in probability.

Countable reductions

A common separability check is to find a countable \(\mathcal F_0\subset\mathcal F\) such that for every \(f\in\mathcal F\) there are \(f_m\in\mathcal F_0\) with \(f_m(X)\to f(X)\) sufficiently well. Then the supremum over \(\mathcal F\) can often be replaced by one over \(\mathcal F_0\).
