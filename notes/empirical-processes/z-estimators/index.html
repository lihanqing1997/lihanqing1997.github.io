Z-estimators solve empirical estimating equations. Empirical processes control the uniform remainder in the linearization.

Definition. A Z-estimator satisfies \(P_n\psi_{\hat\theta}=o_p(n^{-1/2})\). If \(P\psi_{\theta_0}=0\) and \(P\psi_\theta\) is differentiable with derivative \(A\), then linearization targets \(A(\hat\theta-\theta_0)\).

Linearization ingredients

  • The stochastic term is \(\mathbb G_n\psi_{\theta_0}\).
  • Equicontinuity justifies replacing \(\mathbb G_n\psi_{\hat\theta}\) by \(\mathbb G_n\psi_{\theta_0}\).
  • The final expansion is usually \(\sqrt n(\hat\theta-\theta_0)=-A^{-1}\mathbb G_n\psi_{\theta_0}+o_p(1)\).

Empirical-process remainder

This page is the empirical-process layer beneath score equations, GMM, estimating equations, and semiparametric influence-function expansions.

Z-estimator expansion

Proposition. A clean proof checks three ingredients: consistency of \(\hat\theta\), differentiability of \(\theta\mapsto P\psi_\theta\), and stochastic equicontinuity of \(\mathbb G_n(\psi_\theta-\psi_{\theta_0})\) near \(\theta_0\).

Equicontinuity of estimating functions

The empirical-process contribution is the third ingredient. It prevents the empirical noise from changing too much when \(\theta_0\) is replaced by the random \(\hat\theta\). Without it, the Taylor expansion of the estimating equation is not justified uniformly.
