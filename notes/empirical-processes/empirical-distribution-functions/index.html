The one-dimensional empirical distribution function is the historical prototype. It shows how a simple indexed process leads to the Brownian bridge.

Definition. \(F_n(t)=P_n1_{(-\infty,t]}\), \(F(t)=P1_{(-\infty,t]}\), and \(\alpha_n(t)=\sqrt n\{F_n(t)-F(t)\}\). If \(F\) is continuous, \(\alpha_n\) converges to a Brownian bridge indexed by \(F(t)\).

Brownian bridge limit

  • Glivenko-Cantelli gives \(\sup_t|F_n(t)-F(t)|\to0\).
  • Donsker's theorem gives the process limit of \(\sqrt n(F_n-F)\).
  • Kolmogorov-Smirnov and Cramér-von Mises statistics are continuous or nearly continuous functionals of this process.

Distribution-free transformation

Keep this example in mind: most later theorems are high-dimensional versions of the same story, with richer function classes replacing intervals.

Brownian bridge covariance

Theorem. For continuous \(F\), the limiting Brownian bridge \(B_F(t)=B(F(t))\) has covariance \[ \operatorname{Cov}\{B_F(s),B_F(t)\}=F(s\wedge t)-F(s)F(t). \] This covariance is exactly the covariance of the indicators \(1_{(-\infty,s]}\) and \(1_{(-\infty,t]}\).

Probability integral transform

The probability integral transform reduces the continuous case to \(F(t)=t\) on \([0,1]\). This is why distribution-free limits appear for Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistics under a fully specified continuous null.
