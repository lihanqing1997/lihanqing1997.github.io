课程 5. 动态传输与连续性方程

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="上一章" back_url="/notes/optimal-transport/course-quadratic-transport/" next_label="下一章" next_url="/notes/optimal-transport/course-gradient-flows/" %}

The Kantorovich problem compares only initial and final measures. The dynamic formulation resolves the transport into a time-dependent density and velocity field. For quadratic cost, the squared Wasserstein distance is the least kinetic energy needed to move one measure into the other.

1. The continuity equation

For a smooth density \(\rho_t(x)\) transported by a smooth velocity \(v_t(x)\), conservation of mass reads \[ \partial_t\rho_t+\nabla\cdot(\rho_t v_t)=0. \] The weak formulation extends this equation to arbitrary probability measures.

Definition. A narrowly continuous curve \(t\mapsto\mu_t\in\mathcal P(\mathbb R^d)\) and a Borel velocity field \(v_t:\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^d\) solve the continuity equation if \[ \int_0^1\int_{\mathbb R^d} \left[\partial_t\zeta(t,x)+\nabla\zeta(t,x)\cdot v_t(x)\right] \,\mathrm d\mu_t(x)\,\mathrm dt=0 \] for every \(\zeta\in C_c^\infty((0,1)\times\mathbb R^d)\). Endpoint terms are included when test functions do not vanish at \(t=0,1\).

If \(X_t\) solves the ordinary differential equation \(\dot X_t=v_t(X_t)\) and \(\mu_t=(X_t)_\#\mu_0\), then the chain rule proves the weak equation: \[ \frac{\mathrm d}{\mathrm dt}\int\zeta(t,x)\,\mathrm d\mu_t(x) =\int[\partial_t\zeta+\nabla\zeta\cdot v_t]\,\mathrm d\mu_t. \]

2. Action and admissible curves

Definition. The kinetic action of an admissible pair \((\mu_t,v_t)\) is \[ \mathcal A(\mu,v)=\int_0^1\int_{\mathbb R^d}|v_t(x)|^2\,\mathrm d\mu_t(x)\,\mathrm dt. \] More generally, the \(p\)-action uses \(|v|^p\).

The measure-valued momentum \(m_t=v_t\mu_t\) is often preferable in optimization. With \(\mu_t=\rho_t\,\mathrm dx\), the action becomes \(\int|m_t|^2/\rho_t\), interpreted as \(+\infty\) where \(m_t\) is not absolutely continuous with respect to \(\rho_t\). The map \((\rho,m)\mapsto|m|^2/\rho\) is jointly convex, so the dynamic problem is a convex optimization problem.

3. The Benamou-Brenier formula

Benamou-Brenier theorem. For \(\mu_0,\mu_1\in\mathcal P_2(\mathbb R^d)\), \[ W_2^2(\mu_0,\mu_1) =\inf\left\{ \int_0^1\int|v_t|^2\,\mathrm d\mu_t\,\mathrm dt: \partial_t\mu_t+\nabla\cdot(v_t\mu_t)=0,\ \mu_{t=0}=\mu_0,\ \mu_{t=1}=\mu_1 \right\}. \]

Upper bound. Let \(\pi\) be an optimal coupling and place a probability measure on straight paths \(\omega_{x,y}(t)=(1-t)x+ty\) by sampling \((x,y)\sim\pi\). Its time marginal is \(\mu_t=((1-t)x+ty)_\#\pi\), and each particle has velocity \(y-x\). The induced Eulerian velocity is the conditional mean of \(y-x\) given the current position. Jensen's inequality shows \[ \int_0^1\int|v_t|^2\,\mathrm d\mu_t\,\mathrm dt \le\int|y-x|^2\,\mathrm d\pi=W_2^2(\mu_0,\mu_1). \]

Lower bound. A superposition principle represents every finite-action solution of the continuity equation by a probability measure on absolutely continuous particle paths \(\omega\), with \(\dot\omega(t)=v_t(\omega(t))\) almost everywhere. The endpoint law is a coupling \(\pi\) of \(\mu_0,\mu_1\). For each path, Cauchy-Schwarz gives \[ |\omega(1)-\omega(0)|^2 \le\int_0^1|\dot\omega(t)|^2\,\mathrm dt. \] Integrating over paths gives \(W_2^2(\mu_0,\mu_1)\le\mathcal A(\mu,v)\). Combining the bounds proves the formula.

4. Absolutely continuous curves in Wasserstein space

Definition. A curve \(\mu_t\) is \(2\)-absolutely continuous in \((\mathcal P_2,W_2)\) if there exists \(g\in L^2(0,1)\) such that \[ W_2(\mu_s,\mu_t)\le\int_s^t g(r)\,\mathrm dr. \] Its metric derivative is \[ |\dot\mu_t|=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{W_2(\mu_{t+h},\mu_t)}{|h|} \] whenever the limit exists.

Velocity characterization. A curve \(\mu_t\) is \(2\)-absolutely continuous if and only if it admits a velocity field \(v_t\in L^2(\mu_t)\) solving the continuity equation with \(\int_0^1\|v_t\|_{L^2(\mu_t)}^2\,\mathrm dt<\infty\). Among all such velocities there is a unique minimal-norm velocity, and \[ |\dot\mu_t|=\|v_t\|_{L^2(\mu_t)} \quad\text{for almost every }t. \]

The tangent space at \(\mu\) is naturally identified with \[ T_\mu\mathcal P_2=\overline{\{\nabla\phi:\phi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R^d)\}}^{L^2(\mu)}. \] Components orthogonal to gradients do not change the measure to first order and only increase kinetic energy.

5. Geodesics and velocity potentials

Along a smooth \(W_2\)-geodesic, the minimal velocity has gradient form \(v_t=\nabla\phi_t\). Formally, the pair satisfies \[ \partial_t\mu_t+\nabla\cdot(\mu_t\nabla\phi_t)=0,\qquad \partial_t\phi_t+\tfrac12|\nabla\phi_t|^2=0. \] The second equation is the Hamilton-Jacobi equation. Its gradient is the pressureless Euler equation \[ \partial_t v_t+(v_t\cdot\nabla)v_t=0, \] expressing that particles travel at constant velocity along straight geodesics.

Translation. If \(\mu_t=(x\mapsto x+ta)_\#\mu_0\), then \(v_t\equiv a\), the continuity equation holds, and the action is \(|a|^2=W_2^2(\mu_0,\mu_1)\).

Dilation. If \(T(x)=\lambda x\) with \(\lambda>0\), then \(X_t(x)=(1-t+t\lambda)x\). The Eulerian velocity is \[ v_t(z)=\frac{\lambda-1}{1-t+t\lambda}z. \] This example is useful for checking continuity-equation calculations and scaling laws.

6. Dynamic duality

The Benamou-Brenier problem has a Hamilton-Jacobi dual: \[ \frac12W_2^2(\mu_0,\mu_1) =\sup_\phi\left\{ \int\phi_1\,\mathrm d\mu_1-\int\phi_0\,\mathrm d\mu_0: \partial_t\phi+\tfrac12|\nabla\phi|^2\le0 \right\}. \] To see weak duality, differentiate \(\int\phi_t\,\mathrm d\mu_t\), use the continuity equation, and complete the square: \[ \partial_t\phi+\nabla\phi\cdot v \le \partial_t\phi+\tfrac12|\nabla\phi|^2+\tfrac12|v|^2 \le\tfrac12|v|^2. \]

7. Otto's formal Riemannian calculus

For smooth positive densities, a tangent vector \(\dot\rho\) is represented by a potential \(\phi\) through \[ \dot\rho=-\nabla\cdot(\rho\nabla\phi). \] The formal metric is \[ \langle\dot\rho_1,\dot\rho_2\rangle_\rho =\int\nabla\phi_1\cdot\nabla\phi_2\,\rho\,\mathrm dx. \] This is not merely notation. Its induced path length is exactly \(W_2\), and it predicts the correct gradient-flow PDEs. Rigorous metric-gradient-flow theory replaces the formal differential geometry when densities or energies are nonsmooth.

8. Static and dynamic viewpoints

The static optimizer is a joint law of endpoints. The dynamic optimizer is a least-action path of measures. Straight-line interpolation of an optimal endpoint plan connects them. The dynamic language is indispensable for tangent vectors, gradient flows, fluid formulations, and numerical solvers based on continuity equations.
