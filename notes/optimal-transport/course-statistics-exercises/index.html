课程 8. 统计、习题与延伸阅读

完整课程正文目前以英文撰写。

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="上一章" back_url="/notes/optimal-transport/course-computational-transport/" next_label="课程目录" next_url="/notes/optimal-transport/" %}

This final chapter connects optimal transport to probability and statistics, explains its main limitations, and provides exercises with solutions. The exercises are part of the course rather than decoration: they test the compactness, duality, geometric, dynamic, and computational arguments developed earlier.

1. Transport inequalities

Definition. A reference measure \(\mu\) satisfies a \(T_2(C)\) inequality if \[ W_2^2(\nu,\mu)\le2C\,\operatorname{KL}(\nu\,\|\,\mu) \] for every \(\nu\ll\mu\), where \[ \operatorname{KL}(\nu\,\|\,\mu)=\int\log\left(\frac{\mathrm d\nu}{\mathrm d\mu}\right)\,\mathrm d\nu. \]

Talagrand inequality for strongly log-concave measures. If \[ \mu(\mathrm dx)=Z^{-1}e^{-V(x)}\,\mathrm dx \] on \(\mathbb R^d\) and \(D^2V\succeq\lambda I\) for some \(\lambda>0\), then \[ W_2^2(\nu,\mu)\le\frac{2}{\lambda}\operatorname{KL}(\nu\,\|\,\mu). \]

Transport inequalities convert an information discrepancy into a geometric discrepancy. Combined with the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality, a \(T_1\) inequality yields concentration bounds for Lipschitz observables. Combined with entropy dissipation, \(T_2\) gives convergence in \(W_2\) for Fokker-Planck and sampling dynamics.

2. Empirical measures and sample complexity

For i.i.d. samples \(X_1,\ldots,X_n\sim\mu\), define \[ \widehat\mu_n=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n\delta_{X_i}. \] Under a finite \(p\)-moment, \(W_p(\widehat\mu_n,\mu)\to0\) almost surely. This follows from the law of large numbers for narrow convergence plus convergence and uniform integrability of empirical \(p\)-moments.

The rate can be slow in high dimension. For regular \(d\)-dimensional distributions and \(d>2p\), the characteristic scale is \(n^{-1/d}\) for \(W_p\). This is the curse of dimensionality. Low-dimensional structure, smoothness, projections, regularization, or model assumptions are needed to obtain faster behavior in genuinely high-dimensional problems.

One dimension is special. The quantile representation reduces empirical transport to an empirical-process problem. This often yields root-\(n\)-type behavior under regularity assumptions, far faster than the generic high-dimensional rate.

3. Statistical uses

  • Two-sample comparison. \(W_p(\widehat\mu_n,\widehat\nu_m)\) detects changes in location, scale, and shape, but calibration depends on dimension and regularization.
  • Distributional robustness. Wasserstein balls define uncertainty sets around an empirical law. Duality often turns worst-case expectations over the ball into tractable regularized objectives.
  • Barycenters and aggregation. Wasserstein barycenters average distributions while preserving geometric features such as translated peaks.
  • Domain adaptation. A coupling aligns source and target feature distributions. The scientific validity depends on whether the chosen ground cost represents meaningful cross-domain similarity.
  • Generative modeling. Wasserstein objectives provide geometrically meaningful gradients when supports are separated, especially through the \(W_1\) dual.
  • Bayesian computation. Wasserstein distances compare posterior approximations and quantify perturbations, but should not replace problem-specific loss functions without justification.

4. Modeling choices and limitations

  1. The ground metric is part of the model. Rescaling coordinates changes transport costs. Units, feature weights, and invariances must be specified.
  2. Outliers can be expensive. Large \(p\) strongly penalizes distant mass. Robust, trimmed, or unbalanced variants may be preferable.
  3. High-dimensional estimation is difficult. Exact OT can have poor sample complexity even when optimization is solved perfectly.
  4. Entropic OT changes the estimand. It improves computation and smoothness but introduces regularization bias.
  5. A coupling is not automatically causal. An optimal statistical matching is determined by marginals and cost; it does not identify a causal correspondence without additional assumptions.

5. Exercises

  1. Moment bound. Prove directly that the product coupling has finite \(p\)-cost when \(\mu,\nu\in\mathcal P_p(X)\).
  2. Diagonal plans. Show that a coupling of \(\mu,\nu\) concentrated on \(\{(x,x)\}\) implies \(\mu=\nu\).
  3. Dirac endpoint. Prove \(W_p(\mu,\delta_y)^p=\int d(x,y)^p\,\mathrm d\mu(x)\).
  4. Bernoulli transport. On \(\{0,1\}\) with distance \(|x-y|\), compute \(W_p\) between Bernoulli laws with success probabilities \(a\) and \(b\).
  5. Uniform intervals. Find the optimal map from the uniform law on \([0,1]\) to the uniform law on \([2,5]\), and compute \(W_2^2\).
  6. Translation lower bound. If \(\nu=(x\mapsto x+a)_\#\mu\), prove \(W_2(\mu,\nu)=|a|\) using only Jensen's inequality and the translation coupling.
  7. Dual certificate. Let \(c(x,y)=|x-y|\). Show that every \(1\)-Lipschitz \(f\) gives a feasible dual pair \((f,-f)\).
  8. Two Gaussians in one dimension. Compute \(W_2^2(N(m_0,\sigma_0^2),N(m_1,\sigma_1^2))\).
  9. Geodesic action. For the translation curve \(\mu_t=(x\mapsto x+ta)_\#\mu_0\), verify the continuity equation and Benamou-Brenier action.
  10. Entropy flow. Starting from \(\mathcal H(\rho)=\int\rho\log\rho\), derive the heat equation formally.
  11. Sinkhorn marginals. If \(u=a\oslash(Kv)\), show that \(\operatorname{diag}(u)K\operatorname{diag}(v)\) has row sums \(a\).
  12. Complementary slackness. In a finite transport problem, prove that a feasible \(P\) and feasible \((f,g)\) are optimal if \(P_{ij}>0\) implies \(f_i+g_j=C_{ij}\).

6. Solutions

  1. For \(x_0\in X\), use \[ d(x,y)^p\le2^{p-1}[d(x,x_0)^p+d(y,x_0)^p] \] and integrate against \(\mu\otimes\nu\).
  2. For any Borel \(A\), diagonal concentration gives \[ \mu(A)=\pi(A\times X)=\pi(A\times A)=\pi(X\times A)=\nu(A). \]
  3. There is only one coupling with second marginal \(\delta_y\): \(\mu\otimes\delta_y\). Evaluating its cost gives the formula.
  4. Exactly \(|a-b|\) mass must cross between \(0\) and \(1\). Since every crossing has \(d^p=1\), \[ W_p^p=|a-b|,\qquad W_p=|a-b|^{1/p}. \]
  5. Quantiles are \(F_\mu^{-1}(u)=u\) and \(F_\nu^{-1}(u)=2+3u\). Thus \(T(x)=2+3x\), and \[ W_2^2=\int_0^1(2+2u)^2\,\mathrm du=\frac{28}{3}. \]
  6. The translation coupling gives \(W_2^2\le|a|^2\). Every coupling satisfies \[ \int|x-y|^2\,\mathrm d\pi\ge\left|\int(x-y)\,\mathrm d\pi\right|^2=|a|^2 \] because the means differ by \(a\).
  7. The Lipschitz condition is \(f(x)-f(y)\le|x-y|\), which is precisely \(f(x)+[-f(y)]\le c(x,y)\).
  8. Use the quantile representation \(m+\sigma\Phi^{-1}(u)\): \[ W_2^2=(m_0-m_1)^2+(\sigma_0-\sigma_1)^2, \] because \(\int_0^1\Phi^{-1}(u)\,\mathrm du=0\) and \(\int_0^1\Phi^{-1}(u)^2\,\mathrm du=1\).
  9. The velocity is \(v_t\equiv a\). For a test function, differentiation of \(\int\zeta(t,x+ta)\,\mathrm d\mu_0(x)\) gives the weak continuity equation. The action is \(\int_0^1\int|a|^2\,\mathrm d\mu_t\,\mathrm dt=|a|^2\).
  10. The first variation is \(1+\log\rho\), so \[ \partial_t\rho=\nabla\cdot(\rho\nabla\log\rho)=\Delta\rho. \]
  11. The \(i\)th row sum is \(u_i(Kv)_i=[a_i/(Kv)_i](Kv)_i=a_i\).
  12. Complementary slackness gives \[ \langle C,P\rangle =\sum_{ij}(f_i+g_j)P_{ij} =a^\top f+b^\top g. \] Weak duality then forces equality of the primal and dual optima.

7. A serious reading sequence

  1. Cedric Villani, Topics in Optimal Transportation, for duality, \(c\)-convexity, and early theory.
  2. Filippo Santambrogio, Optimal Transport for Applied Mathematicians, for a direct route from measures to PDE and applications.
  3. Luigi Ambrosio, Nicola Gigli, and Giuseppe Savare, Gradient Flows, for metric gradient flows and the rigorous dynamic theory.
  4. Cedric Villani, Optimal Transport: Old and New, for broad geometric and analytic developments.
  5. Gabriel Peyre and Marco Cuturi, Computational Optimal Transport, for discrete, entropic, and algorithmic methods.

8. Mastery checklist

You should now be able to define couplings and pushforwards, prove existence of optimal plans, derive weak duality, use \(c\)-transforms and complementary slackness, prove the metric property of \(W_p\), compute one-dimensional transport, explain Brenier's theorem and its assumptions, derive the Benamou-Brenier formula, identify basic Wasserstein gradient flows, derive Sinkhorn scaling, and separate optimization error from statistical and regularization error. Those abilities form a durable foundation for reading research papers in optimal transport.
