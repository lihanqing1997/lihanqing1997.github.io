Exploration is the statistical cost of learning what actions do. Regret measures this cost by comparing the learner's accumulated reward with an oracle policy.

Regret

Regret converts online learning into a sequence of excess-risk comparisons.

Definition. For rewards and optimal value benchmark \(V^*\), cumulative regret over horizon \(T\) is \[ \operatorname{Reg}(T)=\sum_{t=1}^T\{V^*(S_t)-R_t\} \] in episodic or continuing forms adapted to the model. For bandits, \(\operatorname{Reg}(T)=T\mu^*-\sum_{t=1}^T\mu_{A_t}\).

Remark. Sublinear regret means the average cost of exploration vanishes.

Optimism

Optimism chooses actions that could plausibly be best given current uncertainty.

Proposition. In a bandit problem, if upper confidence bounds \(U_t(a)\) satisfy \(\mu_a\le U_t(a)\) for all arms on a high-probability event, choosing \(A_t\in\operatorname*{argmax}_aU_t(a)\) explores arms whose uncertainty can still affect optimality.

Proof. On the confidence event, the optimal mean \(\mu^*\le U_t(a^*)\le U_t(A_t)\). If \(A_t\) is suboptimal, its upper bound must still be high enough to cover the optimal mean. Thus either its empirical mean is unusually high or its uncertainty radius is still large; confidence rules out the former and limits the number of times the latter can occur.

Regret decomposition

For finite bandits, regret is a sum over suboptimal arm pulls.

Proposition. For arm gaps \(\Delta_a=\mu^*-\mu_a\), \[ \mathbb E\operatorname{Reg}(T)=\sum_{a:\Delta_a>0}\Delta_a\,\mathbb E N_a(T). \]

Proof. Each time arm \(a\) is pulled, the expected loss relative to the best arm is \(\Delta_a\). Sum over time and group by arm, then take expectations.

Exploration in MDPs

MDP exploration is harder because information is gathered through state visitation.

Remark. Optimism and posterior sampling extend to MDPs by adding uncertainty bonuses or sampling plausible models. The difficulty is that actions affect both immediate reward and future information through the transition kernel.
