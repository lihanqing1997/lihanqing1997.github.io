16. Multi-armed bandits

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/reinforcement-learning/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/reinforcement-learning/exploration-and-regret/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/reinforcement-learning/off-policy-evaluation/" %}

Bandits are the simplest reinforcement-learning problems with exploration. There is no state transition, only repeated action choice under uncertainty.

Stochastic bandit model

Each arm has an unknown reward distribution, and the learner observes only the reward of the chosen arm.

Definition. A \(K\)-armed stochastic bandit has reward distributions \(\nu_1,\ldots,\nu_K\) with means \(\mu_a\). At time \(t\), the learner chooses \(A_t\), observes \(R_t\sim\nu_{A_t}\), and seeks small regret \[ \operatorname{Reg}(T)=T\mu^*-\sum_{t=1}^TR_t. \]

Proposition. The expected regret is \(\sum_a\Delta_a\mathbb E N_a(T)\), where \(\Delta_a=\mu^*-\mu_a\).

Proof. Take expectation of \(T\mu^*-\sum_tR_t\). Conditional on \(A_t=a\), \(\mathbb E R_t=\mu_a\). Group the resulting \(\mu^*-\mu_a\) terms by arm.

Upper confidence bounds

UCB balances empirical mean and uncertainty radius.

Definition. For sub-Gaussian rewards, UCB chooses \[ A_t\in\operatorname*{argmax}_a\left\{\hat\mu_a(t)+\sqrt{\frac{2\log t}{N_a(t)}}\right\}. \]

Theorem. For each suboptimal arm \(a\), UCB pulls arm \(a\) \(O((\log T)/\Delta_a^2)\) times in expectation, yielding regret \(O(\sum_a(\log T)/\Delta_a)\).

Proof. On the high-probability event that all empirical means stay within their confidence radii, a suboptimal arm can be selected only if its confidence radius is large enough to bridge the gap \(\Delta_a\). This implies \(N_a(t)\lesssim(\log T)/\Delta_a^2\). The failure event contributes only a bounded additional amount by concentration and summability.

Thompson sampling

Posterior sampling randomizes according to uncertainty about which arm is best.

Definition. Thompson sampling maintains a posterior distribution over arm means, samples a plausible parameter vector from the posterior, and pulls the arm optimal for that sample.

Remark. For conjugate Bernoulli-beta bandits, the update is explicit. Regret analysis shows that posterior sampling often matches logarithmic gap-dependent rates under regularity.

Lower bounds

Logarithmic regret is unavoidable for uniformly good algorithms in parametric bandits.

Theorem. For many one-parameter reward families, any uniformly efficient algorithm must satisfy asymptotic lower bounds of Lai-Robbins type: \[ \liminf_{T\to\infty}\frac{\mathbb E N_a(T)}{\log T}\ge \frac{1}{\operatorname{KL}(\nu_a,\nu_{a^*})} \] with the appropriate alternative distribution in the denominator.

Proof. If a suboptimal arm is sampled too rarely, the data cannot statistically distinguish the true instance from an alternative in which that arm is optimal. A change-of-measure argument relates the probability of correct behavior under the two instances to the accumulated KL information, forcing at least logarithmically many samples.
