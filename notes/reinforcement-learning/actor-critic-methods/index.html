Actor-critic methods combine policy optimization with value estimation. The actor updates the policy; the critic estimates value or advantage information used by the actor.

Actor and critic

The two time scales correspond to two coupled stochastic approximation problems.

Definition. An actor-critic method maintains policy parameters \(\theta\) and critic parameters \(w\). A typical update is \[ w_{t+1}=w_t+\beta_t\,\widehat{\Delta}_w, \qquad \theta_{t+1}=\theta_t+\alpha_t\,\widehat{\Delta}_\theta, \] where the critic estimates \(v_{\pi_\theta}\), \(q_{\pi_\theta}\), or \(A_{\pi_\theta}\), and the actor uses this estimate in a policy-gradient step.

Remark. The critic is not an objective by itself; it supplies a lower-variance approximate gradient direction for the actor.

TD critic for policy gradient

A one-step TD error can estimate an advantage under the current policy.

Proposition. If \(v=v_\pi\), then \[ \mathbb E\{R_{t+1}+\gamma v(S_{t+1})-v(S_t)\mid S_t=s,A_t=a\}=A_\pi(s,a). \]

Proof. The conditional expectation of \(R_{t+1}+\gamma v_\pi(S_{t+1})\) given \((s,a)\) is \(q_\pi(s,a)\). Subtracting \(v_\pi(s)\) gives \(A_\pi(s,a)\).

Two-time-scale convergence

Classical actor-critic theory often assumes the critic equilibrates faster than the actor.

Theorem. Under standard smoothness, ergodicity, boundedness, and stepsize conditions with \(\alpha_t/\beta_t\to0\), two-time-scale actor-critic tracks an ODE in which the critic is near its fixed point for the current actor.

Proof. The critic recursion evolves on the faster time scale \(\beta_t\) and converges to the stable solution of its projected Bellman equation for quasi-static \(\theta\). The actor recursion sees the critic error as asymptotically negligible and follows the mean policy-gradient ODE. This is the standard two-time-scale stochastic approximation argument.

Bias from approximate critics

A critic can reduce variance but introduce bias if it estimates the wrong quantity.

Remark. Compatible function approximation and advantage centering are designed to make the critic's approximation error orthogonal to policy-score directions, thereby reducing gradient bias.
