Policy-gradient estimators can have high variance. Baselines reduce variance without changing the mean when they do not depend on the sampled action in a way that breaks the score identity.

Unbiased baselines

The baseline principle is an immediate consequence of policy normalization.

Theorem. For any state-dependent baseline \(b(S_t)\), \[ \mathbb E_{\pi_\theta}\left[\nabla\log\pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t)b(S_t)\mid S_t\right]=0. \]

Proof. Condition on \(S_t=s\). Then \[ \int \nabla\log\pi_\theta(a\mid s)b(s)\pi_\theta(\mathrm{d}a\mid s) =b(s)\int\nabla\pi_\theta(\mathrm{d}a\mid s) =b(s)\nabla1=0. \]

Optimal scalar baseline

For a single score vector, the variance-minimizing constant baseline has a weighted least-squares form.

Proposition. For an estimator \(g=(G-b)Z\), where \(Z=\nabla\log\pi_\theta(A\mid S)\), the constant \(b\) minimizing \(\mathbb E\|g\|_2^2\) is \[ b^*=\frac{\mathbb E(G\|Z\|_2^2)}{\mathbb E\|Z\|_2^2}. \]

Proof. Expand \(\mathbb E\|(G-b)Z\|^2=\mathbb E\{(G-b)^2\|Z\|^2\}\) as a quadratic function of \(b\). Differentiating and setting the derivative equal to zero gives the displayed ratio.

Reward-to-go

Removing past rewards from a score term lowers variance and keeps unbiasedness.

Proposition. In the likelihood-ratio gradient, the score at time \(t\) may be multiplied only by future return \(\sum_{k\ge t}\gamma^kR_{k+1}\); past reward terms have zero expectation with that score.

Proof. Past rewards are measurable with respect to the history before \(A_t\). Conditional on that history and \(S_t\), the score has mean zero by policy normalization. Therefore products with past rewards integrate to zero.

Control variates

Baselines are a special case of control variates.

Remark. A critic, generalized advantage estimation, and learned value baselines all try to approximate the conditional expectation structure that removes avoidable return noise while preserving the policy-gradient mean.
