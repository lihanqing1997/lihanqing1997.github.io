Policy-gradient methods optimize a differentiable policy directly. The policy-gradient theorem expresses the gradient without differentiating the transition law.

Objective and score identity

The log-derivative trick moves differentiation from the trajectory density to the policy score.

Definition. For a differentiable policy \(\pi_\theta\), define \[ J(\theta)=\mathbb E_{\mu,\pi_\theta}\sum_{t=0}^\infty\gamma^tR_{t+1}. \] The score identity is \(\nabla_\theta p_\theta(x)=p_\theta(x)\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x)\) whenever \(p_\theta(x)>0\).

Lemma. For any trajectory functional \(F(\tau)\), \[ \nabla_\theta\mathbb E_\theta F(\tau) =\mathbb E_\theta\{F(\tau)\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(\tau)\}, \] provided differentiation and integration may be interchanged.

Proof. Write the expectation as \(\int F(\tau)p_\theta(\tau)\mathrm{d}\tau\), differentiate under the integral, and substitute \(\nabla p_\theta=p_\theta\nabla\log p_\theta\).

Policy-gradient theorem

The gradient weights policy scores by action values under the discounted occupancy measure.

Theorem. For a discounted MDP with differentiable policy, \[ \nabla J(\theta)=\frac{1}{1-\gamma} \int d_{\mu,\pi_\theta}(\mathrm{d}s)\int \pi_\theta(\mathrm{d}a\mid s) \nabla\log\pi_\theta(a\mid s)q_{\pi_\theta}(s,a). \]

Proof. The trajectory density factors into the initial law, transition kernels, and policy probabilities. Transition kernels do not depend on \(\theta\), so \[ \nabla\log p_\theta(\tau)=\sum_{t\ge0}\nabla\log\pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t). \] Apply the score identity to the discounted return. Terms in which a score at time \(t\) is multiplied by rewards before time \(t\) vanish after conditioning on the past, since the conditional expectation of the score is zero. The remaining future rewards give \(q_{\pi_\theta}(S_t,A_t)\). Summing discounted state-action distributions yields the occupancy-measure form.

Compatible baselines

The theorem is unchanged when a state-dependent baseline is subtracted.

Proposition. For any bounded \(b(s)\), \[ \int \pi_\theta(\mathrm{d}a\mid s)\nabla\log\pi_\theta(a\mid s)b(s)=0. \]

Proof. The integrand equals \(b(s)\int\nabla\pi_\theta(\mathrm{d}a\mid s)\). Since \(\int\pi_\theta(\mathrm{d}a\mid s)=1\), differentiating the normalization gives zero.

Advantage form

Subtracting \(v_\pi(s)\) gives the advantage, often reducing variance.

Definition. The advantage function is \[ A_\pi(s,a)=q_\pi(s,a)-v_\pi(s). \] The policy gradient can use \(A_\pi\) in place of \(q_\pi\) because \(v_\pi(s)\) is a valid baseline.
