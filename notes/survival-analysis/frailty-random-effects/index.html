9. Frailty and random effects

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/survival-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/survival-analysis/time-dependent-covariates-risk-sets/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/survival-analysis/semiparametric-efficiency-cox/" %}

Shared frailty

Frailty models introduce latent heterogeneity. They are survival analogues of random-effects models, but the random effect enters multiplicatively in the hazard.

Definition. A shared frailty model for cluster \(g\) has \[ \lambda_{ig}(t\mid Z_{ig},W_g)=W_g\lambda_0(t)\exp(Z_{ig}^\top\beta), \] where \(W_g>0\) is a cluster-level random variable, often normalized by \(E W_g=1\).

Proposition. If \(W\sim\operatorname{Gamma}(a,a)\), then \(E W=1\) and \(\operatorname{var}(W)=1/a\). Marginal survival conditional on \(Z=z\) is \[ E\exp\{-W\Lambda_0(t)e^{z^\top\beta}\}=\left(1+{\Lambda_0(t)e^{z^\top\beta}\over a}\right)^{-a}. \]

Proof. Use the Laplace transform of a gamma random variable: \(E e^{-sW}=(1+s/a)^{-a}\). Substitute \(s=\Lambda_0(t)e^{z^\top\beta}\).

Remark. Frailty induces dependence between event times in the same cluster. Ignoring this dependence can underestimate uncertainty even if the regression coefficient is approximately unbiased.

Marginal dependence

Proposition. Shared frailty induces positive association among event times in the same cluster. Conditional on \(W_g\) the subjects are independent, but marginally a large \(W_g\) simultaneously increases all hazards in the cluster.

Proof. Conditional survival probabilities factor given \(W_g\). After integrating out \(W_g\), products of decreasing functions of the same positive random variable are positively associated; high frailty lowers all conditional survivals together.

Identifiability of frailty variance

Remark. Frailty variance is weakly identified when clusters are small or follow-up is short. Apparent heterogeneity can also be produced by omitted fixed covariates, so frailty should be read as a dependence model, not automatically as a biological random effect.
