12. Bayesian Cox models and BvM questions

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/survival-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/survival-analysis/high-dimensional-cox-regression/" %}

Bayesian semiparametric structure

A Bayesian Cox model must put prior mass on both \(\beta\) and the baseline hazard or cumulative hazard. For BvM questions, the main issue is whether uncertainty in the nuisance baseline distorts the first-order posterior for \(\beta\).

Definition. A Bayesian Cox prior may take \(\Pi=\Pi_\beta\otimes\Pi_{\Lambda_0}\), where \(\Pi_\beta\) is a prior on \(\mathbb R^p\) or sparse subsets and \(\Pi_{\Lambda_0}\) is a prior on monotone cumulative hazards.

Proposition. A semiparametric BvM statement for \(\beta\) has the form \[ \left\|\Pi\left(\sqrt n(\beta-\hat\beta)\in\cdot\mid X^{(n)}\right)-N(0,\mathcal I_{\mathrm{eff}}^{-1})\right\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\to0, \] where \(\hat\beta\) is an efficient centering estimator and \(\mathcal I_{\mathrm{eff}}\) is the Cox efficient information.

Remark. In sparse Cox BvM, three forces must be controlled simultaneously: posterior concentration on sparse neighborhoods, local quadratic expansion of the partial likelihood, and negligible nuisance-baseline remainder.

Why Cox is harder than GLM

Proposition. Compared with GLMs, Cox models add a random denominator, a time-indexed nuisance parameter, and martingale rather than independent summand score structure.

Proof. The GLM log likelihood is a sum over observations with fixed covariates. The Cox partial likelihood contains risk-set sums \(S^{(k)}(t,\beta)\), and the baseline hazard enters through the compensator. Thus Taylor expansions require uniform control over time-indexed risk-set processes, not only independent scalar likelihood terms.

Priors for the baseline hazard

Definition. Common baseline priors include gamma-process priors for \(\Lambda_0\), neutral-to-the-right priors, monotone spline priors, and piecewise-constant hazards with random or fixed partitions.

Proposition. For first-order inference on \(\beta\), the baseline prior must put enough mass near the true cumulative hazard and must not introduce a bias in the least favorable direction for \(\beta\).

Proof. Semiparametric BvM proofs expand the likelihood along paths that perturb \(\beta\) and \(\Lambda_0\) jointly. If the prior is thick near the least favorable path, the nuisance integration preserves the efficient quadratic expansion; if not, a prior-induced bias can remain.

Sparse Cox BvM

Remark. Sparse Cox BvM combines model selection, martingale LAN, and nuisance-baseline integration. The posterior must localize on sparse supports quickly enough that risk-set empirical-process remainders are still negligible.
