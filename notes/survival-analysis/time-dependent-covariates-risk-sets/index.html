Predictability

Time-dependent covariates are allowed only when the covariate value used at time \(t\) is known just before \(t\). This prevents using future information to predict present failures.

Definition. A time-dependent covariate process \(Z_i(t)\) is admissible in the Cox model when it is predictable with respect to the observed filtration. The intensity is \[ \lambda_i(t)=Y_i(t)\lambda_0(t)\exp\{Z_i(t)^\top\beta\}. \]

Proposition. With predictable \(Z_i(t)\), the Cox score keeps the same form as before, replacing \(Z_i\) by \(Z_i(t)\): \[ U(\beta)=\sum_i\int_0^\tau\{Z_i(t)-\bar Z(t,\beta)\}\,\mathrm{d}N_i(t). \]

Proof. At each event time, the conditional probability of the failing subject is computed using the covariate values measurable just before that time. Differentiating the resulting product gives the displayed score.

Risk-set dependence

Remark. Risk sets make the Cox likelihood nonproduct. Removing one subject changes denominators at all later event times while that subject is at risk. This dependence is one reason high-dimensional Cox theory is subtler than high-dimensional GLM theory.

External and internal covariates

Definition. An external time-dependent covariate evolves independently of the subject's event process after conditioning on the observed history. An internal covariate is itself affected by the subject's health or event history.

Remark. Predictability is necessary but not always sufficient for causal interpretation. Internal covariates may be valid for prediction while still failing to define a simple intervention effect.
