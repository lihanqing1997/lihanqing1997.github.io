Partial likelihood

At an event time, the Cox model gives the conditional probability that the observed failing subject is the one that fails among all subjects in the risk set. Multiplying these conditional probabilities gives the partial likelihood.

Definition. For distinct event times and no ties, the Cox partial likelihood is \[ L_p(\beta)=\prod_{i:\Delta_i=1}{\exp(Z_i^\top\beta)\over \sum_{j:X_j\ge X_i}\exp(Z_j^\top\beta)}. \] The log partial likelihood is \(\ell_p(\beta)=\log L_p(\beta)\).

Proposition. Let \[ S^{(k)}(t,\beta)=\sum_{j=1}^nY_j(t)Z_j^{\otimes k}\exp(Z_j^\top\beta),\quad k=0,1,2, \] and \(\bar Z(t,\beta)=S^{(1)}(t,\beta)/S^{(0)}(t,\beta)\). Then the score is \[ U(\beta)=\sum_{i=1}^n\int_0^\tau\{Z_i-\bar Z(t,\beta)\}\,\mathrm{d}N_i(t). \]

Proof. Differentiate \(\ell_p(\beta)=\sum_i\int Z_i^\top\beta\,\mathrm{d}N_i(t)-\int\log S^{(0)}(t,\beta)\,\mathrm{d}N_\cdot(t)\), where \(N_\cdot=\sum_iN_i\). The derivative of the first term is \(Z_i\); the derivative of \(\log S^{(0)}\) is \(S^{(1)}/S^{(0)}\).

Observed information

Proposition. The negative Hessian is \[ I_n(\beta)=\sum_i\int_0^\tau V(t,\beta)\,\mathrm{d}N_i(t),\quad V(t,\beta)={S^{(2)}\over S^{(0)}}(t,\beta)-\bar Z(t,\beta)^{\otimes2}. \] Thus \(V(t,\beta)\) is the weighted risk-set covariance of \(Z\).

Proof. Differentiate \(\bar Z=S^{(1)}/S^{(0)}\). The quotient rule gives \(S^{(2)}/S^{(0)}-(S^{(1)}/S^{(0)})^{\otimes2}\), which is precisely the covariance matrix under weights proportional to \(Y_j(t)\exp(Z_j^\top\beta)\).

Concavity and uniqueness

Proposition. The negative Cox partial log likelihood is convex in \(\beta\). It is strictly convex on directions that have nonzero weighted risk-set variance at event times.

Proof. The Hessian of \(-\ell_p(\beta)\) is the sum over event times of weighted covariance matrices \(V(t,\beta)\). Covariance matrices are positive semidefinite; strict positivity holds exactly on directions whose risk-set values are not almost surely constant.

Ties

Definition. When several events occur at the same recorded time, the Breslow approximation treats the risk-set denominator as unchanged across the tied failures, while the Efron approximation partially removes the tied failures across the tied substeps.

Remark. The exact partial likelihood for ties is combinatorial. Breslow is simple, Efron is usually more accurate for moderate tie frequency, and both coincide asymptotically when ties are artifacts of time discretization that vanish with finer measurement.
