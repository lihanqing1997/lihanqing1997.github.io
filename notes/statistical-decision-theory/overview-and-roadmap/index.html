Statistical decision theory studies procedures through their full risk functions. Le Cam's asymptotic theory adds one more layer: a sequence of statistical problems is understood by the simpler experiment to which it converges.

Experiments as decision objects

Definition. An experiment is a family \(\mathcal E=\{P_\theta:\theta\in\Theta\}\) together with the observation space on which the measures live. A decision problem adds actions \(\mathcal A\), losses \(L(\theta,a)\), and decision rules \(\delta\).

Asymptotic comparison of experiments

Proposition. The guiding problem is not only whether an estimator converges, but whether \(\mathcal E_n\) can be replaced by a tractable limit \(\mathcal E\) without changing risks by more than a small amount. This is the role of transitions, deficiencies, and Le Cam distance.

Proof architecture

Proof. Most arguments follow four steps: compare experiments by kernels, prove likelihood-ratio convergence, solve the decision problem in the limit experiment, and transfer the resulting risk statement back to the original sequence.

Interfaces with probability and empirical processes

The probability notes supply weak convergence, contiguity, martingales, and Gaussian limits. The empirical-process notes supply uniform laws needed when the experiment is indexed by rich function classes.
