The risk function is the central mathematical object. It records the loss of a rule at every parameter value and makes comparisons between procedures precise.

Statistical decision problem

Definition. A statistical decision problem is \((\Theta,\mathcal X,\{P_\theta\},\mathcal A,L)\). A nonrandomized rule \(\delta:\mathcal X\to\mathcal A\) has risk

\[ R(\theta,\delta)=E_\theta L\{\theta,\delta(X)\}. \]

Randomized rules and convex risk sets

Proposition. Randomized rules choose a probability measure on \(\mathcal A\) after observing \(X\). If rules are allowed to randomize, the attainable set of risk functions is convex.

Convexification by mixing rules

Proof. If \(\delta_1,\delta_2\) have risks \(R_1,R_2\), then the rule that uses \(\delta_1\) with probability \(\lambda\) has risk \(\lambda R_1+(1-\lambda)R_2\). Hence mixtures of rules give mixtures of risks.

Risk functions as procedure comparisons

A scalar summary is chosen only after a principle is chosen: Bayes risk averages \(R\), minimax risk takes its supremum, and admissibility uses the partial order induced by pointwise domination.
