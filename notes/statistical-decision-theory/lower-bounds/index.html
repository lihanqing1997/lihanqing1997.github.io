Lower bounds are decision-theoretic statements: no rule can achieve risk below a certain value. In product experiments they are usually proved by testing arguments.

Two-point testing reduction

Lemma. If two parameters are separated by loss at scale \(s\), then

\[ \inf_{\hat\theta}\sup_{\theta\in\{\theta_0,\theta_1\}} E_\theta L(\theta,\hat\theta)\ge \frac{s}{2}\{1-\|P_{\theta_0}-P_{\theta_1}\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\}. \]

Fano packing bound

Theorem. For a packing \(\{\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_M\}\), small average KL divergence relative to \(\log M\) forces any estimator to confuse many hypotheses, giving a minimax lower bound.

Risk from testing error

Proof. Convert an estimator into a test for the finite packing. If the estimator has small loss, it identifies the true packing point. Testing lower bounds therefore imply estimation lower bounds.

Hellinger balls and Birgé tests

Le Cam's product-experiment testing bounds use Hellinger geometry to build tests between sets, then turn those tests into estimators and minimax rates.
