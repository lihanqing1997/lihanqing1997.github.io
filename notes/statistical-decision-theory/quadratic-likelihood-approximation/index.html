Quadratic likelihood approximation is the computational face of LAN. It explains one-step estimators, Wald statistics, score tests, and likelihood-ratio tests.

Local quadratic form

Definition. Around a center \(\tilde\theta_n\), write \(u=r_n(\theta-\tilde\theta_n)\). A quadratic likelihood approximation has form

\[ \ell_n(\tilde\theta_n+u/r_n)-\ell_n(\tilde\theta_n) \approx u^\top S_n-\frac12u^\top J_nu. \]

Argmax of the quadratic likelihood

Proposition. If \(J_n\) is nonsingular, the maximizer of the quadratic approximation is \(u_n^\star=J_n^{-1}S_n\). This gives the one-step update \(\tilde\theta_n+J_n^{-1}S_n/r_n\).

Completing the square

Proof. The quadratic equals \(-\frac12(u-J_n^{-1}S_n)^\top J_n(u-J_n^{-1}S_n)+\frac12S_n^\top J_n^{-1}S_n\). The maximum is therefore at \(J_n^{-1}S_n\).

Observed information

When \(J_n\) is the observed information, the approximation adapts to local curvature. In regular models \(J_n\) converges to Fisher information.
