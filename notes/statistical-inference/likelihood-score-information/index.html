Likelihood is the central object of parametric inference. It treats the observed data as fixed and compares parameter values by how much probability density they assign to the observation.

Definition. In a dominated model with i.i.d. observations, the likelihood and log-likelihood are \[ L_n(\theta)=\prod_{i=1}^np_\theta(X_i),\qquad \ell_n(\theta)=\sum_{i=1}^n\log p_\theta(X_i). \] The score is \(S_n(\theta)=\dot\ell_n(\theta)\), and the observed Hessian is \(H_n(\theta)=\ddot\ell_n(\theta)\).

Fisher information

For one observation write \(s_\theta(X)=\partial_\theta\log p_\theta(X)\). Under regularity conditions, the Fisher information is \[ I(\theta)=E_\theta s_\theta(X)s_\theta(X)^\top. \]

Information identity. If differentiation may be passed under the integral sign and \(p_\theta\) is twice differentiable, then \[ E_\theta s_\theta(X)=0,\qquad I(\theta)=-E_\theta\partial_\theta^2\log p_\theta(X). \]

Proof. Since \(\int p_\theta\,\mathrm{d}\mu=1\), differentiating gives \[ 0=\int \dot p_\theta\,\mathrm{d}\mu =\int \frac{\dot p_\theta}{p_\theta}p_\theta\,\mathrm{d}\mu =E_\theta s_\theta(X). \] Differentiate \(E_\theta s_\theta=0\) once more. The derivative of the density contributes \(s_\theta s_\theta^\top\), while the derivative of the score contributes \(\partial_\theta s_\theta=\partial_\theta^2\log p_\theta\). Thus \(0=E_\theta\partial_\theta^2\log p_\theta+E_\theta s_\theta s_\theta^\top\).

Reparametrization

If \(\eta=g(\theta)\) is one-to-one and differentiable, information transforms by the chain rule. For scalar \(\theta\), \[ I_\eta(\eta)=I_\theta(\theta)\left(\frac{\mathrm{d}\theta}{\mathrm{d}\eta}\right)^2. \] This is why standard errors should be transformed with the delta method rather than by informal algebra alone.
