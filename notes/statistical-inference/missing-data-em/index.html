Missing data change the likelihood because the observed data are a coarsening of complete data. EM turns incomplete-data likelihood maximization into repeated conditional expectation and maximization steps.

Definition. Let \(Y=(Y_{\mathrm{obs}},Y_{\mathrm{mis}})\) be complete data. The observed likelihood is \[ L_{\mathrm{obs}}(\theta)=\int p_\theta(Y_{\mathrm{obs}},y_{\mathrm{mis}})\,\mathrm{d}y_{\mathrm{mis}}. \] EM defines \[ Q(\theta\mid\theta^{(t)})=E_{\theta^{(t)}}\{\log p_\theta(Y)\mid Y_{\mathrm{obs}}\}. \]

EM monotonicity. If \(\theta^{(t+1)}\) maximizes \(Q(\theta\mid\theta^{(t)})\), then \(\ell_{\mathrm{obs}}(\theta^{(t+1)})\ge \ell_{\mathrm{obs}}(\theta^{(t)})\).

Proof. Write the observed log-likelihood as \[ \ell_{\mathrm{obs}}(\theta)=Q(\theta\mid\theta^{(t)})-\operatorname{KL}\{p_{\theta^{(t)}}(Y_{\mathrm{mis}}\mid Y_{\mathrm{obs}}),p_\theta(Y_{\mathrm{mis}}\mid Y_{\mathrm{obs}})\}+C. \] The KL term is nonnegative and equals zero at \(\theta=\theta^{(t)}\). Increasing \(Q\) therefore cannot decrease \(\ell_{\mathrm{obs}}\).

Missingness assumptions

MCAR, MAR, and MNAR describe how missingness depends on data. Ignorability requires assumptions about both the missingness mechanism and parameter distinctness; otherwise the missingness process must be modeled.
