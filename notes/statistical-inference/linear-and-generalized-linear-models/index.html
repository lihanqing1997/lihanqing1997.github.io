13. Linear and generalized linear models

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/statistical-inference/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/statistical-inference/finite-sample-optimality/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/statistical-inference/misspecification-robust-covariance/" %}

Linear model as baseline

Linear models are the Euclidean case of likelihood geometry. GLMs replace constant Gaussian variance by a mean-variance relation and a link function.

Definition. A GLM specifies independent observations with \(E(Y_i\mid x_i)=\mu_i\), variance \(\operatorname{var}(Y_i\mid x_i)=a(\phi)V(\mu_i)\), and link \(g(\mu_i)=x_i^\top\beta\).

Canonical exponential-family likelihood

Definition. In canonical form, \[ \ell(\beta)=\sum_{i=1}^n {y_ix_i^\top\beta-b(x_i^\top\beta)\over a(\phi)}+c(y_i,\phi). \] Logistic regression has \(b(\eta)=\log(1+e^\eta)\); Poisson regression has \(b(\eta)=e^\eta\).

Proposition. The score and observed information are \[ U(\beta)=X^\top(y-\mu)/a(\phi),\qquad I(\beta)=X^\top W(\beta)X/a(\phi), \] where \(W_{ii}=b''(x_i^\top\beta)\) in the canonical case.

Proof. Differentiate the canonical log likelihood. The first derivative of \(b\) gives \(\mu_i\), and the second derivative gives the weight \(b''(\eta_i)\). Stacking the derivatives gives the matrix formula.

Canonical and noncanonical links

Definition. A link is canonical when the natural parameter equals the linear predictor: \(\theta_i=x_i^\top\beta\). For a noncanonical link, \(\theta_i=(b')^{-1}(g^{-1}(x_i^\top\beta))\), and the score contains an extra derivative factor.

Remark. Canonical links simplify likelihood curvature and Fisher scoring. Noncanonical links can be scientifically natural, but their Hessians include additional terms and require more care in asymptotic expansions.

Fisher scoring and IRLS

Theorem. The Fisher scoring step for a GLM can be written as an iteratively reweighted least-squares update \[ \beta^{(k+1)}=(X^\top W^{(k)}X)^{-1}X^\top W^{(k)}z^{(k)}, \] where \(z^{(k)}=\eta^{(k)}+(y-\mu^{(k)})\,\mathrm{d}\eta/\mathrm{d}\mu\) is the working response.

Proof. Quadratically approximate the log likelihood at the current \(\beta^{(k)}\). The gradient gives the linear term and expected information gives the quadratic weight matrix. Completing the square produces a weighted least-squares problem in the working response.

Examples

Proposition. Logistic, Poisson, Gamma, negative-binomial, and probit models differ in link, variance function, and tail behavior. Logistic and Poisson are canonical for Bernoulli and Poisson families; probit is noncanonical but often useful for latent-Gaussian modeling.

High-dimensional likelihood geometry

Remark. In high-dimensional GLMs, asymptotic normality and BvM approximations require sparse curvature, score concentration, and localization away from separation or explosive mean regions.

Full exponential-family calculus

Definition. A regular one-parameter exponential family has density \[ p_\theta(y)=\exp\{y\theta-b(\theta)+c(y)\}, \] with mean \(b'(\theta)\), variance \(b''(\theta)\), and cumulants given by higher derivatives of \(b\).

Proposition. For canonical GLMs, the log likelihood is concave whenever \(b\) is convex, and its curvature is exactly a weighted Gram matrix.

Proof. Convexity of \(b\) gives \(b''\ge0\). Differentiating twice yields \(-\ddot\ell(\beta)=X^\top W(\beta)X/a(\phi)\), with nonnegative diagonal weights. Hence the negative Hessian is positive semidefinite.

Likelihood expansion and asymptotic normality

Theorem. If \(p\) is fixed, \(n^{-1}X^\top W(\beta_0)X\to I(\beta_0)\) nonsingular, and a Lindeberg condition holds, then the GLM maximum likelihood estimator satisfies \[ \sqrt n(\hat\beta-\beta_0)\Rightarrow N(0,I(\beta_0)^{-1}). \]

Proof. The score at the truth is a sum of independent mean-zero vectors. The central limit theorem gives \(n^{-1/2}U(\beta_0)\Rightarrow N(0,I)\). Taylor expansion \(0=U(\hat\beta)=U(\beta_0)-I_n(\tilde\beta)(\hat\beta-\beta_0)\), together with information convergence, gives the result.

Canonical examples

Example. Bernoulli logistic regression has \(b(\eta)=\log(1+e^\eta)\), \(\mu=e^\eta/(1+e^\eta)\), and weight \(\mu(1-\mu)\). Poisson log-linear regression has \(b(\eta)=e^\eta\), \(\mu=e^\eta\), and weight \(\mu\). Gaussian linear regression has \(b(\eta)=\eta^2/2\), identity link, and constant weight.

Separation, overdispersion, and misspecification

Remark. Logistic separation can make the MLE diverge. Poisson overdispersion can make model-based standard errors too small. Under misspecification, inference should use the sandwich form \(A^{-1}BA^{-1}\), with \(A\) the Hessian limit and \(B\) the score variance.
