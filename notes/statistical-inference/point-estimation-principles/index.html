A point estimator compresses the data into a proposed value of the target parameter. Its quality can be studied exactly, asymptotically, or by robustness to modeling perturbations.

Definition. An estimator of \(\theta(P)\) is a statistic \(T_n=T(X_1,\ldots,X_n)\). Its bias is \(E_P T_n-\theta(P)\), its variance is \(\operatorname{Var}_P(T_n)\), and its mean squared error is \[ \operatorname{MSE}_P(T_n)=E_P\{T_n-\theta(P)\}^2. \]

Consistency and rates

The estimator is consistent if \(T_n\to\theta(P)\) in probability. It is \(r_n\)-consistent if \(r_n\{T_n-\theta(P)\}=O_p(1)\). Parametric regular problems usually have \(r_n=\sqrt n\), while nonparametric problems can have slower rates.

Bias-variance decomposition. For scalar \(T_n\), \[ \operatorname{MSE}_P(T_n)=\operatorname{Var}_P(T_n)+\{E_PT_n-\theta(P)\}^2. \]

Proof. Write \(T_n-\theta=(T_n-E_PT_n)+(E_PT_n-\theta)\). Squaring and taking expectation gives the variance term, the squared bias term, and a cross term equal to \(2(E_PT_n-\theta)E_P(T_n-E_PT_n)=0\).

Method of moments and plug-in

The method of moments solves equations \(P_nm_j=P_\theta m_j\). Plug-in estimators replace \(P\) by \(P_n\) in a functional \(\theta(P)\). The serious inferential task is not only to compute \(T_n\), but to obtain a linear expansion for \(T_n-\theta\) so uncertainty can be quantified.
