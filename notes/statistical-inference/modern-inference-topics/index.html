This page collects modern topics that extend classical inference while keeping the same central concern: valid uncertainty statements after modeling, selection, or prediction.

Definition. A multiple-testing procedure controls the false discovery rate at level \(q\) if \[ \operatorname{FDR}=E\left[\frac{V}{R\vee1}\right]\le q, \] where \(V\) is the number of false rejections and \(R\) the total number of rejections.

Benjamini-Hochberg procedure. For independent valid p-values, ordering \(p_{(1)}\le\cdots\le p_{(m)}\) and rejecting all \(p_{(i)}\le iq/m\) up to the largest such \(i\) controls FDR at level \(q\) under the standard independent-null setting.

Proof. Let \(\mathcal H_0\) be the true nulls and \(m_0=|\mathcal H_0|\). For a true null \(j\), independence and validity give \[ E\left[\frac{1_{\{p_j\le qR/m\}}}{R\vee1}\right]\le \frac q m, \] because conditioning on the other p-values fixes the step-up threshold through \(R\), and the super-uniformity of \(p_j\) bounds the conditional rejection probability. Summing this inequality over \(j\in\mathcal H_0\) yields \(\operatorname{FDR}\le m_0q/m\le q\).

Other directions

Selective inference adjusts for choosing a model before inference. Debiased estimators target low-dimensional parameters after high-dimensional regularization. Conformal prediction gives distribution-free predictive coverage under exchangeability. These topics connect to statistical learning but remain inferential because the final object is uncertainty quantification.
