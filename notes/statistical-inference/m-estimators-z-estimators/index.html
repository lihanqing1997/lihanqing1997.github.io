Many estimators are defined either by optimizing an empirical criterion or by solving estimating equations. These two forms are often equivalent when the criterion is differentiable.

Definition. An M-estimator approximately maximizes \(P_nm_\theta\). A Z-estimator approximately solves \[ P_n\psi_\theta=0. \] When \(m_\theta\) is differentiable, a first-order condition gives \(\psi_\theta=\partial_\theta m_\theta\).

Z-estimator normality. Suppose \(P\psi_{\theta_0}=0\), \(A=\partial_\theta P\psi_\theta|_{\theta_0}\) is nonsingular, and \[ P_n\psi_{\hat\theta}=o_p(n^{-1/2}),\qquad P_n\psi_{\hat\theta}-P_n\psi_{\theta_0}-A(\hat\theta-\theta_0)=o_p(n^{-1/2}). \] Then \[ \sqrt n(\hat\theta-\theta_0) =-A^{-1}n^{-1/2}\sum_{i=1}^n\psi_{\theta_0}(X_i)+o_p(1). \]

Proof. Combine the estimating equation and the linearization: \[ 0=P_n\psi_{\theta_0}+A(\hat\theta-\theta_0)+o_p(n^{-1/2}). \] Solve for \(\hat\theta-\theta_0\). Multiplying by \(\sqrt n\) yields the expansion; the CLT then gives asymptotic covariance \(A^{-1}BA^{-\top}\), where \(B=P\psi_{\theta_0}\psi_{\theta_0}^\top\).

Sandwich covariance

The covariance \(A^{-1}BA^{-\top}\) is called sandwich covariance. For a correctly specified likelihood score, \(A=-I\) and \(B=I\), so the sandwich reduces to \(I^{-1}\). Under misspecification, this cancellation fails.
