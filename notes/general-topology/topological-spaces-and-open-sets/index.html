1. Topological spaces and open sets

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/general-topology/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/general-topology/bases-and-subbases/" %}

Topological spaces and open sets records the objects, main results, proof mechanisms, and boundary cases needed for the general topology note.

Definition

Definition. A topology on a set \(X\) is a collection of open sets closed under arbitrary unions and finite intersections, containing \(\varnothing\) and \(X\).

Proposition

Proposition. Closed sets, neighborhoods, interiors, closures, and boundaries are all determined by the open sets. A map is continuous exactly when inverse images of open sets are open.

Proof

Proof. Definitions are dualized by complements and tested through inverse images. The topology is the primitive object; all derived notions must be invariant under it.

Remark

Remark. Topology abstracts the language of nearness without requiring distances. It is the common foundation for analysis, geometry, and convergence.
