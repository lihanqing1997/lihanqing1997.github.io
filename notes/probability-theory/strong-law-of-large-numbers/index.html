Strong Law of Large Numbers

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/borel-cantelli-lemma/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/triangular-arrays-truncation/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Strong Law of Large Numbers.

Definition. The strong law strengthens the weak law from convergence in probability to almost sure convergence. It asserts that the random empirical averages themselves settle down on almost every sample path.

Central result. If \(X_1,X_2,\ldots\) are i.i.d. and \(\mathbb E|X_1|\lt\infty\), then \(n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^nX_i\to\mathbb EX_1\) almost surely.

Proof. For bounded centered variables, apply exponential or fourth-moment bounds along dyadic subsequences and use Borel-Cantelli; then control the oscillation between \(2^k\) and \(2^{k+1}\). For integrable variables, truncate at level \(n\), show the discarded tails occur only finitely often or have negligible average by summability, and apply the bounded argument to the truncated centered part.

How to read it

The strong law is the pathwise foundation of empirical probability. It justifies replacing expectations by long-run averages on almost every realization.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
