The strong law of large numbers upgrades stabilization of averages from probability to almost sure convergence. It is one of the main bridges from randomness to deterministic long-run behavior.

Almost-sure averages

The strong law controls the entire tail of the sequence of averages.

Definition. For i.i.d. integrable \(X_i\) with mean \(\mu\), the strong law states \[ \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^nX_i\to\mu\qquad\text{almost surely}. \]

Kolmogorov strong law

Integrability is the sharp natural assumption in the i.i.d. case.

Theorem. If \(X_i\) are i.i.d. and \(\mathbb E|X_1|\lt\infty\), then \(n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^nX_i\to\mathbb E X_1\) almost surely.

Proof. For bounded centered variables, apply maximal inequalities to dyadic blocks and use Borel-Cantelli to show dyadic averages converge almost surely, then fill gaps between powers of two. For integrable variables, truncate \(X_i\) at levels comparable to \(i\). Borel-Cantelli controls the discarded large terms, and the centered truncated variables satisfy the bounded or square-summable variance argument. Finally the truncation bias tends to zero by integrability.

Kolmogorov criterion

Independent non-identically distributed variables are controlled by variance summability.

Proposition. If \(X_n\) are independent, centered, square-integrable, and \(\sum_n \operatorname{Var}(X_n)/n^2\lt\infty\), then \(n^{-1}\sum_{k=1}^nX_k\to0\) almost surely.

Proof. Kronecker's lemma reduces the assertion to almost-sure convergence of \(\sum_nX_n/n\). The latter follows from \(L^2\)-boundedness of partial sums because independence gives \(\sum_n\mathbb E(X_n/n)^2\lt\infty\), so the series converges in \(L^2\) and almost surely along a standard subsequence/maximal argument.

Remark. Strong laws are often proved by combining three tools: truncation, maximal inequalities, and Borel-Cantelli.
