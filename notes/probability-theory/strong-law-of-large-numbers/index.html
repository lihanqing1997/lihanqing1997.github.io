Strong law of large numbers

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/borel-cantelli-lemma/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/triangular-arrays-truncation/" %}

This topic belongs to classical limit theory. The main question is how random quantities behave as the sample size or time scale grows. Here the focus is Strong law of large numbers.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. The strong law strengthens the weak law from convergence in probability to almost sure convergence. It asserts that the random empirical averages themselves settle down on almost every sample path.

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. If \(X_1,X_2,\ldots\) are i.i.d. and \(\mathbb E|X_1|\lt\infty\), then \(n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^nX_i\to\mathbb EX_1\) almost surely.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

For bounded centered variables, apply exponential or fourth-moment bounds along dyadic subsequences and use Borel-Cantelli; then control the oscillation between \(2^k\) and \(2^{k+1}\). For integrable variables, truncate at level \(n\), show the discarded tails occur only finitely often or have negligible average by summability, and apply the bounded argument to the truncated centered part.

Remarks

The strong law is the pathwise foundation of empirical probability. It justifies replacing expectations by long-run averages on almost every realization.
