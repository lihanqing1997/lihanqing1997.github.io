Ergodic theorems identify long-run time averages with conditional or space averages. They are limit theorems for dependence generated by a measure-preserving transformation or stationary process.

Measure-preserving transformations

Dynamics enter probability through transformations preserving the law.

Definition. A map \(T:\Omega\to\Omega\) is measure preserving if \(P(T^{-1}A)=P(A)\) for all \(A\in\mathcal F\). The invariant \(\sigma\)-field is \(\mathcal I=\{A:T^{-1}A=A\ \text{mod }P\}\).

Birkhoff ergodic theorem

Time averages converge to the conditional expectation on invariant information.

Theorem. If \(T\) is measure preserving and \(f\in L^1(P)\), then \[ \frac1n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}f\circ T^k\to \mathbb E[f\mid\mathcal I] \] almost surely and in \(L^1\) under the usual integrability conclusion for the averages.

Proof. The proof uses a maximal ergodic lemma. Applied to \(f-c\), it controls the set where partial sums have positive maximal excess. This yields inequalities for upper and lower limits of averages. Applying the inequalities to a countable dense set of levels forces the limsup and liminf to agree and identifies the limit by invariance and integration over invariant sets.

Ergodicity

In an ergodic system, invariant information is trivial.

Proposition. If every invariant event has probability \(0\) or \(1\), then \(T\) is ergodic and the Birkhoff limit equals \(\mathbb E f\) almost surely.

Proof. When \(\mathcal I\) is trivial, every \(\mathcal I\)-measurable integrable random variable is almost surely constant. Since conditional expectation preserves the mean, \(\mathbb E[f\mid\mathcal I]=\mathbb E f\).

Remark. For stationary Markov chains, ergodic theorems turn sample path averages into expectations under an invariant distribution.
