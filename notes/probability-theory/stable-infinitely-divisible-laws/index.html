Stable and Infinitely Divisible Laws

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/poisson-convergence-processes/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/filtrations-stopping-times/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Stable and Infinitely Divisible Laws.

Definition. A law \(\mu\) is infinitely divisible if for every \(n\) it can be written as the \(n\)-fold convolution of some law \(\mu_n\). Stable laws are non-degenerate limits of normalized sums and satisfy distributional scaling identities.

Central result. The Lévy-Khintchine formula says that the characteristic exponent of an infinitely divisible law has the form \[\psi(t)=iat-\frac12\sigma^2t^2+\int_{\mathbb R}\left(e^{itx}-1-itx1_{\{|x|\le1\}}\right)\nu(dx),\] where \(\nu\) is a Lévy measure.

Proof. Infinitely divisible laws arise as limits of triangular-array sums. Separating small Gaussian-scale increments from rare jumps yields the drift, Gaussian variance, and jump measure terms. Conversely, each triplet defines a convolution semigroup whose characteristic functions are \(e^{t\psi(u)}\).

How to read it

This theorem is the gateway from elementary sums to Lévy processes, where randomness is decomposed into drift, Brownian fluctuation, and jumps.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
