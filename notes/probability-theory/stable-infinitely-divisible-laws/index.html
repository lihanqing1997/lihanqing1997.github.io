20. Stable and infinitely divisible laws

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/poisson-convergence-processes/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/filtrations-stopping-times/" %}

Stable and infinitely divisible laws are the possible limits of normalized sums beyond the finite-variance Gaussian setting. They are organized by characteristic exponents.

Infinite divisibility

A law is infinitely divisible if it can be split into any number of i.i.d. summands.

Definition. A probability law \(\mu\) on \(\mathbb R^d\) is infinitely divisible if for every \(n\ge1\) there exists a law \(\mu_n\) such that \(\mu=\mu_n^{\ast n}\).

Levy-Khintchine representation

Infinitely divisible laws are exactly those with Levy-Khintchine exponents.

Theorem. A law on \(\mathbb R^d\) is infinitely divisible if and only if its characteristic function has the form \(\exp\{\psi(t)\}\), where \[ \psi(t)=i\langle b,t\rangle-\frac12\langle t,At\rangle +\int_{\mathbb R^d}\left(e^{i\langle t,x\rangle}-1-i\langle t,x\rangle1_{\{|x|\le1\}}\right)\nu(\mathrm{d}x), \] with \(A\) nonnegative definite and \(\nu\) satisfying \(\int(1\wedge |x|^2)\nu(\mathrm{d}x)\lt\infty\).

Proof. The Gaussian part and drift are visibly infinitely divisible. The integral part is obtained as the limit of compound Poisson exponents after truncating \(\nu\) away from zero. Conversely, taking \(n\)th roots of characteristic functions and using tightness of triangular arrays yields a canonical triplet \((b,A,\nu)\), with the small-jump compensation forced by convergence near zero.

Stable laws

Stable laws are fixed points of summation up to scaling and centering.

Proposition. A law \(\mu\) is stable if for independent \(X_1,X_2\sim\mu\) and every \(a,b\gt0\), there are \(c\gt0\) and \(d\in\mathbb R\) such that \(aX_1+bX_2\stackrel d=cX+d\). Non-Gaussian stable laws have heavy tails and index \(\alpha\in(0,2)\).

Proof. The stability equation becomes a functional equation for the log characteristic function. Homogeneity of the exponent identifies the scaling index \(\alpha\). For \(\alpha=2\) the Levy measure vanishes and the Gaussian law appears; for \(\alpha\lt2\), the Levy measure has power-law radial behavior.

Remark. Stable laws are the natural limits when variance is infinite and sums are normalized by \(n^{1/\alpha}\) rather than \(\sqrt n\).
