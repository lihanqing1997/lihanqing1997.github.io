Stable and infinitely divisible laws

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/poisson-convergence-processes/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/filtrations-stopping-times/" %}

Definition. A law \(\mu\) is infinitely divisible if for every \(n\) it can be written as the \(n\)-fold convolution of some law \(\mu_n\). Stable laws are non-degenerate limits of normalized sums and satisfy distributional scaling identities.

Result. The Lévy-Khintchine formula says that the characteristic exponent of an infinitely divisible law has the form \[\psi(t)=iat-\frac12\sigma^2t^2+\int_{\mathbb R}\left(e^{itx}-1-itx1_{\{|x|\le1\}}\right)\nu(dx),\] where \(\nu\) is a Lévy measure.

Proof.

Infinitely divisible laws arise as limits of triangular-array sums. Separating small Gaussian-scale increments from rare jumps yields the drift, Gaussian variance, and jump measure terms. Conversely, each triplet defines a convolution semigroup whose characteristic functions are \(e^{t\psi(u)}\).

Remarks

This theorem is the gateway from elementary sums to Lévy processes, where randomness is decomposed into drift, Brownian fluctuation, and jumps.
