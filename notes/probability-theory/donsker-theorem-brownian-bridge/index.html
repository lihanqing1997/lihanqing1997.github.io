Donsker theorem and Brownian bridge

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-martingales/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/multidimensional-brownian-motion/" %}

This topic belongs to Brownian motion. Brownian motion is both a universal scaling limit and the basic continuous martingale. Here the focus is Donsker theorem and Brownian bridge.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. Let \(S_k=X_1+\cdots+X_k\) for i.i.d. centered variables with variance \(1\). The linearly interpolated process \(W_n(t)=n^{-1/2}S_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}\) is a random element of \(C[0,1]\) after interpolation.

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. Donsker's theorem says \(W_n\Rightarrow B\) in \(C[0,1]\). If \(F_n\) is the empirical distribution function, then \(\sqrt n(F_n-F)\) converges to a Brownian bridge after transforming by \(F\).

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

Finite-dimensional convergence follows from the multivariate CLT applied to increments. Tightness follows from moment bounds on increments, such as Kolmogorov criteria or maximal inequalities. The Brownian bridge arises by subtracting the endpoint component: \(B^0(t)=B(t)-tB(1)\).

Remarks

Donsker's theorem is the functional CLT. It explains why Brownian motion and Brownian bridge appear in asymptotic statistics.
