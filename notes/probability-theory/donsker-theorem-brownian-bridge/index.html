Donsker's theorem is the functional central limit theorem: normalized random walks converge as random paths to Brownian motion. Conditioning the endpoint yields the Brownian bridge.

Random-walk interpolation

A partial-sum process turns sums into paths.

Definition. For i.i.d. centered variance-one \(X_i\), define \[ W_n(t)=n^{-1/2}\sum_{i=1}^{\lfloor nt\rfloor}X_i \] with linear interpolation on \([0,1]\).

Donsker theorem

The central limit theorem lifts to path space.

Theorem. In \(C[0,1]\) for interpolated paths, \(W_n\Rightarrow B\), where \(B\) is standard Brownian motion.

Proof. Finite-dimensional convergence follows from the multivariate CLT applied to sums over disjoint time increments. Tightness follows from moment or truncation bounds controlling the modulus of continuity, for example Kolmogorov-type criteria after blocking. Prokhorov's theorem gives subsequential limits, and the finite-dimensional limits identify every subsequential limit as Brownian motion.

Brownian bridge

The empirical-process limit is a Brownian motion pinned at zero at time one.

Proposition. The Brownian bridge is \(\mathbb B(t)=B_t-tB_1\), \(0\le t\le1\). It is centered Gaussian with covariance \(\operatorname{Cov}(\mathbb B(s),\mathbb B(t))=s\wedge t-st\).

Proof. Since \(\mathbb B\) is a linear transformation of a Gaussian process, it is Gaussian. Its covariance is \[ \operatorname{Cov}(B_s-sB_1,B_t-tB_1)=s\wedge t-st-s t+st=s\wedge t-st. \] Also \(\mathbb B(0)=\mathbb B(1)=0\).

Remark. The bridge is the limit of centered empirical distribution functions and appears throughout empirical process theory.
