Donsker Theorem and Brownian Bridge

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-martingales/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/multidimensional-brownian-motion/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Donsker Theorem and Brownian Bridge.

Definition. Let \(S_k=X_1+\cdots+X_k\) for i.i.d. centered variables with variance \(1\). The linearly interpolated process \(W_n(t)=n^{-1/2}S_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}\) is a random element of \(C[0,1]\) after interpolation.

Central result. Donsker's theorem says \(W_n\Rightarrow B\) in \(C[0,1]\). If \(F_n\) is the empirical distribution function, then \(\sqrt n(F_n-F)\) converges to a Brownian bridge after transforming by \(F\).

Proof. Finite-dimensional convergence follows from the multivariate CLT applied to increments. Tightness follows from moment bounds on increments, such as Kolmogorov criteria or maximal inequalities. The Brownian bridge arises by subtracting the endpoint component: \(B^0(t)=B(t)-tB(1)\).

How to read it

Donsker's theorem is the functional CLT. It explains why Brownian motion and Brownian bridge appear in asymptotic statistics.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
