29. Kolmogorov extension theorem

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/martingale-clt/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/path-spaces-finite-dimensional-distributions/" %}

Kolmogorov's extension theorem constructs stochastic processes from consistent finite-dimensional distributions. It is the foundational existence theorem behind random sequences and processes.

Finite-dimensional distributions

A process law is specified by all finite coordinate projections.

Definition. For an index set \(T\) and state space \(S\), finite-dimensional distributions are probability measures \(\mu_{t_1,\dots,t_n}\) on \(S^n\) for finite tuples \((t_1,\dots,t_n)\). They are consistent if they are invariant under permutation and marginalization of coordinates.

Extension theorem

Consistency is exactly the condition needed for a measure on the product path space.

Theorem. If \(S\) is a standard Borel space and the finite-dimensional distributions are consistent, then there exists a probability measure \(P\) on \(S^T\) with coordinate process \(X_t(\omega)=\omega(t)\) having those finite-dimensional distributions.

Proof. Cylinder sets form an algebra. Define a premeasure on cylinders by the prescribed finite-dimensional distributions. Consistency makes this well-defined when the same cylinder has several coordinate descriptions. Countable additivity on the cylinder algebra follows from standard Borel regularity and tightness on finite products. Caratheodory extension then gives a probability measure on the product \(\sigma\)-field.

Path regularity is separate

The extension theorem gives coordinate processes, not continuous or càdlàg paths.

Proposition. Additional regularity criteria, such as Kolmogorov-Chentsov continuity, are needed to obtain a modification with continuous paths.

Proof. The product-space process has the desired finite-dimensional laws but may live on all functions \(T\to S\). If a modification with regular paths exists, one pushes the law to a smaller path space such as \(C[0,1]\) or \(D[0,1]\). Finite-dimensional laws alone usually do not force path regularity.

Remark. This distinction is essential for Brownian motion: Gaussian finite-dimensional laws come first, continuous Wiener paths come after a regularity theorem.
