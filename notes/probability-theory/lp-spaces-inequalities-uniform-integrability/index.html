Lp spaces, inequalities, and uniform integrability

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/expected-value/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/pushforward-measures-densities/" %}

This topic belongs to the measure-theoretic foundation of probability. The main purpose is to make the objects measurable before one starts computing. Here the focus is Lp spaces, inequalities, and uniform integrability.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. For \(p\ge1\), \(L^p(P)\) consists of random variables with \(\|X\|_p=(\mathbb E|X|^p)^{1/p}\lt\infty\), identified up to almost sure equality. A family \(\mathcal X\subset L^1\) is uniformly integrable if \(\sup_{X\in\mathcal X}\mathbb E[|X|1_{\{|X|>K\}}]\to0\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. If \(X_n\to X\) in probability, then \(X_n\to X\) in \(L^1\) if and only if \(\{|X_n-X|:n\ge1\}\) is uniformly integrable. This is Vitali's convergence theorem.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

The \(L^1\) convergence implies uniform integrability by absolute continuity of the integral and a finite approximation. Conversely, split \(\mathbb E|X_n-X|\) over \(|X_n-X|\le\varepsilon\), a large-probability bounded set, and the uniformly integrable tail; then let \(n\to\infty\), \(K\to\infty\), and \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\).

Remarks

Uniform integrability is the condition that prevents mass from escaping to rare huge values. It is often the exact missing ingredient when convergence in probability is too weak.
