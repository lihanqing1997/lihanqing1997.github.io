Expectation is Lebesgue integration against a probability measure. Its power comes from limit theorems that allow one to pass from simple variables to general random variables.

Lebesgue expectation

The expectation is first defined for nonnegative variables, then by difference.

Definition. For \(X\ge0\), \(\mathbb E X=\int_\Omega X\,\mathrm{d}P\), possibly infinite. For general \(X\), the expectation exists if \(\mathbb E X^+\) and \(\mathbb E X^-\) are not both infinite; \(X\in L^1\) if \(\mathbb E|X|\lt\infty\).

Monotone and dominated convergence

Most expectation manipulations reduce to two convergence principles.

Theorem. If \(0\le X_n\uparrow X\), then \(\mathbb E X_n\uparrow\mathbb E X\). If \(X_n\to X\) almost surely and \(|X_n|\le Y\in L^1\), then \(\mathbb E X_n\to\mathbb E X\).

Proof. The first statement is the monotone convergence theorem for Lebesgue integrals, proved by approximating \(X\) from below by simple functions and using countable additivity. For dominated convergence, apply Fatou's lemma to \(Y+X_n\) and \(Y-X_n\); the resulting upper and lower bounds force convergence of expectations.

Tail integration

Nonnegative expectations can be recovered from tail probabilities.

Proposition. For \(X\ge0\), \[ \mathbb E X=\int_0^\infty P(X\gt t)\,\mathrm{d}t. \]

Proof. Since \(X(\omega)=\int_0^\infty 1_{\{t\lt X(\omega)\}}\,\mathrm{d}t\), Tonelli's theorem gives \(\mathbb E X=\int_0^\infty \mathbb E1_{\{X\gt t\}}\,\mathrm{d}t=\int_0^\infty P(X\gt t)\,\mathrm{d}t\).

Remark. Tail integration is often the quickest route to moment bounds, especially for maxima and stopping times.
