Measures, Extension, and Monotone Classes

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/probability-spaces/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/distributions-random-variables/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Measures, Extension, and Monotone Classes.

Definition. A \(\pi\)-system is a class closed under finite intersections. A \(\lambda\)-system contains \(\Omega\), is closed under relative complements of nested sets, and is closed under countable disjoint unions. These classes let us extend identities from generators to generated \(\sigma\)-fields.

Central result. If \(\mathcal C\) is a \(\pi\)-system, then the smallest \(\lambda\)-system containing \(\mathcal C\) is \(\sigma(\mathcal C)\). Hence two probability measures that agree on \(\mathcal C\) agree on \(\sigma(\mathcal C)\).

Proof. Let \(\mathcal L\) be the class of sets on which two measures agree. If they agree on \(\mathcal C\), then \(\mathcal C\subset\mathcal L\). Finite additivity and continuity from below show that \(\mathcal L\) is a \(\lambda\)-system. Since \(\mathcal C\) is a \(\pi\)-system, \(\sigma(\mathcal C)\subset\mathcal L\).

How to read it

This is the standard way to prove identities: check rectangles, intervals, or cylinder sets first, then extend by a monotone class argument.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
