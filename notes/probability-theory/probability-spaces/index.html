Probability Spaces

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/measures-extension-monotone-classes/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Probability Spaces.

Definition. A probability space is a triple \((\Omega,\mathcal F,P)\), where \(\mathcal F\) is a \(\sigma\)-field on \(\Omega\) and \(P:\mathcal F\to[0,1]\) is countably additive with \(P(\Omega)=1\). Events are elements of \(\mathcal F\), and statements that hold outside a null event are said to hold almost surely.

Central result. If \(A_n\uparrow A\), then \(P(A_n)\uparrow P(A)\). If \(A_n\downarrow A\), then \(P(A_n)\downarrow P(A)\). These are the continuity properties of probability.

Proof. For increasing sets, write \(A= A_1\cup\bigcup_{n\ge2}(A_n\setminus A_{n-1})\). Countable additivity gives \(P(A)=P(A_1)+\sum_{n\ge2}P(A_n\setminus A_{n-1})=\lim_n P(A_n)\). For decreasing sets, apply the increasing result to complements \(A_n^c\uparrow A^c\).

How to read it

Almost every probabilistic construction begins by specifying the measurable events first. Without \(\mathcal F\), expressions such as \(P(X\in B)\) have no mathematical meaning.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
