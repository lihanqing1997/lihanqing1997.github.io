Probability spaces

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/measures-extension-monotone-classes/" %}

This topic belongs to the measure-theoretic foundation of probability. The main purpose is to make the objects measurable before one starts computing. Here the focus is Probability spaces.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. A probability space is a triple \((\Omega,\mathcal F,P)\), where \(\mathcal F\) is a \(\sigma\)-field on \(\Omega\) and \(P:\mathcal F\to[0,1]\) is countably additive with \(P(\Omega)=1\). Events are elements of \(\mathcal F\), and statements that hold outside a null event are said to hold almost surely.

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. If \(A_n\uparrow A\), then \(P(A_n)\uparrow P(A)\). If \(A_n\downarrow A\), then \(P(A_n)\downarrow P(A)\). These are the continuity properties of probability.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

For increasing sets, write \(A= A_1\cup\bigcup_{n\ge2}(A_n\setminus A_{n-1})\). Countable additivity gives \(P(A)=P(A_1)+\sum_{n\ge2}P(A_n\setminus A_{n-1})=\lim_n P(A_n)\). For decreasing sets, apply the increasing result to complements \(A_n^c\uparrow A^c\).

Remarks

Almost every probabilistic construction begins by specifying the measurable events first. Without \(\mathcal F\), expressions such as \(P(X\in B)\) have no mathematical meaning.
