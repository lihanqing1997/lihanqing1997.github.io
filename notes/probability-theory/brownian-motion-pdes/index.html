Brownian Motion and PDEs

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/multidimensional-brownian-motion/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/continuous-time-martingales-local-martingales/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Brownian Motion and PDEs.

Definition. Brownian motion connects probability to the heat equation. If \(P_tf(x)=\mathbb E_xf(B_t)\), then \(P_t\) is the heat semigroup with generator \(\frac12\Delta\).

Central result. If \(f\) is bounded and continuous, then \(u(t,x)=\mathbb E_xf(B_t)\) solves \(\partial_tu=\frac12\Delta u\) with initial condition \(u(0,x)=f(x)\), in the appropriate classical or weak sense.

Proof. For smooth \(f\), Itô's formula gives \(df(B_t)=\nabla f(B_t)dB_t+\frac12\Delta f(B_t)dt\). Taking expectations removes the martingale term and yields the heat equation. General \(f\) follows by approximation and semigroup continuity.

How to read it

The PDE sees infinitesimal averaging; Brownian motion supplies the random paths whose averaging produces the solution.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
