37. Brownian motion construction

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/gaussian-vectors-processes/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-path-properties/" %}

Brownian motion is the canonical continuous Gaussian process with independent stationary increments. Its construction separates finite-dimensional Gaussian laws from path continuity.

Definition of Brownian motion

The definition combines Gaussian increments, independence, stationarity, and continuity.

Definition. A standard Brownian motion is a process \(B_t\), \(t\ge0\), such that \(B_0=0\) a.s., paths are continuous, increments over disjoint intervals are independent, and \(B_t-B_s\sim N(0,t-s)\) for \(0\le s\le t\).

Wiener measure

The canonical law lives on the space of continuous paths.

Theorem. There exists a probability measure \(W\) on \(C([0,\infty),\mathbb R)\) such that the coordinate process \(B_t(\omega)=\omega(t)\) is Brownian motion. This law is Wiener measure.

Proof. Specify centered Gaussian finite-dimensional distributions with covariance \(\mathbb E[B_sB_t]=s\wedge t\). They are consistent, so Kolmogorov extension gives a process on \(\mathbb R^{[0,\infty)}\). The covariance implies independent increments because disjoint Gaussian increments are uncorrelated. Gaussian moments satisfy \(\mathbb E|B_t-B_s|^p=C_p|t-s|^{p/2}\); Kolmogorov-Chentsov gives a continuous modification. The law of this modification on \(C([0,\infty))\) is Wiener measure.

Scaling and stationarity

Brownian motion is invariant under its natural parabolic scaling.

Proposition. For \(c\gt0\), the process \((c^{-1/2}B_{ct})_{t\ge0}\) is again standard Brownian motion.

Proof. The rescaled process has continuous paths and starts at zero. Its increments over disjoint intervals are independent because Brownian increments over disjoint rescaled intervals are independent. The increment variance is \(c^{-1}(c(t-s))=t-s\), so the Gaussian increment law is correct.

Remark. The canonical viewpoint makes randomness a measure on path space; individual Brownian paths are ordinary continuous functions with exceptional roughness.
