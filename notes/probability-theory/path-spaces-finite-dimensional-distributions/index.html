29. Path spaces and finite-dimensional distributions

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/kolmogorov-extension/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/markov-chains-transition-kernels/" %}

Path spaces let stochastic processes be treated as random elements. The topology chosen on paths determines which convergence statements are meaningful.

Canonical path spaces

The coordinate process is the universal process on a path space.

Definition. For a state space \(S\) and time set \(T\), the raw path space is \(S^T\) with coordinate maps \(X_t(\omega)=\omega(t)\). Common regular path spaces include \(C([0,T],S)\) and \(D([0,T],S)\).

Cylinder sigma-field

Finite-dimensional distributions live on the sigma-field generated by coordinates.

Theorem. The cylinder \(\sigma\)-field on \(S^T\) is \(\sigma(X_t:t\in T)\). If \(S\) is Polish and \(C([0,T],S)\) has the uniform topology, its Borel \(\sigma\)-field is generated by coordinate maps.

Proof. For \(C([0,T],S)\), separability lets one reduce all times to a countable dense set. Continuity of paths recovers coordinates at arbitrary times as limits of coordinates at rational times. Hence the Borel \(\sigma\)-field generated by the uniform topology equals the coordinate-generated \(\sigma\)-field.

Finite-dimensional convergence is not enough

Weak convergence of processes needs tightness in the path topology.

Proposition. If \(X_n\Rightarrow X\) in \(C([0,T])\) or \(D([0,T])\), then all finite-dimensional distributions converge at continuity times of the coordinate maps. The converse requires tightness.

Proof. Coordinate projections are continuous on \(C([0,T])\), so weak convergence of random paths implies convergence of projected vectors by the continuous mapping theorem. Conversely, finite-dimensional convergence determines any possible subsequential limit, but tightness is needed to guarantee subsequential limits exist in the path space.

Remark. Donsker's theorem is exactly finite-dimensional convergence plus tightness of interpolated random-walk paths.
