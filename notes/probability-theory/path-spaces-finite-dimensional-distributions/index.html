Path Spaces and Finite-Dimensional Distributions

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/kolmogorov-extension/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/markov-chains-transition-kernels/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Path Spaces and Finite-Dimensional Distributions.

Definition. For a process \(X=(X_t)_{t\in T}\), the finite-dimensional distributions are the laws of \((X_{t_1},\ldots,X_{t_n})\). The canonical path space is often \(S^T\), \(C[0,1]\), or \(D[0,1]\), with coordinate maps as the process.

Central result. On the canonical space, the cylinder \(\sigma\)-field is generated by coordinate projections. On \(C[0,1]\), the Borel \(\sigma\)-field for the uniform norm is generated by the coordinate maps.

Proof. Evaluation maps are continuous on \(C[0,1]\), so their generated \(\sigma\)-field is contained in the Borel \(\sigma\)-field. Conversely, the uniform norm is determined by values on rational times for continuous functions, so open balls can be generated from countably many coordinate conditions.

How to read it

Finite-dimensional distributions describe pointwise behavior. To prove convergence of processes, one also needs tightness in the chosen path topology.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
