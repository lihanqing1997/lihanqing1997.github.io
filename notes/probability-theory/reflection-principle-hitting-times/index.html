Reflection principle and hitting times

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-path-properties/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-strong-markov-property/" %}

This topic belongs to Brownian motion. Brownian motion is both a universal scaling limit and the basic continuous martingale. Here the focus is Reflection principle and hitting times.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. Let \(\tau_a=\inf\{t\ge0:B_t=a\}\). The reflection principle relates paths that cross \(a\) before time \(t\) to reflected paths ending above \(a\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. For \(a>0\), \[P\left(\sup_{s\le t}B_s\ge a\right)=2P(B_t\ge a).\] Hence \(P(\tau_a\le t)=2\{1-\Phi(a/\sqrt t)\}\).

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

On the event \(\tau_a\le t\), reflect the path after \(\tau_a\): \(B_s\) becomes \(B_s\) for \(s\le\tau_a\) and \(2a-B_s\) for \(s>\tau_a\). Brownian symmetry and the strong Markov property preserve path probabilities, and this bijection maps paths ending below \(a\) to paths ending above \(a\).

Remarks

The reflection principle turns a path maximum into a one-time Gaussian calculation.
