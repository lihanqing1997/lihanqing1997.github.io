55. White noise and stochastic heat equation

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/local-time-tanaka-formula/" %}

White noise and the stochastic heat equation are entry points to infinite-dimensional stochastic analysis. The central issue is that noise is a distribution, not an ordinary function.

Space-time white noise

White noise is defined by its action on test functions.

Definition. Space-time white noise \(W\) on \(\mathbb R_+\times\mathbb R^d\) is a centered Gaussian random distribution such that \[ \mathbb E[W(\phi)W(\psi)]=\int_0^\infty\int_{\mathbb R^d}\phi(t,x)\psi(t,x)\,\mathrm{d}x\,\mathrm{d}t. \]

Mild stochastic heat equation

Solutions are written by convolution with the heat kernel.

Theorem. For \[ \partial_tu=\frac12\Delta u+\sigma(u)\dot W, \] the mild form is \[ u(t,x)=\int p_t(x-y)u_0(y)\,\mathrm{d}y+ \int_0^t\int p_{t-s}(x-y)\sigma(u(s,y))\,W(\mathrm{d}s,\mathrm{d}y). \]

Proof. The deterministic heat semigroup solves the homogeneous equation. Duhamel's principle suggests convolution of the forcing term with the heat kernel. Since the forcing is white noise, the second term is interpreted as a Walsh stochastic integral, defined by an \(L^2\) isometry for predictable integrands.

Walsh isometry

The stochastic integral is controlled by a space-time \(L^2\) norm.

Proposition. For predictable \(H\), \[ \mathbb E\left(\int_0^t\int H(s,y)\,W(\mathrm{d}s,\mathrm{d}y)\right)^2 =\mathbb E\int_0^t\int H(s,y)^2\,\mathrm{d}y\,\mathrm{d}s. \]

Proof. For simple predictable rectangles, independence and covariance of white-noise increments give the identity. Completion under the displayed norm defines the integral for general predictable \(H\). Fixed-point arguments for the mild equation use this isometry plus heat-kernel bounds.

Remark. In high spatial dimensions the heat kernel may not regularize white noise enough; existence depends on dimension and on the noise covariance.
