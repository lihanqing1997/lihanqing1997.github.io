Weak law of large numbers

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/characteristic-functions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/borel-cantelli-lemma/" %}

Definition. The weak law asks when averages converge to their mean in probability. For \(\bar X_n=n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^nX_i\), the target is \(\bar X_n\to\mu\) in probability.

Result. If \(X_1,X_2,\ldots\) are i.i.d. with \(\mathbb E|X_1|\lt\infty\) and \(\mathbb EX_1=\mu\), then \(\bar X_n\to\mu\) in probability.

Proof.

First prove the finite-variance case by Chebyshev: \(\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n)=\operatorname{Var}(X_1)/n\). For the integrable case, truncate \(X_i\) at level \(n\). The truncated averages have negligible variance after normalization, and the tail contribution has expectation bounded by \(\mathbb E[|X_1|1_{\{|X_1|>n\}}]\to0\).

Remarks

The weak law says empirical averages stabilize. It is the first mathematical reason sample means estimate population means.
