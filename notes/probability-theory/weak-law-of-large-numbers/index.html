Weak Law of Large Numbers

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/characteristic-functions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/borel-cantelli-lemma/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Weak Law of Large Numbers.

Definition. The weak law asks when averages converge to their mean in probability. For \(\bar X_n=n^{-1}\sum_{i=1}^nX_i\), the target is \(\bar X_n\to\mu\) in probability.

Central result. If \(X_1,X_2,\ldots\) are i.i.d. with \(\mathbb E|X_1|\lt\infty\) and \(\mathbb EX_1=\mu\), then \(\bar X_n\to\mu\) in probability.

Proof. First prove the finite-variance case by Chebyshev: \(\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n)=\operatorname{Var}(X_1)/n\). For the integrable case, truncate \(X_i\) at level \(n\). The truncated averages have negligible variance after normalization, and the tail contribution has expectation bounded by \(\mathbb E[|X_1|1_{\{|X_1|>n\}}]\to0\).

How to read it

The weak law says empirical averages stabilize. It is the first mathematical reason sample means estimate population means.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
