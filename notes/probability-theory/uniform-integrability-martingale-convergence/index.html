Uniform Integrability and L1/Lp Martingale Convergence

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/optional-stopping-sampling/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/square-integrable-martingales-quadratic-variation/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Uniform Integrability and L1/Lp Martingale Convergence.

Definition. A martingale \((M_n)\) is uniformly integrable if \(\{M_n:n\ge0\}\) is uniformly integrable. Uniform integrability is stronger than boundedness in \(L^1\), but weaker than boundedness in \(L^p\) for some \(p>1\).

Central result. A martingale \((M_n)\) converges in \(L^1\) if and only if it is uniformly integrable. In that case \(M_n=\mathbb E(M_\infty\mid\mathcal F_n)\).

Proof. If \(M_n\to M_\infty\) in \(L^1\), uniform integrability follows from \(L^1\)-relative compactness. Conversely, Doob convergence gives almost sure convergence to \(M_\infty\). Uniform integrability upgrades almost sure convergence to \(L^1\) convergence by Vitali's theorem, and the representation follows from passing \(M_n=\mathbb E(M_m\mid\mathcal F_n)\) to the limit \(m\to\infty\).

How to read it

Uniform integrability is the exact condition that makes the limiting martingale still carry the original mass.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
