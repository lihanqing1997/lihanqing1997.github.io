34. Renewal theory and basic random walks

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/ergodic-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/gaussian-vectors-processes/" %}

Renewal processes and random walks are the basic additive processes of probability. They model repeated waiting times and accumulated increments.

Random walks and renewals

Both objects are built from partial sums.

Definition. For i.i.d. increments \(\xi_i\), the random walk is \(S_n=\sum_{i=1}^n\xi_i\). For positive i.i.d. waiting times \(\tau_i\), renewal times are \(T_n=\sum_{i=1}^n\tau_i\), and the renewal counting process is \(N(t)=\max\{n:T_n\le t\}\).

Elementary renewal theorem

The renewal count grows at reciprocal mean rate.

Random-walk recurrence in one dimension

Return behavior depends on drift and dimension.

Proposition. For a one-dimensional centered random walk with suitable nondegenerate increments, recurrence means the walk returns arbitrarily close to its starting region infinitely often; with nonzero drift it is transient in the drift direction.

Proof. If \(\mathbb E\xi_1=m\ne0\), the strong law gives \(S_n/n\to m\), so \(S_n\to\operatorname{sign}(m)\infty\) linearly and visits bounded sets only finitely often. In the centered lattice case with finite variance, characteristic-function estimates show \(\sum_nP(S_n=0)=\infty\), and the Markov renewal argument gives recurrence.

Remark. Renewal decompositions often convert path questions into sums over independent cycles.
