Renewal theory and basic random walks

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/ergodic-theorems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/gaussian-vectors-processes/" %}

Definition. A renewal process has arrival times \(S_n=X_1+\cdots+X_n\), where \(X_i\) are positive i.i.d. increments. The renewal count is \(N(t)=\max\{n:S_n\le t\}\). A random walk allows increments with signs.

Result. If \(\mathbb EX_1=\mu\in(0,\infty)\), then \(N(t)/t\to1/\mu\) almost surely. This is the elementary renewal theorem in pathwise form.

Proof.

The strong law gives \(S_n/n\to\mu\). Since \(S_{N(t)}\le t\lt S_{N(t)+1}\), divide by \(N(t)\) and use the strong law along the random subsequence \(N(t)\to\infty\) to squeeze \(t/N(t)\to\mu\).

Remarks

Renewal theory is the cleanest example of random time change: invert a sum process to count events.
