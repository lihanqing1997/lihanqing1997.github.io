Markov Processes and Semigroups

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/markov-chains-transition-kernels/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/recurrence-transience-invariant-measures/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Markov Processes and Semigroups.

Definition. For a continuous-time Markov process, the semigroup \(P_t\) acts on functions by \(P_tf(x)=\mathbb E_x f(X_t)\). The Markov property gives \(P_{s+t}=P_sP_t\).

Central result. The generator \(A\) is formally \(Af=\lim_{t\downarrow0}(P_tf-f)/t\). If \(f\) is in the domain of \(A\), then \(M_t=f(X_t)-f(X_0)-\int_0^tAf(X_s)ds\) is a martingale.

Proof. Using the semigroup identity, \(P_{t+h}f-P_tf=P_t(P_hf-f)\). Divide by \(h\) and let \(h\downarrow0\) to get the Kolmogorov backward equation. Integrating the infinitesimal drift along the path gives the martingale after compensating by \(\int Af\).

How to read it

Generators translate random motion into analysis. For diffusions, the generator is a differential operator; for chains, it is a matrix or jump operator.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
