Markov processes and semigroups

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/markov-chains-transition-kernels/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/recurrence-transience-invariant-measures/" %}

This topic belongs to stochastic processes. The central move is to regard randomness as a whole path rather than as a single random variable. Here the focus is Markov processes and semigroups.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. For a continuous-time Markov process, the semigroup \(P_t\) acts on functions by \(P_tf(x)=\mathbb E_x f(X_t)\). The Markov property gives \(P_{s+t}=P_sP_t\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. The generator \(A\) is formally \(Af=\lim_{t\downarrow0}(P_tf-f)/t\). If \(f\) is in the domain of \(A\), then \(M_t=f(X_t)-f(X_0)-\int_0^tAf(X_s)ds\) is a martingale.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

Using the semigroup identity, \(P_{t+h}f-P_tf=P_t(P_hf-f)\). Divide by \(h\) and let \(h\downarrow0\) to get the Kolmogorov backward equation. Integrating the infinitesimal drift along the path gives the martingale after compensating by \(\int Af\).

Remarks

Generators translate random motion into analysis. For diffusions, the generator is a differential operator; for chains, it is a matrix or jump operator.
