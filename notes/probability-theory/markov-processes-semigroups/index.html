Markov processes generalize Markov chains to continuous time. Their transition operators form semigroups, linking probability to analysis and PDE.

Transition semigroups

A Markov semigroup acts on functions by conditional expectation.

Definition. For a Markov process \(X_t\), define \[ P_tf(x)=\mathbb E_x f(X_t) \] for bounded measurable \(f\). The transition probabilities are \(p_t(x,A)=P_t1_A(x)\).

Semigroup property

Time homogeneity turns conditioning at an intermediate time into operator multiplication.

Theorem. For a time-homogeneous Markov process, \(P_{t+s}=P_tP_s\) and \(P_0=I\).

Proof. By the Markov property, \[ P_{t+s}f(x)=\mathbb E_x[f(X_{t+s})] =\mathbb E_x[\mathbb E(f(X_{t+s})\mid\mathcal F_t)] =\mathbb E_x[P_sf(X_t)] =P_t(P_sf)(x). \]

Generators

The infinitesimal generator is the derivative of the semigroup at zero.

Proposition. For a strongly continuous semigroup on a Banach space, the generator is \[ Af=\lim_{t\downarrow0}\frac{P_tf-f}{t} \] on the set of \(f\) for which the limit exists.

Proof. If \(f\) lies in the generator domain, the semigroup identity gives \(P_{t+h}f-P_tf=P_t(P_hf-f)\). Divide by \(h\) and let \(h\downarrow0\) to obtain \(\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}P_tf=P_tAf\). Under domain invariance, also \(\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}P_tf=AP_tf\).

Remark. For Brownian motion, \(A=\frac12\Delta\), which is why Brownian expectations solve the heat equation.
