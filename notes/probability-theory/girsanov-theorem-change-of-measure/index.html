Girsanov Theorem and Change of Measure

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/applications-ito-formula/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/stochastic-differential-equations/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Girsanov Theorem and Change of Measure.

Definition. Girsanov's theorem describes how Brownian drift changes under an equivalent probability measure. Let \(\theta_t\) be adapted and satisfy a condition such as Novikov's criterion. Define \(Z_t=\exp\{-\int_0^t\theta_sdB_s-\frac12\int_0^t\theta_s^2ds\}\).

Central result. If \((Z_t)\) is a martingale and \(dQ=Z_TdP\) on \(\mathcal F_T\), then \[\widetilde B_t=B_t+\int_0^t\theta_sds\] is a Brownian motion under \(Q\) on \([0,T]\).

Proof. Use exponential martingales to compute conditional characteristic functions of increments under \(Q\). The density tilts the Gaussian increment by a linear exponential factor, shifting its mean while preserving its variance. Localization and approximation extend the argument to adapted \(\theta\).

How to read it

Girsanov is the continuous-time likelihood-ratio theorem. It is indispensable for diffusion statistics and stochastic control.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
