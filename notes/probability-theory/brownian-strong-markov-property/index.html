Strong Markov property of Brownian motion

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/reflection-principle-hitting-times/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-martingales/" %}

This topic belongs to Brownian motion. Brownian motion is both a universal scaling limit and the basic continuous martingale. Here the focus is Strong Markov property of Brownian motion.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. The strong Markov property says that Brownian motion restarts after a stopping time. If \(\tau\) is a stopping time, then \((B_{\tau+t}-B_\tau)_{t\ge0}\) is a Brownian motion independent of \(\mathcal F_\tau\), on \(\{\tau\lt\infty\}\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. Brownian motion has the strong Markov property for every almost surely finite stopping time relative to its usual completed right-continuous filtration.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

First prove the result for stopping times taking finitely many deterministic values, where it follows from independent increments. Approximate a general stopping time from above by dyadic-valued stopping times. Right-continuity of paths and of the filtration allows passage to the limit.

Remarks

The strong Markov property is what makes hitting-time arguments and Brownian potential theory work.
