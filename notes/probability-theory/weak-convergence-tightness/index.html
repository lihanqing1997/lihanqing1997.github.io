Weak convergence is convergence of laws against bounded continuous test functions. Tightness is the compactness condition that lets subsequences of laws converge.

Weak convergence of probability measures

Weak convergence lives on topological spaces, usually metric spaces with Borel sigma-fields.

Definition. Probability measures \(\mu_n\) on a metric space \(S\) converge weakly to \(\mu\), written \(\mu_n\Rightarrow\mu\), if \[ \int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu_n\to\int f\,\mathrm{d}\mu \] for every bounded continuous \(f:S\to\mathbb R\).

Portmanteau theorem

The portmanteau theorem gives equivalent setwise and functionwise formulations.

Theorem. For probability measures on a metric space, \(\mu_n\Rightarrow\mu\) if and only if \(\limsup_n\mu_n(F)\le\mu(F)\) for every closed \(F\), equivalently \(\liminf_n\mu_n(G)\ge\mu(G)\) for every open \(G\).

Proof. Approximate indicators of closed sets from above by bounded continuous functions, for example \(f_k(x)=\max(0,1-kd(x,F))\). This gives the closed-set inequality. Complements give the open-set inequality. Conversely, the layer-cake representation and approximation of bounded continuous functions by simple functions built from continuity sets recover convergence of integrals.

Tightness and Prokhorov

Tight families do not let mass escape to infinity.

Proposition. A family \(\mathcal P\) of probability measures on \(S\) is tight if for every \(\varepsilon\gt0\) there exists compact \(K\subseteq S\) such that \(\inf_{\mu\in\mathcal P}\mu(K)\ge1-\varepsilon\). On Polish spaces, tightness is equivalent to relative compactness for weak convergence.

Proof. The compactness direction is Prokhorov's theorem. Tightness gives compact sets carrying most mass and uses separability to diagonalize measures on a countable convergence-determining class. Any subsequential set function limit is countably additive because tightness prevents loss of mass, hence yields a weak limit.

Remark. For real-valued variables, tightness is equivalent to \(\lim_{K\to\infty}\sup_nP(|X_n|\gt K)=0\).
