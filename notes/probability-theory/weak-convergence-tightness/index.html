Weak Convergence and Tightness

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/modes-of-convergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/characteristic-functions/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Weak Convergence and Tightness.

Definition. Probability measures \(\mu_n\) on a metric space \(S\) converge weakly to \(\mu\), written \(\mu_n\Rightarrow\mu\), if \(\int f\,d\mu_n\to\int f\,d\mu\) for every bounded continuous \(f\). A family is tight if for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is compact \(K\) with \(\sup_n\mu_n(K^c)\lt\varepsilon\).

Central result. On a Polish space, Prokhorov's theorem says that a family of probability measures is relatively compact for weak convergence if and only if it is tight.

Proof. One direction is proved by covering a compact set by finitely many continuity neighborhoods and using weak convergence on closed sets. The converse constructs compact sets that capture most mass by diagonalizing over a countable tightness basis. Polish regularity is what makes compact containment equivalent to subsequential weak limits.

How to read it

Tightness prevents probability mass from escaping to infinity or oscillating outside compact control. It is the compactness step in most functional limit theorems.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
