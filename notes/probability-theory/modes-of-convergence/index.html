Modes of convergence

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/conditional-expectation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/weak-convergence-tightness/" %}

Definition. For random variables \(X_n,X\), the main modes are almost sure convergence, convergence in probability, \(L^p\)-convergence, and convergence in distribution. They are defined by \(P(\lim X_n=X)=1\), \(P(|X_n-X|>\varepsilon)\to0\), \(\mathbb E|X_n-X|^p\to0\), and \(\mathbb Ef(X_n)\to\mathbb Ef(X)\) for bounded continuous \(f\).

Result. Almost sure convergence implies convergence in probability. \(L^p\)-convergence implies convergence in probability. Convergence in probability implies convergence in distribution. If \(X_n\Rightarrow c\) for a constant \(c\), then \(X_n\to c\) in probability.

Proof.

The first implication follows from \(1_{\{|X_n-X|>\varepsilon\}}\to0\) almost surely and dominated convergence. The \(L^p\) implication follows from Markov's inequality. The distributional implication follows by approximating bounded continuous tests on the event \(|X_n-X|\le\delta\). The constant-limit converse uses neighborhoods of \(c\) whose boundaries have zero mass.

Remarks

The mode of convergence determines which operations are legitimate. Limit theory is largely a calculus of these implications plus Slutsky and continuous mapping.
