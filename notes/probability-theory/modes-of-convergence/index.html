11. Modes of convergence

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/conditional-expectation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/weak-convergence-tightness/" %}

Probability has several convergence notions because random variables can converge pointwise, in probability, in distribution, or in mean. Knowing the implications prevents many false arguments.

Four convergence modes

The common modes compare random variables at different levels of strength.

Definition. For random variables \(X_n,X\), write \(X_n\to X\) a.s. if \(P(\lim_nX_n=X)=1\), in probability if \(P(|X_n-X|\gt\varepsilon)\to0\) for every \(\varepsilon\gt0\), in \(L^p\) if \(\mathbb E|X_n-X|^p\to0\), and in distribution if \(\mathbb E f(X_n)\to\mathbb E f(X)\) for every bounded continuous \(f\).

Basic implications

Almost-sure and \(L^p\) convergence imply convergence in probability; convergence in probability implies convergence in distribution.

Theorem. If \(X_n\to X\) a.s. or in \(L^p\), then \(X_n\to X\) in probability. If \(X_n\to X\) in probability, then \(X_n\Rightarrow X\).

Proof. Almost-sure convergence gives \(1_{\{|X_n-X|\gt\varepsilon\}}\to0\) a.s.; dominated convergence gives the probabilities tend to zero. \(L^p\)-convergence gives \(P(|X_n-X|\gt\varepsilon)\le\varepsilon^{-p}\mathbb E|X_n-X|^p\). For convergence in distribution, take bounded continuous \(f\); convergence in probability implies \(f(X_n)\to f(X)\) in probability, and boundedness plus subsequence arguments or dominated convergence along almost-sure subsequences gives convergence of expectations.

Subsequence principle

Convergence in probability is exactly almost-sure convergence along subsequences after further extraction.

Proposition. \(X_n\to X\) in probability if and only if every subsequence has a further subsequence converging to \(X\) almost surely.

Proof. If \(X_n\to X\) in probability, choose a subsubsequence \(n_k\) with \(P(|X_{n_k}-X|\gt2^{-k})\le2^{-k}\). Borel-Cantelli gives almost-sure convergence along \(n_k\). The converse follows because if convergence in probability failed, some subsequence would keep \(P(|X_n-X|\gt\varepsilon)\) bounded away from zero, preventing any almost-sure convergent further subsequence.

Remark. Convergence in distribution to a constant is equivalent to convergence in probability to that constant.
