Itô's formula turns stochastic calculus into a method for producing martingales, solving SDEs, and representing PDE solutions.

Exponential martingales

Applying Itô to exponentials gives the basic likelihood process for Brownian drift changes.

Definition. For constant \(\theta\), \(M_t=\exp(\theta B_t-\theta^2t/2)\) is a martingale.

Itô proof

The drift cancels exactly when the compensator \(- heta^2t/2\) is present.

Theorem. The process \(M_t=\exp(\theta B_t-\theta^2t/2)\) satisfies \(\mathrm{d}M_t=\theta M_t\,\mathrm{d}B_t\).

Proof. Let \(X_t=\theta B_t-\theta^2t/2\). Then \(\mathrm{d}X_t=\theta\,\mathrm{d}B_t-\theta^2\mathrm{d}t/2\) and \(\mathrm{d}[X]_t=\theta^2\mathrm{d}t\). Itô's formula for \(e^{X_t}\) gives \[ \mathrm{d}M_t=M_t\,\mathrm{d}X_t+\frac12M_t\,\mathrm{d}[X]_t =\theta M_t\,\mathrm{d}B_t. \] The stochastic integral is a local martingale, and integrability here gives a true martingale.

PDE representation

Itô's formula identifies generators with differential operators.

Proposition. If \(u\in C^{1,2}\) solves \(\partial_tu+\frac12\Delta u=0\), then \(u(t,B_t)\) is a local martingale.

Proof. Itô's formula gives \[ \mathrm{d}u(t,B_t)=\partial_tu(t,B_t)\,\mathrm{d}t+\nabla u(t,B_t)\cdot\mathrm{d}B_t+\frac12\Delta u(t,B_t)\,\mathrm{d}t. \] The PDE cancels the drift, leaving only the stochastic integral term.

Remark. This is the seed of Feynman-Kac formulas and probabilistic solutions of boundary value problems.
