Applications of Itô Formula

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/ito-formula/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/girsanov-theorem-change-of-measure/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Applications of Itô Formula.

Definition. Itô formula produces martingales, solves linear stochastic equations, and computes transformations. Standard choices are \(f(x)=x^2\), \(f(x)=e^{\theta x}\), \(f(x)=\log x\), and harmonic functions.

Central result. If \(u(t,x)\in C^{1,2}\), then \[du(t,B_t)=\left(\partial_tu+\frac12\Delta u\right)(t,B_t)dt+\nabla u(t,B_t)\,dB_t.\] Hence if \(u\) solves the heat equation, \(u(t,B_t)\) is a local martingale.

Proof. Apply multidimensional Itô formula to the time-space process \((t,B_t)\). The drift term is exactly \(\partial_tu+\frac12\Delta u\). If that vanishes, only the stochastic integral remains, and stochastic integrals are local martingales.

How to read it

The usual strategy is: choose a function whose drift vanishes, apply Itô, then use optional stopping to compute probabilities or expectations.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
