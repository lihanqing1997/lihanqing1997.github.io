Doob Inequalities and Martingale Convergence

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/discrete-time-martingales/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/optional-stopping-sampling/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Doob Inequalities and Martingale Convergence.

Definition. Doob inequalities control the maximum of a martingale by its terminal value. For a submartingale \(X_n\), let \(X_n^*=\max_{k\le n}X_k\).

Central result. If \(X_n\) is a nonnegative submartingale, then for \(\lambda>0\), \[\lambda P(X_n^*\ge\lambda)\le \mathbb E[X_n1_{\{X_n^*\ge\lambda\}}]\le\mathbb EX_n.\] If \(M_n\) is bounded in \(L^p\), \(p>1\), then \(M_n\) converges almost surely and in \(L^p\).

Proof. Stop at \(\tau=\inf\{k:X_k\ge\lambda\}\wedge n\). Optional sampling for submartingales gives \(\mathbb EX_n1_{\{\tau\le n\}}\ge\mathbb EX_\tau1_{\{\tau\le n\}}\ge\lambda P(\tau\le n)\). Convergence follows because the upcrossing inequality bounds the expected number of oscillations across any rational interval.

How to read it

The maximum inequality is the technical heart of martingale convergence. It transforms terminal integrability into pathwise control.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
