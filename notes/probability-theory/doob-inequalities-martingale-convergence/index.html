Doob inequalities and martingale convergence

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/discrete-time-martingales/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/optional-stopping-sampling/" %}

This topic belongs to martingale theory. The guiding idea is to describe random evolution relative to information that grows over time. Here the focus is Doob inequalities and martingale convergence.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. Doob inequalities control the maximum of a martingale by its terminal value. For a submartingale \(X_n\), let \(X_n^*=\max_{k\le n}X_k\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. If \(X_n\) is a nonnegative submartingale, then for \(\lambda>0\), \[\lambda P(X_n^*\ge\lambda)\le \mathbb E[X_n1_{\{X_n^*\ge\lambda\}}]\le\mathbb EX_n.\] If \(M_n\) is bounded in \(L^p\), \(p>1\), then \(M_n\) converges almost surely and in \(L^p\).

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

Stop at \(\tau=\inf\{k:X_k\ge\lambda\}\wedge n\). Optional sampling for submartingales gives \(\mathbb EX_n1_{\{\tau\le n\}}\ge\mathbb EX_\tau1_{\{\tau\le n\}}\ge\lambda P(\tau\le n)\). Convergence follows because the upcrossing inequality bounds the expected number of oscillations across any rational interval.

Remarks

The maximum inequality is the technical heart of martingale convergence. It transforms terminal integrability into pathwise control.
