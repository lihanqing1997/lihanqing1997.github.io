Semimartingales are the largest robust class of integrators for stochastic calculus. They decompose into local martingale noise plus finite-variation drift.

Definition by decomposition

The decomposition isolates martingale and finite-variation components.

Definition. A càdlàg adapted process \(X\) is a semimartingale if it can be written \[ X_t=X_0+M_t+A_t, \] where \(M\) is a local martingale and \(A\) is an adapted finite-variation process.

Stability under smooth maps

Itô's formula shows that smooth functions of semimartingales are semimartingales.

Theorem. If \(X\) is a continuous semimartingale and \(f\in C^2\), then \(f(X)\) is a semimartingale with decomposition given by Itô's formula.

Proof. Write \(X=M+A\). Taylor expand \(f(X_{t_{i+1}})-f(X_{t_i})\) over partitions. First-order terms against \(A\) converge to a pathwise Lebesgue-Stieltjes integral, first-order terms against \(M\) converge to the stochastic integral, and second-order martingale terms converge to \(\frac12\int f''(X_s)\,\mathrm{d}[M]_s\). Higher-order terms vanish by continuity and finite quadratic variation.

Why semimartingales are maximal

Good stochastic integration requires stability under bounded predictable integrands.

Proposition. The Bichteler-Dellacherie theorem characterizes semimartingales as exactly those càdlàg adapted processes for which stochastic integrals of bounded predictable simple integrands have good continuity properties.

Proof. One direction follows from decomposing the integral into a martingale integral plus a finite-variation integral and applying martingale inequalities and variation bounds. The converse is deep: if all bounded predictable simple integrals are controlled, the process must admit a local martingale plus finite-variation decomposition.

Remark. This theorem explains why stochastic calculus is built on semimartingales rather than arbitrary adapted processes.
