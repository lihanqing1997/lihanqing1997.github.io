Product Spaces, Independence, and Fubini-Tonelli

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/distributions-random-variables/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/expected-value/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Product Spaces, Independence, and Fubini-Tonelli.

Definition. Sub-\(\sigma\)-fields \(\mathcal G_i\subset\mathcal F\) are independent if \(P(G_1\cap\cdots\cap G_n)=\prod_iP(G_i)\) for all finite choices \(G_i\in\mathcal G_i\). Random variables are independent when their generated \(\sigma\)-fields are independent.

Central result. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent with laws \(\mu\) and \(\nu\), then \((X,Y)\) has law \(\mu\otimes\nu\), and for nonnegative or integrable \(h\), \[\mathbb E h(X,Y)=\int\!\int h(x,y)\,\mu(dx)\nu(dy).\]

Proof. The identity is true for rectangles because independence gives \(P(X\in A,Y\in B)=\mu(A)\nu(B)\). The rectangles form a \(\pi\)-system generating the product \(\sigma\)-field, so a monotone class argument gives the law. Tonelli and Fubini then give the integral formula.

How to read it

Independence is not merely zero covariance. It is factorization of the whole joint law, which is why product measures and iterated integrals are the right language.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
