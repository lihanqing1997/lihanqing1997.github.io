Independence is factorization of joint probabilities. Measure theory makes it possible to state independence for events, sigma-fields, random variables, and stochastic processes using the same product principle.

Independence of sigma-fields

The cleanest definition is made at the level of information.

Definition. \(\sigma\)-fields \(\mathcal G_i\subseteq\mathcal F\) are independent if for every finite set of indices and every \(G_i\in\mathcal G_i\), \[ P\left(\bigcap_iG_i\right)=\prod_iP(G_i). \] Random variables are independent when their generated \(\sigma\)-fields are independent.

Checking on generators

Independence does not need to be checked on every measurable set.

Theorem. If \(\mathcal C_i\) are pi-systems with \(\sigma(\mathcal C_i)=\mathcal G_i\), and the factorization identity holds for all finite choices \(C_i\in\mathcal C_i\), then \(\mathcal G_i\) are independent.

Proof. For two classes, fix \(C_1\) and collect all \(G_2\) for which \(P(C_1\cap G_2)=P(C_1)P(G_2)\). This is a lambda-system containing \(\mathcal C_2\), hence contains \(\mathcal G_2\). Then fix \(G_2\) and repeat in the first coordinate. The finite case follows by induction.

Products and Fubini-Tonelli

Independence turns joint laws into product measures and makes iterated integration valid.

Proposition. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent with laws \(\mu\) and \(\nu\), then the joint law of \((X,Y)\) is \(\mu\otimes\nu\). Consequently, for nonnegative or integrable \(g\), \[ \mathbb E[g(X,Y)]=\int\!\!\int g(x,y)\,\mu(\mathrm{d}x)\nu(\mathrm{d}y). \]

Proof. The rectangle identity follows from independence: \(P((X,Y)\in A\times B)=P(X\in A)P(Y\in B)=\mu(A)\nu(B)\). Rectangles generate the product \(\sigma\)-field, so uniqueness of product measures gives the joint law. The integral identity is Tonelli's theorem for nonnegative \(g\) and Fubini's theorem for integrable \(g\).

Remark. Pairwise independence is not enough for mutual independence. The definition must hold for every finite subcollection simultaneously.
