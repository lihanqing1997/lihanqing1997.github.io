42. Multidimensional Brownian motion

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/donsker-theorem-brownian-bridge/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-motion-pdes/" %}

Multidimensional Brownian motion combines independent one-dimensional Brownian coordinates and rotationally invariant Gaussian structure.

Definition and covariance

The covariance matrix grows linearly in time.

Definition. A standard \(d\)-dimensional Brownian motion \(B_t\) has \(B_0=0\), continuous paths, independent increments, and \(B_t-B_s\sim N(0,(t-s)I_d)\).

Independent coordinates produce the standard multidimensional process.

Rotational invariance

Orthogonal transformations preserve standard Brownian motion.

Proposition. If \(Q\) is an orthogonal \(d\times d\) matrix and \(B_t\) is standard \(d\)-dimensional Brownian motion, then \(QB_t\) is also standard Brownian motion.

Proof. The paths remain continuous and increments remain independent. Since \(B_t-B_s\sim N(0,(t-s)I_d)\), multiplying by \(Q\) gives a Gaussian vector with covariance \((t-s)QI_dQ^\top=(t-s)I_d\).

Remark. The radial process \(|B_t|\) leads to Bessel processes, while harmonic functions of \(B_t\) generate martingales.
