Multidimensional Brownian Motion

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/donsker-theorem-brownian-bridge/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-motion-pdes/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Multidimensional Brownian Motion.

Definition. A \(d\)-dimensional Brownian motion is \(B_t=(B_t^1,\ldots,B_t^d)\), where the coordinates are independent one-dimensional Brownian motions. Its covariance is \(\mathbb E(B_sB_t^\top)=(s\wedge t)I_d\).

Central result. For any orthogonal matrix \(U\), \((UB_t)_{t\ge0}\) is again a \(d\)-dimensional Brownian motion. The radial process \(\|B_t\|\) is a Bessel-type diffusion.

Proof. Orthogonal transformations preserve centered Gaussian laws and covariance matrices. They also preserve independence and stationarity of increments, so the transformed process satisfies the defining properties. The radial statement follows by applying Itô's formula to \(x\mapsto\|x\|\) away from zero.

How to read it

Rotational invariance is the geometric signature of multidimensional Brownian motion.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
