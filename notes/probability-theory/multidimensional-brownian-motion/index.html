Multidimensional Brownian motion

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/donsker-theorem-brownian-bridge/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-motion-pdes/" %}

This topic belongs to Brownian motion. Brownian motion is both a universal scaling limit and the basic continuous martingale. Here the focus is Multidimensional Brownian motion.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. A \(d\)-dimensional Brownian motion is \(B_t=(B_t^1,\ldots,B_t^d)\), where the coordinates are independent one-dimensional Brownian motions. Its covariance is \(\mathbb E(B_sB_t^\top)=(s\wedge t)I_d\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. For any orthogonal matrix \(U\), \((UB_t)_{t\ge0}\) is again a \(d\)-dimensional Brownian motion. The radial process \(\|B_t\|\) is a Bessel-type diffusion.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

Orthogonal transformations preserve centered Gaussian laws and covariance matrices. They also preserve independence and stationarity of increments, so the transformed process satisfies the defining properties. The radial statement follows by applying Itô's formula to \(x\mapsto\|x\|\) away from zero.

Remarks

Rotational invariance is the geometric signature of multidimensional Brownian motion.
