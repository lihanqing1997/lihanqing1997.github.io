Martingale CLT and applications

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/square-integrable-martingales-quadratic-variation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/kolmogorov-extension/" %}

This topic belongs to martingale theory. The guiding idea is to describe random evolution relative to information that grows over time. Here the focus is Martingale CLT and applications.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. A martingale difference array \(\xi_{n,k}\) satisfies \(\mathbb E(\xi_{n,k}\mid\mathcal F_{n,k-1})=0\). It generalizes independent centered arrays by allowing conditional dependence.

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. If \(\sum_k\mathbb E(\xi_{n,k}^2\mid\mathcal F_{n,k-1})\to1\) in probability and \(\sum_k\mathbb E[\xi_{n,k}^2 1_{\{|\xi_{n,k}|>\varepsilon\}}\mid\mathcal F_{n,k-1}]\to0\), then \(\sum_k\xi_{n,k}\Rightarrow N(0,1)\).

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

The proof expands conditional characteristic functions one step at a time. The martingale difference condition removes the linear term; the conditional variance gives the quadratic term; Lindeberg controls the remainder. Iterating conditional expectations yields convergence to \(e^{-t^2/2}\).

Remarks

This theorem is a workhorse for dependent data: once a statistic has a martingale-difference decomposition, asymptotic normality often follows from conditional variance and Lindeberg checks.
