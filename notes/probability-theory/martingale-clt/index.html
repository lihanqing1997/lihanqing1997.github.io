Martingale CLT and Applications

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/square-integrable-martingales-quadratic-variation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/kolmogorov-extension/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Martingale CLT and Applications.

Definition. A martingale difference array \(\xi_{n,k}\) satisfies \(\mathbb E(\xi_{n,k}\mid\mathcal F_{n,k-1})=0\). It generalizes independent centered arrays by allowing conditional dependence.

Central result. If \(\sum_k\mathbb E(\xi_{n,k}^2\mid\mathcal F_{n,k-1})\to1\) in probability and \(\sum_k\mathbb E[\xi_{n,k}^2 1_{\{|\xi_{n,k}|>\varepsilon\}}\mid\mathcal F_{n,k-1}]\to0\), then \(\sum_k\xi_{n,k}\Rightarrow N(0,1)\).

Proof. The proof expands conditional characteristic functions one step at a time. The martingale difference condition removes the linear term; the conditional variance gives the quadratic term; Lindeberg controls the remainder. Iterating conditional expectations yields convergence to \(e^{-t^2/2}\).

How to read it

This theorem is a workhorse for dependent data: once a statistic has a martingale-difference decomposition, asymptotic normality often follows from conditional variance and Lindeberg checks.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
