Brownian martingales

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-strong-markov-property/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/donsker-theorem-brownian-bridge/" %}

Definition. Brownian motion generates many martingales. With respect to its natural filtration, \(B_t\), \(B_t^2-t\), and exponential transforms are the basic examples.

Result. For each \(\theta\in\mathbb R\), \[M_t=\exp\{\theta B_t-\theta^2t/2\}\] is a martingale. Also \(B_t^2-t\) is a martingale.

Proof.

For \(s\lt t\), write \(B_t=B_s+(B_t-B_s)\), where the increment is independent of \(\mathcal F_s\) and normal with variance \(t-s\). Then \(\mathbb E[e^{\theta(B_t-B_s)-\theta^2(t-s)/2}]=1\), giving the exponential martingale. Similarly, \(\mathbb E(B_t^2-t\mid\mathcal F_s)=B_s^2+(t-s)-t=B_s^2-s\).

Remarks

These martingales are the quickest route to hitting probabilities, tail bounds, and change-of-measure formulas.
