Gaussian vectors and Gaussian processes

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/renewal-random-walks/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-motion-construction/" %}

This topic belongs to Brownian motion. Brownian motion is both a universal scaling limit and the basic continuous martingale. Here the focus is Gaussian vectors and Gaussian processes.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. A vector \(X\in\mathbb R^d\) is Gaussian if every linear combination \(u^\top X\) is one-dimensional normal. A Gaussian process is a process whose every finite-dimensional vector is Gaussian.

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. A Gaussian process is determined by its mean function \(m(t)=\mathbb EX_t\) and covariance function \(K(s,t)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_s,X_t)\). A symmetric positive semidefinite kernel is the covariance kernel of some Gaussian process.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

For finite collections, the characteristic function is \(\exp\{iu^\top m-u^\top\Sigma u/2\}\), so mean and covariance determine the law. Conversely, every positive semidefinite finite covariance matrix defines a Gaussian vector, and consistency over finite sets gives a Gaussian process by Kolmogorov extension.

Remarks

Gaussian processes are linear probability objects: once second moments are known, all finite-dimensional distributions are known.
