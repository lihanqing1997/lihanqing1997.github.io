Gaussian Vectors and Gaussian Processes

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/renewal-random-walks/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-motion-construction/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Gaussian Vectors and Gaussian Processes.

Definition. A vector \(X\in\mathbb R^d\) is Gaussian if every linear combination \(u^\top X\) is one-dimensional normal. A Gaussian process is a process whose every finite-dimensional vector is Gaussian.

Central result. A Gaussian process is determined by its mean function \(m(t)=\mathbb EX_t\) and covariance function \(K(s,t)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_s,X_t)\). A symmetric positive semidefinite kernel is the covariance kernel of some Gaussian process.

Proof. For finite collections, the characteristic function is \(\exp\{iu^\top m-u^\top\Sigma u/2\}\), so mean and covariance determine the law. Conversely, every positive semidefinite finite covariance matrix defines a Gaussian vector, and consistency over finite sets gives a Gaussian process by Kolmogorov extension.

How to read it

Gaussian processes are linear probability objects: once second moments are known, all finite-dimensional distributions are known.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
