35. Gaussian vectors and Gaussian processes

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/renewal-random-walks/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-motion-construction/" %}

Gaussian vectors and processes are determined by first and second moments. This rigidity makes them the central tractable class of probability theory.

Gaussian vectors

A vector is Gaussian when every linear projection is one-dimensional Gaussian.

Definition. A random vector \(X\in\mathbb R^d\) is Gaussian if \(\langle t,X\rangle\) is normally distributed for every \(t\in\mathbb R^d\). Its law is determined by mean \(m=\mathbb EX\) and covariance matrix \(\Sigma=\mathbb E[(X-m)(X-m)^\top]\).

Characteristic function

The Gaussian characteristic function is explicit.

Theorem. If \(X\sim N(m,\Sigma)\), then \[ \mathbb E e^{i\langle t,X\rangle} =\exp\left(i\langle t,m\rangle-\frac12\langle t,\Sigma t\rangle\right). \]

Proof. Diagonalize \(\Sigma=AA^\top\) and write \(X=m+AZ\) with \(Z\) standard normal in a suitable dimension. Independence of standard normal coordinates gives \(\mathbb E e^{i\langle A^\top t,Z\rangle}=\exp(-|A^\top t|^2/2)\), which equals the displayed formula.

Gaussian processes

A Gaussian process is specified by mean and covariance functions.

Proposition. A process \((X_t)_{t\in T}\) is Gaussian if every finite vector \((X_{t_1},\dots,X_{t_n})\) is Gaussian. Its finite-dimensional laws are determined by \(m(t)=\mathbb EX_t\) and \(K(s,t)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_s,X_t)\), where \(K\) must be positive semidefinite.

Proof. For any finite \(t_1,\dots,t_n\), the matrix \(K(t_i,t_j)\) is nonnegative definite because \(a^\top Ka=\operatorname{Var}(\sum_i a_iX_{t_i})\ge0\). Conversely, a positive semidefinite kernel defines consistent finite-dimensional Gaussian laws, and Kolmogorov extension gives a Gaussian process.

Remark. Brownian motion is the centered Gaussian process with covariance \(K(s,t)=s\wedge t\).
