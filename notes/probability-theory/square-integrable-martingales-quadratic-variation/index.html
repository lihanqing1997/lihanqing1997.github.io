Square-integrable martingales and quadratic variation

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/uniform-integrability-martingale-convergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/martingale-clt/" %}

This topic belongs to martingale theory. The guiding idea is to describe random evolution relative to information that grows over time. Here the focus is Square-integrable martingales and quadratic variation.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. For a square-integrable martingale \(M_n\), define increments \(\Delta M_k=M_k-M_{k-1}\). Its predictable quadratic variation is \(\langle M\rangle_n=\sum_{k=1}^n\mathbb E[(\Delta M_k)^2\mid\mathcal F_{k-1}]\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. The process \(M_n^2-\langle M\rangle_n\) is a martingale. Consequently \(\mathbb EM_n^2=\mathbb E\langle M\rangle_n+\mathbb EM_0^2\) when \(M_0\) is square-integrable.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

Expand \(M_n^2-M_{n-1}^2=2M_{n-1}\Delta M_n+(\Delta M_n)^2\). Conditional expectation given \(\mathcal F_{n-1}\) kills the first term and leaves \(\mathbb E[(\Delta M_n)^2\mid\mathcal F_{n-1}]\). Therefore the compensated square has zero conditional drift.

Remarks

Quadratic variation measures accumulated conditional variance. It is the quantity that survives in martingale CLTs and stochastic calculus.
