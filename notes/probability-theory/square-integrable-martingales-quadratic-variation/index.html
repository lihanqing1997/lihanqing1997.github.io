Square-integrable martingales have an \(L^2\) geometry. Orthogonal increments generate predictable quadratic variation, the discrete ancestor of continuous-time bracket processes.

Orthogonal increments

\(L^2\) martingales behave like sums of orthogonal differences.

Definition. If \(M_n\in L^2\) is a martingale, define increments \(\Delta M_k=M_k-M_{k-1}\). Then \(\mathbb E[\Delta M_k\mid\mathcal F_{k-1}]=0\).

Variance decomposition

The second moment splits into conditional variances of increments.

Theorem. For a square-integrable martingale, \[ \mathbb E M_n^2=\mathbb E M_0^2+\sum_{k=1}^n\mathbb E(\Delta M_k)^2. \]

Proof. For \(j\lt k\), \(\mathbb E[\Delta M_j\Delta M_k]=\mathbb E[\Delta M_j\mathbb E(\Delta M_k\mid\mathcal F_{k-1})]=0\), since \(\Delta M_j\) is \(\mathcal F_{k-1}\)-measurable. Expanding \(M_n=M_0+\sum_k\Delta M_k\) in \(L^2\) gives the identity.

Predictable quadratic variation

The compensator of squared martingale increments is predictable.

Proposition. Define \[ \langle M\rangle_n=\sum_{k=1}^n\mathbb E[(\Delta M_k)^2\mid\mathcal F_{k-1}]. \] Then \(M_n^2-\langle M\rangle_n\) is a martingale when \(M_0=0\).

Proof. The increment of \(M_n^2\) is \(2M_{n-1}\Delta M_n+(\Delta M_n)^2\). Conditional expectation given \(\mathcal F_{n-1}\) kills the first term and leaves \(\mathbb E[(\Delta M_n)^2\mid\mathcal F_{n-1}]\). Subtracting the increment of \(\langle M\rangle\) gives conditional mean zero.

Remark. In continuous time, \(\langle M\rangle\) becomes the predictable bracket and controls stochastic integrals.
