Markov chains are processes whose future depends on the past only through the present. Transition kernels encode this dependence in a measurable way.

Transition kernels

A kernel is a probability measure depending measurably on the current state.

Definition. A transition kernel \(K\) from \(S\) to \(S\) assigns to each \(x\in S\) a probability measure \(K(x,\cdot)\), and \(x\mapsto K(x,A)\) is measurable for each measurable \(A\).

Markov property

The Markov property is a conditional distribution identity.

Theorem. A process \(X_n\) is a time-homogeneous Markov chain with kernel \(K\) if \[ P(X_{n+1}\in A\mid\mathcal F_n)=K(X_n,A) \] for every measurable \(A\).

Proof. It is enough to check the identity against bounded measurable test functions. For indicators it is the stated property; simple functions follow by linearity and bounded measurable functions by monotone-class arguments. Iterating the one-step identity gives conditional laws of the future depending only on \(X_n\).

Chapman-Kolmogorov equations

Multi-step transitions are powers of the kernel.

Proposition. Define \(K^{m+n}(x,A)=\int K^m(y,A)K^n(x,\mathrm{d}y)\). Then \[ P(X_{m+n}\in A\mid X_0=x)=K^{m+n}(x,A). \]

Proof. Condition on \(X_n\). The Markov property gives the future transition \(K^m(X_n,A)\), and integrating over the law \(K^n(x,\mathrm{d}y)\) gives the kernel convolution formula.

Remark. For countable state spaces, kernels are transition matrices and the formula becomes ordinary matrix multiplication.
