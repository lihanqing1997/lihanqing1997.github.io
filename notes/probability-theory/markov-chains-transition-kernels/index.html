Markov Chains and Transition Kernels

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/path-spaces-finite-dimensional-distributions/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/markov-processes-semigroups/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Markov Chains and Transition Kernels.

Definition. A transition kernel \(K(x,A)\) gives the conditional law of the next state given the present state. A Markov chain satisfies \(P(X_{n+1}\in A\mid\mathcal F_n)=K(X_n,A)\).

Central result. The \(n\)-step kernels satisfy the Chapman-Kolmogorov equations \[K^{m+n}(x,A)=\int K^m(y,A)K^n(x,dy).\] If \(\mu_0\) is the initial law, then \(\mu_n=\mu_0K^n\).

Proof. Condition on \(X_n\). The probability of reaching \(A\) after \(m+n\) steps is the integral over the intermediate state \(y\) of the probability of reaching \(A\) in \(m\) more steps times the probability of being at \(dy\) after \(n\) steps.

How to read it

A Markov chain compresses history into the current state. Transition kernels are the operator form of that compression.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
