Characteristic functions convert convolution and weak convergence into pointwise analytic statements. They are the main Fourier tool of classical limit theory.

Fourier transform of a law

The characteristic function is always defined and always bounded.

Definition. For a real random variable \(X\), its characteristic function is \[ \varphi_X(t)=\mathbb E e^{itX},\qquad t\in\mathbb R. \] For \(X\in\mathbb R^d\), use \(\varphi_X(t)=\mathbb E e^{i\langle t,X\rangle}\).

Uniqueness and Levy continuity

A law is determined by its characteristic function, and pointwise convergence at a continuous limit identifies weak convergence.

Theorem. If \(\varphi_X=\varphi_Y\), then \(X\stackrel d=Y\). If \(\varphi_n(t)\to\varphi(t)\) pointwise and \(\varphi\) is continuous at \(0\), then \(\varphi\) is the characteristic function of a probability law \(\mu\), and the corresponding laws \(\mu_n\Rightarrow\mu\).

Proof. Uniqueness follows by convolving the measures with a Gaussian density: equality of characteristic functions implies equality of the smoothed densities by Fourier inversion, and letting the Gaussian variance go to zero recovers the original measures. For the continuity theorem, pointwise convergence plus continuity at zero gives tightness, then every weakly convergent subsequence has characteristic function \(\varphi\), hence the same limit by uniqueness.

Sums and moments

Independence changes sums into products.

Proposition. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, then \(\varphi_{X+Y}(t)=\varphi_X(t)\varphi_Y(t)\). If \(\mathbb E|X|^k\lt\infty\), then \(\varphi_X\) has derivatives up to order \(k\) at zero with \(\varphi_X^{(k)}(0)=i^k\mathbb E X^k\).

Proof. For independence, factor \(\mathbb E[e^{itX}e^{itY}]\) into the product of expectations. For moments, differentiate \(e^{itX}\) under the expectation; domination by \(|X|^k\) justifies the interchange near zero.

Remark. Characteristic functions are especially robust for triangular arrays because no densities or smoothness of distributions are required.
