Characteristic functions

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/weak-convergence-tightness/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/weak-law-of-large-numbers/" %}

This topic belongs to classical limit theory. The main question is how random quantities behave as the sample size or time scale grows. Here the focus is Characteristic functions.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. The characteristic function of a real random variable \(X\) is \(\varphi_X(t)=\mathbb E e^{itX}\). It always exists, is uniformly continuous, satisfies \(\varphi_X(0)=1\), and determines the law of \(X\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. Lévy's continuity theorem: \(X_n\Rightarrow X\) if and only if \(\varphi_{X_n}(t)\to\varphi_X(t)\) for every \(t\in\mathbb R\). More generally, pointwise convergence to a function continuous at \(0\) gives weak convergence to the law with that characteristic function.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

Weak convergence implies convergence of characteristic functions because \(e^{itx}\) is bounded and continuous. Conversely, inversion formulas recover probabilities of intervals from characteristic functions at continuity points; pointwise convergence plus tightness extracted from continuity at zero passes these interval probabilities to the limit.

Remarks

Characteristic functions turn convolution into multiplication. This makes them ideal for sums of independent random variables and for proving central limit theorems.
