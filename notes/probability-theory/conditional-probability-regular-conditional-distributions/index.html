Conditional probability is probability after observing information. Regular conditional distributions make this idea a measurable kernel rather than only an almost-sure identity.

Conditioning on a sigma-field

Conditional probabilities are conditional expectations of indicators.

Definition. For an event \(A\in\mathcal F\) and a sub-\(\sigma\)-field \(\mathcal G\), define \[ P(A\mid\mathcal G)=\mathbb E[1_A\mid\mathcal G]. \] It is a \(\mathcal G\)-measurable random variable, unique almost surely.

Regular conditional laws

A regular conditional distribution chooses versions consistently over all target sets.

Theorem. If \(X\) takes values in a standard Borel space \(S\), then there exists a probability kernel \(K(\omega,B)\) such that \(K(\cdot,B)\) is \(\mathcal G\)-measurable, \(K(\omega,\cdot)\) is a probability measure, and \(K(\cdot,B)=P(X\in B\mid\mathcal G)\) a.s. for every \(B\in\mathcal B(S)\).

Proof. The proof uses the fact that standard Borel spaces are measurably isomorphic to Borel subsets of \([0,1]\). One constructs conditional distribution functions on a countable generating class with rational endpoints, chooses right-continuous versions outside a null set, and extends from the generating class to all Borel sets by the monotone-class theorem.

Disintegration formula

Regular conditionals turn conditional expectations into integrals against kernels.

Proposition. If \(K\) is a regular conditional law of \(X\) given \(\mathcal G\), then for nonnegative or integrable \(g\), \[ \mathbb E[g(X)\mid\mathcal G](\omega)=\int_S g(x)\,K(\omega,\mathrm{d}x) \] for a suitable version.

Proof. For indicators \(g=1_B\), this is the defining property of \(K\). Linearity gives simple functions, monotone convergence gives nonnegative measurable functions, and positive-negative decomposition gives integrable functions.

Remark. Regular conditional probabilities may fail on arbitrary measurable spaces; standard Borel hypotheses are not decorative.
