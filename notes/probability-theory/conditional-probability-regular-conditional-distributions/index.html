Conditional Probability and Regular Conditional Distributions

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/pushforward-measures-densities/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/conditional-expectation/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Conditional Probability and Regular Conditional Distributions.

Definition. A probability kernel from \((S,\mathcal S)\) to \((T,\mathcal T)\) is a map \(K(s,B)\) such that \(K(s,\cdot)\) is a probability measure and \(s\mapsto K(s,B)\) is measurable. A regular conditional distribution of \(Y\) given \(X\) is a kernel \(K(X,B)=P(Y\in B\mid X)\).

Central result. If \(S,T\) are standard Borel spaces, then regular conditional distributions exist. They satisfy \[P(X\in A,Y\in B)=\int_A K(x,B)\,\mu_X(dx).\]

Proof. The construction is first made on a countable generating class of \(\mathcal T\), where conditional expectations of indicators exist. Standard Borel regularity allows these conditional probabilities to be chosen consistently so that they extend to a probability measure in \(B\). The displayed identity characterizes the kernel.

How to read it

Regular conditional distributions let us speak rigorously about the conditional law at \(X=x\), even when \(P(X=x)=0\).

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
