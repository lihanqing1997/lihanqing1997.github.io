Radon-Nikodym derivatives, densities, and change of measure

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/lp-spaces-inequalities-uniform-integrability/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/conditional-probability-regular-conditional-distributions/" %}

Definition. If \(\nu\ll\mu\), the Radon-Nikodym theorem gives a measurable function \(d\nu/d\mu\) such that \(\nu(A)=\int_A(d\nu/d\mu)d\mu\). In probability this derivative is a density or likelihood ratio.

Result. For every nonnegative or \(\nu\)-integrable \(f\), \[\int f\,d\nu=\int f\frac{d\nu}{d\mu}\,d\mu.\] If \(Q\ll P\) with \(Z=dQ/dP\), then \(\mathbb E_QX=\mathbb E_P[XZ]\).

Proof.

The identity is true for indicators by the definition of the derivative. Linearity gives it for simple functions. Monotone convergence gives the nonnegative case, and positive-negative decomposition gives the integrable case.

Remarks

Change of measure turns one probability model into another by reweighting paths. This is the common language behind likelihood ratios, importance sampling, Bayes formula, and Girsanov's theorem.
