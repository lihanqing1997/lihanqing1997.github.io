Densities and Radon-Nikodym derivatives are the measure-theoretic language of changing reference measure. They unify likelihood ratios, change of variables, and change of probability.

Absolute continuity and densities

A density is a derivative of one measure with respect to another.

Definition. For measures \(\nu,\mu\) on \((S,\mathcal S)\), write \(\nu\ll\mu\) if \(\mu(A)=0\) implies \(\nu(A)=0\). A Radon-Nikodym derivative \(\mathrm{d}\nu/\mathrm{d}\mu\) is a nonnegative measurable function \(f\) such that \(\nu(A)=\int_A f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\) for every \(A\in\mathcal S\).

Radon-Nikodym theorem

The theorem is the rigorous existence result behind densities.

Theorem. If \(\nu\ll\mu\) and both measures are \(\sigma\)-finite, then there exists a measurable \(f\ge0\), unique \(\mu\)-a.e., such that \(\nu(A)=\int_A f\,\mathrm{d}\mu\) for all \(A\).

Proof. The standard proof first handles finite measures by maximizing \(\int g\,\mathrm{d}\mu\) over simple functions whose induced measures are dominated by \(\nu\), or equivalently by Hilbert-space projection for finite measures. The residual measure is shown singular to \(\mu\); absolute continuity forces that residual to vanish. The \(\sigma\)-finite case follows by decomposing the space into finite-measure pieces and patching the derivatives.

Change of probability

Likelihood ratios reweight expectations.

Proposition. If \(Q\ll P\) with \(Z=\mathrm{d}Q/\mathrm{d}P\), then for every nonnegative or \(Q\)-integrable \(X\), \[ \mathbb E_QX=\mathbb E_P[ZX]. \]

Proof. For \(X=1_A\), the identity is \(Q(A)=\int_AZ\,\mathrm{d}P\). Extend to nonnegative simple functions by linearity, to nonnegative measurable functions by monotone convergence, and to integrable functions by decomposing into positive and negative parts.

Remark. In statistics, \(Z\) is a likelihood ratio; in stochastic calculus, carefully chosen \(Z_t\) changes drift while preserving martingale structure under the new law.
