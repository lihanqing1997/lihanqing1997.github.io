Berry-Esseen and Rates of Convergence

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/central-limit-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/poisson-convergence-processes/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Berry-Esseen and Rates of Convergence.

Definition. A limit theorem gives convergence; a rate asks how fast. Let \(F_n\) be the distribution function of \((S_n-n\mu)/(\sigma\sqrt n)\), and let \(\Phi\) be the standard normal cdf.

Central result. If \(X_i\) are i.i.d. with \(\mathbb E|X_1-\mu|^3=\rho\lt\infty\), then \[\sup_x|F_n(x)-\Phi(x)|\le C\frac{\rho}{\sigma^3\sqrt n}\] for a universal constant \(C\).

Proof. The proof compares characteristic functions and then applies a smoothing inequality that turns Fourier error into Kolmogorov distance. The third absolute moment controls the third-order Taylor remainder in the characteristic function expansion, producing the \(n^{-1/2}\) rate.

How to read it

Rates matter when asymptotics are used quantitatively. Berry-Esseen identifies the third moment as the first natural obstruction to a uniform normal approximation.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
