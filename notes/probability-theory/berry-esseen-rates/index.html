Berry-Esseen and rates of convergence

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/central-limit-theorem/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/poisson-convergence-processes/" %}

This topic belongs to classical limit theory. The main question is how random quantities behave as the sample size or time scale grows. Here the focus is Berry-Esseen and rates of convergence.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. A limit theorem gives convergence; a rate asks how fast. Let \(F_n\) be the distribution function of \((S_n-n\mu)/(\sigma\sqrt n)\), and let \(\Phi\) be the standard normal cdf.

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. If \(X_i\) are i.i.d. with \(\mathbb E|X_1-\mu|^3=\rho\lt\infty\), then \[\sup_x|F_n(x)-\Phi(x)|\le C\frac{\rho}{\sigma^3\sqrt n}\] for a universal constant \(C\).

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

The proof compares characteristic functions and then applies a smoothing inequality that turns Fourier error into Kolmogorov distance. The third absolute moment controls the third-order Taylor remainder in the characteristic function expansion, producing the \(n^{-1/2}\) rate.

Remarks

Rates matter when asymptotics are used quantitatively. Berry-Esseen identifies the third moment as the first natural obstruction to a uniform normal approximation.
