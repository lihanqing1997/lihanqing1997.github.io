Itô's formula is the chain rule for semimartingales. The extra second-order term is the analytic signature of quadratic variation.

One-dimensional continuous formula

For Brownian-driven processes, the second derivative appears with quadratic variation.

Definition. If \(X_t=X_0+\int_0^t b_s\,\mathrm{d}s+\int_0^t\sigma_s\,\mathrm{d}B_s\) and \(f\in C^2\), then \[ f(X_t)=f(X_0)+\int_0^t f'(X_s)\sigma_s\,\mathrm{d}B_s +\int_0^t\left(f'(X_s)b_s+\frac12f''(X_s)\sigma_s^2\right)\mathrm{d}s. \]

Taylor expansion proof

The formula is Taylor's theorem plus quadratic variation.

Theorem. Itô's formula holds for continuous semimartingales, with \(\frac12\int f''(X_s)\,\mathrm{d}[X]_s\) replacing the Brownian variance term.

Proof. Apply Taylor's formula along a partition: \[ f(X_{t_{i+1}})-f(X_{t_i})=f'(X_{t_i})\Delta X_i+\frac12f''(X_{t_i})(\Delta X_i)^2+r_i. \] The first-order martingale terms converge to the stochastic integral, finite-variation first-order terms converge pathwise, the squared martingale increments converge to quadratic variation, and the remainder vanishes because continuous semimartingales have controlled quadratic variation.

Multidimensional form

Cross-variation supplies the Hessian contraction.

Proposition. For a continuous \(\mathbb R^d\)-valued semimartingale \(X\) and \(f\in C^2(\mathbb R^d)\), \[ \mathrm{d}f(X_t)=\sum_i\partial_if(X_t)\,\mathrm{d}X_t^i +\frac12\sum_{i,j}\partial_{ij}f(X_t)\,\mathrm{d}[X^i,X^j]_t. \]

Proof. Use the multivariate Taylor expansion. The linear terms converge to stochastic integrals against each coordinate. Quadratic products of increments converge to covariations \([X^i,X^j]\), while higher-order terms vanish along refining partitions.

Remark. Every Itô formula should be read together with the table \(\mathrm{d}B_t^2=\mathrm{d}t\), \(\mathrm{d}t\,\mathrm{d}B_t=0\), and \(\mathrm{d}t^2=0\).
