The Itô integral with respect to Brownian motion is first an \(L^2\) isometry for predictable square-integrable integrands, then a localized construction.

Simple predictable integrands

The integral is defined by summing known coefficients times future increments.

Definition. For \(H_t=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}H_k1_{(t_k,t_{k+1}]}(t)\), with \(H_k\in\mathcal F_{t_k}\), define \[ \int_0^T H_t\,\mathrm{d}B_t=\sum_kH_k(B_{t_{k+1}}-B_{t_k}). \]

Itô isometry

The isometry is the central construction theorem.

Theorem. For square-integrable predictable \(H\), \[ \mathbb E\left(\int_0^T H_t\,\mathrm{d}B_t\right)^2 =\mathbb E\int_0^T H_t^2\,\mathrm{d}t. \]

Proof. For simple \(H\), cross terms vanish because disjoint Brownian increments are conditionally centered and independent of the past. The diagonal terms give \(\mathbb E[H_k^2(t_{k+1}-t_k)]\). Thus the map from simple predictable processes into \(L^2\) is an isometry. Complete the construction by taking the \(L^2(\Omega\times[0,T])\)-closure of simple predictable processes.

Martingale and bracket

The stochastic integral is a martingale with bracket equal to accumulated squared integrand.

Proposition. If \(M_t=\int_0^tH_s\,\mathrm{d}B_s\), then \(M_t\) is a square-integrable martingale and \[ \langle M\rangle_t=\int_0^tH_s^2\,\mathrm{d}s. \]

Proof. For simple integrands, the martingale property follows from conditional mean-zero Brownian increments. The squared martingale minus \(\int H^2\,\mathrm{d}s\) has zero conditional increments by the same calculation. Approximation by simple processes and the Itô isometry pass both identities to general square-integrable predictable \(H\).

Remark. The notation \(\mathrm{d}B_t\) is not an ordinary differential; the integral is defined by martingale limits.
