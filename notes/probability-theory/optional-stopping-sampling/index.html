25. Optional stopping and optional sampling

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/doob-inequalities-martingale-convergence/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/uniform-integrability-martingale-convergence/" %}

Optional stopping explains when a martingale remains fair at random times. The hypotheses are not cosmetic: without boundedness, integrability, or uniform integrability, stopping can create paradoxes.

Stopped processes

Stopping a martingale at a stopping time preserves the martingale property under mild hypotheses.

Definition. For a process \(M_n\) and stopping time \(\tau\), the stopped process is \(M_{n\wedge\tau}\).

Bounded optional stopping

Bounded stopping times are the safest form of the theorem.

Theorem. If \(M_n\) is a martingale and \(\tau\le N\) is a bounded stopping time, then \(\mathbb E M_\tau=\mathbb E M_0\). More generally, \(M_{n\wedge\tau}\) is a martingale.

Proof. Write \(M_{(n+1)\wedge\tau}-M_{n\wedge\tau}=1_{\{\tau\gt n\}}(M_{n+1}-M_n)\). The indicator is \(\mathcal F_n\)-measurable, so its conditional expectation times the martingale increment is zero. Thus the stopped process is a martingale. Since \(\tau\le N\), \(M_\tau=M_{N\wedge\tau}\), and expectations are constant.

Uniform integrability condition

Unbounded stopping requires a limiting argument with uniform integrability.

Proposition. If \(M_n\) is a martingale, \(\tau\) is finite a.s., and \(\{M_{n\wedge\tau}:n\ge0\}\) is uniformly integrable, then \(M_\tau\in L^1\) and \(\mathbb E M_\tau=\mathbb E M_0\).

Proof. Bounded optional stopping gives \(\mathbb E M_{n\wedge\tau}=\mathbb E M_0\). Since \(\tau\lt\infty\) a.s., \(M_{n\wedge\tau}\to M_\tau\) a.s. Uniform integrability upgrades this to \(L^1\)-convergence, so expectations converge to \(\mathbb E M_\tau\).

Remark. Classic gambler's ruin computations are optional-stopping arguments with bounded hitting times or carefully justified limits.
