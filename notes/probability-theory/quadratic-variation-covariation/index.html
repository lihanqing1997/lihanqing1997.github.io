Quadratic Variation and Covariation

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/continuous-time-martingales-local-martingales/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/semimartingales/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Quadratic Variation and Covariation.

Definition. For a continuous semimartingale \(X\), its quadratic variation \([X]_t\) is the limit in probability of \(\sum_i(X_{t_{i+1}}-X_{t_i})^2\) over partitions with mesh tending to zero. Covariation is defined by polarization: \([X,Y]=([X+Y]-[X]-[Y])/2\).

Central result. Brownian motion satisfies \([B]_t=t\). A finite-variation continuous process \(A\) has \([A]=0\), and \([M+A]=[M]\) for continuous local martingales \(M\).

Proof. For Brownian motion, independent increments give expectation \(t\) and variance tending to zero for the squared-increment sums. Finite variation contributes at most \(\sup_i|\Delta A_i|\sum_i|\Delta A_i|\to0\). Cross terms vanish by Cauchy-Schwarz and polarization.

How to read it

Quadratic variation records second-order path roughness. It is the correction term that distinguishes stochastic calculus from ordinary calculus.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
