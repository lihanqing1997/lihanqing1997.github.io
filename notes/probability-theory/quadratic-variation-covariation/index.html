Quadratic variation and covariation

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/continuous-time-martingales-local-martingales/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/semimartingales/" %}

This topic belongs to stochastic calculus. Ordinary calculus must be modified because martingale increments have non-negligible quadratic variation. Here the focus is Quadratic variation and covariation.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. For a continuous semimartingale \(X\), its quadratic variation \([X]_t\) is the limit in probability of \(\sum_i(X_{t_{i+1}}-X_{t_i})^2\) over partitions with mesh tending to zero. Covariation is defined by polarization: \([X,Y]=([X+Y]-[X]-[Y])/2\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. Brownian motion satisfies \([B]_t=t\). A finite-variation continuous process \(A\) has \([A]=0\), and \([M+A]=[M]\) for continuous local martingales \(M\).

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

For Brownian motion, independent increments give expectation \(t\) and variance tending to zero for the squared-increment sums. Finite variation contributes at most \(\sup_i|\Delta A_i|\sum_i|\Delta A_i|\to0\). Cross terms vanish by Cauchy-Schwarz and polarization.

Remarks

Quadratic variation records second-order path roughness. It is the correction term that distinguishes stochastic calculus from ordinary calculus.
