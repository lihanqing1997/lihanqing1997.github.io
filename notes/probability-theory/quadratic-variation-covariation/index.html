46. Quadratic variation and covariation

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/continuous-time-martingales-local-martingales/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/semimartingales/" %}

Quadratic variation measures accumulated squared increments. It is the pathwise trace left by martingale roughness and is the source of Itô correction terms.

Quadratic variation

Finite variation paths have zero quadratic variation; Brownian paths do not.

Definition. For a process \(X\), quadratic variation along partitions \(\pi_n\) is the limit, when it exists, \[ [X]_t=\lim_{|\pi_n|\to0}\sum_{t_i\le t}(X_{t_{i+1}}-X_{t_i})^2. \]

Brownian quadratic variation

Brownian motion accumulates quadratic variation at deterministic rate.

Theorem. For Brownian motion, \([B]_t=t\) in probability, and almost surely along suitable refining deterministic partitions.

Proof. For a partition of \([0,t]\), independence gives expectation of the sum of squared increments equal to \(t\). The variance is \(2\sum_i(\Delta t_i)^2\), which tends to zero as the mesh tends to zero. Hence the sums converge to \(t\) in \(L^2\) and in probability. Almost-sure convergence follows along dyadic partitions by summability of variances plus Borel-Cantelli.

Covariation and polarization

Cross-variation is obtained by polarizing quadratic variation.

Proposition. For semimartingales \(X,Y\), \[ [X,Y]_t=\frac14([X+Y]_t-[X-Y]_t). \] For independent Brownian coordinates \(B^i,B^j\), \([B^i,B^j]_t=0\) when \(i\ne j\).

Proof. The identity follows algebraically from \((\Delta X+\Delta Y)^2-(\Delta X-\Delta Y)^2=4\Delta X\Delta Y\), then passing to limits over partitions. Independent Brownian coordinates have products of increments with mean zero and variances summing to zero along fine partitions.

Remark. The Itô rule \(\mathrm{d}B_t\,\mathrm{d}B_t=\mathrm{d}t\) is shorthand for this quadratic variation statement.
