38. Brownian path properties

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-motion-construction/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/reflection-principle-hitting-times/" %}

Brownian paths are continuous but extremely irregular. Their sample path properties explain why stochastic calculus differs from ordinary calculus.

Holder regularity

Brownian paths are Hölder continuous below order \(1/2\), but not at or above \(1/2\).

Definition. A function \(f\) is locally Hölder of order \(\alpha\) if on each compact time interval \(|f(t)-f(s)|\le C|t-s|^\alpha\) for some finite random or deterministic \(C\).

Kolmogorov continuity estimate

Gaussian moments imply sub-half Hölder regularity.

Theorem. For every \(\alpha\lt1/2\), Brownian paths are locally Hölder continuous of order \(\alpha\) almost surely.

Proof. Gaussian moments give \(\mathbb E|B_t-B_s|^p=C_p|t-s|^{p/2}\). Choose \(p\) large enough that \(p/2\gt1\). Kolmogorov-Chentsov yields a modification whose paths are Hölder of every order less than \((p/2-1)/p=1/2-1/p\). Letting \(p\) range over large integers gives every \(\alpha\lt1/2\).

Nowhere differentiability

Brownian motion is almost surely nowhere differentiable.

Proposition. With probability one, \(t\mapsto B_t\) has no finite derivative at any time \(t\ge0\).

Proof. At dyadic scales, increments of size \(2^{-n}\) are Gaussian with standard deviation \(2^{-n/2}\), much larger than the differentiable scale \(2^{-n}\). Reflection estimates and Borel-Cantelli show that on every interval there are increments whose ratio \(|B_{t+h}-B_t|/h\) is arbitrarily large. Applying this on a countable base of intervals rules out differentiability anywhere.

Remark. Brownian continuity is topological regularity; quadratic variation, not ordinary variation, is the correct calculus scale.
