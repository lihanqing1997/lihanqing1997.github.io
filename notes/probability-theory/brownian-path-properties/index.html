Brownian path properties

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/brownian-motion-construction/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/reflection-principle-hitting-times/" %}

Definition. Brownian paths are continuous but rough. They are Hölder continuous of every order \(\alpha\lt1/2\) on compact intervals, but not of order \(1/2\), and they are almost surely nowhere differentiable.

Result. For every \(T\lt\infty\) and \(\alpha\lt1/2\), Brownian motion has an \(\alpha\)-Hölder continuous modification on \([0,T]\). Also \(\sum_i(B_{t_{i+1}}-B_{t_i})^2\to T\) along refining partitions of \([0,T]\).

Proof.

Gaussian moment bounds give \(\mathbb E|B_t-B_s|^p=C_p|t-s|^{p/2}\). Kolmogorov's criterion yields Hölder regularity below \(1/2\). For quadratic variation, independent increments give expectation \(T\) and variance tending to zero for regular partitions; refinement arguments extend the result.

Remarks

The roughness is exactly why ordinary calculus fails. Brownian increments are order \(\sqrt{dt}\), so squared increments accumulate to order \(dt\).
