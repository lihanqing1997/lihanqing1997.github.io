Central limit theorems

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/triangular-arrays-truncation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/berry-esseen-rates/" %}

Definition. The central limit theorem studies normalized sums \(S_n=\sum_{i=1}^nX_i\). For i.i.d. variables with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), the natural normalization is \((S_n-n\mu)/(\sigma\sqrt n)\).

Result. If \(X_i\) are i.i.d. with \(\mathbb EX_1=\mu\) and \(0\lt\sigma^2=\operatorname{Var}(X_1)\lt\infty\), then \[\frac{S_n-n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt n}\Rightarrow N(0,1).\]

Proof.

Assume \(\mu=0\), \(\sigma=1\). The characteristic function of \(X_1/\sqrt n\) is \(1-t^2/(2n)+o(1/n)\). Hence the characteristic function of \(S_n/\sqrt n\) is \((1-t^2/(2n)+o(1/n))^n\to e^{-t^2/2}\), the characteristic function of \(N(0,1)\).

Remarks

The CLT explains why Gaussian approximations appear across statistics. The theorem is not about normal data; it is about aggregation.
