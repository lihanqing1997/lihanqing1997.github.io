Central limit theorems explain why Gaussian laws appear as universal fluctuation limits. The correct scale is \(\sqrt n\), and the mechanism is accumulation of many small independent effects.

Standard normalization

Centering removes deterministic drift; scaling reveals random fluctuations.

Definition. For i.i.d. \(X_i\) with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(0\lt\sigma^2\lt\infty\), define \[ Z_n=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^nX_i-n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt n}. \]

Lindeberg-Levy theorem

The classical central limit theorem is the i.i.d. finite-variance case.

Theorem. Under the assumptions above, \(Z_n\Rightarrow N(0,1)\).

Proof. After standardizing, assume \(\mathbb E X_i=0\) and \(\mathbb E X_i^2=1\). The characteristic function satisfies \(\varphi(u)=1-u^2/2+o(u^2)\) at zero. Independence gives \[ \mathbb E e^{itS_n/\sqrt n}=\varphi(t/\sqrt n)^n =\left(1-\frac{t^2}{2n}+o(n^{-1})\right)^n\to e^{-t^2/2}. \] The limit is the characteristic function of \(N(0,1)\), so Lévy's theorem gives weak convergence.

Cramer-Wold and multivariate limits

Multivariate Gaussian limits are checked through one-dimensional projections.

Proposition. Random vectors \(Y_n\in\mathbb R^d\) satisfy \(Y_n\Rightarrow Y\) if and only if \(\langle t,Y_n\rangle\Rightarrow\langle t,Y\rangle\) for every \(t\in\mathbb R^d\).

Proof. If \(Y_n\Rightarrow Y\), the continuous mapping theorem gives convergence of projections. Conversely, convergence of all projections gives pointwise convergence of characteristic functions \(\varphi_{Y_n}(t)\to\varphi_Y(t)\). Lévy's continuity theorem on \(\mathbb R^d\) gives \(Y_n\Rightarrow Y\).

Remark. The theorem says normalized sums are asymptotically normal; it does not say the original observations are normal.
