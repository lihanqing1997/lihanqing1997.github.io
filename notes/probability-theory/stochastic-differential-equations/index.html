Stochastic Differential Equations

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/girsanov-theorem-change-of-measure/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/local-time-tanaka-formula/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Stochastic Differential Equations.

Definition. An SDE has the form \(dX_t=b(t,X_t)dt+\sigma(t,X_t)dB_t\), with integral meaning \(X_t=X_0+\int_0^tb(s,X_s)ds+\int_0^t\sigma(s,X_s)dB_s\). A strong solution is adapted to the Brownian filtration and uses the given Brownian motion.

Central result. If \(b\) and \(\sigma\) are globally Lipschitz in \(x\) and have linear growth, then the SDE has a unique strong solution. Moreover, solutions depend continuously on initial data in suitable \(L^p\) norms.

Proof. Picard iteration defines \(X^{(m+1)}_t=X_0+\int_0^tb(s,X^{(m)}_s)ds+\int_0^t\sigma(s,X^{(m)}_s)dB_s\). Burkholder-Davis-Gundy, Itô isometry, Lipschitz bounds, and Gronwall's inequality show the iterates are Cauchy on small intervals; concatenation gives all times. The same estimates give pathwise uniqueness.

How to read it

SDEs are random dynamical systems whose infinitesimal noise is Brownian. Existence is a fixed-point theorem in a space of adapted processes.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
