Stochastic differential equations

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/girsanov-theorem-change-of-measure/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/local-time-tanaka-formula/" %}

This topic belongs to stochastic calculus. Ordinary calculus must be modified because martingale increments have non-negligible quadratic variation. Here the focus is Stochastic differential equations.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. An SDE has the form \(dX_t=b(t,X_t)dt+\sigma(t,X_t)dB_t\), with integral meaning \(X_t=X_0+\int_0^tb(s,X_s)ds+\int_0^t\sigma(s,X_s)dB_s\). A strong solution is adapted to the Brownian filtration and uses the given Brownian motion.

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. If \(b\) and \(\sigma\) are globally Lipschitz in \(x\) and have linear growth, then the SDE has a unique strong solution. Moreover, solutions depend continuously on initial data in suitable \(L^p\) norms.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

Picard iteration defines \(X^{(m+1)}_t=X_0+\int_0^tb(s,X^{(m)}_s)ds+\int_0^t\sigma(s,X^{(m)}_s)dB_s\). Burkholder-Davis-Gundy, Itô isometry, Lipschitz bounds, and Gronwall's inequality show the iterates are Cauchy on small intervals; concatenation gives all times. The same estimates give pathwise uniqueness.

Remarks

SDEs are random dynamical systems whose infinitesimal noise is Brownian. Existence is a fixed-point theorem in a space of adapted processes.
