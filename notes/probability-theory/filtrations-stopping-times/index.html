A filtration models the flow of information through time. Stopping times are random times whose occurrence can be decided using only the information available at that time.

Filtrations and adaptedness

Processes are compared to the information revealed over time.

Definition. A filtration is an increasing family \((\mathcal F_t)_{t\in T}\) of sub-\(\sigma\)-fields of \(\mathcal F\). A process \(X_t\) is adapted if \(X_t\) is \(\mathcal F_t\)-measurable for every \(t\).

Stopping times

A stopping time cannot look into the future.

Theorem. A random time \(\tau:T\to[0,\infty]\) is a stopping time if \(\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal F_t\) for every \(t\). In discrete time this is equivalent to \(\{\tau=t\}\in\mathcal F_t\).

Proof. If \(\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal F_t\), then \(\{\tau=t\}=\{\tau\le t\}\setminus\{\tau\le t-1\}\in\mathcal F_t\) in discrete time. Conversely, \(\{\tau\le t\}=\bigcup_{s\le t}\{\tau=s\}\), a finite union of events in \(\mathcal F_t\).

Stopped sigma-field

The information available at a random time is itself a sigma-field.

Proposition. For a stopping time \(\tau\), define \[ \mathcal F_\tau=\{A\in\mathcal F:A\cap\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal F_t\ \text{for all }t\}. \] If \(X_t\) is adapted and has suitable path regularity, then \(X_\tau\) is \(\mathcal F_\tau\)-measurable.

Proof. The defining class is closed under complements and countable unions because each operation can be intersected with \(\{\tau\le t\}\) and checked inside \(\mathcal F_t\). For simple stopping times, \(X_\tau=\sum_tX_t1_{\{\tau=t\}}\), which is measurable with respect to \(\mathcal F_\tau\). General cases follow by approximation of \(\tau\) by simple stopping times and path regularity.

Remark. Typical examples are hitting times of closed sets by continuous adapted processes.
