Poisson convergence and Poisson processes

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/berry-esseen-rates/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/stable-infinitely-divisible-laws/" %}

This topic belongs to classical limit theory. The main question is how random quantities behave as the sample size or time scale grows. Here the focus is Poisson convergence and Poisson processes.

Start with the precise object. The definition states what the object is and what assumptions make the later formulas meaningful.

Definition. Poisson limits describe many rare almost-independent events. A Poisson process \((N_t)_{t\ge0}\) with rate \(\lambda\) has independent increments and \(N_t-N_s\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda(t-s))\).

These assumptions are part of the mathematics. They tell us where each expression lives and prevent informal notation from hiding a real domain issue.

The result below is the fact one usually uses in calculations or proofs. Read it as the operational content of the definition.

Result. If \(X_n\sim\operatorname{Binomial}(n,p_n)\) and \(np_n\to\lambda\), then \(X_n\Rightarrow\operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda)\). More generally, rare independent indicators converge to Poisson laws when the total success probability converges and the maximum success probability vanishes.

Proof.

The proof is easiest to read by separating the elementary case from the extension step.

For the binomial case, \(P(X_n=k)=\binom nkp_n^k(1-p_n)^{n-k}\to e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k/k!\). For the general law of small numbers, compare the probability generating functions \(\prod_i(1+p_{n,i}(z-1))\) with \(\exp\{(z-1)\sum_ip_{n,i}\}\).

Remarks

Poisson approximation is the natural discrete analogue of Gaussian approximation when individual contributions are rare rather than small and symmetric.
