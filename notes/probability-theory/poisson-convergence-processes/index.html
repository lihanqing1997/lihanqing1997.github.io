Poisson Convergence and Poisson Processes

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/berry-esseen-rates/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/stable-infinitely-divisible-laws/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Poisson Convergence and Poisson Processes.

Definition. Poisson limits describe many rare almost-independent events. A Poisson process \((N_t)_{t\ge0}\) with rate \(\lambda\) has independent increments and \(N_t-N_s\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda(t-s))\).

Central result. If \(X_n\sim\operatorname{Binomial}(n,p_n)\) and \(np_n\to\lambda\), then \(X_n\Rightarrow\operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda)\). More generally, rare independent indicators converge to Poisson laws when the total success probability converges and the maximum success probability vanishes.

Proof. For the binomial case, \(P(X_n=k)=\binom nkp_n^k(1-p_n)^{n-k}\to e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k/k!\). For the general law of small numbers, compare the probability generating functions \(\prod_i(1+p_{n,i}(z-1))\) with \(\exp\{(z-1)\sum_ip_{n,i}\}\).

How to read it

Poisson approximation is the natural discrete analogue of Gaussian approximation when individual contributions are rare rather than small and symmetric.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
