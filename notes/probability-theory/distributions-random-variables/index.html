Random variables and distributions

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/measures-extension-monotone-classes/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/independence/" %}

Definition. A random variable with values in a measurable space \((S,\mathcal S)\) is a measurable map \(X:(\Omega,\mathcal F)\to(S,\mathcal S)\). Its distribution, or law, is the pushforward measure \(\mu_X=P\circ X^{-1}\), defined by \(\mu_X(B)=P(X\in B)\).

Result. For every nonnegative or integrable measurable \(g:S\to\mathbb R\), \[\mathbb E g(X)=\int_S g(x)\,\mu_X(dx).\] Thus the law of \(X\) determines every expectation that depends only on \(X\).

Proof.

For indicators \(g=1_B\), both sides equal \(P(X\in B)\). Linearity gives the claim for nonnegative simple functions. Monotone convergence extends it to all nonnegative measurable \(g\), and decomposition into positive and negative parts gives the integrable case.

Remarks

The distribution is the intrinsic object. Different probability spaces may support random variables with the same law, and then they are indistinguishable for distributional questions.
