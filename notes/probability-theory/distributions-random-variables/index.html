Random Variables and Distributions

{% include probability-note-nav.html back_label="Back" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/probability-theory/measures-extension-monotone-classes/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/probability-theory/independence/" %}

This page records the core mathematical facts for Random Variables and Distributions.

Definition. A random variable with values in a measurable space \((S,\mathcal S)\) is a measurable map \(X:(\Omega,\mathcal F)\to(S,\mathcal S)\). Its distribution, or law, is the pushforward measure \(\mu_X=P\circ X^{-1}\), defined by \(\mu_X(B)=P(X\in B)\).

Central result. For every nonnegative or integrable measurable \(g:S\to\mathbb R\), \[\mathbb E g(X)=\int_S g(x)\,\mu_X(dx).\] Thus the law of \(X\) determines every expectation that depends only on \(X\).

Proof. For indicators \(g=1_B\), both sides equal \(P(X\in B)\). Linearity gives the claim for nonnegative simple functions. Monotone convergence extends it to all nonnegative measurable \(g\), and decomposition into positive and negative parts gives the integrable case.

How to read it

The distribution is the intrinsic object. Different probability spaces may support random variables with the same law, and then they are indistinguishable for distributional questions.

The point is not to memorize the displayed formulas separately, but to understand how the definition, the result, and the proof mechanism fit together.
