Nonlinear equation solving begins with two basic paradigms: topological bracketing and contraction-based iteration.

Root-finding problem

Definition. Given \(F:X\to Y\), the nonlinear equation problem is to find \(x\) such that \(F(x)=0\). In one dimension this can sometimes be solved by bracketing; in higher dimensions one usually needs local linearization or fixed-point structure.

Bisection theorem

Theorem. If \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and \(f(a)f(b)\le0\), bisection produces intervals of length \((b-a)2^{-k}\) containing a root. It is slow but globally reliable under only continuity and a sign change.

Fixed-point iteration

Definition. A fixed-point method rewrites \(F(x)=0\) as \(x=G(x)\) and iterates \(x_{k+1}=G(x_k)\). The same equation can lead to stable or unstable iterations depending on the chosen \(G\).

Contraction mapping theorem

Theorem. If \(G\) maps a complete metric space into itself and \(d(Gx,Gy)\le qd(x,y)\) with \(q\lt1\), then \(G\) has a unique fixed point and the iteration converges linearly to it.

Aitken acceleration

Remark. When a scalar fixed-point iteration converges linearly, sequence acceleration can estimate and remove the leading geometric error. Acceleration is useful only when the underlying iteration is already in its asymptotic regime.
