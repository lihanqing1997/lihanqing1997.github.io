Eigenvalue algorithms approximate invariant directions and spectra. The symmetric case is far better behaved than the general non-normal case.

Eigenvalue problem

Definition. The eigenvalue problem asks for \((\lambda,v)\) with \(Av=\lambda v\) and \(v\ne0\). The numerical problem may seek one eigenpair, a few extreme eigenpairs, or a Schur form containing all eigenvalues.

Power method

Proposition. If \(A\) has a dominant eigenvalue separated in modulus from the rest and the starting vector has a component in its eigenvector direction, normalized powers \(A^k x\) converge to the dominant invariant direction.

QR algorithm

Theorem. The QR algorithm repeatedly factors \(A_k=Q_kR_k\) and sets \(A_{k+1}=R_kQ_k\). With shifts and preliminary Hessenberg reduction, it is the standard stable method for dense eigenvalue computation.

Symmetric perturbation

Proposition. For symmetric matrices, eigenvalues are Lipschitz in operator norm: \(\max_j |\widehat\lambda_j-\lambda_j|\le \|E\|_2\) after suitable ordering. Eigenvectors require eigenvalue gaps for stability.

Pseudospectra

Remark. For non-normal matrices, eigenvalues can be much more sensitive than the norm of the perturbation suggests. Pseudospectra visualize where eigenvalues of nearby matrices may lie and are often more informative than the spectrum alone.
