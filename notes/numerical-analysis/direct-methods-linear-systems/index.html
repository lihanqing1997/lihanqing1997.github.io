Direct methods solve finite-dimensional linear systems by finite algebraic transformations. Their analysis is the model case for backward stability.

Gaussian elimination

Definition. Gaussian elimination factors a matrix into triangular factors by eliminating subdiagonal entries. In exact arithmetic, solving \(Ax=b\) reduces to forward and backward substitution.

LU factorization

Proposition. If the leading pivots are nonzero, \(A=LU\) with \(L\) unit lower triangular and \(U\) upper triangular. With row pivoting, one obtains \(PA=LU\), which greatly improves numerical reliability.

Backward stability with pivoting

Theorem. Gaussian elimination with partial pivoting computes the exact solution of \((A+\Delta A)\widehat x=b\), where \(\|\Delta A\|\) is small relative to \(\|A\|\) up to a growth factor. In most practical problems this gives stable solutions.

Pivot growth

Remark. Partial pivoting can have large worst-case growth, although such cases are rare. Complete pivoting, rook pivoting, scaling, or structure-aware methods may be used when the matrix is especially delicate.

Sparse systems

Remark. For sparse matrices, the symbolic pattern of elimination matters as much as arithmetic. Fill-in control, ordering, and exploiting symmetry are numerical-analysis questions, not merely programming details.
