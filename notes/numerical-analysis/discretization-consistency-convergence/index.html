Discretization replaces an infinite or continuous object by a finite one. The main question is whether the finite objects return to the original problem as resolution increases.

Discretization

Definition. A discretization is a family of finite problems \(\Phi_h(x_h)=y_h\), indexed by a resolution parameter \(h\downarrow0\), together with restriction and reconstruction maps between the continuous and discrete spaces.

Consistency

Definition. A discretization is consistent if the exact continuous solution, when inserted into the discrete equations, leaves a residual that tends to zero as \(h\downarrow0\). The rate of this residual is the local truncation order.

Stability

Definition. A discretization is stable if perturbations in the discrete data or residual produce uniformly controlled perturbations in the discrete solution. Stability is a bound on the inverse discrete operator, not a formal Taylor expansion.

Lax equivalence principle

Theorem. For a well-posed linear initial-value problem and a consistent finite-difference discretization, stability is equivalent to convergence. In short: consistency tells us we approximated the right equation, stability tells us the approximation error is not amplified, and convergence is the consequence.

Meaning of order

Remark. An order statement such as \(O(h^p)\) describes asymptotic behavior under fixed regularity assumptions. It is not a promise that the visible error decreases at rate \(p\) before the mesh is fine enough or after roundoff dominates.
