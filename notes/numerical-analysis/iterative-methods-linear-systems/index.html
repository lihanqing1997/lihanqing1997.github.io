16. Iterative methods for linear systems

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/eigenvalue-problems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/krylov-subspace-methods/" %}

Iterative methods replace exact factorization by a sequence of approximate solutions, which is essential for large sparse systems.

Stationary iteration

Definition. A stationary linear iteration has the form \(x_{k+1}=Gx_k+c\). It converges for every initial point exactly when the spectral radius \(\rho(G)\lt1\).

Jacobi and Gauss-Seidel

Proposition. Splitting \(A=D-L-U\) gives Jacobi and Gauss-Seidel iterations by solving with different easy parts of \(A\). Their convergence depends on matrix structure such as diagonal dominance or positive definiteness.

Residual correction

Definition. Most iterative methods can be read as repeatedly correcting the residual \(r_k=b-Ax_k\). A good correction approximately solves \(Ae_k=r_k\) without solving the full system exactly.

Conjugate gradient

Theorem. For symmetric positive definite \(A\), conjugate gradient minimizes the \(A\)-norm of the error over \(x_0+\mathcal K_k(A,r_0)\). Its convergence depends on the distribution of eigenvalues, especially the effective condition number.

Stopping rules

Remark. A small residual is not the same as a small error unless conditioning is controlled. Practical stopping rules combine relative residuals, preconditioned residuals, iteration budgets, and the accuracy required by the outer problem.
