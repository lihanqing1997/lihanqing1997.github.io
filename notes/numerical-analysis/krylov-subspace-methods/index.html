17. Krylov subspace methods

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/iterative-methods-linear-systems/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/preconditioning/" %}

Krylov methods approximate large matrix problems using only matrix-vector products, making them central for sparse systems and large eigenvalue problems.

Krylov subspace

Definition. The \(k\)-th Krylov subspace generated by \(A\) and \(r\) is \(\mathcal K_k(A,r)=\operatorname{span}\{r,Ar,\ldots,A^{k-1}r\}\). It contains polynomial approximations \(p_{k-1}(A)r\).

Arnoldi iteration

Proposition. Arnoldi iteration constructs an orthonormal basis of \(\mathcal K_k\) and a Hessenberg projection of \(A\). The projected small problem provides approximations to eigenvalues or linear-system corrections.

Lanczos iteration

Proposition. For symmetric \(A\), Arnoldi reduces to Lanczos iteration with a tridiagonal projected matrix. This three-term recurrence is efficient but may require reorthogonalization in finite precision.

GMRES

Theorem. GMRES chooses \(x_k\in x_0+\mathcal K_k(A,r_0)\) minimizing \(\|b-Ax_k\|_2\). Its convergence is governed by polynomial approximation of zero on the relevant spectral or pseudospectral set.

Finite precision behavior

Remark. Krylov methods are mathematically projection methods, but their actual behavior depends on loss of orthogonality, restart choices, and preconditioning. These are part of the numerical method, not implementation afterthoughts.
