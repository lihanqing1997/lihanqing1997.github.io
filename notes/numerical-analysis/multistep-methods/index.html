24. Multistep methods

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/stability-and-stiffness/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/boundary-value-problems/" %}

Multistep methods reuse previous values and slopes. They are efficient, but their stability theory has an additional root condition.

Linear multistep method

Definition. A linear multistep method has the form \(\sum_{j=0}^k \alpha_j y_{n+j}=h\sum_{j=0}^k\beta_j f(t_{n+j},y_{n+j})\). It is explicit if \(\beta_k=0\) and implicit otherwise.

Consistency

Definition. Consistency requires the method to reproduce constant solutions and first derivatives correctly. In coefficient form this imposes \(\rho(1)=0\) and \(\rho'(1)=\sigma(1)\), where \(\rho,\sigma\) are the characteristic polynomials.

Zero-stability

Theorem. A linear multistep method is zero-stable exactly when every root of \(\rho\) lies in the closed unit disk and roots on the unit circle are simple. This prevents parasitic modes from growing when \(h\to0\).

Dahlquist equivalence

Theorem. For linear multistep methods, consistency plus zero-stability is equivalent to convergence. This is the ODE analogue of the consistency-stability-convergence pattern.

Adams and BDF families

Remark. Adams methods are built from polynomial interpolation of the right-hand side. Backward differentiation formulas approximate \(\mathrm{d}y/\mathrm{d}t\) using backward differences and are important for stiff equations.
