14. Least squares problems

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/matrix-factorizations/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/eigenvalue-problems/" %}

Least squares is both a geometric projection problem and a basic numerical subroutine for data fitting, inverse problems, and discretized equations.

Linear least squares

Definition. The linear least-squares problem seeks \(x\) minimizing \(\|Ax-b\|_2\). If \(A\) has full column rank, the solution is unique and satisfies the normal equations \(A^T A x=A^T b\).

Orthogonal projection

Proposition. The residual \(r=b-A\widehat x\) of a least-squares solution is orthogonal to the column space of \(A\). Thus least squares computes the orthogonal projection of \(b\) onto \(\operatorname{col}(A)\).

Normal equations warning

Theorem. Solving least squares through \(A^TA\) squares the condition number: \(\kappa(A^TA)=\kappa(A)^2\). QR or SVD methods are therefore preferred when numerical accuracy matters.

Rank deficiency

Definition. When \(A\) is rank deficient or nearly rank deficient, the minimum-norm least-squares solution is expressed by the pseudoinverse \(A^+b\). Truncated SVD gives a regularized version by discarding small singular directions.

Weighted and generalized least squares

Remark. Weighted least squares changes the geometry of the residual norm. In statistical problems this geometry is often determined by the covariance of the noise, but the numerical question remains how to solve the resulting system stably.
