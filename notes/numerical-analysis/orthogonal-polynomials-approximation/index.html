7. Orthogonal polynomials and approximation

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/polynomial-interpolation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/splines-piecewise-approximation/" %}

Approximation theory asks for the best finite representation of a function under a chosen norm or inner product. Orthogonality turns this into geometry.

Best approximation

Definition. For a normed space \(X\) and finite-dimensional subspace \(V_n\), a best approximation to \(f\) is an element \(v_n\in V_n\) minimizing \(\|f-v\|\) over \(v\in V_n\). The answer depends strongly on the norm.

Projection theorem

Theorem. In a Hilbert space, the best approximation from a closed subspace \(V\) is the orthogonal projection \(P_Vf\), characterized by \(\langle f-P_Vf,v\rangle=0\) for every \(v\in V\). This is the prototype of Galerkin methods.

Orthogonal polynomial expansion

Proposition. If \(\{p_k\}\) is orthogonal for weight \(w\) on \([a,b]\), then the least-squares polynomial approximation is obtained by truncating the series of coefficients \(\langle f,p_k\rangle_w/\langle p_k,p_k\rangle_w\).

Chebyshev approximation

Remark. Chebyshev polynomials organize near-minimax approximation on an interval. Their equioscillation behavior explains why they appear in interpolation nodes, spectral methods, and stable polynomial evaluation.

Approximation versus computation

Remark. A good approximation space is not enough; the basis must also be numerically usable. Orthogonal or nearly orthogonal bases usually behave better than monomial bases because coefficient perturbations are less amplified.
