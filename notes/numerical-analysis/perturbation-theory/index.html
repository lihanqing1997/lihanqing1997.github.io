Perturbation theory turns sensitivity into inequalities. It is the language behind condition numbers for roots, linear systems, eigenvalues, and least squares.

First-order perturbation expansion

Lemma. If \(\Phi\) is differentiable at \(x\), then \(\Phi(x+\Delta x)-\Phi(x)=D\Phi(x)\Delta x+o(\|\Delta x\|)\). The derivative is therefore the first-order amplifier of data error.

Linear systems

Proposition. For \(Ax=b\), perturbations satisfy approximately \(A\Delta x=\Delta b-\Delta A\,x\). Hence \(\|\Delta x\|/\|x\|\) is controlled by \(\kappa(A)(\|\Delta A\|/\|A\|+\|\Delta b\|/\|b\|)\), when the perturbations are small.

Simple eigenvalues

Theorem. If \(A v=\lambda v\) and \(w^\ast A=\lambda w^\ast\) with \(w^\ast v\ne0\), then a perturbation \(E\) changes the eigenvalue by \(\Delta\lambda=(w^\ast E v)/(w^\ast v)+o(\|E\|)\). Non-normal matrices may therefore have very sensitive eigenvalues.

Least squares

Proposition. The sensitivity of a least-squares solution depends on both the condition of \(A\) and the size of the residual. Problems with nearly rank-deficient design matrices require orthogonal factorizations or regularization rather than the normal equations alone.

Proof strategy

Proof. Perturbation bounds are usually obtained by linearizing the defining equation, solving the linearized equation, and then bounding the neglected higher-order term. The same template recurs in roots, eigenpairs, singular values, and discretized differential equations.
