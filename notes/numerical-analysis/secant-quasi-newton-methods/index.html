Secant and quasi-Newton methods approximate derivative information when exact Jacobians are expensive, noisy, or unavailable.

Secant method

Definition. For scalar root finding, the secant method replaces \(f'(x_k)\) by the slope through \(x_{k-1}\) and \(x_k\). It uses two previous points and avoids explicit derivative evaluation.

Order of secant convergence

Theorem. Under standard smoothness and simple-root assumptions, the secant method converges superlinearly with order \((1+\sqrt5)/2\). It is slower than Newton per iteration but often cheaper per step.

Quasi-Newton equation

Definition. For systems, a quasi-Newton matrix \(B_k\) approximates the Jacobian and is updated to satisfy the secant equation \(B_{k+1}s_k=y_k\), where \(s_k=x_{k+1}-x_k\) and \(y_k=F(x_{k+1})-F(x_k)\).

Broyden methods

Proposition. Broyden updates choose a low-rank correction satisfying the secant equation while changing the current Jacobian approximation as little as possible in a chosen norm. They are useful for large nonlinear systems.

Derivative-free boundary

Remark. Derivative-free methods are not automatically more robust. They trade derivative cost for interpolation, sampling, or finite-difference error, so conditioning and noise still decide what accuracy is realistic.
