Finite difference methods approximate partial derivatives on grids. Their analysis combines Taylor expansion, stability, and discrete operator theory.

Finite difference operator

Definition. A finite difference operator replaces derivatives by differences of nearby grid values. For example, the centered second difference \((u_{i-1}-2u_i+u_{i+1})/h^2\) approximates \(\partial^2 u/\partial x^2\).

Elliptic model problem

Proposition. The standard finite difference discretization of the Dirichlet Poisson problem produces a sparse linear system whose matrix is a discrete Laplacian. Maximum principles and energy estimates give stability.

Heat equation stability

Theorem. For the explicit heat scheme, stability requires a parabolic step restriction of the form \(\Delta t\le C h^2\). Implicit schemes relax this restriction but require linear solves.

Hyperbolic CFL condition

Theorem. For wave or transport equations, the time step must respect a Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy condition: numerical information must propagate at least as fast as physical information. Otherwise convergence fails even if the formal truncation error is small.

Discrete conservation

Remark. For conservation laws, the discretization should respect flux balance and entropy conditions. Smooth Taylor analysis alone does not capture shocks or weak solutions.
