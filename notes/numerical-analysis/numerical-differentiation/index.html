Numerical differentiation is delicate because differentiation amplifies high-frequency error and rounding noise.

Finite differences

Definition. The forward difference \((f(x+h)-f(x))/h\), backward difference \((f(x)-f(x-h))/h\), and centered difference \((f(x+h)-f(x-h))/(2h)\) approximate \(\mathrm{d}f/\mathrm{d}x\).

Truncation error

Proposition. If \(f\) is smooth, Taylor expansion gives first-order error for one-sided differences and second-order error for centered differences. Higher-order formulas are obtained by forcing more Taylor coefficients to cancel.

Roundoff barrier

Theorem. For finite precision data, the differentiation error has the typical form \(C_1h^p+C_2u/h\). The optimal step size balances truncation error against cancellation and is not obtained by taking \(h\) as small as possible.

Ill-posedness

Remark. Differentiation is not a continuous operation from uniform noisy data to uniform derivative estimates. Reliable numerical differentiation therefore needs smoothness assumptions, regularization, or model-based structure.

Complex-step derivative

Proposition. For analytic real-valued functions extendable to complex inputs, \(\operatorname{Im} f(x+ih)/h\) approximates \(f'(x)\) without subtractive cancellation. This is a useful computational trick when complex arithmetic is available.
