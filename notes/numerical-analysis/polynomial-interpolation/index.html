Polynomial interpolation is the simplest finite representation of a function, but it already shows the tension between approximation power and numerical stability.

Interpolation polynomial

Definition. Given distinct nodes \(x_0,\ldots,x_n\) and values \(f(x_j)\), the interpolation polynomial \(p_n\) is the unique polynomial of degree at most \(n\) satisfying \(p_n(x_j)=f(x_j)\) for all \(j\).

Lagrange formula

Proposition. The interpolant is \(p_n(x)=\sum_{j=0}^n f(x_j)\ell_j(x)\), where \(\ell_j(x)=\prod_{m\ne j}(x-x_m)/(x_j-x_m)\). This formula proves existence and uniqueness, but is not always the best computational form.

Error formula

Theorem. If \(f\in C^{n+1}[a,b]\), then for each \(x\) there is \(\xi_x\in(a,b)\) such that \(f(x)-p_n(x)=f^{(n+1)}(\xi_x)\prod_{j=0}^n(x-x_j)/(n+1)!\). The node geometry is therefore as important as smoothness.

Runge phenomenon

Remark. High-degree interpolation at equally spaced nodes can diverge near the endpoints even for smooth functions. Chebyshev nodes reduce the growth of the interpolation operator and are usually the right baseline for global polynomial interpolation.

Divided differences

Definition. Divided differences build the Newton form of the interpolating polynomial incrementally. They are useful for adding nodes, estimating derivatives, and understanding interpolation as repeated slope extraction.
