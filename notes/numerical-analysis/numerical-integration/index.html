10. Numerical integration

{% include note-topic-nav.html back_label="Back" back_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/" prev_label="Last page" prev_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/numerical-differentiation/" next_label="Next page" next_url="/notes/numerical-analysis/adaptive-integration/" %}

Numerical integration replaces an integral by a weighted finite sum. The central questions are exactness, smoothness, and error control.

Quadrature rule

Definition. A quadrature rule approximates \(\int_a^b f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x\) by \(\sum_{j=1}^n w_j f(x_j)\). The nodes \(x_j\) and weights \(w_j\) determine the degree of exactness and stability of the rule.

Newton-Cotes rules

Proposition. Newton-Cotes rules integrate the polynomial interpolant through equally spaced nodes. The trapezoidal and Simpson rules are the most stable low-order examples; high-order closed Newton-Cotes rules may have undesirable weights.

Gaussian quadrature

Theorem. For a positive weight on an interval, the \(n\)-point Gaussian quadrature rule chooses nodes as zeros of an orthogonal polynomial and is exact for all polynomials of degree at most \(2n-1\).

Error representation

Proposition. Quadrature error can be expressed through derivatives, Peano kernels, or orthogonal polynomial remainders. Each representation emphasizes a different cause of error: smoothness, endpoint behavior, or projection residual.

Oscillatory and singular integrals

Remark. No single quadrature rule is universally best. Oscillation, endpoint singularities, high dimension, and nonsmoothness each require specialized transformations or adaptive strategies.
