Spectral methods use global basis functions such as Fourier or Chebyshev modes. For smooth solutions, they can converge much faster than low-order local methods.

Spectral approximation

Definition. A spectral method approximates a function by a truncated expansion in global basis functions, typically Fourier modes for periodic domains or Chebyshev polynomials on intervals.

Spectral convergence

Theorem. For analytic functions, coefficients in suitable spectral expansions decay exponentially. Truncation error can therefore decay faster than any algebraic power of the resolution parameter.

Pseudospectral collocation

Definition. A pseudospectral method represents the solution globally but enforces equations at collocation nodes. Fast transforms make many Fourier and Chebyshev implementations efficient.

Aliasing

Proposition. Nonlinear products generate frequencies beyond the truncated basis. Without dealiasing, these unresolved modes fold back into lower modes and contaminate the computation.

Smoothness boundary

Remark. Spectral methods are excellent for smooth solutions on simple domains. Discontinuities, shocks, complex geometry, and local singularities often favor finite volume, finite difference, or finite element methods.
